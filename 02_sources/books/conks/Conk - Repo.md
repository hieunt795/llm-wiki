# The Repo Market Blindspot - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-blindspot
> the most opaque area of financial markets is about to be unveiled

---
Monetary leaders are about to uncover the inner workings of an obscure $2 trillion market, the most opaque ecosystem within the U.S. dollar funding complex. This, however, is not the much-hyped Eurodollar system. Instead, the Repo Market Blindspot™ is about to be unveiled.

In the midst of the 2008 crisis, the Fed and the U.S. Treasury received an abrupt wake-up call. The failure of Lehman Brothers started a run in every major market for dollars, from FX swaps to money market funds to commercial paper, not only onshore but globally. The illusion that onshore dollars and offshore dollars were identical had been obliterated. The rate to borrow (real) interbank dollars, [Federal Funds](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr), disconnected from LIBOR, the — now [defunct](https://www.db.com/news/detail/20230703-the-end-of-libor?language_id=1) — rate to borrow unsecured dollars offshore. “Eurodollars” were in scarce supply.

Faced with unrivaled monetary complexity, leaders had little choice but to not only inject billions into — what had become — global Wall Street banks but quell a global dollar shortage by firing up its [swap lines](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/bst_liquidityswaps.htm). By supplying as many dollars as needed via its funding facilities, the Fed preserved the stability of the global dollar paradigm. The major financial players and wider market participants then deemed this as an unofficial pledge from authorities to provide enough liquidity to stem any future shortage. A permanent bridge between onshore and offshore dollars had been constructed.

Ever since the GFC (global financial crisis), the implicit promise from authorities to make onshore and offshore dollars fungible persisted, no matter the cost. By delivering endless dollar liquidity through FX swap lines and a cluster of other programs, the Fed solidified its “[global jaws](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7612582f-5986-40f3-919d-49cde7f70f88_3880x2860.png)” — minus a [few defects](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-global-dilemma). But even this became a sideshow. In the decade following the subprime tumult, the global regulatory establishment set out to transform the monetary system entirely.

Slowly but surely, leaders chose to drive systemic risk away from banks and park it elsewhere. Through a series of ever more stringent [regulatory actions](https://www.bis.org/basel_framework/), banks were prohibited from taking on too much risk and leverage, and the shadow banks assumed the banks’ riskier duties. Using U.S. Treasuries and agency MBS (mortgage-backed securities) to secure most of their trades via repo, money market funds and dealers — the new major plumbers of the monetary system — happily absorbed new hazards, assuming they were now backed by U.S. authorities’ willingness to intervene.

Fast forward to today, as shown by the COVID market meltdown, every subsequent dollar emergency has failed to push the system beyond its boundaries. With unlimited liquidity support from monetary leaders, the shadow banking layer has remained resilient. Sensing victory, however, authorities have made it their number-one mission to stamp out all remaining chokepoints in the system.

NCCBR (non-centrally cleared bilateral repo) highlighted in red

One particularly obscure market has been highlighted as a potential blindspot: “non-centrally cleared bilateral repo”, the uncleared market for secured cash loans. Known unofficially as NCCBR, uncleared bilateral repo is a market primarily for hedge funds to obtain enormous amounts of leverage at very low costs.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2mzN!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F36eb2711-1c95-48b7-ab69-263219c71792_5519x3581.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2mzN!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F36eb2711-1c95-48b7-ab69-263219c71792_5519x3581.png)

Even so, it has not only grown into the most obscure part of the repo market. It has become the largest, sporting an estimated $2 trillion in outstanding agreements. Authorities are now moving in to subdue the rising risk of instability in this obscure ecosystem. But as we’ll soon discover, it’s not that straightforward.

The biggest shift caused by regulators’ obsession with removing systemic risk was a move from “bank-based” to “market-based” finance. With [regulations](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-global-dilemma) like the [SLR](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa45fc144-56b1-465b-a9db-3630f405c80d_864x385.jpeg) (Supplementary Leverage Ratio) and [LCR](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb2818dc5-0acc-4b96-b2e5-c241d0bc4b92_4300x1394.png) (Liquidity Coverage Ratio) in play, the ability of big banks to not only make markets but lend to one another was impeded. Activity in the interbank market collapsed, with Fed Funds (bank reserves) volumes dropping around 90%.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!i1vT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F272990fc-d05a-4979-8204-c58842d53aa7_1424x988.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!i1vT!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F272990fc-d05a-4979-8204-c58842d53aa7_1424x988.png)

Source: New York Fed

Global financial players started to transition away from unsecured lending almost entirely and onto a [secured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-silent-monetary-revolution), where [repo](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdf6f675f-fe9c-482e-85ae-1783a892bbc5_2781x2388.png) — the secured cash loans market — began to play a dominant role. Eventually, trillions of dollars in secured lending took place daily. But as leaders started to declare triumph, reality quickly demonstrated that even the secured standard was not enough to eliminate market disruptions.

In September 2019, the “[repocalypse](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/epr/2021/epr_2021_market-events_afonso.html)” struck. Repo rates shot above the Fed’s target range, destroying the illusion of stability in the new “secured era”. The most common explanation was a sea of corporate tax payments and Treasury issuance sucking too many bank reserves out of the system. Yet despite all the financial players involved knowing the liquidity drain was approaching, repo market turmoil still ensued. Something else was afoot. The sheer complexity and opaqueness of the repo markets made the causality impossible to diagnose, even today. The Fed had no option but to intervene.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1Rh3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0253e212-cbf8-4a05-a4aa-b81fa33b822c_800x574.png)

Source: Chicago Fed

The repocalypse wasn’t the only episode of instability in repo markets. Fast forward to the COVID-19 market meltdown, and repo markets once again started to collapse. Economic and political indecision surrounding the pandemic, combined with very restraints regulators had imposed on monetary plumbers, led to severe disruptions. A global “dash for U.S. dollars” caused dealers’ Treasury inventories to expand enough to hit their balance sheet limits, impeding their ability to make markets. Meanwhile, hedge funds [started](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBr_2020_01_Basis-Trades.pdf) to unwind popular trades, exacerbating the repo turmoil. To handle distress, the Fed [expanded](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2020/08/the-federal-reserves-large-scale-repo-program/) its operations by offering to lend up to $500 billion dollars daily via its repo facilities. Market players perceived this as the Federal Reserve willing to provide as much liquidity as was needed to regain stability. Subsequently, rates and volatility decreased rapidly. Disaster had been averted.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3s06!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F134a0cdd-ed16-446c-a014-1f1fadf7565d_2500x1854.png)

But even though the secured standard had proven shaky, monetary leaders persisted. Having already committed to a system overhaul for over a decade, the unsecured age of global finance was done and dusted.

Though delicate, the secured standard has arguably proven to be more stable than its predecessor. But like the unsecured standard, monetary leaders will once more try to paint over weaknesses in the new paradigm, with uncleared bilateral repo at the top of their agenda. The COVID-19 repo turmoil has attracted the gaze of the regulatory establishment, as hedge funds continue to use high amounts of leverage on trades they don’t have to report to regulators. Sweeping measures, however, are set to be introduced that will, for the first time, reveal detailed information on activities in the NCCBR market.

Back in January 2023, six months after gathering pilot data from major securities dealers, the Office of Financial Research (OFR) [announced](https://www.financialresearch.gov/press-releases/2023/01/05/office-of-financial-research-releases-proposal-to-collect-data-on-certain-repo-transactions/) it was introducing a regular NCCBR data collection. Until then, no central source on the most opaque corner of the most important dollar funding market had existed. The initial release revealed a potential $2 trillion — $1.19 trillion in reverse repos and $0.94 trillion in repos — in outstanding repo agreements. The U.S. Treasury [responded](https://www.federalregister.gov/documents/2023/01/09/2022-28615/collection-of-non-centrally-cleared-bilateral-transactions-in-the-us-repurchase-agreement-market#:~:text=The%20final%20segment%20of%20the,settled%20on%20a%20DVP%20basis.) with proposed standards to liberate the inner workings of this obscure market.

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!6Vh-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff0420015-d5ee-43dc-af23-9d279f008c03_1501x1255.png)

Still, mandating NCCBR participants like hedge funds to report highly leveraged trading activities may fail to increase financial stability. The dynamics of the NCCBR market are intricate. The ultimatum by regulators will be to persuade the hedge fund community to exit from dubious uncleared markets and transition toward the “central clearing” complex, run by the sole CCP (central counterparty) in repo: the [FICC](https://www.dtcc.com/about/businesses-and-subsidiaries/ficc) (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation). With centrally cleared trades, a central counterparty takes both sides, known as netting, which reduces counterparty risk and balance sheet constraints for dealers.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GuAr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fae8c224a-c4bd-4c34-8daa-9a5c516ea954_2290x1167.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GuAr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fae8c224a-c4bd-4c34-8daa-9a5c516ea954_2290x1167.png)

But as regulators dive into the particulars, they will discover central clearing will seem unappealing for the very hedge funds they’re trying to convince. In uncleared bilateral repo, dealers make markets for hedge funds with lower “haircuts” on collateral, meaning they obtain more cash for the securities pledged, hence obtaining higher amounts of leverage. This is key to executing some of the most popular trades, like the Treasury Futures basis. Moreover, cleared repo markets only support the pledging of Treasuries and agency securities to secure trades, whereas participants in NCCBR like to pledge a wider range of collateral — even equities.

With few benefits, data transparency and the move onto central clearing will likely fail to have a sizable impact on financial stability. When turmoil inevitably erupts, the only tool that can save the day is Fed’s monetary alchemy. Even without opaqueness, the complexity of the NCCBR dooms the Fed into intervening. The U.S. central bank’s “volatility suppressor” will, once again, come to the rescue.

# Demystifying the Repo Market - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market
> a deep dive into the "base layer" of repo

---
Every day in global finance, trillions of dollars are transacted in an obscure but crucial funding market: the market for repurchase agreements, or “repo” for short. The world has slowly shifted onto a [secured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-rise-of-the-secured-standard), where shadow banks play an increasingly central role in the monetary system, using repos as a major wheel greaser. Uncovering the mysteries of the repo market has thus become crucial to understanding modern monetary plumbing.

[

In this comprehensive subscriber special, we’ll demystify the seemingly intricate triparty repo market — the “base layer” of repo — unveiling in detail its origins, daily cycle, monetary plumbing, and flows, all mapped out using our trademark visuals. Let’s dive in.

Back in the 1950s, a band of Wall Street dealers rejoined the banking industry, armed with what was deemed a highly remarkable practice. By selling a security and vowing to repurchase it at a later date, these major participants had created a financial instrument that served as a bank deposit but did not function as one, thereby evading banking regulations. In what was originally dubbed “non-deposit deposits,” repos involved one financial entity lending cash, secured against another entity’s collateral, for a fee. Corporations and institutions with surplus cash temporarily purchased debt securities from so-called shadow banks (i.e. dealers), who were looking to acquire short-term dollar funding. After the cash lender paid for the security using traditional deposits at its commercial bank, the cash borrower issued the lender a “cash equivalent liability”, essentially an agreement to repurchase the security sometime in the future. In short, repos became a financial mechanism to not only bypass regulation via reshaping traditional bank deposits but also to reduce the risk of financial loss if borrowers fell into bankruptcy.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GZsP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fff45e75f-f563-4eb8-8374-46cf10a2f879_6415x5418.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GZsP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fff45e75f-f563-4eb8-8374-46cf10a2f879_6415x5418.png)

Repos are widely associated with evading state regulation, yet the U.S. government grew to become the major party responsible for the market’s expansion. The epic rise of repos in the 20th century was driven by monetary leaders’ desire to skirt their own rules to preserve financial stability. Pushing the boundaries of the Federal Reserve Act in the 1950s, officials began lending cash overnight via repos to a select few entities. This enabled prominent dealers — who came to be known as primary dealers — to easily absorb Treasury supply that other private sector entities couldn’t, while also enabling the Federal Reserve to preserve its independence from the U.S. Treasury. Two decades later, the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC) approved the first money market fund (MMF), the [Reserve Primary Fund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reserve_Primary_Fund), which became the primary cash lender in repo markets. The Reserve Fund’s assets under management rose so rapidly that it created demand for more deposit-like investments, such as repos.

By the 1990s, the U.S. government had fully embraced shadow banking. Congress amended Section 13(3) of the Federal Reserve Act to allow the Fed to backstop dealers in times of crisis. The Fed’s use of repos to support dealers gave money funds even more confidence to “deposit” money with shadow banks. By providing an implicit bailout, the Fed drove cash lenders to treat repos more like bank deposits and caused dealers to become more “bank-like”. Repo was thus set to become one of the largest funding markets on the planet.

Today, even though laws against shadow banks accepting deposits remain in effect, the repo market has grown into a $4 trillion-a-day funding market. Bank reserves and deposits are regularly swapped for Treasuries and other securities, which are then swapped for repos used to fund loans, obtain leverage, and many other functions. The major objective of the repo market is to provide consistent liquidity to financial players that need it, and — [most of the time](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-global-dollar-endgame) — it succeeds. For all systemically important players, today’s repo markets make Treasuries and other government securities virtually equivalent to cash.

Powering the multi-trillion-dollar repo engine is a hidden hierarchy of dealers, who provide liquidity by running “matched books” resembling a typical balance sheet (_see the “triparty repo market makers” section in the infographic below_). The repo hierarchy is determined by the size, power, and relationships of dealers and also the different dynamics, players, and rates present in each type of repo market. The most powerful and prominent have access to every market, hence they borrow at the cheapest rates and lend at the widest spreads. Smaller dealers, meanwhile, pick up the leftovers.

The most noteworthy market is triparty repo, the “base layer” of repo markets. Here, nearly all players operate solely as cash lenders or borrowers. The Fed’s primary dealers, large broker-dealers, and other financial participants with significant market power obtain cash at the cheapest rates primarily from MMFs (money market funds) in triparty repo, then lend at a spread in other repo markets (i_n the diagram above, that means borrowing at green rates and lending at blue, pink, and red rates)_.

While the major cash borrowers are the Fed’s primary dealers…

…the major cash lenders are asset managers (primarily money market funds and some large hedge funds), commercial banks, and securities lenders (banks and other participants that lend out their securities inventories). Smaller lenders, meanwhile, include government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs), like Federal Home Loan Banks (FHLBs), Fannie Mae, and Freddie Mac, plus state and municipality treasurers. The Federal Reserve is also active in triparty repo, as it uses the venue to conduct open market operations. Moreover, the U.S. central bank is the only major participant to engage in both triparty repo borrowing and lending.

Due to the fairly regulated nature of triparty repo, the vast majority of cash loans made by these triparty lenders are backed by U.S. Treasuries and other government debt securities, such as agency bonds and agency MBS (mortgage-backed securities).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V7H3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc97c0c37-0b85-41bd-8d93-dbdaacadffd6_7911x3075.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V7H3!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc97c0c37-0b85-41bd-8d93-dbdaacadffd6_7911x3075.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Transactions are facilitated by the BNYM (Bank of New York Mellon), a triparty custodian, which lowers the risks for cash lenders compared to “hold in custody” (HIC) repos. With a HIC repo, the cash borrower doesn’t deliver the collateral but merely pledges it to the cash lender. This exposes them to fraud and operational blunders, plus it makes recovery difficult if the borrower defaults. The BNYM’s triparty platform has allowed less experienced market players to participate in repo lending while minimizing the risks associated with it. By handling the most difficult and dangerous tasks related to clearing and settlement, the custodian bank has made it possible to borrow and lend in repo without the operational burdens and risks. As a result, the triparty repo market has become incredibly efficient.

In 2016, BNYM became the only custodian in repo after JPMorgan exited the business. Post-crisis regulation made the Wall Street giant’s repo clearing operation — a high-volume, low-margin enterprise — too capital-intensive compared to other banking businesses. After the [secured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-rise-of-the-secured-standard) was enforced, regulators deemed JP Morgan the most complex and interconnected G-SIB (global systemically important bank), resulting in major penalties ([capital surcharges](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/ccyb_gsib/index.htm)) for clearing government securities. When it threw in the towel completely, JP Morgan was only clearing about 15% of the market, compared to BNYM’s dominant 85% share.

When the BNYM’s triparty repo platform opens at 7am (EST time), the trading day begins. Repos are overnight loans, so most participants have already decided by market open whether or not they need to lend or borrow cash. Peak activity therefore lies between 8am to 9am, with the vast majority of transactions cleared before lunchtime.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0twS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05964def-a3f0-4082-b3db-73aff50e96bc_7529x4181.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!0twS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05964def-a3f0-4082-b3db-73aff50e96bc_7529x4181.png)

Source: BNY in 2021 (recent data is not publicly available)

In the most common transaction, the Fed’s primary dealers (such as UBS and Deutsche Bank) and asset managers (like Fidelity and Vanguard) enter into a triparty repo trade agreement early in the morning, usually before 9am. Between 10am and 3pm, both parties submit trade instructions via data files to the BNYM’s triparty settlement platform. These instructions summarize the details, including the duration of the loan, the amount of cash lent, the type and value of collateral pledged, and the repo rate.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I_6x!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5cf9046d-48ed-44d4-a313-bce8a373e325_6462x6054.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!I_6x!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5cf9046d-48ed-44d4-a313-bce8a373e325_6462x6054.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Two hours before the triparty market closes, the Fed opens its RRP (reverse repo) facility and accepts offers to lend cash against securities from its mammoth portfolio. Via the BNYM’s triparty platform, the Fed pledges a U.S. Treasury security from its [SOMA](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/soma-holdings) (system open market account) to receive cash from RRP (reverse repo) [counterparties](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/rrp_counterparties) — authorized money market funds, banks, and GSEs (government-sponsored enterprises). While both the cash and the Treasuries are custodied at BNYM, the Fed pays the cash lender an overnight interest rate: O/N RRP. Because this is considered the risk-free repo rate, nobody is willing to lend below it. O/N RRP therefore keeps rates within the Fed’s policy target range while creating a floor, a base layer for money market rates via the triparty system. Rates can still drop below what the Fed pays via its O/N RRP facility as not all entities have access to the Fed’s facility. Only a select number of banks, government-sponsored enterprises — Federal Home Loan Banks, and money market funds (MMFs) have gained admission. Moreover, overnight reverse repos through the Fed’s triparty repo platform are, by far, the worse choice for those looking to invest overnight cash. Since liquidity is locked in until late afternoon, some entities with RRP access will prioritize access to their funds over higher returns. All this results in a “leaky” Fed floor in money markets.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3Hyw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F097dc00e-b0c6-4f86-b779-10f46c418cfe_5309x3511.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3Hyw!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F097dc00e-b0c6-4f86-b779-10f46c418cfe_5309x3511.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Recently, a large part of the volume in triparty repo involves the Fed lending to RRP counterparties (mostly MMFs) at O/N RRP. Since the financial system is already flush with cash — as evidenced by [$1 trillion](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD) in the RRP facility — dealers have access to all the liquidity they need. This has driven private repo rates low enough to equal what the Fed pays at the RRP facility. As lending from the Fed is considered “risk-free”, MMFs mostly lend to the U.S. central bank instead of private sector borrowers unless it’s at a significant premium. Until a large amount of liquidity somehow leaves the system, this scenario will persist.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1_ZL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb0d9233c-70d8-4198-a9ca-b3c8f87248a3_5574x3768.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1_ZL!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb0d9233c-70d8-4198-a9ca-b3c8f87248a3_5574x3768.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Fifteen minutes after the RRP closes at 1:15pm, the Fed opens its standing repo facility (SRF), allowing [counterparties](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/standing-repo-facility-counterparties#standing-repo-counterparties) to borrow cash via the BNYM triparty platform. The SRF opens every business day from 1:30pm to 1:45pm, unless “otherwise stated,” which implies that the facility will remain open all hours during repo market disruption. Morning operations will also be available from June 2025. Eligible participants pledge either U.S. Treasuries, agency mortgage-backed securities, or agency debt to receive cash funding. In the opposite dynamic to the RRP facility, the SRF keeps rates within the Fed’s policy target range but this time enforces a “ceiling”. No sane entity will be willing to borrow at a higher cost than the Fed’s penalty rate, because it’s likely the cheapest alternative.

Almost a couple of hours later at 3:30 pm, the triparty platform starts processing and settling trades. Two types require execution: maturing trades that both parties want to roll over and new trades that need initiating. The BNYM system allocates dollars provided by the cash lender and collateral provided by the cash borrower. Both parties transfer their assets from external accounts into accounts on the triparty platform. The BNYM’s settlement system then transfers the cash from the lender’s cash account to the borrower’s cash account and the collateral from the borrower’s security account to the lender’s security account.

Collateral from triparty trades remains locked in the BNYM’s settlement system overnight until the trade unwinds at 3:30 pm the following day. At this time, the triparty platform executes the closing leg of the repo trade by returning securities to the cash borrower and cash plus repo interest to the cash lender. Any triparty trades without further instruction to roll over will unwind. If the cash borrower cannot fully pay off the loan, the BNYM will provide intraday credit via a dedicated facility to complete the transaction.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XuFI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F708a1e0a-9427-4e0c-a236-93fa3deba460_6462x6054.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XuFI!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F708a1e0a-9427-4e0c-a236-93fa3deba460_6462x6054.png)

the same graphic as above; added for convenience

As is now apparent, the triparty repo market has grown into a well-oiled dollar funding machine, providing trillions in cash loans to the most systemic financial participants. Systemic hazards, however, still linger.

Once the surplus of cash in the Fed’s RRP facility dries up, the stability of the triparty repo market will once again be tested. The Fed’s QT (quantitative tightening) and the U.S. Treasury’s giant debt issuance will continue to pull funding away from repo, perhaps causing a repeat of the September 2019 “[repocalypse](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-global-dollar-endgame)”. Sensing a recurrence, a large number of banks have begun [signing up](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/standing-repo-facility-counterparties#additions-and-removals) to the Fed’s standing repo facility, granting themselves the ability to receive emergency loans and front-run a repo rate spike. If turmoil re-emerges, more monetary theatrics from the Federal Reserve await us, and the triparty repo market will be front and center.

# The Reverse Repo Endgame - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-reverse-repo-endgame
> as the RRP hits zero, the "repo hierarchy" is about to be activated

---
After the Fed’s largest liquidity injection on record, the “excess cash” era is coming to an end. Trillions of reserves once trapped in the Fed’s RRP (reverse repo) facility have been released to replenish the U.S. government’s bank account and fund QT (quantitative tightening). As the RRP balance reaches zero, the dynamics in the repo market are about to transform. The return to an “excess collateral” era awaits us.

In April 2021, the Federal Reserve began unwinding measures that enabled its largest monetary expansion on record. During the COVID-19 panic, the Fed allowed banks to absorb vast flows from QE (quantitative easing) by [exempting](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/bcreg20200401a.htm) reserves and Treasuries from its SLR (Supplementary Leverage Ratio) — a constraint that requires banks to hold a minimum amount of capital against all assets, no matter how risky or safe. But by early 2021, the Fed decided to let this exemption expire. The banks, meanwhile, had absorbed so many low-yielding reserves that the SLR began to threaten their bottom line. Making room for more profitable operations, banks initiated a “great shedding”, pushing deposits (and thus reserves) off their balance sheets. This money could only end up in one place: the Fed’s RRP, the sole shock absorber for excess liquidity. The multi-trillion-dollar RRP buildup had commenced.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rO79!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46c85ef7-7479-44d5-9674-a9c504515341_7162x5700.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rO79!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46c85ef7-7479-44d5-9674-a9c504515341_7162x5700.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Around the same time in 2021, the U.S. Treasury ended its mammoth issuance of Treasury bills used to fund emergency COVID operations. From a [peak](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2023/11/treasury-bill-supply-and-on-rrp-investment/) of $5 trillion outstanding, the value of t-bills in circulation fell by hundreds of billions each month, reducing the supply of safe assets significantly. MMFs (money market funds), which had developed a taste for short-term government debt, were forced to turn to the Fed’s RRPs as an alternative investment. These money funds were also the recipients of the banks’ deposit exodus, further increasing their need to invest in RRPs. As the inflows continued into 2022, the volatility of the largest bond market drawdown in recent history encouraged even greater demand for RRPs while also preventing outflows. If that wasn’t enough, soon after, retail and institutional investors began moving money into MMFs to chase superior yield and avoid the SVB-induced banking panic. By the start of 2023, the RRP balance was housing a whopping [$2.5 trillion](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD) in excess liquidity.

After the resolution of the latest debt ceiling debacle in June 2023, however, the U.S. Treasury once again issued a deluge of Treasuries into the market, ending the safe asset deficit. The RRP balance had finally peaked, initiating the RRP drain.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LOgX!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9a684a0-3edb-47f4-b1f4-92be49adfe22_6821x6942.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LOgX!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9a684a0-3edb-47f4-b1f4-92be49adfe22_6821x6942.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

As heavy issuance made yields on government debt more attractive than the Fed’s RRP, the primary RRP drainers, MMFs, used cash stored in the Fed’s facility to fund a sea of Treasury purchases. Banks also likely contributed by attracting depositors they once pushed out, restocking dwindling reserve balances lost from QT and MMF outflows. The two other types of RRP counterparties had no reason to use the facility either. Since the Fed paid less than the rate to borrow in private repo, primary dealers would make a loss deploying cash into RRPs. Meanwhile, GSEs (government-sponsored enterprises) likely exited after finding better yields elsewhere. With every entity locating superior investments, the RRP drain had entered beast mode.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gS-w!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6d4015da-da95-4c60-a9cf-7f545d8445bf_8273x7836.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gS-w!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6d4015da-da95-4c60-a9cf-7f545d8445bf_8273x7836.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Today, the RRP [balance](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD) has plunged below $800 billion and is fast approaching the zero bound. Eventually, cash available for repos will grow scarce, awakening once undead parts of the market. The upper levels of the Fed’s “repo hierarchy” will be activated, altering the status quo in the most crucial funding market globally. What does that mean in practice? It’s time to go deeper into the mechanics.

At the bottom of the hierarchy lies O/N RRP, the overnight rate on reverse repos conducted by the Fed through its facility. With none of the U.S. central bank’s authorized counterparties willing to lend below it, O/N RRP acts as the repo market’s “risk-free” rate, establishing a (soft) rates floor while eliminating safe asset shortages. [Primary dealers](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/primarydealer_survey_questions.html), plus most major banks, MMFs (money market funds), and GSEs (government-sponsored enterprises), can park their surplus cash at the RRP, relieving downward pressure not only on repo but other money market rates.

For years, the amount [stored](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYD) in the RRP remained insignificant, but the QE flood from COVID-19 suddenly drove the largest cash inflow into repo markets on record. Over the next two years, trillions in excess deposits from the banking system shifted into MMFs. Money funds first invested heavily in repos from the triparty market, the “[base layer](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market)” of repo, where major dealers obtain funding from major cash lenders — such as MMFs.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TPGH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3be8c7c3-3e26-4839-ab60-8870bbf7542d_6265x4927.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!TPGH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3be8c7c3-3e26-4839-ab60-8870bbf7542d_6265x4927.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

This repo “cash deluge” created an enormous imbalance between a rising number of lenders and a steady number of borrowers, suppressing triparty rates so much that they quickly fell toward O/N RRP. Knowing the Fed was a risk-free counterparty — and O/N RRP was a risk-free rate, MMFs began to park more investor cash in the U.S. central bank’s facility instead of lending it to private sector entities. At the same time, borrowers were spoilt for choice, with more cash than they could readily absorb. The Fed’s O/N RRP had thus begun to determine rates in repo’s primary funding market (triparty repo).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BocC!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F298e0534-d05e-4d5b-8e98-3d0ee36a8f0c_5309x3511.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!BocC!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F298e0534-d05e-4d5b-8e98-3d0ee36a8f0c_5309x3511.png)

Meanwhile, at the next level higher in the repo hierarchy, private repos (where entities trade among themselves and not with the Fed) experienced declining volumes. Throughout all of 2022, the rate for dealers to borrow from cash lenders remained tightly pinned to O/N RRP. This showed the triparty market was not only flush with cash but the Fed’s facility was still determining rates. Before the QE flood, whenever cash grew scarce, repo rates climbed above O/N RRP, turning the private sector into the prevailing rate setter. [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr) (Secured Overnight Financing Rate), the Fed’s broadest measure of repo rates, exceeded O/N RRP, compelling cash lenders to lend more profitably at higher rates in private repo, while the Fed’s facility took a backseat.

Banks, believe it or not, had not been willing to enter the market as regular cash lenders. Only a large positive spread between private repo rates ([SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr)) and interest on reserve balances ([IORB](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/reserve-balances.htm)) could persuade banks to trade “Wall Street repos”, the second-to-last level in the repo hierarchy. As the central bank pays interest on bank reserves, repo rates have had to rise above both O/N RRP and IORB for banks to fund repo trades with reserves, which they’d otherwise park at the Fed for superior returns. Each time the spread between SOFR and IORB has widened significantly, Wall Street behemoths have begun to allocate cash to repo trades, supplying liquidity to the most crucial funding market. Yet even Wall Street’s presence proved insufficient to create stable repo markets. Another level in the Fed’s repo hierarchy was needed.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!C06_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F845030d8-8923-464c-8b77-a0fc190ac782_7392x6050.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!C06_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F845030d8-8923-464c-8b77-a0fc190ac782_7392x6050.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

In 2019, the “repocalypse” emerged. A cash shortage, prompted by scarce bank reserves, constrained dealer balance sheets, and a segmented repo market, not only caused repo but Fed Funds and FX swap rates to [spike](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb4499dd1-fd82-48cb-ba63-00789648d543_800x574.png), surpassing the Fed’s upper rate boundaries. Monetary leaders responded by [instituting](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/repo-agreement-ops-faq) a Standing Repo Facility, or SRF for short. The U.S. central bank’s primary dealers and other approved counterparties borrowed cash from the Fed, secured against U.S. Treasuries, and then distributed emergency funds to cash borrowers further down the hierarchy. Shortly after the dust settled, the Fed transformed the SRF into a permanent facility, adding a ceiling to its repo hierarchy.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qxpb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F36433749-036d-4b08-a08a-08e0860b2f5d_5574x3768.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Qxpb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F36433749-036d-4b08-a08a-08e0860b2f5d_5574x3768.png)

Fast forward to today, markets remain at the lower levels of the repo hierarchy. The latest “excess cash” era, however, is slowly concluding. The Fed’s RRP balance, which once held trillions in surplus liquidity, has dropped below a trillion dollars outstanding and continues its descent. Once the RRP hovers around zero, trillions of dollars in reserve balances still circulating among banks will be the only force that separates a well-oiled repo market from another repo spike.

It will likely take a year or so for the Fed to shed a trillion or more in reserves to reach its [LCLoR](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/reserve-balances.htm), its lowest comfortable level. Nevertheless, with QT removing $95 billion each month and the RRP approaching zero, the Fed’s liquidity drain will eventually become effective. As the U.S. central bank finally removes trillions in reserves from the banking system, the excess cash era will make way for a long-awaited excess collateral era, where dollar liquidity grows scarce. Demand for cash via repos will persist alongside declining supply, driving repo rates higher. The upper levels of the Fed’s repo hierarchy will thus be activated. Private market participants, not the Fed’s RRP, will have the greatest influence on repo rates. As liquidity tightens further, rates may rise sufficiently over IORB to enable profitable Wall Street repos, prompting banks to re-enter the repo market as major participants.

By then, only the top level of the repo hierarchy will have yet to be reached. But if dollar scarcity increases enough to send repo rates soaring, the Fed’s SRF will again become active. This time, a surge in SRF usage will be regarded as a standard reaction to tight liquidity conditions (such as a larger-than-usual [deleveraging](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2017/08/regulatory-incentives-and-quarter-end-dynamics-in-the-repo-market/) during quarter-end dates) and may provoke further official or “stealth” easing by monetary leaders. This uncertainty around possible instability will vanish once reserves fall to Fed officials’ lowest comfortable level, as QE likely resumes (or the Fed buys bills) before a repo spike can even emerge. Even so, any measures the central bank implements to avert repo turmoil beforehand will also appear stimulative to most market participants. The Federal Reserve’s “repo put” lies in waiting.

# Demystifying the Repo Market: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part?utm_source=publication-search
> a deep dive into the repo market for dealers

---
— _link to_ _[part one](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) of the Demystifying the Repo Market series_

Despite evolving into the most crucial dollar funding mechanism, the U.S. repo (repurchase agreements) market has remained relatively obscure. In just the [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) segment (covered in _[Part I](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market)_) alone, trillions of dollars change hands daily with little transparency. Behind the scenes, the Fed’s primary dealers raise dollars from global cash investors to enable the speculative activities of numerous financial players, from highly leveraged hedge funds to Wall Street behemoths. Still, triparty only scratches the surface of the repo colossus, acting merely as a funding layer for numerous other markets, each more elaborate and specific in its purpose than its closest counterpart.

Next in this hierarchy lies the interdealer (dealer-to-dealer) market, known officially as [GCF](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov/gcf-repo) repo, where traders on dealer desks battle among themselves to earn the juiciest market-making spreads while providing liquidity to the wider repo ecosystem.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-u1F!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0a46e67d-4a36-4ceb-b960-a70c7260923f_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!-u1F!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0a46e67d-4a36-4ceb-b960-a70c7260923f_7863x6663.png)

GCF repo, the interdealer market (highlighted in green)

In this subscriber special, we’ll demystify the inner workings of the interdealer market, unveiling in detail through visuals and plain English (wherever possible) how GCF repos help to form the most influential chains in global finance. Let’s dive in.

In the late 1990s, repurchase agreements (or “repos” for short) — where cash borrowers pledge securities as collateral to obtain short-term cash loans — had become prominent dollar funding instruments. This enabled dealers to create highly liquid markets and their customers to lend and borrow cash and collateral easily and securely.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Vvoi!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F20f07543-af22-4625-98d1-fe3cc81d0b20_6415x5418.png)

The vast increase in volumes, however, caused repo trading among market makers to become costly and inefficient. Making up most repo activity, prominent dealers and brokers racked up large exposures while trading among themselves, generating enormous transaction costs through trades that could have been offset before settlement. In 1998, aiming to promote liquidity by reducing costs and removing unnecessary trades, the [FICC](https://www.dtcc.com/about/businesses-and-subsidiaries/ficc) (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation) and its two clearing banks, JPMorgan Chase and BNYM (Bank of New York Mellon), introduced the [GCF](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov/gcf-repo) (General Collateral Financing) Repo Service. Though this was only available to FICC members, all major dealers and brokers facilitating interdealer trades already held memberships. “GCF repo”, as it came to be known informally, thus quickly became the primary interdealer market.

As with other asset markets, the interdealer market for repos (secured cash borrowing) and reverse repos (secured cash lending) originally emerged out of dealers’ increasing need for abundant liquidity. Dealers had to engage in extra repo shorts (repos) and repo longs (reverse repos) to hedge risks plus fine-tune their exposures amassed during market-making and speculative trading. Even before GCF repo was introduced, interdealer trades were facilitated among dealers anonymously via interdealer brokers (IDBs) and centrally cleared by the FICC, boosting market efficiency and competition among even the most dominant players. Interdealer trades were also mostly “general collateral” (GC) repos, where the dealer lending cash accepted any piece of collateral from a basket of similar securities — such as U.S. Treasuries maturing in 10 years or less or agency bonds issued by government-sponsored enterprises. Repos conducted outside the interdealer market were most commonly “specific collateral” repos (also known as [specials](https://www.icmagroup.org/market-practice-and-regulatory-policy/repo-and-collateral-markets/icma-ercc-publications/frequently-asked-questions-on-repo/9-what-is-a-special-in-the-repo-market/)), where a specific issue of a security was obtained by the cash lender — most usually hedge funds aiming to exploit price differences between two similar issues that traded in the [secondary market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-treasury-market). In the interdealer segment, though, a wider range of securities could be pledged by dealers to secure cash loans, improving the liquidity and distribution of collateral in the wider repo market.

Still, this wasn’t sufficient. Monetary architects at the FICC, along with dealers they served, introduced the GCF service to enhance repo market efficiency and liquidity even further. GCF repo’s major innovation was the elimination of redundant trades via “netting”. Instead of dealers settling cash and securities for individual repo trades, the FICC received all interdealer activity submitted throughout the day and then calculated every dealer’s net positions for each specific basket of securities. Dealers became net lenders or borrowers with the FICC for each defined group of collateral backing their trades. For instance, when a dealer traded $30 billion in repos and $20 billion in reverse repos backed by U.S. Treasuries maturing in 10 years or less, the dealer was obligated to deliver just the net amount ($10 billion in cash) to the FICC. This process was repeated for every [defined basket](https://www.dtcc.com/-/media/Files/Downloads/Clearing-Services/FICC/GOV/GCF-Collateral-Eligibility-and-Types.pdf#page=6) of securities the FICC accepted through its GCF service, dramatically reducing transaction costs and the movement of securities and cash between dealers’ accounts at settlement. Soon enough, efficiency not only in GCF but in the entire repo market began to soar.

Two and a half decades on, the FICC’s GCF service has prevailed as the repo market’s interdealer segment. What’s more, minus a few worthy enhancements and modifications, its inner workings have barely transformed.

On a typical day, dealers trade repos anonymously among themselves via the electronic order books and voice platforms of interdealer brokers (IDBs). Once both parties have confirmed trade details — namely the loan amount (say, $1 billion), the length (term) of the repo (say, overnight or one week), and the lending rate (say, 5.00%), the IDB immediately submits two trades marked as GCF repo transactions on behalf of the participants to the FICC — the sole central clearer of U.S. repos. Unlike trades in other repo segments, GCF repos never require a haircut on the collateral chosen later to secure the trade, which protects the cash lender from the pledged security losing significant value during the loan period. Instead, as every entity in the interdealer market holds an FICC membership, they post haircuts in the form of margin (cash) to secure their overall position, monitored by the FICC’s systems. Dealers preemptively post sufficient capital to the FICC’s clearing fund, shielding the central clearing provider and its members from substantial losses. Moreover, during trading hours, dealers post additional margin to counteract any [mark-to-market](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/marktomarket.asp) changes in the collateral they initially pledged. Upon receiving confirmation from the IDB, the FICC also “novates” the trade, jargon for absorbing losses from any counterparty default to guarantee settlement.

As with [triparty repo](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market), dealers submit most of their GCF trades to the FICC between 7:00 am (the market open) and 9:00 am, when liquidity and demand from customers such as hedge fund clients remain the strongest. Following the early morning rush, trading soon plateaus, with hourly volumes peaking around 10:00 am.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2wRm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4e60ad6a-efee-402d-bc67-1b0c60384cba_1739x1426.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2wRm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4e60ad6a-efee-402d-bc67-1b0c60384cba_1739x1426.png)

After an 8-hour trading day — in which dealers monitor and manage existing and newly submitted positions through online interfaces, the GCF trade submission window closes promptly at 3:00 pm. Just five minutes later, the GCF netting process commences, calculating each FICC member’s net obligations to pledge collateral or deliver cash per [defined security basket](https://www.dtcc.com/-/media/Files/Downloads/Clearing-Services/FICC/GOV/GCF-Collateral-Eligibility-and-Types.pdf#page=6). For instance, despite submitting around $300 billion in trade volume during the day, Member X has only become a net $5 billion borrower of “TU10” — the FICC’s basket identifier for U.S. Treasuries maturing in ten years or less — thereby needing to deliver $5 billion in cash to receive $5 billion in Treasuries. Meanwhile, Member X has accumulated a $10 billion net long position in both “TU30” (U.S. Treasuries maturing in thirty years or less) and “AGCY” (U.S. agency bonds), thus needing to deliver $20 billion in securities to receive $20 billion cash at settlement (as shown in the “Netting” part of the infographic below).

same graphic as above, added for convenience

After completing the netting process, the FICC begins clearing GCF trades by sending the necessary settlement instructions to the BNYM (Bank of New York Mellon), the designated clearing bank for U.S. repos, at 3:30 pm. JPMorgan used to also settle repo trades, but after post-subprime crisis regulations penalized high-volume, low-margin activities for the largest banks, the megabank wound down its government securities clearing business. That means every afternoon, shortly after trading and netting concludes, the BNYM allocates collateral pledged by borrowers and sends cash wired by lenders to the FICC’s BNYM clearing account. The clearing bank then assigns cash and collateral to trades and settles the obligations of each member, with the FICC’s clearing account acting as a central counterparty.

Since dealers are not depository institutions, dealers as cash lenders instruct their banking partners to wire reserves to the BNYM’s Fed master (reserve) account. The BNYM then credits the clearing account of the cash lender, sends the cash to the FICC’s account, and settles the trade by transferring the cash from the FICC’s account to the cash borrower. At the same time, dealers as cash borrowers assign collateral to trades from their BNYM “longbox account,” a link to one or more custodial accounts on the commercial book-entry system (CBES) — the Federal Reserve’s system for maintaining official records of securities ownership. With any repo trade settled via the BNYM’s triparty service, securities pledged by the cash borrower are allocated to a segregated BNYM account provided to the cash lender, which contains a record of collateral it has been allocated. As the BNYM merely allocates collateral and settles trades on its own books, no actual transfer of securities on the official system (CBES) occurs. Consequently, securities pledged in GCF trades can only be repledged into the triparty and GCF repo markets, not into markets that don’t use the BNYM’s triparty services — such as the dealer-to-customer [markets](https://dtcclearning.com/products-and-services/fixed-income-clearing/gsd/dvp-service.html). After allocation, the BNYM assigns the collateral to FICC’s account first and then reallocates it to the cash lender’s segregated account, fulfilling the FICC’s role as a central counterparty to GCF trades.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9Ruk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F62d9b171-2535-4fb6-a0ac-3a190a2f24ee_6706x7111.png)

As repos are technically overnight loans, trades automatically unwind at 3:30 pm (not 8:30 am like most recent reports suggest) the next day, unless the remaining loan period exceeds the current date or parties involved decide to roll over the repo for another day. But before the trade unwinds, the FICC and the BNYM offer mechanisms that enhance repo market liquidity and the efficient use of collateral. Because dealers, during the day, often find better uses for securities pledged in existing triparty trades, the FICC and the BNYM have introduced a “collateral substitution” service, which allows cash borrowers (the collateral lenders in a trade) to replace collateral with other securities or cash. Before 3:00 pm, dealers can substitute collateral with another security in their BNYM longbox account or with funds wired to their clearing account to settle existing trades. This mechanism is available for any dealing that uses general collateral repos: GCF (the cleared market for general collateral repos) and the triparty repo market (the uncleared market for general collateral repos).

At the 3:30 pm unwind, the flow of cash (plus interest earned by the cash lender) and collateral reverses via the BNYM accounts of the FICC, which once again serves as a central counterparty. After a successful exchange, the GCF repo trade has been deemed as completed.

To truly understand the goal of interdealer trading in the wider repo market, we must turn to the repo rate hierarchy, composed of three distinguishable layers: triparty _(flows in yellow)_ — the major dealer funding market, GCF — the interdealer repo market _(flows in green)_, and bilateral — the dealer-to-customer or “specific collateral” repo market _(flows in blue)_. The two upper layers, DVP and GCF, also include a “Sponsored Service” offered by the FICC, which allows dealers to trade with larger clients who aren’t members of the clearing agency.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!USeO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbb747c09-25a4-45ce-89c2-b124914c5cc3_7476x5622.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!USeO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbb747c09-25a4-45ce-89c2-b124914c5cc3_7476x5622.png)

By running a “matched” repo book, dealers borrow cash (while pledging securities) via a repo at one layer and lend cash (while borrowing securities) in a corresponding reverse repo, either within the same layer at a slightly higher rate or in an above layer at a much higher rate, earning a profit from the spread. At the bottom of the rate hierarchy [lies](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) the triparty market, where primary dealers and other large dealers raise funding from risk-averse cash investors such as money market funds (MMFs) and government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs). Since triparty cash lenders have a lower risk tolerance, they will only lend to dealers with strong capital buffers. Consequently, smaller dealers with weaker balance sheets don’t have access to triparty and must use GCF repo as their primary funding market, the next layer up in the repo rate hierarchy.

After negotiating a repo (pledging securities to receive cash) in triparty, the major dealers will back their repos using securities from their inventories or, most often, a reverse repo (lending cash and receiving collateral) with a dealer in the GCF market. They can even lend cash straight to customers in the bilateral (DVP) markets at a much higher rate, earning a juicer spread. Dealers also earn “intermarket” spreads while market making. For example, dealers in GCF repo will borrow at just below the average GCF repo rate and lend to another dealer just above it. Ultimately, if a dealer can earn a profitable spread, it will lend and borrow at any two layers in the repo rate hierarchy.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mtwv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F14bad071-dee1-45c4-9ece-0e7c17440daf_5893x4654.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mtwv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F14bad071-dee1-45c4-9ece-0e7c17440daf_5893x4654.png)

Dealer’s average spreads (profit) can be witnessed through a group of rates published by the Office of Financial Research (OFR). Major dealers borrow at TPR (the OFR’s average rate in the triparty market) and lend at GCF (the OFR’s average rate in GCF repo). At the next level, dealers either lend to each other at small positive spreads within GCF repo near the GCF rate or lend to hedge fund clients and other customers in the bilateral (specific collateral) markets at DVP (the OFR’s average rate in cleared bilateral repo), earning an even greater profit. Alas, the OFR collects regular data solely on the cleared bilateral market (the FICC’s DVP service), not the non-centrally cleared DVP bilateral market ([NCCBR](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-blindspot)), the latter of which has rates that trade higher than GCF. In the private repo rates complex above, DVP trading below GCF is not a mistake. The OFR only measures centrally cleared trades, where participants are willing to borrow at much lower “cleared” bilateral rates, even lower than GCF, compared to uncleared rates in NCCBR that trade above GCF — which we’ve estimated in yellow for clarity. As is evident, the intricacy of the most crucial funding market is somewhat hard to fathom.

But just like the [Treasury market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-treasury-market), the repo market is now due for a cleanup. With the SEC (Securities and Exchange Commission) [mandating](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247) central clearing for practically every systemic repo trade by mid-2026, the repo rate hierarchy could be changing. Differing only by central clearing, the triparty and GCF markets will likely merge into one, as rates in both markets converge. The repo market rewire is approaching.

# The Repo Market Rewire - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire
> the structure of the most crucial dollar funding market is changing

---
The major shift to a [secured](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-silent-monetary-revolution) monetary standard is now complete. Trillions in dollar loans, once priced by major financial players, are priced using rates in the [repo market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market), the market for secured cash lending. But after several [mishaps](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-global-dollar-endgame), even the secured standard has proven defective. Subsequently, with leaders vowing to fix the latest flaws in their new system, the most systemically crucial funding market is set to evolve once again. The Repo Market Rewire™ is approaching.

On December 13th, 2023, U.S. monetary officials [announced](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247) sweeping changes to the Treasury market, not only to strengthen America’s sovereign debt goliath but enhance its major liquidity greaser: the repurchase agreements market, or “repo” for short.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yCMx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F123b0df5-98ec-41a2-8ab3-d0eba7f46636_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!yCMx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F123b0df5-98ec-41a2-8ab3-d0eba7f46636_7863x6663.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market))

Every day, trillions of dollars in cash and Treasury securities change hands temporarily via repos to facilitate both short-term cash investments and highly leveraged speculative trades, fostering a liquid market for U.S. sovereign debt. Money market funds (MMFs) must earn a return on investors’ cash while dealers need funding to provide leverage to their customers, primarily hedge funds seeking to profit from arbitrage in the [Treasury cash market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash). Meanwhile, the Fed stands guard to bridge any imbalances emerging from repo market turmoil, supplying emergency cash investments via its reverse repo facility, [the RRP](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-triparty-repo-market), and emergency cash loans via its Standing Repo Facility, [the SRF](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-triparty-repo-market). Even with these central bank defenses in action, however, the COVID market panic and the 2019 “[repocalypse](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-global-dollar-endgame)” — among other [episodes](https://www.sifma.org/resources/news/revisiting-us-treasury-market-capacity-and-resiliency-part-i/#:~:text=As%20we%20discussed%20in%20a,where%20the%20depth%20of%20the) — have urged leaders to reinforce the repo market’s plumbing with a more effective glue: the widespread expansion of central clearing. Let’s dive deeper.

Repo has not only grown more systemic but also more fragmented. The market remains divided into several unique regions, the number of which increases seemingly every year. Each one defines itself by its distinct participants, the type of securities lent and borrowed in exchange for cash, and how cash and securities are transferred between parties i.e. clearing and settlement[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire#footnote-1-141664517). Although separate, these regions form a [hierarchy](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-money-markets) ordered by the price of lending and borrowing via repo. At the bottom lies the triparty[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire#footnote-2-141664517) market, acting as the “[base layer](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2)” or “funding layer” of repo markets. Here, cash investors — primarily MMFs — earn a yield by entering into reverse repos with cash borrowers, mostly the major securities dealers in need of cheap dollar funding. As investors earn interest on their cash, dealers earn a spread by lending the funds in other repo segments to dealers plus other repo actors engaging in [riskier](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets) transactions.

To foster a liquid market for cash lending, triparty operates as a “general collateral” (GC) market, where cash borrowers pledge any issue of a security from a pre-approved basket of high-quality collateral — usually U.S. Treasuries and government-backed [MBS](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/a/agency-mbs-purchase.asp) — to obtain dollars easily. Because the types of cash lenders in triparty, namely MMFs and other asset managers, don’t speculate using the securities they accept as collateral, triparty market plumbing has evolved so that random securities are assigned to secure repo loans automatically. Operating this infrastructure is the Bank of New York Mellon (BNYM), which serves as the third-party middleman in triparty repo and every other general collateral (GC) market. Via its triparty platform, the BNYM efficiently clears and settles over $3 trillion in repos per day, transferring cash between lenders’ and borrowers’ accounts while assigning securities to back these cash loans via computer algorithms.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!67HE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd3c285ea-8c86-4ece-9f0a-f8f002e9598d_7432x4978.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!67HE!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd3c285ea-8c86-4ece-9f0a-f8f002e9598d_7432x4978.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-triparty-repo-market))

Still, despite clearing trillions of dollars in repos a day, the triparty market does not employ central clearing, where a central counterparty (CCP) stands between buyers and sellers to guarantee settlement. As for the interdealer (dealer-to-dealer) markets, however, trades are always centrally cleared to reduce risk among the most systemic players. In the interdealer triparty market, known officially as the FICC’s GCF (general collateral financing) repo service, major dealers — the only cash borrowers that most MMFs will lend to — distribute the cash they receive in triparty to smaller dealers in GCF at a profit, often using the collateral assigned to them in these GCF trades to secure their cash borrowing in triparty. To confirm, “triparty” refers to the non-centrally cleared triparty market, whereas “GCF” refers to the centrally-cleared triparty market, which is also primarily an interdealer venue. Both triparty and GCF markets conduct repos on the BNYM’s triparty platform with one major difference. In GCF, cash is exchanged and securities are pledged among dealers’ BNYM accounts but via the [accounts](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part) of the sole central counterparty for U.S. repos, [the FICC](https://www.dtcc.com/about/businesses-and-subsidiaries/ficc) (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation). Dealers benefit from the increased liquidity and risk controls that a CCP offers, as the FICC rakes in fees.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PpHE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa4c06851-5bff-4f52-8b87-a57ef9019a5b_7489x6946.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!PpHE!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa4c06851-5bff-4f52-8b87-a57ef9019a5b_7489x6946.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-interdealer-repo-market))

Major dealers also distribute triparty cash to smaller dealers in the other interdealer market, the FICC’s [DVP](https://dtcclearning.com/products-and-services/fixed-income-clearing/gsd/dvp-service.html) (Delivery Versus Payment) Repo Service, or “FICC DVP” for short. DVP is the industry standard for securities transfers, in which settlement is guaranteed to occur only after payment has been made. Unlike in GC markets, where the BNYM’s platform merely assigns securities as collateral to trades, DVP repo markets use [Fedwire](https://www.frbservices.org/financial-services/securities) (the Federal Reserve’s settlement system) to actually transfer the securities between participants’ accounts on CBES (the Commercial Book-Entry System), the Fed’s official securities depository.

Rather than randomly accepting one out of a range of similar securities to back the repo, the cash lender (who’s also a securities borrower in the transaction) requests to receive a _specific_ issue of a security — for instance, a recently issued 10-year Treasury — in its account on the Fed’s official system. DVP markets are thus also known as specific collateral (SC) markets. Participants are mostly hedge funds that obtain one or more specific securities to [speculate](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets) on for a brief period. Subsequently, they need to acquire the securities in their accounts to buy and sell in [secondary](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash) markets, before delivering the securities back to close out their repo trades. Since all triparty (GC) markets use the BNYM’s platform, which “locks” securities in place during transactions, hedge funds use the SC markets for speculative trading. Risk-averse cash investors like MMFs, meanwhile, favor GC markets for engaging in simple cash lending.

Although the FICC’s DVP service is classed officially as an interdealer market, it’s more of a dealer-to-customer market, as hedge funds are the biggest (net) cash borrowers and MMFs are the largest (net) cash lenders, with dealers serving as market makers. In truth, FICC DVP acts as a centrally-cleared version of what’s now likely the largest repo segment: non-centrally cleared bilateral[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire#footnote-3-141664517) (DVP) repo, or [NCCBR](https://www.financialresearch.gov/data/non-centrally-cleared-bilateral-repo-data/) for short — where “bilateral” is just an interchangeable term for “DVP”. While FICC DVP processes around $1 trillion in SC repos per day, the NCCBR market processes several trillion.

Serving as the major dealer-to-customer repo markets, FICC DVP and NCCBR allow dealers to provide securities and cash to “advanced” repo customers: mostly hedge funds and other leveraged investors looking to execute leveraged trades and some banks looking to raise funding. What divides the two markets is that trades in the FICC DVP are centrally cleared by the FICC, while trillions in repo volumes are settled in NCCBR without going through the accounts of a central counterparty.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets))

With the new rules [finalized](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247), it’s clear that reducing the size of the non-centrally cleared parts of repo, namely NCCBR and triparty, has become monetary leaders’ primary objective. Most of the volume in non-centrally cleared repo ends up financing speculative participants that engage in highly leveraged transactions. Yet, these leveraged bets — including [arbitrage](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets) and [basis trades](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets) — not only provide vital liquidity to the Treasury cash, repo, and futures markets but bind these markets together. Monetary leaders have thus decided that enforcing central clearing on all relevant transactions, plus the financial chains funding them, stands as the best course of action to improve market resiliency, and there’s no turning back.

Their latest [agenda](https://www.sec.gov/files/rules/final/2023/34-99149.pdf) — which becomes mandatory by June 30, 2026 — will ultimately cause a rewire of repo markets. As central clearing increases balance sheet space for dealers to intermediate markets[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire#footnote-4-141664517) and decreases overall credit risk between counterparties, the FICC will become a large shock absorber. Serving as the only CCP for repo markets, the central clearing giant will likely become “too big to fail”. At the same time, the non-centrally cleared regions of repo will slowly wither away, with participants shifting to centrally cleared segments. The interdealer and dealer-to-customer markets will converge. Aiding this transition will be the FICC itself, liberating access to its central clearing platform through programs such as [Sponsored GCF and DVP](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov/sponsored-membership). What’s more, regulators have vowed to endorse any new programs and measures to achieve a centrally cleared evolution, one that’s already underway. A gradual exodus from non-centrally cleared to centrally-cleared repo markets is [ensuing](https://www.dtcc.com/charts/membership), the effects and consequences of which remain undetermined.

# The Repo Market Exodus: Part I - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-i
> trillions of dollars in elaborate repo trades are about to be relocated

---
Following numerous market upheavals, U.S. officials have enacted their latest agenda to strengthen America’s sovereign debt market, prompting an extensive rewiring of its monetary plumbing. Subsequently, trillions of dollars in highly leveraged trades conducted in the most opaque parts of the repo market, favored by market players, have begun to shift to the more observable regions preferred by monetary leaders. The Repo Market Exodus™ has been initiated.

Upon announcing various deadlines on December 13th, 2023, the U.S. regulatory establishment has [enforced](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247) rules to fortify not only the U.S. Treasury market but every other market that could threaten its stability. By June 30th, 2026, all trades deemed systemically hazardous in [the repo market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2) — the market for cash lending secured primarily against U.S. Treasuries — must be centrally cleared through a CCA (Covered Clearing Agency), the official term for a central counterparty (CCP) or clearinghouse that provides central clearing.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VzMS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F48eb42ec-e734-4377-8888-ac4eff50d1bc_6126x4783.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VzMS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F48eb42ec-e734-4377-8888-ac4eff50d1bc_6126x4783.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographic-repo-market-central-counterparties))

With centrally cleared [repo trades](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9d8cf42-8df6-4c11-8124-e985d7bfe127_6415x5418.png), cash and securities are transferred back and forth between participants’ accounts but also via the accounts and risk controls of the [FICC](https://www.dtcc.com/about/businesses-and-subsidiaries/ficc) (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation), the only active central clearing provider for U.S. repos. As “FICC members” or “direct participants”, dominant broker-dealers and the dealer arms of large banks must post [margin](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/margin.asp) (in the form of cash and securities) to the FICC to reduce the risks of their trading activities plus that of their customers, classed as “FICC non-members” or “indirect participants” by U.S. officials. Because the FICC operates all centrally cleared repo markets, only its members have direct access to centrally cleared repo trading. For every other participant — i.e. entities that fail to meet the FICC’s strict member requirements designed to protect itself and other members from frequent defaults, they must earn the trust of their larger counterparts to access centrally cleared repos. These indirect participants include not just money market funds and hedge funds but also thousands of smaller banks and broker-dealers. In the repo market hierarchy, size determines ease of access.

In return for its members posting margin, the FICC not only guarantees trade settlement, absorbing losses from defaulting counterparties, but also removes (“nets”) redundant transactions, reducing costs and saving balance sheet space to increase dealers’ market-making capacity. Still, despite these advantages, most of today’s repo dealings remain “uncleared” — a shorthand for “non-centrally cleared”, where cash and securities move directly between participants’ accounts over [Fedwire](https://www.frbservices.org/financial-services/securities) (the Fed’s securities settlement system) or [on the books](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/1ec4b68b-2bc4-4c9a-9906-9406949db103) of the Bank New York Mellon (BNYM), the sole clearing bank for U.S. repos. No central counterparty like the FICC stands between parties to deliver superior risk controls or absorb losses from counterparty defaults.

The dominance of uncleared repos, however, is coming to an end. The SEC (Securities & Exchange Commission), in a lengthy 400-page [release](https://www.sec.gov/files/rules/final/2023/34-99149.pdf), has laid the foundations for a centrally cleared era in repo markets. After the June 2026 deadline, every repo trade secured against U.S. Treasuries involving a direct participant (i.e. an FICC member), minus a few rare exceptions, must be submitted for central clearing. Direct participants consist of all the largest market makers that facilitate most repo activity. Meanwhile, following the 2008 subprime crisis, U.S. Treasuries have become the go-to collateral to secure repo transactions. Thus, the vast majority of trades in the repo market will ultimately pass through the FICC, with the central clearing giant becoming the dominant “glue” binding most repo participants together.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!To0z!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79b225d6-93d3-4da7-a8e0-6ad6db686459_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!To0z!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79b225d6-93d3-4da7-a8e0-6ad6db686459_7863x6663.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market))

It’s become evident that monetary leaders’ endgame is to create an ideal balance between liquidity and stability, especially in times of crisis. Repos involving central banks, state and local governments, international financial organizations, and sovereign entities have been excluded from the final ruling, along with transactions where the FICC itself attempts to obtain emergency liquidity — not when it’s [novating](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/novation.asp) transactions. What’s more, “inter-affiliate” repos, where both counterparties in a repo trade are associated with the same firm, have likewise been excluded by regulators. According to officials, around 25 to 75% of repos executed by the largest dealers occur between their affiliates, such as their broker-dealer and commercial bank subsidiaries. For instance, JPMorgan’s commercial banking arm will regularly transfer cash to its broker-dealer business to fund market-making activities. When its broker-dealer falls short of liquidity, its bank subsidiary will lend U.S. dollars via repos to finance trades such as FX swaps.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F7xv!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40971b63-2f21-41c3-b99a-fa5ceb10ebd2_6709x5789.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!F7xv!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40971b63-2f21-41c3-b99a-fa5ceb10ebd2_6709x5789.png)

click to enlarge

Repos also conducted solely between indirect participants will not require central clearing, such as trades between the thousands of smaller broker-dealers and their institutional customers. Nevertheless, since trades in the [Treasury cash market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-treasury-market) between a direct participant and a registered broker-dealer, government securities dealer, or government securities broker will also require central clearing by the start of 2026, smaller broker-dealers will have to onboard onto central clearing to continue to maintain relationships with their customers and access liquidity, most of which will be provided by larger dealers who must now centrally clear their trades.

But for every other repo transaction, the FICC must eventually stand between participants. That includes all trades in the interdealer markets — which already use FICC’s central clearing — and most trades in the dealer-to-customer markets. In the interdealer segments, any leg of a trade involving systemically important intermediaries must also pass through the FICC’s risk controls. Interdealer brokers (IDBs), which bring dealers together via various trading platforms, are now mandated to centrally clear their activity after regulators have deemed uncleared IDB trading a threat to financial stability. Likewise, in the dealer-to-customer markets, the prominent dealers that hold FICC memberships must enable indirect access to central clearing for a wide variety of customers. The plumbing of the most influential repo trades is therefore about to transform. Let’s dive deeper.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Jc1f!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7bf1a4c5-c640-4fff-bfd5-ccad5aa3f697_6072x5878.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Jc1f!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7bf1a4c5-c640-4fff-bfd5-ccad5aa3f697_6072x5878.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-repo-market-rewire))

Starting at the bottom of the repo [hierarchy](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa936e602-3a6e-456c-bc4f-4b419e21a745_7386x8120.png), most of the volume in the triparty market, an ecosystem that does not use the FICC’s central clearing services, consists of the major broker-dealers — all of which happen to be FICC members — raising dollars from cash investors such as money market funds to lend to repo participants elsewhere. Many triparty actors claimed central clearing was unnecessary in a market that enables simple, secured cash borrowing and lending, where all transactions use the BNYM’s [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2) platform, which provides numerous safeguards to reduce systemic risk. Monetary leaders, however, disagreed. Although the BNYM’s system helps prevent repo turmoil from emerging, it does not provide guaranteed settlement of cash and securities plus other risk controls that central counterparties (like the FICC) deliver. Hence, despite the backlash, monetary leaders have begun forcing most triparty transactions to move from the uncleared triparty market to the centrally cleared triparty market: the FICC’s [GCF](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part) Repo service.

Trillions of dollars in triparty repos executed by financial entities, from dealers to money market funds to asset managers, will move from uncleared to cleared repo markets. Still, perhaps the most significant shift will be from hedge funds that operate almost exclusively in the uncleared dealer-to-customer segments — primarily the non-centrally cleared bilateral repo market ([NCCBR](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2)), sporting trillions of dollars in speculative trade volumes.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets))

Since most hedge funds — and other non-FICC members — aren’t direct participants, they will need to trade centrally cleared repos indirectly, through FICC members, using the FICC’s two [available](https://www.dtcc.com/ustclearing/-/media/Files/Downloads/Microsites/Treasury-Clearing/GSD-Access-Model-Deck2) “access models”: agent clearing and sponsored repo. Indirect participants must develop relationships with various large broker-dealers that possess FICC memberships to gain access to sponsored repo markets (namely, [Sponsored DVP and GCF](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov/sponsored-membership)) or a dealer’s [prime brokerage](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/microsites/fxc/files/2005/fxc051219a.pdf) or [correspondent clearing](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/equities-trade-capture/correspondent-clearing) arrangements. In a sponsored repo trade, a hedge fund, money market fund, or other asset manager, enters into centrally cleared repos through their dealers, who handle the logistics of central clearing on their behalf, like submitting trades and posting margin to the FICC. In return for brokerage services and absorbing the risk of its potential default, a sponsored member pays fees to the FICC member providing access. Indirect participants can also trade centrally cleared repos via a dealer that acts as an agent through its prime brokerage (bundled clearing and execution services that dealers offer to institutional clients) or via a [correspondent clearing](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/equities-trade-capture/correspondent-clearing) firm.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZCCK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Febb3e36a-36e8-4d8b-a731-ab3a30435756_6431x3046.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZCCK!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Febb3e36a-36e8-4d8b-a731-ab3a30435756_6431x3046.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Right now, many smaller entities or those with minimal repo activity may find these access models too costly. Yet monetary leaders alongside key market providers will do whatever it takes to complete the transition to a centrally cleared era. The FICC, when required, has [expressed](https://www.dtcc.com/ustclearing/-/media/Files/Downloads/Microsites/Treasury-Clearing/GSD-Access-Model-Deck2) that it will expand the flexibility of its offerings for both access models. Also, the SEC’s Treasury clearing mandate stated that from April 2025, the margin that a dealer posts to the FICC to satisfy the positions of its customers, such as facilitating a sponsored repo trade, will no longer count towards the total margin required to fulfill [other](https://smartasset.com/investing/sec-rule-15c33) regulations. Since January 2024, the SEC and CFTC (Commodity Futures & Exchange Commission) have also reduced the need for dealers to pass on costs to customers by merging a dealer’s margin requirements across two key market providers. In what’s known as a cross-margining agreement, U.S. officials have [approved](https://www.dtcc.com/news/2023/september/12/enhanced-treasury-cross-margining-arrangement-launching-january-2024) a dealer’s ability to use margin held in their accounts at the CME (Chicago Mercantile Exchange) — posted to satisfy futures positions likely related to a dealer’s repo exposures — to meet the margin required to be held in their FICC accounts.

These changes will likely relieve pressure on the more sophisticated yet most systemic players. The “true” participants in repo are not unleveraged cash lenders but hedge funds and other leveraged players looking to obtain leveraged positions, which enable the most liquid conditions in the Treasury cash, repo, and futures markets. With all major repo dealers that facilitate leveraged trades holding FICC memberships, leveraged participants will have no choice but to play ball with the coming centrally cleared status quo. The effects of this ensuing repo exodus from uncleared markets on Treasury market liquidity, which is already waning, may soon force monetary leaders to once again alter the playing field.

In Part II, (separate from Part I, as there’s so much information to take in with repo markets!), we’ll dive deeper into the plumbing of hedge fund repo trading and how mandatory central clearing will affect these trades — and thus Treasury market liquidity…

# The Repo Market Exodus: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii
> an exit from opaque repo markets could alter Treasury market liquidity

---
_— to read part one of the repo market exodus, [click here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-i)_

The mass departure from the [repo](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market?utm_source=profile&utm_medium=reader2) market’s shadowy yet most systemic regions has now commenced. Preparing to adapt to a [centrally cleared era](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247) soon to be enforced by monetary officials, key market players have started fleeing the opaque markets for temporary cash loans secured against collateral, or “uncleared repos”[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii#footnote-1-143411951) for short. As most dealings in the U.S. Treasury market will require central clearing by mid-2026, participants who’ve been trading uncleared repos for decades have begun shifting operations to centrally cleared markets. Owned by the [DTCC](https://www.dtcc.com/) — an entity with a near monopoly on clearing and settlement[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii#footnote-2-143411951), the Government Securities Division ([GSD](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov)) of the Fixed Income Clearing Corporation (FICC) remains the lone provider of centrally cleared repo trading. Subsequently, the clearing giant has started to absorb enormous inflows from uncleared repo markets, primarily trades that — without going through central clearing — will eventually be outlawed. With the [latest](https://www.dtcc.com/news/2024/march/12/dtccs-ficc-treasury-clearing-volumes-grow-31-percent) figures revealing a ~30% rise in centrally-cleared Treasury trades since last year, the transition to a centrally-cleared era is already underway.

A vast cluster of obstacles, however, still await both monetary leaders and the most influential repo players impacted by a centrally cleared standard. Though it appears to be a simple vehicle for secured cash lending and borrowing, the repo market’s ultimate goal is to finance the highly leveraged positions of speculative players seeking to profit from perceived mispricings in Treasury securities, the same trades that glue U.S. sovereign debt markets together. Alongside a liquid secondary market — also known as the “cash” market, the world demands both a functional Treasury repo and futures market to deliver, among other things, ample liquidity. All three combine to enable efficient markets for U.S. Treasuries, thus producing relatively stable yields that serve as a benchmark for pricing trillions in dollar-denominated assets. Coincidentally, trades enabling such functionality also dominate volumes in uncleared repo markets, which face near extinction following the latest [regulatory changes](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-247).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!brI5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc8e33fc8-be5b-434d-b315-c4b95df50171_7072x4269.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!brI5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc8e33fc8-be5b-434d-b315-c4b95df50171_7072x4269.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

One of the most influential is the Treasury cash-futures [basis trade](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-treasury-market-unwind?utm_source=publication-search), the primary mechanism for removing dislocations between prices of Treasury securities and their associated futures contracts. Somewhat unintentionally, this supplies unrivaled liquidity to the Treasury cash, futures, and repo markets. As futures prices detach significantly from their underlying securities in the cash market, hedge funds and other leveraged players step in to close the gap. When a Treasury grows cheaper than its futures contract, market participants will go “long the basis”: buying the cheaper Treasury security and shorting the expensive futures contract to profit from a spread upon delivering the Treasury into the futures market[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii#footnote-3-143411951). Eventually, enough players encountering the same opportunity push prices back into balance. But as these dislocations remain tiny, traders require significant leverage to justify entering basis trades in order to make a worthwhile profit. Thus, to eliminate such mispricings, hedge funds must access the uncleared repo markets to borrow cash and securities far exceeding their available cash on hand.

Alongside basis trades, hedge funds also speculate on mispricings between two similar Treasury securities, known as relative value (RV) trading, which produces trillions of dollars in speculative activity in uncleared repo markets. Although RV trades usually revolve around particular events, such as a Treasury auction, relative value trading could involve any two securities from which hedge funds believe they could profit and dealers are willing to make a market. A popular instance revolves around Treasury [reopenings](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/auctions/reopenings/), where the U.S. Treasury issues additional amounts of a previously issued security (such as a 10-year note) one and two months after its initial offering. As a Treasury security issued in a reopening remains “on-the-run,” i.e., the most recently issued of a particular maturity, the associated increase in demand causes its price to outperform older, “[off-the-run](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/off-the-runtreasuries.asp)” securities for a few weeks after the auction. Hedge funds profit from this price appreciation by borrowing an out-of-demand (i.e. off-the-run) security, shorting it in the cash market, and using the proceeds it receives from the short sale to buy the in-demand (i.e. on-the-run) security. In the process, the hedge fund has entered a long and short position in two similar Treasuries, expecting to profit from the on-the-run security outperforming the off-the-run security over a bi-weekly period.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WdNr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd6e0b0e2-56cd-452b-afeb-37f0a76deab7_6170x6608.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WdNr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd6e0b0e2-56cd-452b-afeb-37f0a76deab7_6170x6608.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

Like the cash-futures basis trade, hedge funds require immense leverage on their RV trades, generated through repos, to achieve worthy profits. They must turn to the Fed’s primary dealers and other larger dealers, who finance the hedge fund’s leveraged long and short positions by lending cash and securities via repos, while charging a fee to facilitate these transactions. As they effectively take the other side of hedge funds’ positions, dealers will likely hedge their exposures through derivatives markets or by taking the other side of the trade with another customer.

But for dealers to provide significant leverage, they must operate in the murkier uncleared parts of repo, free from rules and protocols that restrict elevated risk-taking. The greater the leverage, the greater the exposure hedge funds can achieve to engage in arbitrage trades that create efficient markets. Regardless, with the centrally cleared era looming, monetary leaders will have outlawed virtually all uncleared repo activity of leveraged players. Remove that privilege, in exchange for less systemic risk, and the market reduces its ability to remove mispricings in the world’s most systemically important debt market. What does this look like in practice, and what will be the resolution? It’s time to go deeper into the plumbing.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7RvW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F05aa61d7-a3d0-464f-a0c3-0937a0c0fe09_6386x7423.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

Sporting roughly $5 trillion in daily volume, dealer-to-customer (DTC[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii#footnote-4-143411951)) repo markets have emerged to enable the trading activities of sophisticated players who aim to profit using the leverage that repo dealers provide. Less risk-savvy players, including pension funds, sovereign wealth funds, and other institutional investors, still use the centrally cleared DTC market (the FICC’s [DVP service](https://dtcclearning.com/products-and-services/fixed-income-clearing/gsd/dvp-service.html)) — which processes around $1.1 trillion daily, to invest their excess cash or finance more conservatively leveraged positions. These participants, however, comprise only a quarter of the dealer-to-customer market’s volume, which is dominated by hedge funds and other highly leveraged funds exploiting dislocations in U.S. government debt markets.

Though somewhat active in the centrally cleared DTC market, these players overwhelmingly trade in the uncleared DTC segment: Non-Centrally Cleared Bilateral Repo, or NCCBR for short. Bilateral repo is an interchangeable term for a [DVP](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/d/dvp.asp) (delivery vs. payment) repo, jargon for trades in which cash and securities are transferred simultaneously between participants’ accounts, allowing them to trade (e.g. short-sell) the securities they receive as collateral before returning them as the repo unwinds (matures). This is in contrast to “[triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market)” or general collateral ([GC](https://www.concoda.com/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part)) repos, tailored for simple cash lending and borrowing, where securities posted as collateral to secure the loan are merely recorded as “pledged” on the books of a [custodian](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) instead of transferred between players. Accordingly, those looking to speculate using leverage must use DVP (bilateral) repos to obtain Treasuries in their possession, in order to actively trade them in secondary markets.

Despite differences in plumbing and functionality, centrally cleared bilateral repo (the FICC’s DVP service) and uncleared bilateral repo (NCCBR) remain almost identical in their benefits and ultimate objectives. Both inside and outside the FICC’s boundaries, dealers offer “netted trades” in which they facilitate relative value trades of their customers — such as the aforementioned Treasury reopening arbitrage — without needing to raise additional capital. A dealer enters into a repo and reverse repo with a relative value customer to finance both their long and short Treasury positions. But since the dealer is the counterparty to both customers’ repo transactions, which also expire (unwind) on the same day, each side of the relative value trade offsets the other from an accounting perspective. This enables dealers to bypass post-2008 crisis regulations — namely Basel III’s [leverage ratio](https://www.bis.org/publ/bcbs270.htm#:~:text=Basel%20III's%20leverage%20ratio%20is,minimum%20leverage%20ratio%20is%203%25.) and the Fed’s [SLR](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa45fc144-56b1-465b-a9db-3630f405c80d_864x385.jpeg) (supplementary leverage ratio) — where they must raise a certain amount of capital against all their exposures, including Treasury positions.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M6l_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8a62b9c2-9d1a-429d-b8c2-5c367d189f62_6675x6391.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!M6l_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8a62b9c2-9d1a-429d-b8c2-5c367d189f62_6675x6391.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographic-how-dealers-survive-under))

The major difference is that in a centrally cleared environment, the FICC, as a central counterparty, becomes the legal counterparty to both the dealer and its customer instead of each other, known as [novation](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/n/novation.asp). This reduces overall risk to the U.S. Treasury market via risk controls employed by the FICC yet offers no additional benefit to those trying to achieve leverage. In fact, it has become a disincentive. As evidenced by researchers at the Office of Financial Research ([OFR](https://www.financialresearch.gov/data/non-centrally-cleared-bilateral-repo-data/)), hedge funds rarely use centrally cleared trades, only making up around 9% of the volume in the FICC’s DVP service. Meanwhile, in NCCBR, hedge funds are the counterparties to dealers a whopping ~62% of the time — the rest involving banks, [REITs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/reit.asp#:~:text=REITs%2C%20or%20real%20estate%20investment,trade%20on%20major%20stock%20exchanges.), and other leveraged participants.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7rCb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9872f96-d6e1-47bc-a9d2-338b75db7a35_1425x1377.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7rCb!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd9872f96-d6e1-47bc-a9d2-338b75db7a35_1425x1377.png)

dealer counterparties in NCCBR by type (% volume) | Source: OFR

As uncleared repos bypass most regulatory oversight and the risk controls of the FICC, hedge funds obtain the most leverage in the NCCBR market. Dealers usually determine how much leverage their customers can achieve by assessing the risk profile of their trades. This means charging the customer a “haircut” — jargon for posting margin (i.e., a temporary cash deposit) equal to a small percentage of a trade’s risk-adjusted exposure, which better protects the dealer from a customer’s default during Treasury market unrest. Over time, though, the competition among dealers in NCCBR has grown so fierce that earning a hedge fund’s business has resulted in the rise of “zero haircuts”, where the dealer sometimes collects zero margin from a customer to facilitate their uncleared trades. While a 2% haircut provides a hedge fund with substantial leverage for the cost of posting 2% of its exposure in margin, a zero haircut trade provides much greater exposure plus flexibility, as the hedge fund may avoid having to raise additional equity to enter a trade with a dealer. In 2021, almost 70% of trades in the NCCBR market were estimated to employ zero haircuts. Today, these trades likely enable maximum efficiency in the U.S. Treasury market.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!99VR!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F55b24cda-a414-4941-b4c9-25d92b855ede_7565x6482.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!99VR!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F55b24cda-a414-4941-b4c9-25d92b855ede_7565x6482.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographic-how-hedge-funds-obtain))

Nevertheless, in the looming centrally cleared era, the amount of leverage achievable will no longer be determined between market participants but by the FICC. Unlike with uncleared repos, the clearing giant’s complex risk management system imposes margin requirements on dealers for centrally cleared transactions. Soon enough, they must post sufficient cash and high-quality securities to the FICC’s “[clearing fund](https://www.dtcc.com/~/media/Files/Downloads/legal/rules/ficc_gov_rules.pdf#page=12)” to offset the risks of most of their trading activities, plus those of their customers.

These procedures supposedly counteract threats to the FICC and its members, which include all the major dealers, market makers, and other intermediaries. Yet these will also reduce the maximum exposures of participants engaging in key arbitrage trades. When you cut off their power to obtain enormous leverage, the ability to glue the Treasury cash, repo, and futures markets together diminishes. Top speculative players responsible for these systemic trades likely have the cash on hand to compensate for increased margin requirements, but it’s unknown whether they’ll be as willing to step in with the same appetite.

Still, that’s the gamble officials and the FICC are willing to take. The FICC has recently [submitted](https://www.sec.gov/files/rules/sro/ficc/2024/34-99710.pdf) a proposal to enforce minimum margin requirements, effectively eliminating infamous zero haircut transactions. Meanwhile, if mispricings and dislocations grow large enough to threaten Treasury market functioning, regulatory officials will likely capitulate, somewhat easing their latest constraints. Yet the unparalleled freedoms and opaqueness of uncleared repo markets will soon be history, never to return. Monetary leaders have gotten their way, and key market participants will start to feel the burn.

# Demystifying the Repo Market: Part III - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d?utm_source=publication-search
> a deep dive into the repo market for leveraged players

---
— _links to_ _[part one](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) and [part two](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part) of the Demystifying the Repo Market series_

The multi-trillion-dollar repo market remains the most elaborate financial ecosystem on the planet. Unlike most regions of the global financial system, which are well observed and understood, the market for [repos](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff9d8cf42-8df6-4c11-8124-e985d7bfe127_6415x5418.png) — short-term cash loans secured against securities as collateral — has stayed obscure. So much so that the least transparent region has grown to become the largest and most influential: the dealer-to-customer market. Here, dealers provide repos (temporary swaps of securities for cash) and reverse repos (temporary swaps of cash for securities), which they fund by borrowing cash in the [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) market, repo’s “dealer funding” market, and trading in the [interdealer](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part) markets, where dealers trade repos (again, swapping cash and securities temporarily) among themselves.

The end goal of repos traded outside the dealer-to-customer market is to ultimately finance repos inside the dealer-to-customer segment, the plumbing of which is specially designed for dealers to facilitate trading activities of their true “customers”: hedge funds and other speculative players — such as REITs, banks, and insurance companies — engaging in leveraged bets on (mostly government-issued) financial assets, primarily U.S. Treasuries. Somewhat unintentionally, these speculative trades create more liquid, thus more efficient sovereign debt markets, helping to power America’s financial hegemony.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!T3EW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd2bdeac8-2af0-4aa8-b2b9-b1ee699b11b8_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!T3EW!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd2bdeac8-2af0-4aa8-b2b9-b1ee699b11b8_7863x6663.png)

click to enlarge (light mode available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market))

Enabling trillions in speculative trading volumes daily is the defining feature of the dealer-to-customer market: how securities pledged in repos as collateral are “settled” — i.e. transferred between participants. Dealer-to-customer repos remain the only type of repo that enables the transfer of securities between participants’ accounts during trades — unlike other repo markets that merely record the securities as pledged on relevant trading systems. These direct securities transfers allow the repo participant who’s lending cash and receiving securities as insurance to trade this “collateral” (another name for the securities received) in the secondary markets — like the [Treasury cash market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash), before returning them (or other identical securities) to exit repo trades. Hedge funds, for instance, will [enter](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff77c14d0-ae3d-46aa-9d85-292154554053_7072x4269.png) a leveraged long position (far exceeding their available cash on hand) in a U.S. Treasury security by buying one in the secondary Treasury market and enabling this purchase by borrowing cash in a repo. It will then post the security as collateral to the dealer in the same repo used to raise cash for its initial Treasury purchase. To exit the trade, the hedge fund simply sells the Treasury security and uses the cash proceeds to repay (and thus close) the repo it used to enter its leveraged long position.

Only the plumbing of the dealer-to-customer repo market, however, enables such trading. Hedge funds must obtain and deliver securities via their official securities accounts, not only to repo dealers but to buyers and sellers of U.S. Treasuries in the secondary market. For this to happen, these assets move between participants’ official securities accounts housed on [Fedwire](https://www.federalreserve.gov/paymentsystems/fedfunds_about.htm), the Federal Reserve’s system for storing and transferring official “book-entry” (jargon for electronic) records of U.S. government securities, plus securities issued by U.S. government-sponsored enterprises — such as the agency bonds of Fannie Mae & Freddie Mac.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4RI9!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F84c7091a-64d0-402b-978a-b40a7c82cec4_6099x6413.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4RI9!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F84c7091a-64d0-402b-978a-b40a7c82cec4_6099x6413.png)

click to enlarge (light mode available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-how-us-government-securities))

To enable optimal settlement of U.S. government securities trades, the Federal Reserve employs a mechanism known as “DVP”, short for “delivery versus payment”. In what’s known as DVP settlement, the delivery of securities and the associated cash payment must only be settled simultaneously. When either the required funds or securities are missing from participants’ accounts, Fedwire will consequently fail to process both sides of the transaction. Thus, “delivery” occurs only “versus payment.” Because all dealer-to-customer repo trades settle via Fedwire’s DVP mechanism, the dealer-to-customer repo market is also commonly called the “DVP repo market” or “DVP repo” for short. DVP repos are thereby another term for dealer-to-customer repos, with the “customer” in “dealer-to-customer” (thus DVP repos) consisting of sophisticated players looking to speculate and engage in leveraged trading, not risk-averse cash investors such as money market funds (MMFs), which aim solely to earn interest from investing cash — using securities pledged to them in repos as insurance for their cash lending, not for deploying into elaborate trading strategies.

Despite all of its transactions settling via Fedwire’s DVP mechanism, the dealer-to-customer repo market has been split in two, divided by whether or not cash and securities also pass through the accounts of the [FICC](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-services/ficc-gov) (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation), the lone central counterparty (CCP) that provides centrally cleared repo trades. Upon repo participants sending cash and securities (as margin) to its “clearing fund” — essentially a collective pot of money, the FICC guarantees settlement of cash and securities swapped in DVP repos by intervening when necessary via the Fedwire system. CCPs like the FICC will use cash accumulated in their clearing funds to offset a participant’s losses from a counterparty default, dispatching cash or securities to the affected accounts. Trades employing the FICC’s safeguards are known as centrally cleared repos — and sometimes as “cleared repos,” shortened for simplicity, not clarity, as every repo trade goes through clearing (i.e. the process of arranging settlement). For cleared repos settling via Fedwire’s DVP mechanism[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-1-143703559), parties submit trades to the FICC’s [DVP Service](https://dtcclearning.com/products-and-services/fixed-income-clearing/gsd/dvp-service.html), in which the FICC organizes cash and securities traded in a DVP repo to be transferred via Fedwire while also guaranteeing settlement. Though the FICC’s DVP service has been classed officially as an interdealer market, it’s turned into a dealer-to-customer market, with dealers mainly intermediating cash from MMFs to hedge funds.

Meanwhile, the market for DVP repos that settle outside the FICC, i.e. directly between participants’ accounts over Fedwire, has been assigned the unfortunate name of “non-centrally cleared bilateral repo” or NCCBR. Its convoluted name derives from how trades aren’t centrally cleared by a CCP, hence “non-centrally cleared” or sometimes “uncleared” — again, shortened for simplicity, not clarity, as all repo trades are cleared in some capacity. “Bilateral” describes how NCCBR trades settle on a “bilateral basis”, jargon for how securities aren’t custodied by a third party during the repo and instead move directly between participants’ securities accounts. Whether centrally cleared or not, all DVP repos settle bilaterally. Accordingly, a “bilateral repo” is (yet another) term to describe DVP repos (thus dealer-to-customer repos). Not confusing at all.

Together with the FICC’s DVP Service, the NCCBR market forms a liquid ecosystem for dealer-to-customer repos, a repo market for leveraged speculators that tops $5 trillion in daily volumes.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ji5U!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F23a25540-6c01-4434-bc31-f325011738fe_6386x7423.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ji5U!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F23a25540-6c01-4434-bc31-f325011738fe_6386x7423.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

To facilitate the world’s sizeable thirst for leveraged trading in U.S. government securities, dealers partake in an endless game of “repo hot potato,” swapping securities for cash via repos and cash for securities via reverse repos and charging a fee for providing liquidity. This game occurs not only in dealer-to-customer markets but in every other repo segment. In the most common chain, an MMF will lend dollars short-term to a dealer in [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market/s/primers?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=menu), the dealer funding market, swapping dollars for securities. The dealer will then swap dollars for securities with another dealer or a speculative customer in the other repo markets and use the collateral received to back its initial dollar borrowing — or finance another chain of repo transactions. The MMFs earn a secure return on investors’ cash, while dealers provide speculators with the ample liquidity they desire. For the Fed’s primary dealers, now providing the vast majority of repo liquidity, this game of hot potato never ends. After streamlining trillions of dollars in repos every day, primary dealers’ repo chains have grown immensely.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!n10y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc7150b36-e6ae-463a-af4b-470e639cb712_7476x5622.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!n10y!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc7150b36-e6ae-463a-af4b-470e639cb712_7476x5622.png)

repo “hot potato” visualized

Yet most of these chains have also expanded in the shadows, specifically within the uncleared — thereby opaque — region of the dealer-to-customer repo market, where the most sophisticated game of repo hot potato ensues. To demystify this contest, plus uncover the intricacies of the most elaborate part of the financial system, let’s take an extensive dive into the plumbing of NCCBR: non-centrally cleared bilateral repo.

To make sense of the repo markets’ most opaque region, we turn to relative value (RV) trading in the secondary Treasury market — which _Conks_ briefly [described](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii) in _The Repo Market Exodus._ RV trades make up most of the volume in the NCCBR market, which is dominated by the Fed’s primary dealers facilitating the activities of the top fifty or so speculative funds. These hedge funds and other leveraged players borrow cash and Treasury securities — usually from the same primary dealer willing to facilitate their levered fixed-income trading — to capitalize on any emerging arbitrage opportunities in securities markets. The most popular [trading](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true) scenarios involve exploiting price differences between recently issued “on-the-run” and older “off-the-run” Treasuries _(as pictured below)_. NCCBR functions as the market for achieving maximum [leverage](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographic-how-hedge-funds-obtain) when deploying these trading strategies. Without heavy oversight from regulators, dealers in NCCBR can facilitate RV trades for hedge funds with barely any margin of safety required upfront. Instead, the regulatory limit for leverage in NCCBR has become the dealer’s tolerance for risk.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fFP_!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7ce18602-2545-4537-82da-4be4c0883653_6170x6608.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fFP_!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7ce18602-2545-4537-82da-4be4c0883653_6170x6608.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

The only sane way to demystify NCCBR is to illustrate, from start to finish, a common RV trade financed in the NCCBR market. Illustrated above, a popular trade involves a relative-value hedge fund seeking to profit from Treasury [reopenings](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/auctions/reopenings/), where the U.S. government issues additional amounts of a previously issued security (such as a 10-year note) one and two months after its initial auction. As a Treasury security issued in a reopening remains “[on-the-run](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/off-the-runtreasuries.asp),” i.e., the most recently issued of a particular maturity, the associated increase in demand causes its price to outperform older “[off-the-run](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/o/off-the-runtreasuries.asp)” securities for a few weeks after the auction. Hedge funds profit from this outperformance by borrowing an out-of-demand (off-the-run) security, selling it short in the [Treasury cash market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash), and using the cash it receives from the short sale to buy the in-demand (on-the-run) security. By the end of this process, the hedge fund has entered a long and short position in two similar U.S. Treasuries, expecting to profit from the on-the-run security outperforming the off-the-run security over a bi-weekly period. Hedge funds use the NCCBR market to execute the same trade but with high amounts of leverage, making the potential profit on the trade worthwhile.

For those executing this RV trade using leverage, both their Treasury positions will require financing via repos (i.e. short-term cash and securities borrowing) provided by dealers in the NCCBR market. The stages of an NCCBR repo trade remain similar to that of a secondary market trade in the U.S. Treasury market. Trade execution (i.e. executing or “booking” a trade) is followed by trade confirmation (i.e. confirming trade details, sometimes known as “trade affirmation” or “trade matching”) followed by trade clearing (i.e. arranging settlement of the securities and the cash used to pay for them) concluding with trade settlement (i.e. the actual transfer cash and securities between participants). The difference is that a repo, being a repurchase agreement (which involves selling a security and agreeing to buy it back at a later date), has opening and closing “legs”.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!rgmB!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F396f4d8c-e281-4651-85f7-02240b4baccd_5881x6561.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-uncleared-dealer))

To enter an RV trade, the hedge fund first executes trades in the “[cash market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash)”, jargon for the secondary market for U.S Treasuries, identifying a potential mispricing, thus a trading opportunity, between an on-the-run and an off-the-run 10-year Treasury note. The hedge fund locates such an opportunity via trading platforms provided by its primary dealers (single dealer platforms or [SDPs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-dealer_platform)) or third-party platforms that collect quotes from multiple dealers (multi-dealer platforms or [MDPs](https://www.kantox.com/blog/the-real-value-of-multi-dealer-fx-trading-platforms)), which monitor and display securities pricing for U.S. Treasuries. Most platforms use RFQ (request-for-quote), where customers request prices from dealers via voice chat or electronic interfaces. Once the hedge fund discovers an opportunity, it executes the necessary trades in the secondary Treasury market via these same platforms, buying $100 million of the Treasury it thinks will appreciate and selling $100 million of the Treasury it thinks will depreciate, ultimately entering a relative value (RV) trade. Most U.S. Treasury trades settle (i.e. cash and securities are exchanged) on “t+1” the next day. “t+n” denotes the day the trade has been executed — represented by “t” — plus the number of days — represented by “n” — until the trade settles. In this RV trade, the hedge fund agrees to settle its Treasury trades with its counterparties the next day on t+1.

In the early morning — usually promptly at 6 am — the day after executing its Treasury trades, the hedge fund will approach a primary dealer in the NCCBR market specializing in repo trading — such as Barclays or Citi — to execute a $100 million repo and $100 million reverse repo, used to fund the settlement of its $200 million in Treasury trades. The dealer charges a 5.75% annualized rate on the repo loan, which the hedge fund will use to fund the on-the-run Treasury and then pledge it to the dealer as collateral for borrowing $100 million in cash. At the same time, the dealer pays a 5.55% annualized rate to the hedge fund in a reverse repo, in which the hedge fund borrows an off-the-run Treasury from the dealer’s inventory, sells it short in the cash market, and sends the cash from the short sale to the dealer as payment. Until exiting the repos, the hedge fund remains in a leveraged RV trade. Meanwhile, for facilitating both repos and Treasury trades that enable the hedge fund to enter this RV position, the dealer earns a 20bps (5.75% - 5.55% = 0.20%) spread.

The dealer, however, may also impose a “haircut” on both the hedge fund’s trades — jargon for requiring margin (i.e., a temporary cash deposit) from the hedge fund that equals a small percentage of the trade’s exposure, which safeguards the dealer from the hedge fund’s potential default if market turmoil emerges. The level of haircut that dealers request, which can be (and is [most often](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-ii)) near zero for RV trading, determines the amount of leverage a hedge fund achieves in the NCCBR market. With a “zero haircut” package that dealers offer their clients, a hedge fund can enter a $200 million position using NCCBRs (non-centrally cleared bilateral repos) without putting up any of its own cash upfront. With a 2% haircut, though, a hedge fund may have to raise an additional $4 million in cash to enter the same trade.

When it comes to the duration[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-2-143703559) of NCCBR repos, two types exist: “overnight repos” and “term repos”. In an overnight repo (or reverse repo), a dealer and its customer exchange cash and securities (or vice versa) almost immediately on the same day of execution and then “unwind” the trade, i.e. return cash and securities to their original owners, the following day on t+1. In practice, hedge funds dominate repo market activity, so most repos finance trading strategies that span over multiple days. Once executing and settling an overnight trade, both parties will likely agree to roll over the trade for longer, delaying the unwinding of cash and securities after the overnight period. The dealer and its hedge fund clients will instead revise the terms of the repo each day, settling any adjustments via cash payments (the technical term being “variation margin”) until deciding to exit the trade that the repo has financed.

For hedge funds and other NCCBR participants, however, they prefer to use “term repos,” repos that last longer than an overnight period. The hedge fund and dealer exchange cash and securities shortly after execution, yet this time they agree to lock in a fixed repo rate until the repo unwinds on a specific future date, “t+n”. Since the potential loss of profit from charging a set rate is more substantial, dealers will charge hedge funds higher rates on term repos than overnight repos. Even so, paying and locking in a higher rate will appeal to hedge funds engaging in RV trades, because these trades have defined timeframes — such as a week after a Treasury reopening where certain newer Treasury securities outperform certain older issues.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZzXV!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc90d0459-3538-48d9-b667-4cdfcd88ec65_5881x6561.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ZzXV!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc90d0459-3538-48d9-b667-4cdfcd88ec65_5881x6561.png)

same opening leg graphic as above, added for convenience

After trade execution, the next step involves verifying trade details, known as trade affirmation or confirmation. Immediately after execution, the dealer and hedge fund agree and verify the terms of the trade, including the securities involved, amounts needed to be exchanged, repo rates, counterparties, and settlement date. Most U.S. Treasury and repo trading systems, however, use automated processes to “match” (compare and verify) trade details stored on data files drafted by each participant. This “trade matching” process allows both parties to prepare the necessary instructions to commence trade settlement.

But to settle repo trades (and the Treasury market trades that these repos have financed), participants must send instructions to their clearing banks — also called custody banks or settlement agents. These banks remain the only entities capable of “clearing” government securities trades, which involves constructing and sending wire messages to the Federal Reserve System to initiate “settlement” i.e. the actual transfer of U.S. government securities between accounts via the Fedwire Securities Service (FSS). FSS consists of the Commercial Book-Entry System (CBES)[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-3-143703559), the U.S. government’s [central securities depository](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_securities_depository) (CSD) that creates and holds records of securities issued by the U.S. government and government-sponsored enterprises, combined with Fedwire’s settlement mechanism that enables transfers of securities records — with (and without) an associated payment.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7OEx!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F69345414-b79b-4243-ad72-ff15cdb83b2c_6099x6413.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!7OEx!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F69345414-b79b-4243-ad72-ff15cdb83b2c_6099x6413.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-how-us-government-securities))

Dubbed “direct participants” by the Federal Reserve, these clearing banks — most of which are commercial banks and custodian banks that provide clearing services — have direct access to the Fedwire Securities Service, granting them the power to store, transfer, and (sometimes) issue U.S. government-related securities. Everyone else, meanwhile, has indirect access via a direct participant acting as their agent. Indirect participants must employ clearing and custody banks and other types of direct participants to custody and transfer government securities on their (and customers’) behalf. Even though most U.S. government securities trades pass through the Fed’s primary dealers, they aren’t direct Fedwire participants and use the Bank of New York Mellon (BNYM) as their clearing bank, a direct Fedwire participant with superior clearing and settlement services. As U.S. government securities are custodied using a tiered ownership system, the Fed provides direct participants such as the BNYM with an “omnibus account” structure, in which they create and manage sub-accounts that hold their clients' securities. Those clients are mostly broker-dealers who, without access to Fedwire, use a clearing or custody bank to store customers’ securities records and then display these to clients on their frontend systems outside Fedwire. Automated notifications sent by Fedwire allow the clearing banks and their institutional customers to update information on their external systems, such as trading interfaces provided to hedge funds and retail investors by their broker-dealers.

To facilitate settlement of both NCCBR repos (and the Treasury cash market trades that these repos financed), the dealer, hedge fund, and the Treasury cash market buyer and seller submit instructions to their clearing banks, usually by 8 am. Unlike the infrastructure of Treasury markets, repo markets have been built to allow settlement on the same day that repo trades are booked and executed — represented by “t+0”. Securities and cash involved in NCCBR repos are settled via Fedwire’s DVP mechanism after Fedwire opens at 8:30 am until the 3:30 p.m. close. The clearing banks work together to construct securities transfer requests to settle cash and securities between the relevant accounts. Using their direct connection to the Fedwire system — provided by their local Federal Reserve bank — they assemble what’s known as a “transfer message” to perform certain actions. The most common transmission will prompt the Fedwire’s DVP mechanism to perform a securities transfer between a seller’s securities account and a buyer’s securities account while simultaneously transferring central bank reserves (Federal Funds) from the buyer’s master account to the seller’s[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-4-143703559).

Settling complex NCCBR trades requires clearing banks to obey the Fedwire’s DVP protocol by sending transfer messages in an order that means cash and securities will always be present in participants’ accounts. To reduce failures, clearing banks may credit the accounts of participants temporarily to facilitate settlement, “lining up” the associated Treasury and repo trade obligations. After trade settlement has been completed, the dealer and the hedge fund will risk-manage the NCCBR trades appropriately. If, for whatever reason, one of the Treasuries pledged in a repo moves violently against the hedge fund, the dealer may call the hedge fund to post more (variation) margin from its cash account, with clearing banks settling the transfer between participants.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VZKf!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb77c58bd-3e84-40d2-b9ad-57cbf9fc5350_5881x6597.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VZKf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb77c58bd-3e84-40d2-b9ad-57cbf9fc5350_5881x6597.png)

click to enlarge (light version available [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-uncleared-dealer))

By the end of the week, the hedge fund decides to close out its RV trade and realize a tidy profit from its long Treasury position outperforming its short Treasury position. In what is a near replication of the RV trade’s opening, the hedge fund will execute two Treasury trades on trading platforms, but this time to close out its positions. By executing these Treasury trades on t+1, the day before its outstanding repos are set to unwind, the hedge fund uses cash and securities received from these Treasury trades to settle (unwind) its outstanding repos with its dealer — the same repos that financed its initial Treasury positions.

To clear this intricate web of transactions, the clearing banks will again work with the dealer, hedge fund, and Treasury cash market participants, extending credit (cash) when needed to settle trades in an order that is compliant with Fedwire’s DVP mechanism. After the legs of each trade have unwound and trade obligations have settled successfully over Fedwire, parties repay their debts to their clearing banks, and the dealer returns any cash that the hedge fund pledged as margin. The money left over from unwinding both Treasury trades, minus the 20bps spread paid to the repo dealer, equals the hedge fund’s profit. In this instance, the hedge fund has banked around $2 million, made possible only by the NCCBR market and its ability to facilitate high amounts of leverage.

Yet, the age of freely available leverage in the repo markets has likely reached its pinnacle. Monetary leaders have initiated [a war](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire) on highly leveraged repo positions by mandating central clearing on related transactions, which (via risk controls imposed by regulators and CCPs) will reduce the maximum leverage achievable. By [mid-2026](https://www.bnymellon.com/us/en/insights/central-clearing-us-treasury-market.html), almost all of the popular RV trades in the NCCBR market, presently uncleared, will have to be ported over to the centrally-cleared market for dealer-to-customer repos: the FICC’s DVP Service. The uncleared age of dealer-to-customer repo is concluding.

# The Repo Market Dislocation: Part I - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search
> key money market rates are about to be altered

---
As financial markets have experienced widely unforeseen turmoil, dollar funding markets have remained surprisingly subdued. Out of the numerous events that triggered the unwinding of leveraged positions across various asset classes, the so-called reversal of the Yen carry trade has been touted as the major causality. Yet the $/¥ (dollar-yen) cross-currency basis[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-1-147445483) — a sharp widening[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-2-147445483) of which implies severe market stress — has barely dipped, while other cross-currency bases have traded [flat](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa725a5c0-30d4-40c6-bd21-cb15d353244d_6239x4251.png), signaling stability. Onshore money market rates for repos and Fed Funds [continue to trade](https://substack.com/@conks/p-146276672) in their semi-zombified state. Compared to volatile FX and equities, money markets have been living in a parallel universe.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J5Cf!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3a5d0695-e118-4b4c-a8dc-95b24d28d1b2_6123x3976.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J5Cf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3a5d0695-e118-4b4c-a8dc-95b24d28d1b2_6123x3976.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

That is except for the most perplexing move in a repo rate benchmark since the [repocalypse](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-global-dollar-endgame) in September 2019. This time, instead of all major repo averages[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-3-147445483) [exploding](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/55/Cfl423-fig1.png) through the [upper limit](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFEDTARU#) of the Fed’s target range, just one has breached the U.S. central bank’s lower boundaries.

Since July 18th, the two prominent benchmarks for measuring [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) repo rates — the cost at which the Fed’s primary dealers borrow dollars via repos from cash investors — have dislocated. The Federal Reserve’s average triparty measure, [TGCR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/tgcr)[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-4-147445483), has printed in its normal tight range. Yet the [TPR](https://www.financialresearch.gov/short-term-funding-monitor/datasets/repo-single/?mnemonic=REPO-TRI_AR_TOT-P)[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-5-147445483) rate, the triparty benchmark published by the OFR[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-6-147445483) (The Office of Financial Research), has descended rapidly (as shown below in yellow).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1xGS!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F64500906-b055-4bac-aa6c-5a84e33f597e_5904x4348.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1xGS!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F64500906-b055-4bac-aa6c-5a84e33f597e_5904x4348.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

The OFR’s triparty rate has fallen so far so fast that, initially, it appeared someone large had been willing to lend cash below the Fed’s _risk-free_ repo rate. Why a major cash lender, the only entity with enough size to suppress a benchmark, would lend at inferior yields to what they’d receive at the Fed’s [RRP](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2b20335b-92d1-4176-bad1-a3b69f7ec956_5309x3511.png) remained a mystery.

At first glance, the most compelling theory was that cash lenders were trying to front-run a greater certainty of rate cuts — and potentially emergency cuts as the early August turmoil in markets unfolded. If repo rates were about to drop by 75 bps (0.75%) or so, sacrificing a few basis points would beat losing a few rate cuts worth in yield. Cash borrowers might have consequently been able to persuade cash lenders to lend cheaply below the overnight rate that the Fed pays on its RRPs (i.e. [O/N RRP](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/RRPONTSYAWARD)) or even under the lower bound of the U.S. central bank’s target range.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MKBm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd8b0afb5-3933-4af4-bf02-e9faad663674_7432x4978.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!MKBm!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd8b0afb5-3933-4af4-bf02-e9faad663674_7432x4978.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

But as the Fed’s triparty benchmark and other repo rates failed to exhibit a similar descent, something else must have been afoot. Instead, the mystery lay within how these benchmarks varied in construction, with the major difference involving the type of collateral backing each repo loan. The Fed’s TGCR measures the average rate of borrowing cash in the triparty repo market (visualized above) but only when secured against U.S. Treasuries on an overnight basis.

The OFR’s TPR benchmark, however, measures the rate on all triparty repos backed by _any_ type of collateral — from U.S. Treasuries to agency MBS to CDOs — that cash lenders are willing to accept from borrowers, provided it’s compatible with the Bank of New York Mellon’s triparty platform — where the triparty repo market lives and gets its name. The OFR calculates TPR by combining the rates on triparty repos secured against various types of collateral, namely U.S. Treasuries, U.S. federal agency securities, corporate bonds, and “other collateral” — the latter being a euphemism for the riskiest securities.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gz0Q!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90880bc5-e90a-4e79-aba7-72dcc4891995_4260x3064.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!gz0Q!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90880bc5-e90a-4e79-aba7-72dcc4891995_4260x3064.png)

repos backed by risky securities should trade at a higher rate than safer ones

On closer inspection, only the rate for triparty repos where the cash borrower pledged “other collateral” has tumbled, but it’s been enough to suppress the OFR’s entire TPR benchmark. Repos secured against collateral outside the “other collateral” category have been trading at ordinary rates, rendering other OFR rates — and the Fed’s TGCR _—_ stable. What’s more, the volume of repos backed by “other collateral,” which could be anything from private-label MBS (mortgage-backed securities) to even equities, has rocketed by $50 billion.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DKaM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4fcaf68a-3aa2-4512-a8cb-54e2e8b0de34_5000x3599.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!DKaM!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4fcaf68a-3aa2-4512-a8cb-54e2e8b0de34_5000x3599.png)

If the data is accurate, and that’s a big if, some large lender with access to the triparty market and supposedly the Fed’s RRP has been willing to lend cash against the riskiest collateral from the largest dealers at a big discount for days, earning increasingly lower returns. If this sounds absurd to you, you’re not alone. This type of trading reflects the opposite of the true dynamic that plays out in the triparty repo market, where safety is everything. Only the prominent financial behemoths — mostly the Fed’s primary dealers — have gained admission to borrow in triparty because the major triparty lenders, MMFs, have learned their lesson from previous crises. They will only lend to entities too big to fail. No considerable triparty lender, out of the blue, would likely loan $50 billion in cash against risky securities at a lower rate than lending against safer collateral, let alone avoid lending at a higher rate to the Fed, risk-free, via its RRP facility.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bWYf!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8110e7d8-d082-467c-b8db-0617363c0995_7911x3075.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!bWYf!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8110e7d8-d082-467c-b8db-0617363c0995_7911x3075.png)

In the [secured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-rise-of-the-secured-standard?utm_source=publication-search), where financial entities are forced into accumulating U.S. sovereign assets, the overwhelming majority of triparty repos involve large dealers and banks borrowing cash secured against U.S. Treasuries and securities issued by GSEs (government-sponsored enterprises), such as Freddie Mac and Fannie Mae. The latest rise in “other collateral” in OFR’s benchmark seems more of a bug than a feature. Whether the OFR’s triparty benchmark has gone haywire due to a technical blunder or not, this repo rate puzzle remains unsolved. Either way, the fact that the riskiest collateral is distorting the entire TPR benchmark suggests that officials will intervene.

Coincidentally, just as one repo benchmark has experienced a disconnect, the Fed’s major repo measure, [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr) (the Secured Overnight Financing Rate), which estimates the average rate across all repo segments, is about to turn slightly defective, according to its architects at the U.S. central bank. As a major shift in repo market structure has commenced, arising from the transition to a [centrally cleared era](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire), the Fed has had to respond. Officials have [proposed](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_240718) to remove an increased number of centrally cleared repos that will find their way into SOFR as this transition intensifies. Without modification, these repos will cause volatility in future SOFR readings due to where centrally cleared repo rates trade inside the Fed’s target band.

Even so, with the [unsecured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-silent-monetary-revolution) based on LIBOR about to fade entirely, SOFR will remain the undisputed king of money market benchmarks. But the new monarch is overdue for his first “major” rework, which history suggests will be the first of many to come.

# The Repo Market Dislocation: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search
> the Fed's major rates benchmark is growing volatile

---
_— click [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part) for The Repo Market Dislocation: Part I_

The most significant monetary shift in recent history has ended. [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr)[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-1-147748085), the Federal Reserve’s primary repo rate benchmark — which measures the average cost to borrow cash secured against U.S. Treasuries — is now employed by global finance to price trillions of dollars in financial assets. Despite intense skepticism, the move away from LIBOR[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-2-147748085), a benchmark based on global banks’ supposed costs to borrow unsecured dollars[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-3-147748085), has been accomplished. An [estimated](https://www.icmagroup.org/assets/Paul-Richards_ICMA-Quarterly-Report-Q4-2022.pdf) $74 trillion in LIBOR exposures[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-4-147748085) will slowly mature, marking the end of an unsecured monetary standard. But now, as another prominent shift in market structure has emerged, LIBOR’s successor is also becoming defective. With officials already responding, the Fed’s latest dollar rate benchmark, SOFR, is about to transform.

In 2014, the Fed called upon numerous branches of government, plus various private and public sector [giants](https://www.newyorkfed.org/arrc/about#governance), to architect a new dollar standard. The U.S. central bank and its newly established committee, the [ARRC](https://www.newyorkfed.org/arrc/about), vowed to replace LIBOR with one out of multiple proposed alternatives, including Treasury bill yields, swap rates, and even [Fed Funds](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-death-of-the-interbank-market) (EFFR). This list, however, fell swiftly to two leading candidates: a rate based on overnight [repos](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) backed by U.S. Treasuries (soon to be SOFR) and OBFR, the Overnight Bank Funding Rate. Published by the Fed since early 2016, OBFR aimed to measure banks’ funding costs in unsecured onshore markets, namely Fed Funds and dollars deposited at U.S. banks, as well as opaque offshore markets known as [Eurodollars](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-eurodollar-system?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web). The Fed’s proliferation of its swap lines after the Great Financial Crisis (GFC) had driven market participants to view onshore and offshore dollars as equals. Fed Funds and Eurodollars thus began trading as near substitutes, forming a _global_ unsecured dollar standard.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nTVl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdab8ca0a-d628-4f46-a0de-3ab58b72e792_8414x3235.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nTVl!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fdab8ca0a-d628-4f46-a0de-3ab58b72e792_8414x3235.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

Yet the GFC forever tainted monetary leaders’ and financial giants’ mindsets toward unsecured lending. As a result, trading volumes in the unsecured markets collapsed as Basel III and other regulations slowly became binding. As of 2015, volumes measured by the Fed via OBFR totaled just $250 billion. Soon after, via significant [reforms,](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2014-143) the SEC discouraged prime money funds (MMFs) from lending dollars unsecured in the Carribean-based Eurodollar market, the largest Eurodollar segment, reducing OBFR volumes to below $100 billion by late 2016. As OBFR measured a steady or decreasing[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-5-147748085) amount of unsecured dollar dealings, it was destined to fail as a reliable benchmark. Meanwhile, activity in secured money markets was flourishing.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LNpH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8b250da3-9de1-48cb-8efd-1b22971ceeaf_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LNpH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8b250da3-9de1-48cb-8efd-1b22971ceeaf_7863x6663.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktops)

By 2017, repo market volumes, which the Fed could observe, had reached up to $1 trillion daily. With secured dollar funding markets possessing greater depth than OBFR, the Fed’s decision to implement a secured rate benchmark was assured. LIBOR’s longstanding reign as king of funding markets had ended. A dollar standard based on secured rates was emerging.

On June 22nd, 2017, the ARRC chose the Secured Overnight Financing Rate (SOFR) as the successor to LIBOR, spurring the Fed to grow its library of dollar rate benchmarks. Officials deemed the repo market far too fragmented to justify producing a single measure and finalized plans to release various monitors. In April 2018, the Fed [began](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_180228) publishing two additional counterparts alongside SOFR to track rates on “general collateral” (GC) repos, i.e. “cash-driven” repos[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-6-147748085), where counterparties care more about obtaining and lending cash than the specific collateral securing the transaction.

The first was TGCR, the Triparty General Collateral Rate. Using data from the triparty market — i.e. the Bank of New York Mellon’s (BNYM) triparty platform where GC repos are primarily traded — TGCR aimed to measure rates on the most common triparty transaction: cash investors lending to large dealers and banks seeking to finance their market-making activities. The Fed captured these rates by excluding triparty trades between dealers in the interdealer segment ([GCF Repo](https://www.dtcc.com/clearing-and-settlement-services/ficc-gov/gcf-repo)) and transactions involving the Federal Reserve[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-7-147748085). Officials then combined TGCR data with trades from the interdealer segment to produce BGCR, the Broad General Collateral Rate, contrasting TGCR rates with the overall triparty market. The Fed now had a broad benchmark that monitored both “interdealer” and “customer-to-dealer” triparty trades, providing a clear overview of GC (i.e. cash-driven) repo rates.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Av2Z!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F74dec7f9-019e-461e-8c73-18e418f62c7e_5051x5355.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Av2Z!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F74dec7f9-019e-461e-8c73-18e418f62c7e_5051x5355.png)

the Fed’s library of rate benchmarks | Conk’s Fed benchmark monitor

Then, there was SOFR, the Secured Overnight Financing Rate, which combined rates captured by TGCR and BGCR with rates from markets where “specific collateral” (SC) repos traded. In contrast to GC repos, SC repos are “security-driven” repos, where the cash lender, who’s more of a securities borrower in the transaction, cares more about the specific security they receive than the yield they earn for lending dollars to the cash borrower (who’s also a securities lender in the transaction). This is because SC repos trade exclusively in the [DVP](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d) (delivery vs. payment[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-8-147748085)) repo markets, characterized by how securities actually move between securities accounts[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-9-147748085) (via [Fedwire DVP](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-how-us-government-securities)), which allows the cash lender receiving the specific security to trade it in the [secondary markets](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash) (for instance, to short-sell) before the repo trade matures.

At the time of SOFR’s creation, two SC repo markets existed: the centrally cleared [DVP market](https://dtcclearning.com/products-and-services/fixed-income-clearing/gsd/dvp-service.html) operated by the FICC (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation) and the non-centrally cleared DVP market, [NCCBR](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20the%20market%20for%20DVP%20repos%20that%20settle%20outside%20the%20FICC%2C%20i.e.%20directly%20between%20participants%E2%80%99%20accounts%20over%20Fedwire%2C%20has%20been%20assigned%20the%20unfortunate%20name%20of%20%E2%80%9Cnon%2Dcentrally%20cleared%20bilateral%20repo%E2%80%9D%20or%20NCCBR.). As data was only available in the centrally-cleared market, the Fed had no choice but to use the FICC’s DVP service to monitor SC repos and ignore its opaque counterpart. Nevertheless, with SOFR, TGCR, and BGCR now in action, the Fed could track a variety of repos from (almost all) secured lending markets.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3mUF!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2493b8cb-34ff-463b-ab67-c0a2d188e106_6386x7423.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!3mUF!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2493b8cb-34ff-463b-ab67-c0a2d188e106_6386x7423.png)

the DVP repo markets visualized (available in [light mode](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true))

While the U.S. central bank architected its new benchmarks, the global regulatory complex enforced its [secured standard](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-rise-of-the-secured-standard). Financial behemoths worldwide had thus not only pulled back from unsecured dollar lending but also secured loans backed by private sector assets. Since the GFC, every regulation the U.S. empire ended up enforcing has manufactured demand for U.S. sovereign debt or securities issued by government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs). Basel III has [compelled](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-central-bank-pledgening) banks to hoard U.S. Treasuries and agency bonds, while the Volcker rule has banned banks from engaging in excess speculation, forcing them into short-term government debt. What’s more, the SEC’s money market reforms have encouraged MMFs to invest more of their giant dollar pools in repos backed by U.S. government securities and avoid investment in riskier, unsecured debt, like CP and CDs (commercial paper and certificates of deposits).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9Myz!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F28f1ede9-6d56-4ff2-b05f-da374988dd0d_848x544.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!9Myz!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F28f1ede9-6d56-4ff2-b05f-da374988dd0d_848x544.png)

Source: U.S. Treasury

The repo market has adapted accordingly. Unlike in the past, when toxic private-sector assets were pledged to back numerous trades, almost all repos today have been secured by U.S. Treasuries — with agency debt and agency MBS from GSEs playing backup[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-10-147748085). As market participants consider just sovereign debt, mainly U.S. government securities, to be pristine collateral, many private sector funding markets, like commercial paper, have been cut in half. Subsequently, despite data still being available for other types of collateral, the Fed has only included repos backed by U.S. Treasuries in its secured rate benchmarks.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!P4md!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Feae4931c-a2b3-4bb9-9577-34645ef80845_5520x4340.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!P4md!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Feae4931c-a2b3-4bb9-9577-34645ef80845_5520x4340.png)

When LIBOR ceased to be published in June 2023, the ARRC had architected a secured rate benchmark to their fancy. SOFR was based on transactional data, not data exposed to (supposed) manipulation. Volumes in the underlying repo market had risen into the tens of trillions, and SOFR, as a risk-free benchmark, was moving in tandem with other money market rates. With their mission accomplished, the ARRC dissolved in November 2023. From then on, SOFR was set to monitor trillions of dollars in repo trades within diverse segments of the market: triparty (_in yellow_), GCF Repo (_in green_), and the FICC’s DVP repo service (_in blue_) — but still excluding the elusive NCCBR market _(in purple)_.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LNpH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8b250da3-9de1-48cb-8efd-1b22971ceeaf_7863x6663.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LNpH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8b250da3-9de1-48cb-8efd-1b22971ceeaf_7863x6663.png)

the U.S. repo market today

Fast forward to today, and the secured standard has endured. SOFR remains the king of dollar benchmarks. Nevertheless, another evolution in market structure has surfaced, provoking the Fed to begin its first meaningful SOFR makeover.

On July 18th, the Fed [announced](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_240718) two proposed modifications, one trivial and the other significant. The trivial change involves the Fed stripping out “inter-affiliate” repos, transactions between entities belonging to the same organization, such as a bank’s commercial bank subsidiary and its broker-dealer. Banks will lend reserves from their commercial bank subsidiary’s Fed master (reserve) account via repos to their broker-dealer (the affiliate), facilitating trades in other markets, such as FX swaps. Strangely, as most of these trades occur in repo markets where counterparties remain anonymous, bank desks often don’t know they’re obtaining funding from their own affiliates_._ In the Canadian repo market, most trades between affiliates print [above](https://www.bankofcanada.ca/wp-content/uploads/2019/02/update-carr-inter0affiliate-repos-enhanced-corra-28012019.pdf) average market rates, meaning raising cash is more costly when those involved belong to the same firm.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LAUy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F573b6b5a-5b65-426a-8575-6abccf9ca8ae_6709x5789.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LAUy!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F573b6b5a-5b65-426a-8575-6abccf9ca8ae_6709x5789.png)

an inter-affiliate repo trade by JPM, funding an FX swap

Although inter-affiliate trades have made up half the volume in repo markets, eliminating such dealings on historical data collected by the Fed has failed to impact where SOFR prints. Fed officials’ other modification, though, will remove forces driving volatility in future readings. The transition to a centrally cleared era in the U.S. Treasury market and, hence, the highly interconnected repo market has begun, prompting an [exodus](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-i) from the murky, non-centrally cleared NCCBR market to the centrally cleared DVP market — the latter being the transparent region of DVP repo monitored by U.S. agencies and included in SOFR.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SYkp!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa561bcad-a444-4062-ac55-20219db1584e_6632x5342.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SYkp!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa561bcad-a444-4062-ac55-20219db1584e_6632x5342.png)

As _Conks_ [anticipated](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part?utm_source=publication-search#:~:text=Differing%20only%20by%20central%20clearing%2C%20the%20triparty%20and%20GCF%20markets%20will%20likely%20merge%20into%20one%2C%20as%20rates%20in%20both%20markets%20converge), overall repo rates have converged, and the market has reached maximum fragmentation. Alas, now the centrally cleared era has produced new unintended outcomes. Previously hidden from regulators in the opaque regions of NCCBR, large spikes in volume at certain rates have emerged in the FICC’s DVP market and, consequently, into the calculation of SOFR.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WmnZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd43088b8-c2f2-49dd-9d95-e2c754f92989_1049x475.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!WmnZ!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd43088b8-c2f2-49dd-9d95-e2c754f92989_1049x475.png)

Source: New York Fed

The spikes in volume near the lowest recorded rates reveal the cost at which various [elaborate trades](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-bilateral-repo-markets?r=njk1&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web&triedRedirect=true), from relative value to the cash-futures basis, have been facilitated via repo. DVP repo markets contain a market for “specials”, where a specific security experiences increased demand after becoming critical to a trade. During key timings, market participants compete to acquire a particular security via DVP repos, driving the repo rate[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-11-147748085) on the security below rates on other similar securities — i.e. general collateral (GC). A security starts “trading special” when cash lenders (securities borrowers) charge cash borrowers (securities lenders) a much lower repo rate solely to increase their chances of obtaining the security over other market players. This pushes the repo rate lower than GC (cash-driven) rates, but not all SC (security-driven) repos involve specials. Typically, only when a security becomes essential to a popular trade do rates decline significantly, and the security starts trading special. SC repos could trade at GC rates when the security is no longer desirable.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z-vX!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F885e1522-120d-4480-b36b-ef2abf5d71a6_2099x1118.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Z-vX!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F885e1522-120d-4480-b36b-ef2abf5d71a6_2099x1118.png)

GC rates minus the repo rate on U.S. Treasuries trading special | Source: New York Fed

A classic example involves U.S. Treasury auctions, which create demand for the latest issue of a U.S. Treasury, i.e. the on-the-run issue. Because of the increased liquidity, market participants desire the latest issue enough for it to trade special. The “specialness,” the spread between regular repo rates (GC rates) and the repo rate on the security trading special, expands until the Treasury announces the auction of the next issue (highlighted in grey). The increase in spread over time reflects the reduced supply of bonds available in repo markets as more securities are allocated to portfolios.

Another prominent instance is the Treasury cash-futures basis trade, which employs leverage via the repo market. The CME defines a basket of U.S. Treasuries that market participants must deliver into the futures contract (i.e., exchange the bond for cash). One of these securities will become the cheapest to deliver (CTD)[12](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-12-147748085) and, therefore, the most profitable. Subsequently, that security or close alternatives could end up trading special in repo markets.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oOLO!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa378fb6d-6966-48d1-b3e1-bc9f943d491e_7072x4269.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oOLO!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa378fb6d-6966-48d1-b3e1-bc9f943d491e_7072x4269.png)

As the centrally cleared era matures, more repo specials will find their way into SOFR. Presently, the Fed trims the lower quarter of DVP rates but on a “volume-weighted basis”, not a hard cutoff. The Fed is consequently proposing a fresh approach for handling specials: remove 20% of the lowest-rate transactions from their DVP repo collection. The Fed released a chart showing the net effects of these changes — proposed minus current SOFR readings.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8ahH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c909c8e-0733-412d-9328-8067b2ab8202_565x462.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8ahH!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5c909c8e-0733-412d-9328-8067b2ab8202_565x462.png)

Source: New York Fed, Bank of New York Mellon, New York Fed Staff Calculations, Office of Financial Research

It appears harmless, but other plumbing dynamics have muted increased volatility in the Fed’s benchmark data. Covering short positions on securities in high demand is the most common driver of specials. Yet, COVID-19 and geopolitical turmoil have driven the U.S. empire to issue mammoth amounts of sovereign debt. The growth in Treasury auction sizes has increased the supply of bonds available, which allows traders to easily close out their short positions, reducing pressure on repo rates and, hence, specials. Moreover, as the Fed has jacked up short-end rates, the yield curve remains inverted. A steep, uninverted curve generally offers more opportunities for cash-futures basis traders. Thus, the full effect of specials on SOFR has yet to be realized, i.e. when the yield curve steepens and the U.S. deficit subsides. With greed politics in play, the chance of reduced U.S. deficits remains a pipe dream. A steeper yield curve, however, may be just around the corner. The Fed will act sooner rather than later.

# The Fed's Repo Defensive: Part I - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search
> the U.S. central bank's emergency repo facility is set to evolve

---
For the first time, more than three years after becoming an official installation, the Fed’s [SRF](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/standing-overnight-repurchase-agreement-facility.htm) (standing repo facility) has been tapped — for its true purpose — by major market makers. A stormy quarter-end[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-1-149657448) forced hundreds of billions of dollars in [repos](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) to trade above the Fed’s [target range](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4dbdda33-e432-47b4-acea-4d3dad6d4477_6157x4578.png), causing primary dealers to borrow from the central bank’s cheaper alternative. Even so, dealers only tapped a fraction of overall volumes via the SRF. This not only prevented the Fed from enforcing a “hard ceiling” on rates[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-2-149657448) but exposed notable flaws in its defense mechanism, which may fail to prevent turmoil from spreading in the next repo upheaval. With market pressures building and liquidity draining, the U.S. central bank is thus set to reinforce its SRF and attempt to deter spillovers[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-3-149657448) into FX swaps and the interbank[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-4-149657448) market. The Fed’s “Repo Defensive” is looming.

Seven years ago, aiming to reduce its supposedly mammoth balance sheet, the Fed launched its first official QT[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-5-149657448) (quantitative tightening) operation. Two years of balance sheet runoff (i.e. reduction) later, the U.S. central bank [decreased](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WRESBAL) reserve balances from $2.5 trillion to a mere $1.3 trillion. Interbank liquidity began hovering at unstable levels. Then, on September 17th, 2019, money market rates in repo, FX swaps, and Fed Funds [skyrocketed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/September_2019_events_in_the_U.S._repo_market#/media/File:Cfl423-fig1.png), all blowing through the top of the Fed’s target range — i.e. its upper jaws. The “repocalypse” of 2019 had arisen.

As panic circulated, some repo trades captured by [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr), the Fed’s secured lending [benchmark](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-dislocation-part-two), reached almost four times the Fed’s upper jaws of 2.25%, while [EFFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr), the U.S. central bank’s official policy rate, poked through narrowly. Liquidity grew so scarce that banks and dealers scrambled for cash in various opaque markets. First, U.S. dollars dried up in repo, then the FX swap market — repo’s closest alternative, forcing large banks to turn to their funding market of last resort: Fed Funds (i.e. the market for bank reserves). These prominent market makers bid for overnight Fed Funds (o/n FF) and lent reserves into other money markets, profiting from much wider cross-market spreads[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-6-149657448). Regardless, extreme imbalances in money markets persisted, which not even JPMorgan — the _private_ lender of last resort — or the [FHLBs](https://fhlbanks.com/) (Federal Home Loan Banks) — the public lender of _first_ resort — could counteract. Only a public lender (and soon-to-be dealer) of _last_ resort, with the sole power to create interbank liquidity, could bring money market rates back into balance.

As Fed Funds ([EFFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr)) soon breached the upper limit of the Fed’s target range, a mammoth intervention was inevitable. Central bank architects were about to unveil their latest round of monetary alchemy, turning the Fed into not only a lender but a dealer of last resort.

Via its trading desk, known as The Desk, the Fed unleashed a liquidity bazooka. From September 17th, officials held daily repo operations at 9:30 am, offering up to $75 billion in total to primary dealers against a pledge of U.S. Treasuries, agency bonds, or agency MBS (mortgage-backed securities). The stress in repo markets was so extreme that the Fed repeated its auction every day of the week, with dealers tapping the maximum each morning. Two days later, to ease pressures further, officials initiated a textbook “technical adjustment”, lowering [IORB](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/reserve-balances.htm) (the rate the Fed pays banks on their reserves) and [O/N RRP](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/domestic-market-operations/monetary-policy-implementation/repo-reverse-repo-agreements#:~:text=The%20Overnight%20Reverse%20Repo%20Facility,reserve%20balances%20(IORB)%20rate.) (the rate the Fed pays on reverse repos via its RRP facility). By offering lower risk-free returns via its facilities, monetary leaders made private lending appear more attractive, spurring activity among non-Fed participants[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-7-149657448). Soon after, overnight rates in money markets declined, though tensions persisted.

To contain any further money market turmoil over quarter-end and beyond, the Fed then scheduled a series of overnight and “term”[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-8-149657448) repo operations to last until at least January 2020. Moreover, at the start of October 2019, the Fed [initiated](https://www.latimes.com/business/story/2019-10-11/federal-reserve-to-buy-60-billion-of-treasury-bills-per-month) what some commentators called “not-QE”. Vowing to prevent a replay of the repocalypse, the U.S. central bank boosted interbank liquidity by creating newly minted reserves and engaging in asset swaps with primary dealers, buying t-bills at a pace of $60 billion per month. Officials avoided coupons (i.e. longer-term U.S. Treasuries) to signal they were preserving market functioning, not restarting full-blown QE (quantitative easing).

A few months later, the COVID-19 market panic quickly adjusted their stance. But as hysteria subsided, following immense liquidity injections, money markets entered a lengthy period of calm. More than a year after COVID-19 generated further breaches of its upper jaws, the Fed [announced](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/pressreleases/monetary20210728b.htm), at its July 2021 meeting, an official standing repo facility — the SRF. Yet, a plumbing mystery remained: the trigger of the September 2019 repocalypse.

To this day, the intricacy of money markets has left the biggest repo tantrum unsolved. Many liquidity-draining catalysts were present, before and during the repocalypse, which could have spurred the epic rate spike. A day earlier, on September 16th, corporate tax payments (due every quarter) pulled reserves from the banking system. Corporations paid either with bank deposits or cash stored in money market funds (MMFs), both resulting in banks wiring reserves (interbank liquidity) to the U.S. government’s bank account, the [TGA](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN) (Treasury General Account), reducing available reserves in the banking system[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-9-149657448). MMFs then had less cash on hand, reducing their ability to lend into the repo markets. What’s more, a large settlement of Treasury securities had the same effect while constraining already-stuffed dealers — who had to absorb an extra ~$80 billion in supply while weak customer demand persisted.

Consequently, all these forces caused repo rates to climb. Still, lower reserve balances and increased government bond issuance weren’t enough to prompt a repocalypse. On multiple prior occasions, these catalysts arose with no rate spikes emerging. Another catalyst was needed to set off money market fireworks.

Unfortunately for the Fed, potential triggers were in abundance. The U.S. government had been slowly increasing its cash balances[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-10-149657448), reducing reserves that banks could use to finance repo transactions. Another debt ceiling battle in U.S. Congress had pushed Treasury officials to use cash in the TGA instead of issuing more debt, increasing reserve levels temporarily. Just before the repo rate spike, though, the debt ceiling conflict was resolved. Once again, TGA inflows began sucking reserves from the Fed master accounts of large U.S. banks serving as private repo dealers of last resort. Repo markets had come to rely on the largest four U.S. banks for over half their funding[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-11-149657448). Thus, on the day of the repocalypse, despite the potential benefit of borrowing at juicer spreads, these banks gave up lending in the early morning, only recommencing after the Fed announced an emergency backstop at 9:00 am (as the chart below illustrates). Blurred communications then developed between the major cash lenders and cash borrowers, with MMFs and primary dealers failing to take advantage of supposedly attractive repo borrowing and lending rates.

MMFs lacked substantial market intelligence, while large banks bridging the usual imbalances[12](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-12-149657448) grew reserve-constrained, likely by the onslaught of liquidity requirements imposed via Basel III and Dodd-Frank. Required to maintain adequate end-of-day and intraday liquidity, banks ceased deploying reserves into repo to prevent falling toward (let alone below) regulatory limits.

On top of all this, on Sept 16th, GSEs (government-sponsored enterprises), which hold reserve accounts and act as major interbank liquidity providers, used cash to pay out principal and interest on MBS — funds they usually deployed into money markets to bridge market imbalances.

This cluster of catalysts provided hints of what caused the repocalypse. Recent episodes, however, have revealed new triggers that could prompt a replay. During the last quarter-end, over $600 billion in repos traded above the Fed’s upper jaws, compelling market participants to bid for cash above the cheapest SRF rate, signaling a genuine cash shortage. According to our talks with market participants, real concerns grew over the trading day that liquidity in repo markets was too scarce. Other sources suggested that “breaking the seal” — i.e. being the first to tap the facility above the minimum bid rate — was an unserious yet unprohibited reason for tapping “emergency” liquidity.

Either way, the recent lack of uptake has exposed many SRF imperfections, which keep pushing repo rates above the Fed’s target range as if it were routine. For starters, market timings create the potential for negative spreads[13](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-13-149657448), a repo trader and dealer’s worst nightmare. When you lend cash in the morning and can’t forecast where borrowing rates will trade in the afternoon (as the SRF opens at 1:30 pm), interest rate risk arises. Thus, dealers will pay market rates in the morning, ignoring potentially cheaper Fed repos available after midday. What’s more, the longstanding stigma of using a Fed facility — i.e. the fear of others perceiving your central bank borrowings as a sign of financial weakness — has spread to the SRF. According to _Conks’_ dialogues with prominent market makers, emergency repo stigma exists, similar to the Discount Window. These weaknesses, alongside the latest quarter-end episode, reveal that transmitting cash via Fed repos to all market participants is not as easy as the Fed envisions, a consequence of its own designs and traditions. By permitting only banks and dealers, not every type of repo player, to access the SRF, the Fed prevents losses to itself at the expense of greater liquidity, and the U.S. central bank will likely keep it that way. Yet, in a future where repos continue to dominate money markets, plumbing alchemy is required to maintain stability while restricting direct access to the SRF and the Fed’s balance sheet. Now, the clock is ticking to find a solution.

Financial conditions remain loose, and liquidity is [ample](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/money-market-update-aca) by every measure. Spillovers from repo into other key dollar markets have thus failed to materialize. Even so, with the U.S. central bank continuing QT, dealer balance sheets remaining constrained, and hedge funds demanding increased repo funding, money market pressures will continue to build. The strength of the Fed’s repo facility will be tested again but, next time, by much greater forces.

# The Fed's Repo Defensive: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search
> the inner workings of the Fed's emergency repo facility

---
_— click [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one) for The Fed’s Repo Defensive: Part I_

The Fed is set to embark on a “Repo Defensive”: the first rework of its Standing Repo Facility ([SRF](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/repo-agreement-ops-faq)), a mechanism for providing emergency [cash loans](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) to banks and primary dealers. Another [cash squeeze](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/money-market-update-5a8) at the end of September revealed key market makers’ reluctance to use the SRF, a sign that the Fed’s facility may fail to stem money market contagion. Yet, even with [upcoming](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-rewire) fortifications, the repo market will remain the most systemic yet fragmented part of the dollar system. Consequently, Fed architects must remove barriers deterring dealers from tapping the SRF before the next storm emerges. The first iteration of the Fed’s repo “defense mechanism” is approaching.

During the latest September quarter-end[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-1-150280822), another liquidity squeeze arose. Dealers, as [usual](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-great-flattening), were pulling back from market-making to tidy their balance sheets. Meanwhile, money funds (MMFs) reached their self-imposed counterparty lending limits, parking their leftover cash in the [Fed’s RRP](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-mechanics-of-the) while rejecting bids from major market makers. By lunchtime, dealers glanced at their screens and witnessed repo rates blowing through the top of the Fed’s [target range](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFEDTARU). A mild cash shortage had developed. At 1:30pm, primary dealers were forced to tap the Fed’s repo facility, with the U.S. central bank again becoming a dealer of last resort, bridging all market imbalances.

That day, over $600 billion in repos traded above “SRFR”[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-2-150280822): the cheapest rate offered on overnight (o/n) Fed repos — i.e. cash loans from the U.S. central bank secured against sovereign bond collateral. Rather than tapping the $500 billion available at the facility, dealers borrowed a mere $2.6 billion, suggesting market makers failed to take advantage of cheaper cash lending rates. But in complex markets, things aren’t always what they seem. It appeared dealers could earn a juicier spread by lending cash from their clearing accounts[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-3-150280822) in the morning, waiting until 1:30pm (when the SRF opens), and covering the amount via the Fed rather than in costly private markets. In reality, however, timings dictate dealers’ actions. During morning trading, market makers provide most liquidity between 7-9am to maintain key client relationships and avoid negative balances (i.e. overdrafts) in their trading accounts — which incur fees levied by their clearing banks[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-4-150280822) at 8:30am. What’s more, repo rates remain volatile intraday. Lending in the morning in anticipation of borrowing from the Fed in the afternoon creates interest rate risk for repo desks operating “matched books”[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-5-150280822). Market makers will thus execute their trading activities as soon as possible, many hours before the SRF opens.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!o0At!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd834fe98-a912-4153-a887-42e7439f163e_1703x1403.png)

Subsequently, as intended, the Fed’s o/n repo facility serves as a dealer of last resort, not a “hard ceiling” that holds repo rates within central bank [targets](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F418dbce5-6b77-4f83-8d2b-daa416153818_6157x4578.png). Almost every month-end and quarter-end, [SOFR](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F4dbdda33-e432-47b4-acea-4d3dad6d4477_6157x4578.png) — the Fed’s repo rate benchmark — exceeds the U.S. central bank’s target range while officials barely react. Instead, they hope the SRF’s significant defects will only be revealed when the next money market crisis arises and fails to provoke mayhem. Recent episodes, meanwhile, show the SRF needs to evolve so that market makers access emergency repos without friction, preventing spillovers[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-6-150280822) into other markets that prompt a (reluctant) Fed to [end QT](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-global-put-part-i) early. The U.S. central bank must now introduce more alluring features.

Coincidentally, in both macro and plumbing realms, a previously reactive Fed is becoming proactive. An SRF rewire will shortly hit their agenda. Even so, revamping an o/n repo facility is far from straightforward. To illustrate the elaborate task Fed architects face, we must turn to the SRF’s inner workings.

[

Borrowing cash via the Fed’s repo defense mechanism creates an elaborate web of repo (and reverse repo[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-7-150280822)) transactions, with the Bank of New York (BNY) serving as a clearing and settlement[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-8-150280822) agent for the Fed and all twenty-four primary dealers. From start to finish, these chains not only demonstrate the intricacy of repo markets but also show why market makers only tap emergency repos during the extremes. In the latest cash squeeze, the reason dealers needed to tap the SRF remained unclear. It was either an attempt to arbitrage cheaper rates from the Fed versus private sector lenders or a genuine fear of a cash shortage. _Conks’_ Best Guess™ was a few primary dealers got caught holding too much collateral (i.e. too short cash) and found no private cash lenders willing to offset their positions[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-9-150280822).

Earlier that morning, primary dealers had completed most of their dealings, with most trading activity and liquidity available at or near the market open around 7am (New York time). Throughout sunrise, they supplied cash via repos to clients — mainly hedge funds, needing dollars to enter leveraged positions in the U.S. Treasury cash market[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-10-150280822). Hedge funds had executed trades the previous day, agreeing to settle that day using funding from a repo. Repo desks loaned cash to hedge funds, who then sent the securities they purchased to dealers as collateral, thereby entering [typical trades](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-relative-value-trading) — such as relative value (RV) and the cash-futures basis.

[

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!53LG!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff388743f-1d51-4a58-be8a-24ddd971cfe4_7565x6482.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktop)

Participants conducted these transactions in the [DVP (delivery vs. payment) market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d), the only repo arena where speculators can obtain securities in their accounts to trade actively in secondary markets[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-11-150280822). Shortly after execution, the BNY (acting as a clearing bank for all involved) sent transfer messages to Fedwire — the Fed’s real-time gross settlement system, which began settling trades from 8:30am. Upon receiving valid instructions, Fedwire’s mechanism transferred official securities records between hedge funds, dealers, and cash market participants, who hold accounts with the BNY and other direct Fedwire participants[12](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-12-150280822). The Fedwire system then sent transfer notifications, enabling custodians to settle the cash leg of trades between their clients’ trading accounts automatically (as shown below).

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NMPZ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F81da3920-7321-434d-8d97-94ba6d4c2375_6099x6587.png)

By midday, hedge funds had entered their positions, and security sellers had received their cash. Some dealers, however, held imbalanced books. Moreover, the usual cash lenders were either absent (MMFs) or too costly (other dealers and banks). Lunchtime trading volumes grew thin, dealers had [engaged](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-great-flattening) in window dressing, and money market funds had parked their remaining liquidity in the Fed’s RRP — between 12:45pm and 1:15pm. As most cash lenders fled by 1:30pm, dealers desperate for funding could borrow from the SRF at much lower rates than their usual counterparties. The Fed subsequently became the optimal lender.

Between 1:30pm and 1:45pm, via its money market desk — known as “The Desk”, the Fed conducted its daily SRF repo operation through FedTrade[13](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-13-150280822), the U.S. central bank’s proprietary trading system, open exclusively to [approved](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/standing-repo-facility-counterparties) SRF counterparties: all primary dealers and pre-approved depository institutions (i.e. banks). These market makers submitted what the Fed calls “propositions”: bids to receive cash in exchange for pledging [collateral](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/domestic-market-operations/monetary-policy-implementation/repo-reverse-repo-agreements/TOMO-Repo-Collateral-Schedule) — U.S. government or government-sponsored securities (e.g., U.S. Treasuries, agency bonds, and agency MBS) — at a rate equal to or above SRFR. The higher the quality of collateral, the more cash that dealers obtained[14](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-14-150280822).

But unlike the Fed’s [primary defense mechanism](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-feds-primary-defense) — the Discount Window, which lends to all eligible borrowers at the same rate, SRF counterparties do battle in competitive auctions to obtain the $500 billion offered in each daily operation. If a dealer feels the urge, it will bid at one or multiple prices slightly (or, in extreme situations, considerably) above SRFR to boost its chances of acquiring Fed liquidity over others. The fact that dealers felt the need to bid at 5.01% instead of the cheapest rate (5.00%) at the latest September quarter-end indicated a real fear of a cash shortage, not a desire to arbitrage cheaper repo rates. Some dealers likely held a trading book deemed too one-sided for their risk department’s liking. Without a private alternative, they tapped enough Fed liquidity to balance their books — even paying a premium to get the job done.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!UhN-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa8ae5c49-7659-46b9-8c48-89352ddcc859_848x895.webp)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!UhN-!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa8ae5c49-7659-46b9-8c48-89352ddcc859_848x895.webp)

Source: New York Fed, Federal Reserve Repo Operations [Webpage](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/desk-operations/repo)

Though the Fed stopped accepting bids (as usual) at 1:45pm, dealers received central bank cash a few hours later. Unlike repos in the DVP market, SRF repos are [triparty](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) repos, which means they trade exclusively on the BNY’s platform. Following a series of reforms introduced after the GFC (Great Financial Crisis), triparty settlement is now only available from 3:30pm[15](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-15-150280822). Beforehand, the BNY extended large amounts of intraday cash to dealers to help settle their triparty trades, which exposed the BNY to increased systemic risk. In reply, the custodial giant altered its schedule so that trades on its platform no longer settled from 8:30am but settled from 3:30pm until the market closed in the evening. This discouraged dealers from tapping cash intraday from the BNY, reducing exposures between itself and key market makers. Since then, dealers have booked repo trades with hedge funds in the [DVP market](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market-part-93d) that settle immediately in the morning while booking an offsetting trade in the triparty market, mostly with MMFs (and now the Fed), that settle by mid-afternoon[16](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-16-150280822).

Accordingly, on September quarter-end at 3:30pm, the settlement of SRF repos commenced. As with other [triparty repos](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market), cash moved between clearing accounts that the Fed and dealers held at the BNY, and securities were merely recorded as pledged by the BNY’s system in a segregated account during the trade, serving as collateral in a default scenario. The Fed increased the supply of reserves in the banking system by crediting the Fed “master account”[17](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-17-150280822) of the BNY, which credited the Fed’s triparty (i.e. cash) account in return. Next, the BNY allocated securities from the dealer’s “longbox” — a link to one or multiple accounts holding official securities records. Upon allocating securities, the BNY credited U.S. dollars to the dealer’s clearing account, who finally obtained liquidity to balance its books. The repo market backstop had been accomplished.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!20ZP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F75bc0327-5340-4fff-b75c-4edda22b89f5_6147x5924.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!20ZP!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F75bc0327-5340-4fff-b75c-4edda22b89f5_6147x5924.png)

click to enlarge (requires desktop/tablet with a zoom function)

Yet, the September quarter-end was a snooze compared to money market storms. Despite the SRF doing its job in normal times, countless obstacles will prevent major makers from tapping Fed liquidity with ease in a crisis. If officials wanted to thwart any emergency, they’d open the facility early during peak trading hours at around 8:00am, not late at 1:30pm. This modification would thwart most money market spikes and a repeat of the [repocalypse](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedb0f599-4cf4-489a-85be-dcc20b2f6fb7_7392x6050.png) while placing a hard ceiling on repo rates. But it would transform the Fed from a dealer of last resort to a dealer of first resort, with the size of its balance sheet fluctuating dramatically. Whenever rates surpass the top of the Fed’s target range — the same as SRFR, dealers would likely tap the facility en masse.

Instead, the SRF’s major roles, [advocated](https://www.newyorkfed.org/newsevents/speeches/2024/per241112) by top officials, are to prevent spillovers into other funding markets like Fed Funds and FX swaps and deliver a mechanism for market makers to bypass “firesales”: forced selling of assets to raise cash. Regulation and the subsequent rise in demand for U.S. government debt have forced dealers to hold a persistent [long position](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/counterparties/primary-dealers-statistics) in Treasuries. Hence, a dash to raise cash is the new destabilizer. Even large amounts of government securities cannot be dumped for dollars without instability arising — as the COVID-19 market panic demonstrated in spectacular style. With the SRF now in action, dealer and banks can temporarily swap their Treasuries for cash[18](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-18-150280822) via o/n Fed repos. This — in theory — thwarts instability, but the official installation has still to be tested by a heavy bombardment of securities sales.

What’s more, stigma and market timing — the SRF’s worst enemies — will make its job even more challenging. In the heat of early morning trading, making markets remains optimal, even at rates above SRFR. Dealers, above all else, care most about earning the juiciest spreads, not the absolute level of repo rates[19](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-19-150280822). As the SRF opens five hours later at 1:30pm, dealers can secure a lending rate but must suffer a grueling wait for cheaper borrowing costs from o/n Fed repos. Consequently, the risk of lending at a negative spread — a market maker’s hell — increases, reducing a dealer’s willingness to tap the Fed’s repo facility even in times of trouble.

Odds are further stacked against the SRF’s ability to stem contagion, with the fear of using emergency liquidity engrained in corporate banking culture. Recently, the Fed has [attempted](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/files/barr20240926a.pdf) to encourage more SRF uptake via various incentives, such as allowing banks to use[20](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-20-150280822) o/n Fed repos to prove they can raise sufficient liquidity in stress scenarios. Even so, as _Conks’_ talks with primary dealers confirmed, the latest stormy episode revealed stigma persists not only at the Discount Window but also at the SRF. The market-making behemoths demand greater incentives.

Numerous fixes have thus been proposed but remain far from flawless or painless to implement. The U.S. central bank could [centrally clear](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-repo-market-exodus-part-i) SRF trades, boosting dealers’ balance sheet capacity[21](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one?utm_source=publication-search#footnote-21-150280822) and incentivizing usage or open the facility to hedge funds and others without direct access to Fed liquidity. These refinements, however, may increase moral hazard while exposing the U.S. central bank to greater, more frequent losses. Since most solutions create new problems, the design of the SRF remains imperfect for a reason. Architecting a repo facility has become one of the toughest tasks for Fed alchemists, and updating the SRF will be just as formidable.

# The Shadow Cash Market: Part I - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i
> the Treasury market's covert twin is boosting liquidity

---
— _click here for_ _[Pro](https://pro.conks.plumbing/p/pro-the-shadow-cash-market)_

The mammoth endeavor to strengthen the U.S. Treasury cash market[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-1-163852625) has entered a new iteration. In the face of immense supply, ever-increasing measures to fortify the most systemically important debt market have now been paired with a new admin’s desire to greatly suppress yields on U.S. Treasuries (USTs). Monetary heads of state have hinted at enabling greater incentives[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-2-163852625) for the largest market makers to absorb incoming issuance, potentially boosting the balance sheet capacity of dealers enough to initiate a “plumbing liberation”[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-3-163852625). Officials are now in a semi-race against time to toughen the pipes of the UST plumbing before the next market episode arises.

The fortification of the U.S. Treasury market is now fully underway. While Sec Bessent and POTUS execute policies (aiming) to dampen yields, thought leaders both inside and outside the Federal Reserve have expanded their efforts to instill confidence in America’s sovereign debt goliath. The release of regular, optimistic [liquidity checkups](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2024/09/has-treasury-market-liquidity-improved-in-2024/), plus the [removal of obstacles](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/Microsites/tmpg/files/Best-Practices-on-Treasury-Repo-Risk-Management.pdf) preventing hedge funds from arbitraging dislocations, are just a few examples. Elsewhere, under the all-seeing eye of the agencies[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-4-163852625), the FICC (Fixed Income Clearing Corporation) — the sole [central counterparty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_counterparty_clearing) for USTs — has [begun to liberalize](https://www.dtcc.com/news/2025/march/25/dtccs-ficc-now-live-with-new-treasury-clearing-capabilities) access to its centrally cleared markets, an ecosystem believed to quash systemic risk. As leaders enforce relentless measures to bolster market stability, however, obscure regions of financial markets, unknown to most, continue to provide a substantial liquidity boost.

Over the past few decades, financial alchemy created by major liquidity providers, both traditional (i.e. banks with primary dealer arms) and contemporary (other non-bank market makers[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-5-163852625)), has been largely concealed from the mainstream while giving rise to fresh techniques that grease cash bond liquidity. Dealer banks and other “legacy” market players have somewhat retreated, leaving nascent market makers to fill in the liquidity gap[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-6-163852625). _The_ _Shadow Cash Market_, where “bond-like” exposures allow market makers to intermediate trillions of dollars in fixed-income securities, has thus arisen. Liquidity in the most systemic market is consequently being underestimated, but also a subsequent batch of constraints that could bite major players during the next UST market upheaval.

Over the past few decades, the Treasury cash market has experienced several immense transformations. At the beginning of this century, major dealer banks, along with large broker-dealers, dominated trading volumes across all domains. As with every major market in recent history, the UST cash market has consisted of two major segments: one where market makers trade among themselves, the “dealer-to-dealer” or inter-dealer market, and another where market makers extend liquidity to end-customers, known as the “dealer-to-customer” (DTC) segment.

In the interdealer markets, trades are executed either bilaterally without a broker, in a “pure” dealer-to-dealer transaction, or via an interdealer broker, commonly known as an IDB. Around the turn of the century, all trading in the IDB segment was executed through voice-assisted platforms, where brokers provided dealers with screens on which they could post prices and subsequent quantities. Dealers executed trades by calling their brokers, who then displayed the resulting trade details on screen. Thus, for a fee, IDBs matched buyers and sellers while also maintaining anonymity. By 1999, however, voice-assisted brokers encountered serious competition. Soon-to-be primary dealer [Cantor Fitzgerald](https://www.cantor.com/) had introduced eSpeed, the first fully electronic interdealer broker platform, prompting dealers to launch a rival venue. One year later, [BrokerTec](https://www.cmegroup.com/markets/brokertec.html) went live, driving competition that ultimately led to the demise of “voice” as the dominant intermediary. Electronic trading platforms were destined to take over the market, and the icing on the cake arrived soon after.

In 2004, the interdealer market opened up to participants other than traditional dealers, allowing a new group of sophisticated players to alter the cash market structure once again. BrokerTec unleashed principal trading firms, known as [PTFs](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/how-do-principal-trading-firms-and-dealers-trade-around-fomc-statement-releases-20201231.html), (algorithmic trading firms that trade for their own account), along with a select group of large buyside firms, onto its dealer platform. The phrase “interdealer market” was about to become a misnomer. By deploying high-frequency trading (HFT) algorithms onto IDB platforms, PTFs began to conquer the interdealer market, with liquidity and trading volumes soon quadrupling. These automated players dominated the bid and offer on BrokerTec’s [central limit order book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_limit_order_book) (CLOB), the primary trading protocol used by interdealer brokers. A decade went on, and HFT firms evolved into the chief liquidity providers in interdealer markets, so much so that they were soon regarded as the “liquidity providers of dealers”.

Fast forward to today, and PTFs haven’t stopped their onslaught. Top HFT firms have infiltrated the other segment of the interdealer market: the pure, unbrokered dealer-to-dealer (DTD) region, continuing to take market share from other types of participants. Using the latest trading protocols, PTFs provide clients with live, executable prices electronically, thereby sidestepping the key market behemoths that operate major IDB platforms.

As for the dealer-to-customer (DTC) market, however, PTFs have yet to overthrow their traditional dealer “rivals”. The major trading protocol in the DTC market remains electronic [Request for Quote](https://hummingbot.org/blog/exchange-types-explained-clob-rfq-amm/) (RFQ), launched around the same time as electronic IDBs. With RFQ-based protocols, a customer typically requests a quote from multiple dealer counterparties, who then send back prices, the best of which the customer accepts. Yet, due to the mechanics of fixed-income securities, PTFs have been unable to establish a significant presence in the DTC space. Unlike equities, bonds tend to reach maturity[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-7-163852625). Each Treasury market segment can thus be further divided into the trading of on-the-runs (the latest issue of a given maturity) and off-the-runs (older issues of the same maturity). Unfortunately for PTFs, a large portion of trades between dealers and customers involve off-the-run securities, which remain significantly less liquid. Subsequently, negotiating off-the-run trades tends to involve more intimate negotiations primarily via voice trading, away from the electronic order books in which PTFs dominate. What’s more, without immediate buyers, “seasoned” securities require warehousing on the balance sheets of dealer banks. The original market makers are therefore unlikely to be toppled anytime soon.

Still, with HFT firms siphoning large volumes from traditional dealers while achieving the same post-trade outcomes, the definition of “dealer” has become clouded enough to catch the attention of the regulatory establishment. A recent 2024 SEC [ruling](https://www.srz.com/en/news_and_insights/alerts/sec-targets-certain-proprietary-trading-firms-and-private-funds-with-expanded-dealer-definition) has forced the majority of PTFs to register as dealers, thereby absorbing higher regulatory burdens. With PTFs now categorized as more regulatory-friendly entities, though, the most recent [cash market evolution](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-treasury-market-evolution) can potentially progress much more swiftly.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Fn52!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58fb5692-679c-4282-b583-202619c1af69_6250x5000.png)

After causing the slow demise of centrally cleared volumes, major PTFs are now becoming official members of the FICC, gaining direct access to centrally cleared markets. Citadel’s dealer arm[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-8-163852625) and other HFT giants have already made the switch, with other behemoths joining in. Eventually, all large PTFs will become members, while their smaller counterparts gain access through “[indirect models](https://www.dtcc.com/ustclearing/-/media/Files/Downloads/Microsites/Treasury-Clearing/Covered-Treasury-Clearing-Activity-Decision-Tree.pptx),” where their agents — large dealer banks and broker-dealers with FICC membership — clear their trades. Even so, frictions between PTFs and their potential clearers have [persisted](https://www.risk.net/risk-management/7961212/ptfs-clash-with-banks-over-done-away-us-treasury-clearing), causing the SEC to [increase](https://www.sec.gov/newsroom/press-releases/2025-43) its deadline by an entire year. Monetary leaders will now have to provide enough incentives to achieve a centrally cleared evolution — turning the FICC into the major “glue” of the cash market[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-9-163852625).

But while the traditional cash market approaches its peak “nothing ever happens” phase, a covert twin has been indirectly greasing fixed-income markets. Despite a centrally cleared evolution incoming, balance sheet continues to grow scarcer, stimulating the growth of a “shadow” market, one that’s just beginning to flourish. It’s well known that shadow banks, i.e. non-bank financial institutions, have absorbed business that “legacy” banks lost from having to obey the post-GFC regulatory complex. The Dodd-Frank Act eradicated prop trading desks of major banks, like the powerhouses of Deutsche and Goldman, paving the way for non-bank market makers to dominate high-frequency trading. Hallmarked by the Fed’s G-SIB surcharge forcing JPMorgan to withdraw as one of two official clearing banks[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i#footnote-10-163852625), many “high-volume, low-margin” businesses became no longer viable for banking giants.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eK56!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d359b1f-0093-46b8-803f-34fa404157a6_6250x4750.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eK56!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7d359b1f-0093-46b8-803f-34fa404157a6_6250x4750.png)

What’s not understood, meanwhile, is the rise of the “shadow cash market”, where financial alchemy is used to gain exposure to or provide liquidity in bonds, such as USTs or high-yield, _without_ directly tapping the traditional cash markets. As shadow banks have absorbed business that banking giants have lost to a harsher regulatory landscape, other types of liquidity provision have emerged with little mainstream attention. Enough time has passed for shadow banks to not only emerge but also to enable new forms of liquidity through “bond-like” instruments, mostly via [total return swaps](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/t/totalreturnswap.asp) (TRS) and ETFs. Obtaining liquidity the usual way has demanded balance sheet and increased levels of capital, leading participants to tap “bond-like” instruments for exposure whenever the regular cash market can’t (or won’t) deliver. HFTs dominate activity in interdealer markets, but primary dealers and other dealers continue to preserve their significant share of intermediation. As demand for the long-end wanes globally, and dealers, under the Primary Dealer Act, remain forced buyers, balance sheets worldwide remain congested. With the major market makers struggling to provide cash market liquidity, they have shifted to other sources.

Cash bonds are now competing against cash bond-like assets. In the past few years, providing liquidity via “shadow cash” trades has become a key strategy for market makers, both dealer banks and non-bank dealers. Market participants continually seek opportunities in cash bonds and their associated derivatives, as well as any perceived mispricings between the two. Dealers arbitrage spreads while providing liquidity in a way that complies with a multitude of Dodd-Frank and Basel III restrictions. Well-known primary dealers like TD Securities employ algorithms to not only predict order flow and liquidity conditions but also counter competitors’ maneuvers, including PTFs.

One shadow cash strategy involves employing various popular ETFs to replace cash bonds in trades, utilizing the [creation-redemption mechanisms](https://www.ssga.com/us/en/intermediary/resources/education/how-etfs-are-created-and-redeemed) of ETFs to enter and exit positions, like covering shorts. Unsurprisingly, the same dealer banks and HFT firms happen to be APs (authorized participants) of ETFs, which can redeem shares to obtain baskets of bonds and vice versa. All these entities have further fueled shadow cash liquidity by renting out these mechanisms to other (non-AP) actors. Virtu and other HFTs gladly provide access to their ETF creation/redemption facilities for other institutional players to benefit. And if that’s not good enough, dealers have started offering bond exposure via total return swaps (TRS). Swaps dealers have even started losing out to futures (that provide greater margin efficiency), as the latest year-end funding squeeze [demonstrated](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-year-end-money-market-storm).

Regular cash bond trading, of course, still reigns supreme. Yet the shadow cash market is capturing a more extensive share of the volumes, while also counteracting illiquidity created by post-GFC regulations. The scale and sophistication of its mechanics, as well as its impact on liquidity, particularly during times of crisis, will be crucial to demystify...

...and soon revealed in _Part II_.

# The Shadow Cash Market: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii
> the "bond-like" market and understated liquidity

---
— _click here for [Part I](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-i) and here for_ _[Pro](https://pro.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii-pro)_

The latest phase in the fortification of the U.S. Treasury market has achieved even greater levels of pandemonium. Monetary leaders continue to fret over illiquidity, hallmarked by the U.S. Treasury initiating Operation “Delay Duration”, potentially suspending an expansion in long-end issuance until 2027[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-1-166336828). The latest boost in UST (U.S. Treasury) buybacks will also further stem volatility, neutralizing congested dealer inventories. POTUS, meanwhile, has backtracked on unseating Powell as the central bank chairman after threats to the Fed’s independence dramatically steepened the curve. This occurred just as central bank officials revealed that _[the Fed’s Relief Valve](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-relief-valve-part-i)_ could include exempting USTs at the dealer level[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-2-166336828), providing ([supposedly](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-relief-valve-part-i)) an extra boost to the plumbing. But while “illiquidity hysteria” continues to capture public attention, private players have been driving a quiet liquidity revolution, one that has bolstered the UST pipes in the most restrictive regulatory climate on record.

Financial alchemy has reached new extremes. Traditional cash markets, including the secondary U.S. Treasury market[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-3-166336828), are now competing with the rise of a new “bond-like” ecosystem still in its early stages of expansion. The post-GFC battle for market-making dominance between top liquidity providers, both traditional and emerging, now involves conquering a rising shadow arena. Much like the shadow banking system and the interplay between banks and shadow banks, the “cash market” has gained a counterpart: the “shadow cash market,” which participants turn to when liquidity can no longer be tapped via traditional flows impeded by post-crisis regulations[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-4-166336828).

With fresh market mechanisms, however, come new dangers. Shadow cash trades will expose another inconvenient truth for monetary leaders attempting to prevent further intervention by reinforcing the UST market’s inner workings: in times of crisis, other barriers not enforced or produced via regulation will continue to impede the major market players.

The post-GFC (Global Financial Crisis) era has become widely understood as the period when a series of balance sheet constraints led to riskier dealings being shifted from banks to their shadow bank counterparts, also known as non-bank financial institutions (NBFIs) or simply non-banks. Yet now, this period is making way for a deregulatory successor, in which banks and non-banks aim to assert their dominance while jointly thwarting illiquidity induced by (what many consider to be) misweighted or overcalibrated regulatory ratios[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-5-166336828). One of these remedies involves tapping shadow liquidity. If, for whatever reason, the cash bond markets can’t provide the liquidity, participants turn to shadow cash, typically starting with ETFs and then moving on to swaps. Large dealers, primarily within G-SIBs (global systemically important banks), sit at the bottom of the “shadow cash hierarchy,” less willing to provide balance sheet and liquidity support than before the GFC. When they can’t deliver, non-bank market makers ([primarily HFTs](https://blog.grainstonelee.com/insight/top-50-hft-firms-and-their-history)) step in — even tapping the funding desks of banks that previously rejected the same trades. The HFT giants have thus absorbed “outlawed” volumes that large banking behemoths can or will no longer facilitate. The likes of Citadel and Virtu have consequently gained a substantial share of both shadow cash market trading (i.e. bond or bond-like liquidity provided outside regular cash trading) and regular cash market trading.

Nevertheless, dealer banks aren’t going anywhere. Primary dealers with a parent commercial bank possess superior and, more importantly, direct access to a wider range of dollar funding sources. Imbalances in order flow emerge, unbridgable by HFTs and securities dealers, and must be offset by dealer banks with access to more “exotic” sources of funding. Whoever earns this privilege has both market access and balance sheet, enabling them to tap the scarcest types of funding — repo, o/n FF (Fed Funds), commercial paper, CDs (including retail deposits), and, at the extremes, FHLBs or [a call to your local Federal Reserve bank](https://www.frbdiscountwindow.org/). Any smaller non-bank players sporting HFT prowess, but unable to scale without bank funding, have been gobbled up by their larger counterparts, arrangements based on convenience for both the newcomers and traditional players involved.

Meanwhile, the small number of dominant non-bank market makers, such as Citadel and Virtu, have grown so rapidly that they have gained sufficient market access without the need to merge with Wall Street goliaths. Citadel has direct entry to centrally cleared repo markets, earning a seat among what used to be the exclusive realm of traditional dealer banks. While not a leader in the interdealer repo space, the non-bank goliath now has the option to tap dollars that its competitors can’t obtain with ease. Citadel and other HFT giants will never truly topple or replace dealer banks[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-6-166336828). Still, for the non-bank behemoths, there’s room for [aggressive expansion](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=P10bC0Bxp20) elsewhere. HFTs’ eyes remain fixed on other markets[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-7-166336828).

Unsurprisingly, but in contrast to the prevailing hype over illiquidity, most U.S. Treasury market participants have observed a well-oiled ecosystem, with recent reports indicating that only 4% believed liquidity was subpar and worsening. The ease of buying and selling — _the actual definition of liquidity_ — best measured by bid-ask spreads (the difference between the best bid and best offer), order book depth (the volume or depth of orders), and price impact (how much a trade moves the price of a security) has indicated little dysfunction. The higher the price impact, the wider the bid-ask spreads, and the thinner the book depth, the poorer the liquidity. Yet, minus a few extreme market upheavals, the opposite has materialized in USTs. During the tumultuous events of the COVID-19 pandemic and the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank (SVB), price impact increased, bid-ask spreads widened, and book depth dropped sharply. But since 2023, these metrics have returned to normal.

Whether the U.S. central bank and Treasury should also be actively bolstering sovereign debt markets remains a political matter. Regardless of the controversy, monetary leaders continue to strengthen liquidity and reduce volatility through not one, but two, [buyback programs](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/221/TBACCharge1Q32025.pdf). While the Treasury’s “cash management” buybacks aim to minimize swings in bill issuance plus its TGA balance, its “liquidity support” program extracts illiquid bonds from the most vulnerable parts of the curve. The Fed’s portfolio managers[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-8-166336828), meanwhile, will soon join in the vol dampening at the short end, (reluctantly) becoming a guaranteed buyer of bills to maintain the U.S. central bank’s [ample reserve regime](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/implementing-monetary-policy-in-an-ample-reserves-regime-the-basics-note-1-of-3-20200701.html). Add in “shadow cash” flows, and overall liquidity is being vastly understated, even before monetary leaders’ latest fortification has been completed. The transition to central clearing, the centerpiece of the latest UST market evolution, is still in progress. Bank [SLRs](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-slr?utm_source=publication-search)[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-9-166336828) and other regulatory constraints have yet to be reworked. If liquidity is adequate, eyes should be focused on identifying weak spots _outside_ regulatory boundaries.

Those weaknesses likely won’t arise in the shadow cash ecosystem, which remains underappreciated by the global regulatory complex. An increasing number of market players have gained fixed-income exposure via alternative instruments, such as credit derivatives, largely TRS (total return swaps) and CDS (credit default swaps)[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-10-166336828). By paying a spread and posting margin to dealers, participants achieve leveraged bond exposure plus any cashflows (such as coupon payments). These trades, though, are “simple” shadow cash exposures. The “dark arts” of shadow cash have infiltrated more innocent-looking financial instruments.

The more elaborate shadow cash trades are conducted via exchange-traded funds (ETFs), deceptively straightforward assets that enable market makers, both traditional and nascent, to not only offer bond-like exposures, but also grease liquidity. The secondary ETF market enables investors and traders to gain exposure to assets and strategies through shares listed on highly liquid exchanges, while shadow cash trading involves the primary and secondary ETF markets. The primary ETF market contains a mechanism for swapping bonds for ETF shares and vice versa. ETFs are managed by issuers (also known as sponsors), primarily asset management giants like BlackRock and Vanguard, rather than banking behemoths. To maintain an accurate ETF price, the issuer designates several large market makers to act as “authorized participants” (APs), which have the right but not the obligation to create and redeem ETF shares in exchange for a basket of securities that the ETF issuer sees fit.

A shadow cash arbitrage involves executing trades in both the secondary cash bond markets and the secondary and primary ETF markets. If the ETF price falls below the value of the assets that the ETF issuer holds, arbitrageurs will short bonds in the cash market and buy ETF shares in the intraday session. Then, upon the primary ETF market opening, the “magic” happens. Unlike mutual funds and closed-end funds, ETFs are the only type of investment fund that provides a “shadow cash” mechanism, where either an AP or a firm transacting through an AP can deliver ETF shares and receive bonds to cover its trading desk’s short bond position _without_ transacting in the regular cash markets. Soon after, other APs will become aware of the same dislocation until it’s “arbed” away, and a more realistic price per ETF share is achieved.

Likewise, when the ETF price rises above its underlying basket, APs or their clients will buy bonds and short the ETF, using the primary ETF market to close out the arbitrage via delivering bonds. In both trades, price efficiency has been achieved while forming another avenue for key market makers to acquire cash bonds without going through the traditional cash markets. The top market makers thus maintain the shadow cash “bridge” between market makers and ETF creation-redemption mechanisms.

This, however, provides much more than just arbitrage trading. Only recently have the prominent market makers begun to acknowledge this bridge as an additional liquidity booster. Market makers no longer merely use repos and interest rate derivatives to hedge uncomfortably large long and short positions in bonds. If other traditional backups, including the Fed’s mammoth [SOMA portfolio](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/desk-operations/securities-lending), can’t deliver, dealers call upon ETFs. If a desk has warehoused illiquid bonds, it can short the most correlated ETF that mirrors a typical hedge, thereby minimizing basis risk[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-cash-market-part-ii#footnote-11-166336828) as much as possible, and vice versa, until a buyer emerges. When an attractive arbitrage materializes, i.e. when the desk holds the appropriate long bond and short ETF positions, they can risk manage their book via closing out both exposures via shadow cash trading. If not, positions can be rolled until further notice. The flexibility for market makers is too good to pass up. Which begs the question: what are the hazards?

As it happens, the shadow cash market [does not pose](https://www.sec.gov/spotlight/fixed-income-advisory-committee/100520-sec-conference-bond-etf-behavior-during-covid-volatility.pdf) major illiquidity risks to financial markets. In fact, during the COVID-19 market panic, prior concerns over ETFs did not surface. Instead, the pandemic revealed ETFs [grew more liquid](https://www.sec.gov/spotlight/fixed-income-advisory-committee/100520-sec-conference-bond-etf-behavior-during-covid-volatility.pdf) than even their underlying assets, dampening, not exacerbating, bond market instability. ETFs should now be the least of the regulators' concerns, as officials have yet to seriously factor in other “shadow forces”, those outside their regulatory purview that cause key market makers to step back during market upheavals.

Dealer’s internal risk limits (established within firms’ risk departments and not by regulators) add another dilemma for officials, where dealers’ “personal” constraints prompt them to retreat when bond volatility skyrockets. As government securities desks approach their internal risk appetite during shaky periods, dealer desks grow less likely to make a market, refusing to absorb UST supply and demanding greater rewards (wider spreads) for the risk they assume. The foremost risk limit, VaR (Value at Risk), implemented by all major primary dealers, is treated as gospel, causing dealers to withdraw or even sell inventory in stressed conditions. Since the desk remains under the watchful eye of its risk department, and traders stay under the watchful eye of both their risk managers and superiors, desks overall tend to act in a manner that avoids personal ramifications over preserving market liquidity. Moreover, unfortunately for Bessent, these desks tend to prefer offloading riskier, longer-term USTs.

Combining this with [lackluster plumbing relief](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-relief-valve-part-ii) likely to be passed through Congress, future events similar to the magnitude of COVID-19 and SVB will demonstrate yet again that the Fed’s OMOs (open market operations) remain the true “relief valve”. Having rescued the UST market even before regulatory oversight grew substantial, the SOMA (the Fed’s asset portfolio) is the ultimate shock absorber and “greaser of last resort”. The size of the Fed’s balance sheet is thus unlikely to return to its former self and enter a new “bull market”, but for more reasons than the occasional plumbing adjustment.
