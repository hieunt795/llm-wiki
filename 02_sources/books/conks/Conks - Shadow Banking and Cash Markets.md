# The Shadow Bank Shutdown - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-shadow-bank-shutdown
> the origins and status quo of shadow finance

---
[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!nXYn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbae8f0e6-a3b7-4a1f-997a-2efdb2c3cce3_1017x753.png)

After decades of overlooking runaway financial speculation, Chinese leaders have miraculously deflated a once-giant shadow banking system without widespread turmoil. Yet as the global economy begins to stall, China’s elite may encounter a resurgence of financial alchemy in the shadows. The “Shadow Bank Shutdown” has likely reached its pinnacle.

Fifteen years ago, on 15 September 2008, America’s fourth largest investment bank Lehman Brothers [collapsed](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2008-09-15/lehman-files-for-biggest-bankruptcy-in-u-s-after-suitors-balk), revealing the most intricate network of financial instruments ever assembled. Decades of experimentation in America’s banking golden age, mostly hidden from the public eye, had risen to the surface. The U.S. shadow banking system was unveiled. The reaction to this shadow system’s exposé was one of amazement and disbelief, but it was the peak of a transition to a new type of finance, one that had been evolving since the 1970s. In contrast to the traditional banking textbooks, “[market-based finance](https://www.bis.org/review/r180221a.pdf)” — the intermediation of credit outside the traditional system — had not only emerged but superseded the [original](https://moneyweek.com/glossary/maturity-transformation) banking model. The subprime boom and bust was merely the byproduct of market-based finance’s first modern iteration, and as usual with nascent monetary experiments, it ended in disaster.

But unlike the origins of market-based finance, where private entities created “deposit-like” instruments (such as [repos](https://www.concoda.com/p/the-repo-market-blindspot) and [money market funds](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/money-marketfund.asp)) to evade regulatory hurdles, the origins of America’s shadow banking standard lay within government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs). The [FHLB](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/f/fhlb.asp) (Federal Home Loan Bank) System, dubbed the second-to-last lender of last resort, was the foremost provider of “loan warehousing” — extending credit to those engaging in the [securitization](https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/fandd/2008/09/pdf/basics.pdf) of mortgages. Meanwhile, similar to big banks providing “backstops” to shadow entities engaging in dubious activities, the housing GSEs (Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac) provided the first forms of “credit risk transfer,” much like [CDS](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/creditdefaultswap.asp) (credit default swaps) and [CDOs](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/cdo.asp#:~:text=A%20collateralized%20debt%20obligation%20is,tranches%20having%20the%20lowest%20risk.) (collateralized debt obligations). These government-sponsored entities even founded the “originate-to-distribute” model, where lenders made loans for the sole purpose of selling them on to other investors.

In an even more bizarre dynamic, the infamous rise of CDOs was prompted by the first draft of the [Basel Framework](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fc15acdf5-5341-4d93-8a07-c821016fbb37_5958x5674.png), the regulatory banking standard active in practically every major nation. Following the late-1980s banking crises, monetary authorities enforced Basel I’s minimum capital requirements on banks, aiming to curb credit risk: the probability of loss from counterparties defaulting on loans.

Yet Wall Street swiftly found a way to game the system. By pooling mortgages into securities and “reducing risk” via CDO and CDS products, banks required less capital than traditional lending to generate similar returns. CDOs, essentially securities that yield interest from another pool of securities, were deemed to be “credit risk transfers”. Still, the pieces known as tranches that made up these products failed to reduce risk, merely distributing it among different (but, more importantly, interconnected) shadow banking participants. At the height of the subprime boom, some dealers even issued “CDO-cubeds,” the riskiest protection repackaged into fresh securities to achieve the highest rating. The rest, as global finance soon discovered, was history.

Out of the ashes of what was then the greatest speculative bust on record arose a highly efficient shadow banking machine. After Lehman’s bankruptcy, the toxic markets of the subprime era — such as subprime synthetic CDOs — dried up, leaving only the shadow elements vital for channeling credit to the real economy. America’s shadow banking paradigm transformed to consist primarily of repos (repurchase agreements). Still, asset-backed securities (ABS) and collateralized loan obligations (CLOs) have prevailed, with shadow banks — namely finance companies and other NBFIs (non-bank financial institutions), extending credit via shadow intermediation. The securitized loans, leases, and mortgages packaged into tradable instruments, meanwhile, have never been allowed to reach “CDO-cubed levels” of toxicity. Following another two drafts of the Basel Framework, regulators have contained extreme financial creativity — for now.

Meanwhile, across the globe, as America’s shadow banking sector had reached excessive levels of speculation and fallen back to Earth, another shadow system had achieved its peak and gradual decline: the Chinese shadow banking sector.

The backstory of how China’s shadow sector emerged is quite unlike America’s. The financial alchemy, actors, and instruments involved differ immensely. Yet, both systems have an overall similar flavor. Let’s dive in.

Up until the late 1970s, China operated a Soviet-style monetary system in which the central bank, the People’s Bank of China ([PBOC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People's_Bank_of_China)), was the sole bank in operation. Shortly after America integrated China into its [global security pact](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-eurodollar-system), however, the Chinese elite moved away from an inflexible approach, founding “The Big Four” commercial banks (the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China, the Bank of China, the China Construction Bank, and the Agricultural Bank of China). The pro-market reforms of the 1990s soon followed, bringing China’s private sector to life. The Big Four banks grew more profit-oriented, at one point becoming the four largest banks globally by total assets. The shift to a “capitalist” banking system with socialist characteristics was in motion.

Alongside the Big Four, other smaller banks emerged. Joint-stock commercial banks (JSCBs) started appearing nationwide, with hundreds operating in certain locations. Over time, smaller banks started to swipe business away from the Big Four. In 1995, these large state-owned behemoths held 80% of all deposits. But a decade later, they held 60%, and their share has kept on declining ever since.

As the new millennium arrived, with it came the rise of China’s shadow banking sector. A great game of shadow finance “cat and mouse” between banks and Chinese regulators was about to commence. In response to a rapid expansion of lending, Chinese authorities began tightening liquidity. By prohibiting banks from issuing more loans equal to 75% of their deposits, they could no longer extend credit to what was a large number of customers seeking funding. At the same time, the Big Four tended to discriminate against private firms, favoring loans to large state-owned enterprises. Consequently, other banks were forced to invent a range of “shadow instruments” to provide funding to credit-starved entities.

The Chinese shadow banks were ingenious. Unlike the American way of combining potentially ten or more stages of intermediation to reach the final product, shadow banking in China grew to be less complex, involving fewer steps and fewer intermediaries. The prominent scheme involved funneling money to customers by redefining bank deposits as “financial products” and bank assets as “investment receivables.” The shadow arms of banks — mostly trust companies and securities firms — offered a range of “deposit-like” services, such as “wealth management products” ([WMPs](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/what-are-chinas-wealth-management-products-2021-11-05/)), while extending credit via “[entrusted loans](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0304405X19300066),” “[trust loans](https://www.fitchratings.com/research/banks/heightened-risk-in-chinas-trust-sector-may-have-wider-effect-on-financial-institutions-20-08-2023),” and “shadow bonds”. The list of financial products — and synonyms — grew each year.

During the heydays of China’s shadow banking boom, countless financial products were circulating freely in the darkness. But eventually, in 2016, the music started to slow. Upon the [collapse](https://www.finextra.com/blogposting/17107/the-rise-and-fall-of-p2p-lending-in-china) of a shadow peer-to-peer lending bubble, regulators’ temper had reached boiling point. The Chinese regulatory establishment imposed a series of penalties on the most popular shadow bank products, constraining rampant activity. After Chinese shadow banking reached a whopping 90% share of nominal GDP in 2016, that figure had [fallen](https://futurecfo.net/shadow-credit-in-china-to-remain-subdued-moodys/) to ~39% in Q2 2023.

Today, amidst tough regulatory supervision, shadow assets are forecasted to decline further. China’s banking regulator, the [CRBC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_Banking_Regulatory_Commission), is [looking](https://www.cliffordchance.com/content/dam/cliffordchance/briefings/2011/09/chinas-implementing-rules-on-the-new-regulatory-standard-for-the-banking-industry-stricter-than-basel-iii.pdf) to fully integrate banks into a stricter version of Basel III while implementing a new tiering system to monitor risk levels. Nevertheless, as the global economy begins to deteriorate and financial conditions [tighten](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-10-18/china-tells-banks-to-extend-local-government-loans-amid-crunch), the supply of credit to certain entities could narrow enough to force shadow banks to reignite their creativity. Even in the face of heavy regulatory penalties, another round of shadow finance “cat and mouse” could be just around the corner. And judging by financial history, it shouldn’t be surprising.

# The Money Market Blindspot: Part I - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i
> a liquidity mirage is about to surface

---
A funding squeeze is slowly becoming a liquidity deluge. Following a muted year-end, in which the Fed [announced](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_241219) increased [repo](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market) operations, once-elevated funding rates have [subsided](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/Ggoa7u5XcAAZcy9?format=jpg&name=large) while constraints on market makers have evaporated. Dealer balance sheets are now set to “decongest” as the Trump Trade Steepening™ attracts demand previously sidelined by a curve inversion[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-1-154187719). Nevertheless, just as conditions begin to ease, another debt ceiling[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-2-154187719) battle is about to reignite volatility in money markets. With no [truce](https://edition.cnn.com/2024/12/19/politics/government-shutdown-vote-congress/index.html) in sight, political frictions have forced Treasury leaders to initiate another drawdown of the [TGA](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN)[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-3-154187719), the U.S. government’s bank account, prompting a cash surge in the banking system[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-4-154187719). Upon a debt limit resolution, this flood may cause monetary flows to have overstated liquidity, a mirage that Fed officials (unwilling to terminate QT[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-5-154187719)) continue to [dismiss](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/files/fomcminutes20241107.pdf#page=3). A money market blindspot is looming.

Following the peak of the COVID-19 liquidity injections, the financial system has shed trillions of dollars in excess cash. The [Fed’s RRP](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-mechanics-of-the), its liquidity “shock absorber”, has been depleted by money funds[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-6-154187719) shifting into higher-yielding t-bills and [private repos](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market), all while funding an increasingly mammoth [TGA](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN) housed at the Fed[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-7-154187719). This cash surge, the so-called “RRP drain”, has prevented any severe funding stress from emerging in money markets. Reserve balances that the U.S. central bank once “neutralized”[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-8-154187719) have been set free, providing a continuous feast for the Fed’s “QT shredder”.

[

Now, as money markets again try to enter an “excess collateral” era, another cash flood will saturate the U.S. banking system. For at least another quarter, the barrier protecting dollar funding markets from chronic volatility will be preserved. Another debt limit suspension has expired, prompting the Treasury to reduce its nearly $1 trillion cushion from insolvency, a mix of remaining (and incoming) cash in the TGA plus extraordinary measures[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-9-154187719). The “debt limit bypass” has now been initiated. Its eventual unwind, however, could spark fireworks.

But before any sparks emerge, monetary leaders will commence “spending down” (i.e. emptying) the TGA, where the U.S. government pays its bills while mostly unable to top up cash balances via fresh debt issuance. This spend-down will force America to “consume” much more than it borrows and “earns”, mutating the Fed’s balance sheet into a liquidity booster for megabanks. With the TGA and banks’ reserve balances remaining separate on the central bank balance sheet, the rise in government cash equals the death of banks’ settlement balances (i.e. reserves) and vice versa. All flows in and out of the U.S. government’s bank account are settled by the Fed initiating a “liability swap”, exchanging TGA balances for reserve balances, both liabilities on its balance sheet. As this occurs, the U.S. central bank credits or debits the reserve accounts of banks receiving money from or sending money to Uncle Sam’s central wallet[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-10-154187719). A TGA drawdown thus boosts liquidity among the few Wall Street giants that clear most U.S. dollar payments and money market imbalances.

A few counterforces, meanwhile, will turn the TGA plunge into a mere decline. As maturing debt gets rolled over[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-11-154187719), government revenue will stall the Treasury’s “race” to the zero bound. An enormous tax collection in mid-April, for instance, is set to boost TGA balances substantially in early Q2, reversing the cash flood temporarily before net outflows resume[12](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-12-154187719). Alongside incoming and remaining cash, monetary leaders will also take “extraordinary measures” to stem outflows. In practice, the Treasury will merely delay the issuance of non-marketable securities, i.e. those not tradable in [secondary markets](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-us-treasury-cash), lowering outstanding debt levels. Officials will then auction marketable securities that — unlike their non-marketable counterparts — boost TGA balances but also suck reserves from the banking system.

[

Over ~$300 billion in non-marketable issuance could be postponed, enabling America to pay its bills for longer during the latest debt limit brawl. Just three sources will deliver hundreds of billions of dollars in headroom, the largest being the [“G Fund”](https://www.tsp.gov/funds-individual/g-fund/) — part of the [TSP](https://www.tsp.gov/funds-individual/g-fund/) (Thrift Savings Program). Breaking its usual routine of investing the fund’s entire cash balance in special one-day securities — which, if issued, count towards the debt limit, the Treasury will sell regular securities to raise cash and pay off America’s liabilities. The two other most significant sources of “debt ceiling liquidity” will come from the ESF[13](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-13-154187719) (Exchange Stabilization Fund) and the CSRD (Civil Service Retirement and Disability) Fund, which both possess a series of loopholes that officials can exploit. These, of course, are just some measures the Treasury could take to avoid insolvency. With the issuance of many obscure state and federal securities potentially being deferred, the Treasury will execute as many exploits as possible to delay a U.S. default[14](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-14-154187719). Once the debt ceiling is suspended or lifted, the Treasury will reissue non-marketable securities, paying out all interest omitted. A debt limit bypass doesn’t come cheap, but a close encounter with default will unnerve markets.

Luckily for those pulling the monetary strings, the U.S. empire’s “margin of safety” has widened significantly. Recent circumstances, more influential than prior debt limit standoffs, have granted leaders the luxury of a higher TGA balance. Similar to a Las Vegas casino, internal guidelines dictate that the Treasury must hold enough cash to equal one week of outflows, including the amount of maturing securities. More t-bill issuance creates more short-term maturities, thus producing a chunkier account balance.

Banks, meanwhile, have passed on only half of the sharpest rate hiking cycle in decades to depositors. Low bank deposit rates have driven cash investors to flee from banks to the money market fund complex, which not only offers higher yields but favors t-bills over alternatives. As the regulatory complex mandates a less than 60-day WAM (weighted average maturity) in money fund portfolios, MMFs avoid coupons and stick to 1-month bills and o/n (overnight) repos. Giant inflows into money funds have created a safe asset shortage, so much so that any slowdown in short-term Treasury issuance [fuels](https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2023/11/treasury-bill-supply-and-on-rrp-investment/) a higher balance in the RRP[15](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-15-154187719). Both Fed and Treasury leaders, despite being “independent”, now benefit from the t-bill printer staying on its maximum setting. Ultra-short-term debt has exploded to levels greater than the U.S. empire’s peak funding needs during the COVID hysteria.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uKb5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe2c53231-5bb0-498d-a8c3-08b6e4018054_4735x3275.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!uKb5!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe2c53231-5bb0-498d-a8c3-08b6e4018054_4735x3275.png)

As for the — highly debated — composition of U.S. debt, whether or not Sec. Yellen issued more bills than coupons [to juice the economy](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/635102_Activist_Treasury_Issuance_-_Hudson_Bay_Capital_Research.pdf) matters less than the outcome. Regardless of the intent, issuing more at the ultra-short end of the curve and less elsewhere will always be stimulating. This easing agenda is already baked into the Treasury’s “core”, as its [self-proclaimed](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/276/Debt-Management-Overview.pdf) objective is to issue whatever delivers the lowest cost to the taxpayer. Internal models[16](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-16-154187719) recommend a larger share of t-bills to decrease long-term expenses, while officials have even described the ultra-short-end as a “shock absorber”. Moreover, altering t-bill issuance allows the Treasury to auction longer-term debt (i.e. coupons) with more predictability and less volatility, reducing the odds of spooking markets with a sudden surge in duration issuance[17](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-17-154187719). The Treasury regularly sells coupons on a set schedule[18](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-18-154187719) while selling t-bills — ranging from a day to a few weeks — to counteract a volatile TGA. Endless debt limit battles, among other scenarios, have increased the need to hold more elastic, shorter-term maturities. These catalysts combined have forced Yellen (and soon [Bessent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Bessent)) to target a whopping $850 billion TGA balance.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oH9N!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fad978083-7781-43ff-a964-51ce46437166_8831x6201.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oH9N!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fad978083-7781-43ff-a964-51ce46437166_8831x6201.png)

That’s good news for a Fed wanting an empty RRP and a U.S. Treasury striving to fund itself at the lowest cost to the taxpayer. But politicians have entered the building. With the debt limit now binding, bill supply — though temporarily — is set to plunge, and the resulting lower yields spell trouble for emptying the RRP and the boost in interbank reserves.

As the Treasury spends down its balance, reserves will offset those lost to the Fed’s QT, but most will exit the banking system and manifest as an inflated Fed RRP [balance](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/seriesBeta/RRPONTSYD). As regular _Conks_ readers know, banks already hold above and beyond the reserves needed to meet the demands of the [regulatory colossus](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-coming-volatility-suppression?utm_source=publication-search) — i.e. Dodd-Frank and Basel III. They have even constructed a market to meet intraday liquidity requirements, known as the [IBDA](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-market-for-intraday) market, which has climbed in volume as reserves grow scarcer[19](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-19-154187719). With an overflow of deposits[20](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-20-154187719) from a TGA drawdown threatening profitability in a balance sheet-constrained era, Wall Street won’t want to hold them. Banks will push depositors out, but that’s only if they don’t flee themselves. Money funds offer vastly superior yields to the deposit accounts of banking behemoths.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4AD4!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8e0aafb0-78e4-4ee2-9b15-b172226fd739_4679x3967.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4AD4!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8e0aafb0-78e4-4ee2-9b15-b172226fd739_4679x3967.png)

same TGA infographic as above, added for convenience

Flush with private sector liquidity while facing a near-shortage of safe assets, money funds will transform the RRP drain into an RRP surge. Lower bill supply will suppress bill yields, prompting money funds to park their excess cash — yet again — in the Fed’s liquidity shock absorber. Reserves will consequently flow from commercial banks to the banks of money funds to the Fed’s repo agent, the BNY (Bank of New York), [facilitating](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-feds-standing-repo) RRPs (reverse repos) for the U.S. central bank. Via its signature liability swaps, the Fed will substitute reserves with o/n RRPs on its balance sheet. In tandem, RRPs will replace deposits in money fund portfolios. Interbank liquidity will ultimately rise, yet the Fed’s RRP will gobble it up, returning briefly with a vengeance.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NYp1!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6199bed5-2b8a-454e-8da8-12fa10661637_7162x5768.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!NYp1!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F6199bed5-2b8a-454e-8da8-12fa10661637_7162x5768.png)

click to enlarge (best viewed on desktop)

How briefly will depend on the severity of the TGA drawdown, which will presumably last until April, months before any significant doom ripens. Still, if politicians don’t forge a debt ceiling truce, markets will decide for them. The “drop-dead date” — when the debt limit is hit, TGA is cash depleted, and all other options fade — will spur leaders to collaborate. U.S. sovereign debt, once a flight to safety, will tumble in value, even dislocating from other money market instruments[21](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-21-154187719), forcing a resolve.

Once the latest debt limit battle concludes, a TGA drawdown will turn into a TGA refill. A larger restocking of the U.S. government’s wallet heightens the risk of a “liquidity blindspot” causing another money market upheaval, precisely what the Fed has been striving to capture before it ensues. A liquidity mirage fueled by an increase in reserves and RRP balances will have warped the Fed’s models that measure reserve scarcity. Thus, the TGA reversal may not only spur the usual funding volatility[22](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i#footnote-22-154187719) but also interrupt the Fed’s plans to reduce its mammoth balance sheet to the smallest viable level. The longer the TGA drawdown endures, the greater the chance of QT ending abruptly. The Fed will consequently be hoping for a quick debt ceiling resolution. Yet there are no guarantees their wish will be granted.

# The Money Market Blindspot: Part II - Conks
+ author: Conks
+ source: https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii
> volatile flows may obscure the Fed's "alarm system"

---
_— click [here](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i) for The Money Market Blindspot: Part I_

Another debt ceiling battle is set to induce volatility in money markets. Under the command of newly sworn-in [Scott Bessent](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0001), the U.S. Treasury has [started](https://johncomiskey.substack.com/p/the-first-two-days-at-the-debt-ceiling) employing “[extraordinary measures](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i)” to bypass a now-binding debt limit[1](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-1-155445774). Yet, these gimmicks won’t last forever. Soon, America’s checking account, the [TGA](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/WTREGEN)[2](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-2-155445774), will be drawn down, releasing a flood of liquidity into the banking system. Anticipating a cash surge in the first half of the year, the SOFR-FF (SOFR-Fed Funds) futures curve[3](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-3-155445774) — a measure of expected funding conditions — has thus priced in a looser environment[4](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-4-155445774). This easier climate, however, will soon be altered by a giant collection of tax receipts[5](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-5-155445774), an unwind of liquidity that could be deepened by a parallel debt limit resolution[6](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-6-155445774). With not only the U.S. government’s bank account but the RRP also [complicating](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-i) flows, the Fed’s liquidity “alarm system”, eager to front-run[7](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-7-155445774) the next money market [upheaval](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fedb0f599-4cf4-489a-85be-dcc20b2f6fb7_7392x6050.png), is about to face its first major hurdle.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2UFj!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fad4959c6-4528-485c-99d8-e8b5b9ccb938_4795x3273.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2UFj!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fad4959c6-4528-485c-99d8-e8b5b9ccb938_4795x3273.png)

since futures are quoted as 100 minus the implied market rate, a positive SOFR-FF futures basis means [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr) is priced to trade below (not above) [EFFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr) and vice versa

At the [latest](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcpresconf20240131.htm) FOMC meeting, monetary leaders again confirmed their desire to maintain the smallest possible balance sheet[8](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-8-155445774), superseding their urge to end QT[9](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-9-155445774) and thwart another money market seizure. Consequently, only an unforeseen rate spike will compel officials to abandon their objectives. But for now, reserves lost to the U.S. central bank’s “QT shredder” will be more than offset by Sec. Bessent drawing down (i.e. draining) the TGA[10](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-10-155445774), lowering repo rates — while costs to borrow overnight Fed Funds ([o/n FF](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr)) continue to flatline. The U.S. central bank’s premier gauge of reserve scarcity, RDE (Reserve Demand Elasticity)[11](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-11-155445774), hovers around zero, implying subtle changes in reserves will fail to drive o/n FF toward the top of the [target range](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFEDTARU) and disrupt the Fed’s goals. Without RDE falling sharply negative[12](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-12-155445774), the Fed will have the conviction to continue its balance sheet “runoff”[13](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-13-155445774), an action the U.S. central bank no longer deems tightening but a “normalization”. Volatility in funding markets is subsequently looming.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!42wY!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5dd4e3cc-3e47-4964-9811-c9c2d0162243_6157x4252.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!42wY!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5dd4e3cc-3e47-4964-9811-c9c2d0162243_6157x4252.png)

Unfortunately for officials, upon a debt limit resolution, a TGA drawdown will evolve into a TGA refill[14](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-14-155445774), where both dollar funding and liquidity tighten. Short-term desks and STIR traders[15](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-15-155445774) appear to be pricing an end to easing sometime in Q2, as the SOFR-FF futures curve turns from flat and positive to downward and negative[16](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-16-155445774). A double whammy of tax receipts and a TGA cash reload is about to produce turbulent money market flows, but with volatility comes opportunity.

Presently, the most well-telegraphed [TGA drawdown](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/infographics-the-tga-drawdown) on record has likely been priced in. Over the past few months, the SOFR-FF futures basis has widened (i.e. risen) across the front end of the curve, expecting SOFR to trade well below Fed Funds, reflecting an abundant dollar environment. The market expects reserves (hence deposits) to be diverted or deployed into repo markets and, thereafter, the Fed’s shock absorber — the RRP[17](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-17-155445774). Inflows into the facility will further increase as the [latest](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financing-the-government/quarterly-refunding/most-recent-quarterly-refunding-documents) QRA[18](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-18-155445774) (Quarterly Refunding Announcement) hints at a reduction in t-bill issuance from debt limit constraints — not a u-turn from Yellen’s bill-weighted legacy. The short end of the SOFR-FF futures curve subsequently trades flat and positive for months on end, overlooking an early end to QT or refill of America’s piggy bank.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SG3G!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5d725ae3-b45b-4300-a7c4-85d07411bd42_4795x3273.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SG3G!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5d725ae3-b45b-4300-a7c4-85d07411bd42_4795x3273.png)

the same chart as above added for convenience — a positive SOFR-FF futures basis means [SOFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/sofr) is trading below [EFFR](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/reference-rates/effr) due to the “100-implied rate” quotation

So much so, that the market has begun to grow tired of pricing in looser conditions. In the past week, the February SOFR-FF futures basis[19](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-19-155445774) has tightened from 3bps to zero (_see above_), somewhat pricing out easing. Meanwhile, the March SOFR-FF futures basis, which looks to have benefited from the maximum easing effect of a TGA drawdown, is set to follow (i.e. decline)[20](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-20-155445774).

Further out, however, uncertainty abounds. In the most tentative period, a debt ceiling resolution is priced to occur near the end of April — primarily down to the remarks of Speaker Mike Johnson, which will initiate the next TGA refill. No longer offsetting reserves destroyed by QT, the U.S. government’s bank account will soon experience net inflows, a reserve “rug pull” worsened by tax receipts due by April 15th. Tensions in dollar funding markets will resurface, with the SOFR-FF basis tightening (hence the SOFR-FF futures basis declining), a spread that, around April, appears priced as far too relaxed for such an environment. Alongside the TGA refill, Trump’s tariff threats will [prompt](https://www.reuters.com/markets/currencies/surging-dollar-spurs-jump-corporate-fx-hedging-2025-02-06/) increased dollar hedging, weighing on the FX swaps market and cross-currency bases, while fierce demand for repo adds extra strains on SOFR versus FF.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XASa!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F524c571a-27c5-4588-8844-0099552c537a_4835x3395.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!XASa!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F524c571a-27c5-4588-8844-0099552c537a_4835x3395.png)

Although hard to measure, volumes in the highly prevalent cash-futures [basis trade](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-treasury-market-unwind) — fixed-income arbitrage that exploits (perceived) dislocations between Treasury futures and their underlying securities — have potentially reached all-time highs and will likely persist. The Fed’s QT and a steep(er) yield curve, the two primary drivers of basis trade activity[21](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-21-155445774), have caused repo demand from hedge funds to almost double in the past few years, concentrated among the largest, most dominant players.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h5Dw!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba38dd88-7959-4f78-9730-32dc1ba1639d_7072x4269.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!h5Dw!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fba38dd88-7959-4f78-9730-32dc1ba1639d_7072x4269.png)

The latest SEC data [reveals](https://www.sec.gov/data-research/sec-markets-data) that the basis trade’s most pivotal component, a leveraged Treasury position financed via [repo](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/demystifying-the-repo-market), has risen by over 50% in volume since last year — with the largest funds again accounting for most activity. Long Treasury positioning has well exceeded the period in which the “[repocalypse](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-feds-repo-defensive-part-one)” unfolded. Money market pressures, consequently, aren’t going anywhere shortly.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xoTP!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F011de695-2a33-4503-8564-9c72cacd58bd_4898x3395.png)

given recent events, positioning may have peaked in the next quarterly data release

A debt ceiling resolution will also result in a deluge of bill issuance, as high as $1 trillion, adding upward pressure on SOFR-FF and other money market spreads. When the Treasury restores bill supply, the resulting higher yields will attract MMFs (money market funds) out of the Fed’s RRP into ultra-short-term t-bills. Meanwhile, reserves once “neutralized”[22](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-22-155445774) — removed by the Fed temporarily — via the Fed’s RRP end up neutralized again in the TGA. Interbank liquidity will diminish but remain plentiful, yet significant funding pressures will endure.

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xGik!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F96150e12-e5bf-4afc-932b-6b404b6d1ff4_3596x3351.png)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xGik!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F96150e12-e5bf-4afc-932b-6b404b6d1ff4_3596x3351.png)

“retro” conks infographics, circa 2023

Right now, the SOFR-FF basis seems too loose, with the curve expecting dollar funding markets to tighten only slightly when a TGA refill commences. As the latest pricing looks too good to be true[23](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-23-155445774), those anticipating a harsher environment could be rewarded[24](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-24-155445774). The most volatile money market flows in history are set to increase the risk of a blindspot impeding the Fed’s liquidity “alarm system”[25](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-25-155445774), failing to detect the next storm that “blows out” the SOFR-FF basis[26](https://www.conks.plumbing/p/the-money-market-blindspot-part-ii#footnote-26-155445774). Ceasing QT as these flows arise remains the easy antidote to chaos, but it’s no longer a choice for a Fed vowing to prolong its balance sheet reduction. That’s a risk officials have become willing to take, which may cost them credibility if or when another “repocalypse” emerges. But for traders foreseeing an unwelcome pivot from easy to tight funding constraints, money market unrest could equal a sizeable payoff.
