**Private credit is best understood as the cleanest expression of post-crisis financial repression meeting post-crisis bank constraints**: a long period of ultra-low policy rates compressed the entire public fixed-income stack, while tougher bank capital, liquidity, and supervision made balance-sheet-intensive corporate lending less attractive for regulated banks, and the spread between what borrowers wanted and what banks could economically provide became a structurally exploitable gap for nonbank lenders.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!2qUE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5e131d94-15d9-4171-a5b7-9e36865a1720_867x1167.png)

Bank for International Settlements [\[1\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) research frames the rise as most pronounced where policy rates were lower for longer and bank regulation more stringent, **with investor “search for yield” explicitly named as a channel through which low rates pushed capital toward private credit and lowered funding costs for private credit vehicles**.[\[2\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm) The regulatory side is not abstract: Basel Committee on Banking Supervision [\[3\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) ’s Basel III framework was designed as a response to the 2007–09 crisis with the express goal of strengthening bank regulation, supervision, and risk management, and the practical effect in many jurisdictions was to raise the shadow price of bank balance sheet for anything that looked like leveraged or sub-investment-grade corporate exposure.[\[4\]](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/basel3.htm)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!qghT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F80f7229c-6f41-431a-85f8-6690cf12174a_1279x1144.png)

**Rates mattered twice, first by forcing allocators to accept illiquidity for yield, and second by quietly eroding the relative funding advantage banks historically enjoyed through deposits, which are “sticky” versus wholesale funding in tightening phases**; BIS analysis of US business development companies (BDCs) shows the post-GFC convergence of banks’ and BDCs’ weighted average cost of capital, driven in part by BDCs levering up and by the relative movement of costs of equity and debt over time, which is another way of saying the system slowly made nonbank lending competitive enough to scale.[\[5\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm)

In parallel, central bank balance-sheet policy was the macro accelerant: the Federal Reserve Bank of New York [\[6\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBrief-26-02-measuring-counterparty-exposures-private-credit.pdf) documents that the first round of large-scale asset purchases from late 2008 into 2010 included $1.25 trillion of agency MBS, alongside agency debt and Treasuries, and policy commentary on QE emphasizes repeated waves from 2008–2014 aimed at pushing down borrowing costs beyond what near-zero short rates could accomplish.[\[7\]](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/programs-archive/large-scale-asset-purchases)

In Europe, negative/ultra-low policy rates extended the same incentive set across the euro area under the European Central Bank [\[8\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf), reinforcing the global “duration bid” and making privately originated, negotiated credit a natural clearing mechanism for investors willing to accept opacity and illiquidity in exchange for spread.[\[9\]](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/stats/policy_and_exchange_rates/key_ecb_interest_rates/html/index.en.html)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!updl!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fac160c66-469b-455d-91fa-db2a9739da02_669x631.png)

**The end state of that mix is that private credit is not just an alternative product; it is a shadow-banking channel that increasingly competes with public leveraged credit for marginal corporate financing.** The IMF’s 2024 assessment is blunt that corporate private credit has grown to rival other major credit markets in size, that borrowers tend to be smaller and riskier than public-market counterparts, and that the industry has never been through a severe macro downturn at its current scale, which is exactly the setup where “apparent stability” can be an artifact of regime rather than structure.[\[10\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) In other words, private credit is a post-GFC regime trade that became an ecosystem, and ecosystems have failure modes that only show up when the regime flips.[\[11\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

## From Niche Direct Lending to a Crowded Global Capital Pool

**At a high level, private credit is debt or debt-like financing negotiated directly rather than issued and traded in public markets, and the lack of a robust secondary market is not a footnote but a defining feature that drives both return expectations and risk perception.** National Association of Insurance Commissioners [\[12\]](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/basel3.htm) describes the core trade plainly: customization and limited secondary trading reduce liquidity and transparency, and investors are compensated via higher spreads and yields versus comparable public debt, while valuations are harder because they are infrequent and less transparent.[\[13\]](https://content.naic.org/insurance-topics/private-credit) That combination—custom terms, negotiated lender protections, and illiquidity—started as middle-market problem solving, then scaled as the sponsor ecosystem industrialized it.

**The BIS taxonomy is useful because it clarifies that “private credit” is not a single product but a family: direct lending (cash-flow lending with covenant-heavy floating-rate loans) has dominated over the past decade; mezzanine is junior/subordinated with equity-like participation; asset-based lending is collateral-driven against hard assets; distressed is secondary-market acquisition of troubled assets**.[\[14\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm) This segmentation matters because the system’s stability is not uniform: collateral-heavy credit will break differently than cash-flow lending, and mezzanine will not behave like senior secured, especially when sponsor support weakens.

Scale is now large enough to be macro-relevant even if definitions vary across data providers. BIS notes that global private credit fund AUM exceeds $2.5 trillion and that in the United States it exceeds the total outstanding leveraged loan or high-yield bond markets, citing the IMF’s work.[\[15\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm) A separate measurement approach from the Office of Financial Research [\[16\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) estimates that, as of year-end 2024, the US private credit market including BDCs exceeds $1.6 trillion and is larger than domestic broadly syndicated loans and high-yield corporate bonds, while also stressing that private credit was not even a distinct strategy category on Form PF when it was adopted in 2011, which is a tidy way of showing how quickly the asset class outgrew the original reporting architecture.[\[17\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBrief-26-02-measuring-counterparty-exposures-private-credit.pdf) Even industry-facing sources converge on a similar magnitude: the NAIC puts the market at an estimated $1.8 trillion in 2023.[\[13\]](https://content.naic.org/insurance-topics/private-credit)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!RYxd!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F0dc96ecc-45da-41b5-8701-732fb3752d4e_1227x619.png)

**The inflection point from “underpenetrated opportunity” to “crowded capital pool” is best located where sponsor finance, allocator behavior, and manager scale intersected, because that is where competition moved from price-based to structure-based.** The IMF documents that private credit’s growth followed private equity’s rise and is closely linked to it: managers whose umbrella firm is also active in private equity hold more than three-quarters of private credit assets, and about 70% of private credit deals are sponsored by private equity firms.[\[18\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!J2PL!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fe4b850b8-2f28-450d-8658-445c925d881c_1294x769.png)

It also notes a rapidly growing US segment—BDCs—accounting for 14% of the market, often public and open to retail investors, while some European funds adopted more frequent redemption periods (monthly or more often) to appeal to a wider investor base.[\[18\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) That is the moment the asset class stops being just locked-up institutional capital doing bilateral loans and starts becoming a broader distribution product—exactly where liquidity math starts to matter.

The entry and dominance of mega-scale alternative managers is not just branding; it changes clearing dynamics by making private credit both a platform business and a distribution business. The ecosystem today is shaped by a handful of firms that can run origination at scale and also manufacture perpetual, semi-liquid wrappers, including Apollo Global Management [\[19\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html), Ares Management [\[20\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html), Blackstone [\[21\]](https://www.fsb.org/2025/12/global-monitoring-report-on-nonbank-financial-intermediation-2025/), KKR [\[22\]](https://www.bis.org/bcbs/basel3.htm), Carlyle Group [\[23\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html), Blue Owl Capital [\[24\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm), and large distributors like Goldman Sachs [\[25\]](https://www.jefferies.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/08/Jefferies-Global-Secondary-Market-Review-July-2025.pdf), alongside retail-facing or wealth-channel vehicles that have made liquidity terms a central feature of product design.[\[26\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/goldman-sachs-private-credit-fund-defies-redemption-surge-across-industry-2026-04-06/) The point is not that these are “good” or “bad” actors; the point is that once scale players show up, the market’s marginal deal is priced by platform capacity and fundraising cadence, and in credit that tends to show up as weaker covenants, more borrower optionality, and more creativity in leverage layering rather than simply wider spreads.[\[27\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

## The Plumbing that Creates the Risk

**A private credit deal looks simple from the outside—one lender or a club lends to a sponsor-backed company at a floating spread—but the system risk is created in the layers: how the loan is sourced and underwritten, how documentation allocates control rights, how the lender finances itself, how valuations are set, and how investor liquidity is promised relative to asset liquidity. The IMF emphasizes that private credit loans are unrated, rarely traded, typically “marked to model” by third-party pricing services, and without standardized terms, which makes it difficult to detect rising risk early and creates the potential for delayed loss realization.[\[28\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) The US Financial Stability Oversight Council [\[29\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) adds the incentive layer directly: with limited transparency and limited secondary trading, valuations depend on unobservable inputs and high judgment, fund managers may be incentivized to maintain high valuations and delay recognition of losses, and in extended market stress this can lead to deferred losses and an elevated number of defaults.[\[30\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf)**

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!YH4N!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F992b781b-bab0-4164-8f8a-5d9fb32472aa_582x981.png)

Origination is where private credit’s advertised edge sits: speed, certainty, customization, and fewer disclosure requirements. FSOC notes that, from the borrower’s perspective, private credit offers a relationship with one or a club of lenders, more flexibility, greater ease and speed of execution, and fewer disclosure requirements relative to bank lending and public markets.[\[31\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf) That value proposition becomes particularly powerful for sponsor-backed transactions, because sponsors care about execution certainty and timing, and because sponsor influence can reshape covenant terms and incremental debt capacity even after closing; Reuters reports that borrowers have leaned on lenders for covenant flexibility to raise more debt without full consent, with covenant changes increasing debt capacity by as much as 40% to 300% of EBITDA in some cases, and that all of the sampled agreements involved private-equity-backed borrowers.[\[32\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/more-us-companies-skip-lender-consent-add-debt-moodys-says-2025-08-14/) Once you accept that as the competitive frontier, you also accept that underwriting standards can deteriorate without showing up immediately as spread compression or default spikes, because the deterioration is in legal optionality and lender control rights.

The structure level matters because “unitranche” and “one-stop shop” financing are not just convenience; they also collapse the senior/subordinated negotiation into a single agreement while often relying on behind-the-scenes lender arrangements to allocate first-out/last-out economics, which can complicate workout incentives when things break. Even when the loan is nominally senior secured, the real question in stress is who has control of amendments, whether covenants trigger early intervention, and whether cash interest remains actual cash interest. FSOC explicitly flags payment-in-kind elections as a masking mechanism: PIK allows borrowers to defer cash interest payments and instead accrue more principal, and FSOC points to the rising share of PIK interest in BDCs since 2019 while warning that PIK can mask underlying credit problems and delay recognition of loss.[\[33\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf) In a stable regime, PIK is a liquidity bridge; in a tightening regime, it is an accounting choice that keeps defaults optically low while leverage quietly rises.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vJXD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F2ec0f783-58e6-45d9-9285-d5f94332c7c5_1176x501.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!jpnK!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F952f555e-1d0b-489d-85cb-7d52962168cc_1174x508.png)

**Leverage layering is where private credit starts to look like finance rather than lending, because the lender’s own balance sheet is not always unlevered equity capital.** The OFR describes private credit funds as often originating loans and holding them to maturity while many operate with little or no leverage, but a subset uses debt financing sourced from regulated banks and nonbanks, and quantifies two key exposure channels that link private credit to the broader system: $410–$540 billion for entities that extend debt financing to private credit funds and $300 billion for LP capital commitments.[\[34\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBrief-26-02-measuring-counterparty-exposures-private-credit.pdf) The Fed’s own work adds a concrete map of the bank channel: committed credit lines by the largest US banks to private credit vehicles (private debt funds and BDCs) rose about 145% over five years to about $95 billion as of 2024 Q4, with roughly 60% of those commitments concentrated among five US GSIBs, and utilization rates around the mid-50% range.[\[35\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html) That concentration does not prove system-wide fragility, but it does establish a plausible transmission route: if private credit vehicles draw lines defensively during stress while banks are simultaneously tightening, liquidity can become a correlated problem rather than an idiosyncratic one.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!52LX!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fb3c14a69-a4a2-4718-bb42-1dffcdf238bf_1141x937.png)

Two specific fund-level financing tools deserve professional scrutiny because they sit directly in the “hidden leverage, hidden liquidity” bucket: subscription facilities and NAV lending. The SEC’s marketing-rule guidance describes subscription facilities as fund-level indebtedness secured by unfunded investor commitments and warns that presenting performance metrics inconsistently with and without the impact of subscription facilities can mislead investors, which is an unusually direct regulatory acknowledgment that fund-level borrowing changes the timing, and therefore the optics, of returns.[\[36\]](https://www.sec.gov/rules-regulations/staff-guidance/division-investment-management-frequently-asked-questions/marketing-compliance-frequently-asked-questions) NAV financing takes the next step by borrowing against the value and cash flows of fund investments; legal-market commentary notes that Fitch published a NAV finance rating methodology focused particularly on loans backed by the NAV and cash flows of secondaries funds, with the broader point being that NAV lending has grown enough to require formal rating criteria.[\[37\]](https://www.cadwalader.com/fund-finance-friday/index.php?eid=2409&nid=345) In practice, both tools can be economically rational, but they also create a situation where a “hold-to-maturity, illiquid loan book” is paired with financing instruments whose availability is contingent on lender confidence and collateral valuation, which is exactly how liquidity events become valuation events.

## Marks, Volatility, and Secondaries

**The cleanest professional question in private credit today is whether observed low volatility is a feature of superior underwriting or a feature of delayed price discovery, because the answer changes how you size risk and how you think about drawdowns.** The accounting and valuation infrastructure is more rigorous than critics sometimes claim, but rigor does not eliminate discretion. Kroll’s valuation guidance emphasizes that fair value standards are well established under FASB and IASB frameworks, that investment companies are required to report investments at fair value, and that private credit fair value should move with changes in borrower credit quality and market spreads, often using proxies like leveraged loans or syndicated loans to infer changes in market conditions given private credit’s lack of a homogeneous traded market.[\[38\]](https://www.kroll.com/en/reports/valuation/valuation-insights-h1-2024/private-credit-investments-valuation-best-practices) At the same time, both IMF and FSOC stress the structural condition that makes “smoothing” possible: loans are rarely traded, marked to model, and lack standardized terms, while limited secondary trading and opacity create space for stale valuations and manager discretion.[\[39\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tAuM!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b1f631a-04c3-47ee-b4b5-8928b892d390_1273x799.png)

**This is where secondary markets become more than an LP convenience; they become a price-discovery mechanism that can challenge reported NAVs.** Private credit secondaries are still small relative to the primary market, but they are growing fast because the system now needs an escape valve. Evercore’s market commentary describes credit secondaries as hitting a growth inflection: global volume roughly doubled from about $6 billion in 2023 to about $11 billion in 2024 and was expected to exceed about $18 billion in 2025, with GP-led transactions quickly becoming the majority of deal volume and senior secured direct lending continuation vehicles consistently pricing in the mid-90s to par range of fair market value.[\[40\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf) Jefferies’ secondary market review adds a broader pricing context: in H1 2025 average LP secondary pricing across strategies was around 90% of NAV, while “credit secondaries” priced at 92% of NAV on average, with strong demand for senior direct lending interests and steeper discounts in distressed / mezzanine / opportunistic credit types.[\[41\]](https://www.jefferies.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/08/Jefferies-Global-Secondary-Market-Review-July-2025.pdf) The detail that matters is not “92%”; it is that a secondary market exists, clears at observable discounts, and is growing as dedicated capital grows, which means private credit is slowly losing the ability to remain purely “marked-to-manager” during stress.[\[42\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf)

**Retailization accelerates that shift because retail-friendly vehicles require transaction prices at NAV even when NAV is model-based, and because the presence of a liquid wrapper forces public price signals to emerge.** BIS explicitly argues that private credit is poised for wider retail participation through the rapid growth of BDCs and, more recently, private credit ETFs, and that ETFs in particular could introduce price signals that make the opaque market more transparent, especially in downturns when discounts to NAV could be large and persistent.[\[43\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/bisbull106.htm) The IMF’s framing is consistent: the growth of semi-liquid funds can increase run risk and first-mover advantages even though the “great majority” of private credit funds pose little maturity transformation risk, and rising retail participation raises conduct concerns because retail investors may not fully understand redemption restrictions in an illiquid asset class.[\[10\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) The system implication is that the historical private-credit regime—stable marks, stable flows, stable narrative—becomes less stable as the investor base becomes more liquidity-sensitive and as observable secondary and public-market signals create feedback into fundraising and redemption behavior.

## Investor Base, Positioning, and Reflexive Loop

Private credit did not scale on manager skill alone; **it scaled because the buyer base had a structural problem: it needed yield and “stable income” optics in an environment where public fixed income delivered low real return for years, and where the mark-to-market profile of public credit became politically difficult inside institutions with quarterly governance.** The IMF documents that typical investors in private credit vehicles include pension funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds, and family offices, and its charts show that pension funds and insurers are the main investors in private credit funds globally while rapidly increasing exposure.[\[44\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) The insurer channel is especially important because insurance liabilities can be long duration and illiquid, which makes privately negotiated, long-dated credit a natural fit, and because the post-2008 low-rate regime created pressure on insurers to find spread without taking visible public-market volatility. **A Federal Reserve Bank of Chicago working paper shows life insurers increased private placement lending from $386 billion in 2014 to $849 billion in 2024, reaching 14% of life insurers’ general account assets, and explicitly links the growth to the post-crisis low-rate environment and to private equity’s entry into the life insurance sector as a source of “permanent capital.”** [\[45\]](https://www.chicagofed.org/-/media/publications/working-papers/2025/wp2025-09.pdf?sc_lang=en)

Global insurance supervisors echo both sides: private credit can offer illiquidity premiums and asset-liability matching, but it also creates credit risk, liquidity constraints, valuation uncertainty, and hidden leverage that require strong governance and supervisory oversight. International Association of Insurance Supervisors [\[46\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) [\[47\]](https://www.iais.org/uploads/2025/12/GIMAR-2025-Section-3.2.pdf)

The retail and wealth channel is the marginal buyer story, and marginal buyers determine cycle dynamics. In April 2026, Reuters reported a wave of redemption pressure in semi-liquid private credit funds that offer limited quarterly liquidity, with managers capping withdrawals at standard 5% limits after requests surged, and with investor concerns centered on transparency, valuations, and disruption risk; the report describes this as reminiscent of the 2022 redemption wave that hit non-traded real estate vehicles when valuation jitters unnerved investors.[\[48\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/barings-private-credit-fund-limits-withdrawals-after-redemption-requests-surge-2026-04-06/)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!x9e0!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F03c9cb51-7b9d-491d-a690-4d668d42bea3_1293x886.png)

This is not theoretical: the basic product mechanics of interval funds and non-traded BDCs are explicitly “limited liquidity through periodic repurchases.” PIMCO [\[49\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf) states that interval funds offer quarterly repurchases of 5% to 25% of outstanding shares at NAV but that investors should consider the shares illiquid, and Blackstone’s offering terms for BCRED describe quarterly repurchases at NAV limited to 5% of shares outstanding, with the board retaining discretion to amend or suspend repurchases.[\[50\]](https://www.pimco.com/us/en/investment-strategies/interval-funds) A product that is “illiquid but with periodic liquidity” behaves well when flows are stable; it becomes reflexive when flows reverse, because redemption requests pressure cash buffers, which pressure portfolio management decisions, which pressure marks, which then feed back into redemption demand.

**That reflexive loop is the core of the private credit cycle. BIS points to low interest rates inducing search for yield and steering institutional portfolios toward private credit, lowering funding costs; combined with post-crisis bank regulation reducing willingness to lend to risky firms, the structure becomes self-reinforcing: more capital flows into private credit, competition increases, underwriting shifts, and growth accelerates.**[\[2\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm) The IMF identifies the vulnerability explicitly: rapid growth plus competition with investment banks on large deals plus pressure to deploy capital can deteriorate pricing and nonpricing terms, including weakened covenants, raising the risk of future losses; and a severe adverse scenario could produce delayed loss realization followed by a spike in defaults and large valuation markdowns.[\[10\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) FSOC’s language mirrors the same mechanism at the ground level: rapid growth in dry powder and continued inflows can compromise underwriting standards as managers feel pressure to deploy within a fixed timeframe, and in limited deal activity periods managers may compete by offering more relaxed terms, including covenant-lite loans, or by choosing riskier deals, while PIK can mask stress and delay recognition.[\[51\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf) The upshot is that the “private credit is safer because it is private” narrative is only true if (a) covenants are genuinely stronger, (b) sponsors are willing and able to support borrowers, and (c) liquidity promises do not force discretion out of the manager’s hands. In a late-cycle environment, all three weaken at the margin.

## Stress Testing Private Credit

As of April 6, 2026, the Federal Reserve’s target range upper limit is 3.75%, which puts the system in a still-restrictive policy regime relative to the post-2010 baseline that incubated private credit’s explosive growth.[\[52\]](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/DFEDTARU)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!vYJH!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F40e64c2b-e50b-4394-9d47-e9f3f05b4122_2016x1099.png)

**That matters because corporate private credit is largely floating rate, and both IMF and FSOC highlight rate sensitivity as a defining borrower vulnerability: the IMF notes that borrowers are relatively vulnerable to interest rates because loans have floating rates, while FSOC notes that most private credit loans are floating-rate and that higher rates exerted greater fundamental pressure on some private credit borrowers and could lead to broader deterioration in credit quality.**[\[53\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) In a tightening regime you do not need a recession to create stress; you need sustained higher base rates plus limited refinancing windows plus slowing EBITDA growth, and private credit’s middle-market core is structurally exposed because it generally lacks the capital markets access and flexibility of larger public issuers.[\[54\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

The operational reality of stress in private credit is usually a sequence rather than a cliff. First comes interest coverage compression and covenant pressure, then an amendment wave, then selective PIK, then sponsor triage, then the separation between “good” and “bad” managers becomes visible in non-accruals, realized losses, and secondary pricing. FSOC’s warning that PIK can mask underlying credit problems and delay recognition is essentially the official version of this sequence.[\[33\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf) Evercore’s discussion of elevated rates weighing heavily on M&A and refinancing activity since 2022 is the macro overlay: fewer exits reduce sponsor optionality, slower refinancing reduces the ability to reset capital structures early, and the system moves toward maturity extension and liability management.[\[55\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4nCW!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F8a39dd4f-6609-4fb6-84b9-6176f3db9474_852x660.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VoqC!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F7cd86e3a-edc0-46d2-b7ed-d6c227b9b4b0_1254x636.png)

Reuters’ coverage of a covenant-flexibility trend—companies seeking to add debt without unanimous lender consent—fits exactly into the same stress logic: if public markets are closed or expensive, borrowers and sponsors push for documentation that increases borrowing capacity and flexibility inside existing agreements.[\[32\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/more-us-companies-skip-lender-consent-add-debt-moodys-says-2025-08-14/)

**The clearest late-cycle vulnerability is not just borrower default; it is the interaction between valuations, liquidity, and financing lines. The IMF identifies multiple layers of leverage, stale valuations, semiliquid vehicles, and unclear interconnections as key vulnerabilities.**[\[10\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) OFR’s counterparty mapping quantifies the scale of potential spillovers via leverage providers and LP commitments and emphasizes that leveraged private credit funds defaulting on their obligations could transmit stress to lenders and amplify systemic risk during distress, which is the analytical bridge between “credit losses” and “system problem.” [\[34\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBrief-26-02-measuring-counterparty-exposures-private-credit.pdf) The Fed’s bank-credit-line evidence fills in the balance-sheet channel and adds concentration among the largest banks, which is precisely how a contained asset-class problem could become a broader liquidity episode if lines are drawn in size.[\[35\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html)

**The most visible fault line in early 2026 is the semi-liquid product layer. Reuters describes a pattern of quarterly repurchase requests exceeding contractual limits and managers applying 5% caps, explicitly linking the spike in redemptions to investor concerns about transparency, valuations, and technology-driven business model disruption.**[\[48\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/barings-private-credit-fund-limits-withdrawals-after-redemption-requests-surge-2026-04-06/) This interacts directly with BIS’s 2026 case study on private credit exposure to SaaS and AI disruption: BIS reports outstanding loans to SaaS firms growing from almost $8 billion in 2015 to over $500 billion by end-2025, representing 19% of total direct loans, and notes that software stock declines and investor concerns spilled into BDCs with deeper discounts to NAV and underperformance for higher-exposure vehicles.[\[56\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2603v.htm) You can read that as sector idiosyncratic risk, but the systemic implication is more subtle: as private credit pushes into sectors where business-model risk is harder to underwrite and narrative shocks travel faster, the market’s first real “mark-to-market” becomes the public wrapper (BDCs, ETFs), and that wrapper then feeds back into redemption behavior and fundraising, which is reflexivity in real time.

**Zooming out, private credit is one expression of a broader shift toward nonbank intermediation, and the importance there is nonbank systems tend to fail through interconnectedness and liquidity rather than through traditional solvency metrics alone.** The Financial Stability Board [\[57\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/briefs/files/OFRBrief-26-02-measuring-counterparty-exposures-private-credit.pdf) reports that in 2024 the NBFI sector expanded 9.4% to $256.8 trillion, representing 51% of total global financial assets, and explicitly highlights channels of bank–nonbank interconnectedness including lending/repo/credit exposures from banks to nonbanks and holdings overlap.[\[58\]](https://www.fsb.org/2025/12/global-monitoring-report-on-nonbank-financial-intermediation-2025/) The ECB’s financial stability work makes the same conceptual point for Europe’s private markets: adverse shocks can lead to rising defaults and valuation corrections, and these shocks can be exacerbated by multiple layers of leverage at company, fund, and investor level or by liquidity mismatches in some open-ended private funds, while banks can face risks through lending exposures and competitive pressures that can incentivize lower underwriting standards.[\[59\]](https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/financial-stability-publications/fsr/special/html/ecb.fsrart202405_03~bc23a48dbc.en.html) The professional conclusion is that the mature risk is not “private credit lenders lose money.” The mature risk is that funding, liquidity, and valuation become a closed loop, and closed loops gap when trust breaks.

## Forward Scenarios, Transmission to Liquid Markets

In a base case, private credit normalizes rather than collapses: policy rates stay moderately restrictive but drift lower over time, growth slows without a deep recession, and the asset class absorbs stress through amendments, tighter underwriting on new vintages, and bifurcation where top managers with disciplined origination and conservative valuation survive with acceptable loss rates while weaker managers bleed through non-accruals and lower fundraising. The evidence base for a “contained at present” framework exists even within the IMF’s cautious tone: it states that financial stability risks appear contained today in part because private credit is funded largely with long-term capital and leverage appears modest, even while emphasizing data gaps and the lack of a true stress test at current scale.[\[28\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) In this world, the key observable is that credit secondaries continue to clear in the mid-90s for senior direct lending and around low-90s percent-of-NAV levels on average, which implies price discovery without panic.[\[60\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!mdE8!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd4781d80-ddad-4fe4-a0cc-88a30b639a75_807x478.png)

In a bear case, the market does not need a systemic bank run; it needs a slow-moving impairment cycle that forces NAV realism. The trigger set is sustained restrictive rates plus weak M&A and exit markets plus higher refinancing costs, which Evercore notes already weighed on M&A and refinancing dynamics post-2022, combined with incremental documentation slippage and sponsor-financed leverage maneuvers that shift value within the capital structure.[\[61\]](https://www.evercore.com/wp-content/uploads/2025/09/Private-Credit-Secondary-Market-Commentary-August-2025.pdf) The mechanical path is that cash interest stress increases PIK usage, covenant-lite loans delay intervention, and losses are realized later and more discontinuously, which is exactly the outcome IMF and FSOC warn about when they describe delayed loss realization followed by spikes in defaults and markdowns and the masking effect of PIK.[\[54\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) In that environment, the real-time “tape” is not private marks; it is the public proxy layer: BDC discounts to NAV widen, credit secondaries move from mid-90s to low-90s or worse in stressed segments, and semi-liquid vehicles hit their repurchase caps more frequently, as Reuters describes happening across multiple managers in Q1 2026.[\[62\]](https://www.reuters.com/business/barings-private-credit-fund-limits-withdrawals-after-redemption-requests-surge-2026-04-06/)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!lyIm!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F52a451d7-309c-4dbb-9b42-37b818d5525f_1080x913.png)

**In a stress case, private credit becomes a transmission channel rather than a contained correction. The mechanisms are well defined in the official literature even if probabilities are debated: leverage providers face correlated drawdowns on revolving lines; LPs face capital calls and commitment strain; NAV loans and subscription facilities become harder to roll or more expensive; and confidence in valuations weakens when secondary clearing prices and public proxy prices diverge from reported NAVs.** IMF explicitly points to multiple layers of leverage and unclear interconnections as vulnerabilities and warns that, as the asset class grows under limited prudential oversight, vulnerabilities could become systemic; OFR quantifies counterparty exposure channels and stresses the potential for spillover into banking and broader markets if leveraged funds default; and the Fed shows the scale and concentration of bank commitments to private credit vehicles.[\[63\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf) In this scenario, “liquidity event” and “valuation event” become the same thing: if the system needs to sell to meet repurchases or defend financing, it discovers prices in a market that was not built for continuous clearing, and that is where gaps appear.

**Transmission into liquid markets is not hard to map if you focus on who marks daily, who funds short, and who has explicit leverage constraints.** The first-order signal is public-market wrappers and related financial equities: BDC discounts to NAV and price underperformance are a reliable “stress seismograph” because BIS notes that BDCs provide a window into the opaque private credit space and documents discount deepening and underperformance tied to SaaS exposure in late 2025 to early 2026.[\[64\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2603v.htm)

![Worries about software stocks spilled over to private credit](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!poVD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ffcf14587-80e4-418c-983b-880f80de81f3_1200x879.jpeg)

Worries about software stocks spilled over to private credit

The second-order signal is leveraged public credit, because leveraged loan and high-yield markets are the closest liquid proxies for spread moves that should, in an economically honest system, flow into private credit marks; and OFR notes that higher rates deteriorated interest coverage for leveraged loan borrowers and warns that CLO structures can be forced into fire sales of distressed loans, which is a classic liquidity amplifier.[\[65\]](https://www.financialresearch.gov/the-ofr-blog/2026/03/26/calm-markets-and-underlying-risks/) The third-order signal is bank funding conditions, because bank lines to private credit vehicles can be drawn defensively in stress, and the Fed shows both the growth and concentration of these exposures, while the FSB highlights bank–nonbank interconnectedness as a structural monitoring priority.[\[66\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html) The fourth-order signal is secondaries: Jefferies’ reporting that credit secondaries average pricing was in the low 90s percent of NAV in H1 2025 is effectively a baseline; the “break” is when that clearing level shifts sharply lower and volumes surge because sellers become forced rather than discretionary.[\[67\]](https://www.jefferies.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/08/Jefferies-Global-Secondary-Market-Review-July-2025.pdf)

A monitoring framework that actually works has to be built on observable stress, not on quarterly narratives. The highest-signal indicators are the ones that tighten reflexive feedback loops: sustained increases in PIK share and non-accruals in the BDC universe, which FSOC treats as material because PIK can mask credit stress; repeated breaches of repurchase caps in semi-liquid funds, which Reuters shows are already occurring; widening BDC discounts to NAV and sector-driven underperformance, which BIS documents; and secondary-market clearing levels and GP-led transaction terms, which Evercore and Jefferies show are becoming increasingly measurable and therefore increasingly influential.[\[68\]](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/261/FSOC2024AnnualReport.pdf) Overlay those with bank funding conditions and utilization on credit lines to private credit vehicles, where the Fed provides both size and utilization context, and you have a real-time dashboard that does not depend on manager marks.[\[69\]](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/bank-lending-to-private-credit-size-characteristics-and-financial-stability-implications-20250523.html)

**The judgment is that private credit is both a durable structural shift and, in its current form, a crowded yield trade with embedded fragility.**

**It is durable because banks are unlikely to fully reclaim the middle-market and bespoke-lending terrain for structural reasons tied to regulation, business models, and the economics of balance sheet, which BIS, IMF, ECB, and NAIC all implicitly support through their framing of the bank-retreat and alternative-funding story.[\[70\]](https://www.bis.org/publ/qtrpdf/r_qt2503b.htm)**

**It is crowded and fragile because the asset class’s return optics have been supported by opacity, limited price discovery, strong inflows, and sponsor support during a period without a severe downturn at current size, and because the recent push toward retail and semi-liquid wrappers introduces run dynamics and public price signals that can force faster and more correlated adjustment.**[\[71\]](https://www.imf.org/-/media/files/publications/gfsr/2024/april/english/ch2.pdf)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!ySTQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F3d9ca66b-2487-4728-8c12-ba47ff57e194_1288x774.png)

The invalidation line for “contained and stable” is not a single default cycle; it is a simultaneous move in three channels:

- **persistent secondary discounts widening meaningfully below the low-90s baseline for senior direct lending**,
- **repeated repurchase caps and rising redemption requests across semi-liquid products, and**
- **tightening bank and fund-finance capacity that pushes private credit vehicles to defend liquidity rather than originate credit.[\[72\]](https://www.jefferies.com/wp-content/uploads/sites/4/2025/08/Jefferies-Global-Secondary-Market-Review-July-2025.pdf)**
