## The short answer

The short answer is: Central banks remain banks, and whatever note/deposit/IOU a bank issues is a liability—regardless of the conditions for redemption.

Of course, an even shorter answer is [Section 16](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/section16.htm) of the Federal Reserve Act.[^2]

These were the answers I fitted in the five seconds, and they are correct, but they are not satisfactory.

## Why not an asset?

It is worth taking the contrarian view, Randy would agree.

This person does not have money in the utility function.

Currency generates a stream of revenue to the Fed, and earning a return is what you get from holding an asset, not a liability.

Moreover, currency obviously is of public service—dare I say, a public asset? If not, why is it illegal to burn money? I am pretty sure the Treasury would have no problem if people decided that smoking ribs with T-bills is a good idea.

## Why a liability?

The correct answer is: Because you can pay down taxes with it.

Let’s walk through what you (hopefully) did two weeks ago with your 2025 taxes.

- Assume you had no income withheld in 2025.
- Due April 15th, 2026, you owed $10 to the Treasury, say on Day One 2026. Your taxes are due—effectively, the Treasury is lending to you until tax day. Thus, taxes dye are a liability to you, and an asset to the Treasury.
- You pull a [Nick Strafford from Cedar Buff](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-38603615), Virginia, and pay your taxes down in pennies (last chance!). Please do not.
- You have $10 less in currency, but you are current (no pun intended) with your taxes. Since the taxes due were clearly a liability to you, it must be that those $10 were an asset to you.
- The Treasury has $10 less… in tax claims to the public. That is, it has $10 less in assets. It follows that those $10 in currency must have been a liability of the Treasury.

I also tried another thought exercise with the idea of a date t=0, a “state of nature”, before monies and taxes are introduced. Too complicated.

## This is banking.

Now, I have not said anything superobvious in a while, so…

> A central bank is still a bank.

Actually, it is far from obvious—I just had a terrible vision of Randy asking why. And the Board of Governors is most definitely *not* a bank.

It is, though, a distinctive feature of banks that their liabilities provide services to their clients, aka depositors. It is through those services that banks can make a margin on deposits—well, that, and the FDIC deposit insurance, of course.

The central bank also provides services via its liabilities—this time, to qualified institutions (we call them *deposit* ory institutions, not by accident), the Treasury, or the public at large—the latter via currency.

[Itamar Drechsler](https://sites.google.com/site/idrechsl/) has done a lot of research (and talking) about the “deposit franchise,” the value associated with a bank's deposits via services (or via market power). Similarly, many have talked about the “currency franchise”—including what happens if you end up without one ([Hej, Sweden!](https://www.riksbank.se/en-gb/payments--cash/payments-in-sweden/payments-report-2025/trends-on-the-payments-market/the-swedish-payments-market-is-almost-entirely-digital/)).

## Randy’s Reply

*The fact that you provided such a detailed analysis suggests it’s not a triviality, even if one might say it is a purely semantic issue.*

*Consider just one more example please. Suppose you give me a beer today and in exchange I give you my note saying “IOU $10.” That is my liability. Now suppose you give me a beer in exchange for my note saying “thanks” or even “thanks sucker.” That’s not my liability… unless maybe I accept it for a “tax” I can impose. But wait - if I can impose a tax staying “give me back my note” in exchange for \*nothing\* hardly makes that note a liability - it is like me giving you an IOU that I can cancel whenever I like. Hmmm.*
