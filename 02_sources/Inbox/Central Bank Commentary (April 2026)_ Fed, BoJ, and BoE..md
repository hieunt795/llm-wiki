##### Figure 1. Hormuz transit collapse and Global Supply Chain Pressure Index. Sources: NY Fed GSCPI; IMF PortWatch.

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since 28 February 2026. Daily vessel transits have collapsed from roughly 80 per day before the strikes to fewer than 5 in the latest data (AIS signals only); daily tonnage has fallen from approximately 4 million tonnes to 0.1 million — a 97% reduction sustained over more than two months. Our Hormuz Transit Stress Index stands at 4.1 standard deviations, an all-time high in the available sample and well above the 2.5σ threshold I would indicate for severe disruption. The [Global Supply Chain Pressure Index](https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/policy/gscpi#/overview), the New York Fed’s measure of stress in the global production network, has jumped to 1.82σ in the latest reading — a 1.14σ increase in a single month and the highest level since July 2022.

In a prior commentary, I noted that the March GSCPI at 0.68σ was elevated but below the threshold associated with non-linear dynamics, with the direction of travel mattering as much as the level. The latest reading suggests that a non-linear regime of propagation of supply chain stress through the entire production system is in play despite the geographically concentrated nature of the Hormuz shock. I stressed the role of the structural amplifier, the input-output cascade through multi-stage production networks, as a key transmission channel for second-round effects that bypass the inflation-expectations and wage-price-spiral channels. This current GSCPI reading indicates that the global production network has now entered that regime. The current level is below the 2021–22 GSCPI peak of 4.5σ but is the highest since July 2022. While the disruption remains concentrated in energy and Middle East-sourced commodity flows, its effects are spreading geographically and across sectors, intensifying broader supply chain disruptions. Asian economies dependent on Middle Eastern energy and intermediate-goods imports are at the center of the disruption; European and US exposures run mainly through fertiliser, petrochemicals, and intermediate goods. Specific countries and sectors are crossing their own non-linear thresholds even where the global aggregate has not.

The April central bank meetings reveal a common central-bank theme: treat the Middle East shock as an energy-price shock unless it changes inflation expectations. But the shock may also be propagating through a production-network channel that does not need expectations to move before it becomes inflationary.

The Fed, the BoE, and the BoJ each calibrated their April decisions according to the standard framework. The [BoE’s April Monetary Policy Report](https://www.bankofengland.co.uk/monetary-policy-report/2026/april-2026) formalises it most explicitly through its three-part taxonomy (direct effects, indirect effects, and second-round effects defined as “changes in wage- and price-setting behaviour that outlast the original shock”) and parameterises its three scenarios by paths for oil and gas prices. The BoJ partitions inflation into a temporary oil-driven headline component and an underlying trend approaching 2% from below, with the policy response conditioned on whether the shock develops “secondary effects” in the underlying rate. Powell at the Fed framed the inflation overshoot as a tariff-driven core component and an energy-driven headline component, both treated as supply-shock effects to be looked through unless longer-term expectations move. The Fed is adopting the standard central-bank framework for a negative supply shock, but it may be incomplete if the closure of Hormuz has pushed parts of the global production network beyond buffer capacity. In that case, the relevant shock amplifier is not only behavioural. It is structural.

The two amplifiers are analytically distinct (see ***[The Return of Nonlinear Inflation: Part I](https://gianlucabenigno.substack.com/p/the-return-of-nonlinear-inflation)***). The structural amplifier (rooted in the input-output architecture of the production system, triggered by the cascade of cost increases through multi-stage networks once buffer capacity is exhausted) is automatic and conditional only on shock magnitude and persistence. The behavioural amplifier (what central banks describe as “second-round effects”) is conditional on labour market tightness and on the salience of inflation to wage- and price-setters. Both amplifiers can be present simultaneously. The usual central banks’ framework does engage with the structural channel but subsumes it under the indirect-effects category, rather than treating it as a separate non-linear propagation mechanism with its own policy implications.

The distinction matters for policy timing. A behavioural second-round effect can be monitored through inflation-expectations surveys, wage settlements, and firms’ pricing intentions — data that the central banks routinely track. A structural cascade shows up first in different places: sectoral producer price indices, delivery times, supplier margins, fertiliser and petrochemical input costs, shipping rates on Middle East routes, and inventory drawdowns at firms exposed to disrupted supply chains. Waiting for expectations or wages to move may therefore miss the early phase of the shock entirely. The policy question is not only whether households and firms believe inflation will stay high; it is whether the production network is already transmitting a cost shock through channels that compound rather than attenuate as the disruption persists.

The BoJ, the Fed, and the BoE kept their interest rates on hold with three different dissent patterns, but a single underlying issue. The April decisions are defensible within the framework that the central banks are using. Whether the framework is complete is an open question. The bank-by-bank discussions that follow take up each Committee’s reading of the Middle East shock and its dissent patterns in detail.

##### Central Bank Commentary, March 2026.

**Federal Reserve**

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!o8sE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F186093c4-5c6e-41a0-b14e-2aaba030b0be_936x486.png)

**Policy assessment**

The April FOMC held the federal funds rate at 3.50%–3.75% on an 8-4 vote, the most divided FOMC vote since 1992. Governor Stephen Miran preferred a 25bps cut. Beth Hammack (Cleveland), Neel Kashkari (Minneapolis), and Lorie Logan (Dallas) supported the hold but objected to the easing bias preserved in the statement language (“ *the extent and timing of additional adjustments* ”). For the first time in this cycle, hawkish dissents on statement language and a dovish dissent on the rate decision appeared simultaneously. The meeting was Powell’s last as Chair. Powell announced that he would remain on the Board as Governor after his Chair term expires on 15 May 2026 — the first outgoing Chair to do so since Marriner Eccles in 1948. His Governor term itself runs until 31 January 2028.

Latest inflation data: ***[US CPI – March 2026](https://gianlucabenigno.substack.com/p/us-march-26-cpi-inflation-report).***

**Policy background**

*Policy decision*

The 8-4 vote includes three first-year voters (Hammack, Kashkari, Logan) under the 2026 rotation that the December commentary flagged as carrying a hawkish tilt. Their objection is procedural rather than substantive: they support today’s hold but read the “additional adjustments” language as an easing bias inconsistent with an inflation overshoot that has widened, not narrowed, since January. Christopher Waller, who joined Miran’s January dissent for a 25bps cut, voted with the majority this time, a meaningful shift by a previously dovish Governor. Miran’s continued dovish dissent has a finite shelf life. His term as Governor expired on 31 January 2026; he has been serving pending the confirmation of a successor, and Kevin Warsh’s nomination is technically for Miran’s seat. Once Warsh joins, Miran will leave the Board.

*Inflation assessment*

The CPI release for March, published on April 10th, showed headline jumping to 3.3% YoY from 2.4%, with the energy index up 10.9% month-over-month, and gasoline up 21.2%. Core CPI inched up to 2.6% YoY from 2.5%. Powell’s two-part diagnosis, through energy and tariff shocks, places the inflation overshoot squarely in the supply-shock category rather than the demand category, which is the analytical foundation for the Fed’s holding pattern.

The expectations channel is where Powell’s framing left the most caution. He did not lean on the stronger language of “anchoring”: he said most longer-term measures “ *remain consistent with our 2 percent inflation goal.*” That phrasing is enough to justify a hold but not enough to remove the policy risk; the entire holding pattern rests on the assumption that the energy pass-through does not contaminate longer-term expectations.

*Labour market/activity assessment*

The unemployment rate stood at 4.3% in March, edging down from 4.4% in February. Nonfarm payrolls rose 178,000, with the prior two months revised down by 7,000, leaving Q1 averaging just over 60,000 per month. The unemployment decline came largely from a 396,000 drop in the labour force as participation fell to 61.9%. Powell’s framing: payroll slowing reflects “ *a decline in the growth of the labor force… though labor demand has clearly softened as well* ”, the “low-hire, low-fire” equilibrium discussed in the previous commentary. Average hourly earnings rose 3.5% YoY, reducing the case that wages are amplifying the price shocks at least for now.

*Reading of the Middle East shock*

The statement reduces the channel to generic uncertainty (“ *a high level of uncertainty about the economic outlook* ”). Powell’s press conference operated at the same level. The substantive engagement comes from the three regional dissenters in their 1st May statements. Logan names the channel directly: “ *the conflict in the Middle East raises the prospect of prolonged or repeated supply disruptions that could create further inflationary pressures*.” Hammack frames the asymmetric risk balance (“ *upside risks to inflation and downside risks to growth and employment”*) without extending into the production-side mechanism. Kashkari goes furthest. His piece is the first formal Fed communication this cycle to translate the cascade-disruption channel into a two-scenario structure: a benign one with a hold-then-gradually-ease path, and an adverse one in which “ *Federal funds rate increases, potentially a series of them, could be warranted, even at the risk of further weakness to the labor market.*”

Even Kashkari’s framework remains price-channel: the transmission mechanism is the unanchoring of long-run expectations, and the response variable is the inflation rate. The cascade matters because it can produce inflation effects large enough to threaten the expectations anchor, not because it operates as an independent dynamic. The dissent on the statement language is, in effect, a dissent on the state of inflation, an unusual development with possible implications for the future rate path, though not yet for the immediate rate decision.

*Risk assessment*

The risk profile is asymmetric: upside on inflation, downside on activity. Powell flagged that “ *in the near term, higher energy prices will push up overall inflation* ”; the activity implications “ *remain unclear.*” The three 1st May dissent statements argue the same hold-but-remove-the-bias conclusion from three angles. Hammack: outlook *(“no longer appropriate given the outlook”*). Logan: procedural: forward guidance is “ *an important policy tool* ” that must “ *reflect the policy outlook*,” and with risks two-sided, the bias toward cuts is inconsistent with that requirement. Kashkari proposes a two-way scenario with explicit hike contingency. Together, they constitute a more substantive analytical case against the easing bias than the FOMC statement contains. Logan’s procedural argument is the one most likely to constrain Warsh’s drafting choices: under it, the bias should either be removed or flipped, but not preserved as ambiguous.

A separate institutional risk is now operative. Powell used the press conference to confirm the closure of the DOJ investigation on April 24th with assurances over the weekend that it would not be reopened absent an Inspector General referral, and to commit to remaining on the Board: “ *I have said that I will not leave the Board until this investigation is well and truly over… and I stand by that.*” After Warsh is sworn in, the seven-member Board will comprise Warsh, Bowman, and Waller alongside Cook, Barr, Jefferson, and Powell. Powell’s decision to continue means the Board’s existing composition is preserved through the transition rather than being reshaped immediately.

*Key themes*

- *The hold is being defended on two sides at once***.** The resulting split is one of the most divided FOMC votes in recent decades and reflects a Committee genuinely divided on direction. With three of the four 2026 newcomers objecting to the easing bias, the path of least resistance for the June statement is to soften the language rather than strengthen it.
- *Supply-shock framing depends on expectations remaining anchored***.** Powell’s tariff/energy partition holds only if longer-term expectations track 2%. He did not claim they are anchored — only “ *consistent with* ” the goal. That is a narrower foundation than it appears, and the dissenters’ statements collectively raise the cost of treating it as sufficient.
- *Politics did not shape this meeting, but may shape the next one***.** Three institutional developments converged on this FOMC: Warsh’s Senate Banking Committee advance, Powell’s announcement that he will stay on the Board after his Chair term ends, and the closure of the DOJ investigation against him on 24 April. None visibly affected how the rate decision was made or how Powell explained it. The institutional firewall between politics and policy held. Whether it holds under Warsh, who arrives with a different relationship to the administration, a different communication style, and a Board where the existing composition is preserved through the transition, is the open question.

**Policy reading**

The Fed has settled into a hold near nominal neutral, with the easing cycle paused but not formally ended. Whether the easing bias survives the June statement is the key single question. Hammack and Logan have put the case against it on the record on 1 May; Kashkari has articulated what the alternative outlook contains — an explicit hike path conditional on Hormuz remaining closed. Logan’s procedural argument constrains Warsh’s drafting most directly. The 16–17 June meeting will include the first SEP under the new Chair; Warsh’s confirmation hearing put on the record his preference that the Fed should *“speak less frequently, pull back forward guidance, and stop telegraphing”* decisions in advance, which would itself reshape how the next decision reads.

*Current baseline***:** hold at 3.50%–3.75% in June, with the most likely incremental change being to the statement language. A cut would require a sharper labour-market deterioration (unemployment above 4.5%) or clear Middle East de-escalation.

**Bank of Japan (BoJ)**

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!eyET!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa768c2bc-3ac7-4524-bd5a-aa4285aed43b_936x470.png)

**Policy assessment**

The April meeting held the overnight call rate at around 0.75% on a 6-3 vote, the largest dissent of the Ueda governorship. Tamura and Nakagawa joined Takata in proposing an immediate move to 1.0%. The vote sits against an Outlook Report that has revised median FY2026 CPI excluding fresh food up to 2.8% from 1.9% in January, entirely on the rise in crude oil prices. The hold is, therefore, in Ueda’s own framing, a shift in probability rather than a fundamental reassessment: *“the basic reason for maintaining the current policy is that the likelihood of the baseline outlook has declined quite significantly this time”* (press conference, 28 April).

Latest inflation data: ***[Japan CPI – March 2026](https://gianlucabenigno.substack.com/p/japan-march-26-cpi-inflation-report?r=nm3g)*****.**

**Policy background**

*Policy decision*

The escalation from one rate dissent in January and March to three rate dissents in April is the most consequential development. Nakagawa’s switch is the most striking, since she had voted with the majority throughout the cycle, and her shift signals that the centre of gravity in the hawkish camp is moving. All three dissenters argue that risks to prices are skewed to the upside under accommodative conditions; Tamura is most direct, arguing that the Bank should set the policy rate as close to neutral as possible. The textual dissent has its own precedent worth flagging: at the March meeting, Tamura had already dissented on the Outlook Report’s inflation language, even while voting with the majority on the rate itself. In April, both Takata and Tamura gave formal amendments to the Outlook, with Takata’s stating that consumer prices have already reached levels consistent with the 2% price stability target, and Tamura’s stating that underlying inflation will remain at those target-consistent levels going forward. Both were rejected by a majority vote. The hawkish minority is therefore not just casting alternative votes but contesting the institutional narrative and has been doing so progressively for two consecutive meetings.

A separate change in the forward guidance language detaches normalisation from the activity cycle. The earlier wording committed the Bank to raising rates *“in accordance with improvements in economic activity and prices;”* the April formulation drops *“improvement.*” Ueda confirmed the logic in the press conference: *“Unless there is a major downside to the economy, if prices follow the expected path or if upside risks materialize, rate hikes are possible.”* The phrase no longer fit and was changed.

*Inflation assessment*

March headline CPI rose to 1.5% YoY from 1.3%; CPI excluding fresh food rose to 1.8% from 1.6%. The softness is largely engineered, since government energy subsidies are mechanically depressing the energy component (electricity down 8.0% YoY in March, gas down 5.2%). The Bank now projects CPI excluding fresh food at 2.5–3.0% in FY2026 (median 2.8%, up almost a full point from January), 2.0–2.5% in FY2027, and around 2% in FY2028. CPI excluding fresh food and energy is projected at 2.6% in both FY2026 and FY2027. The projection assumes Dubai crude declining from $105/barrel toward $70-80 by horizon-end. Crucially, the projected timing of underlying CPI reaching target, *“between the second half of fiscal 2026 and fiscal 2027”*, has not changed, which is what allows the majority to hold despite the headline overshoot.

The Bank’s case for holding rates despite the upward inflation revision rests on a single assumption: that medium- to long-term inflation expectations remain consistent with 2%. If they do, the headline overshoot can be looked through as a temporary energy effect. If they drift higher, the look-through justification collapses, and the Bank must hike sooner; if they drift lower, the gradual normalisation timetable itself comes into question. At the press conference, Ueda was unusually direct: medium- to long-term expectations are “approaching 2%” but “in the sense that they have not been moving around that level for a long time, I think it is fair to say they are not fully anchored.”

*Activity and external environment*

The Bank now projects real GDP at 0.4–0.7% in FY2026 (median 0.5%, down from 1.0% in January), median 0.7% in FY2027, and median 0.8% in FY2028. The downgrade is a direct read-through of the oil shock through terms of trade. At the press conference, Ueda framed it carefully: both effects are temporary, but the inflation effect “ *is expected to last somewhat longer”*, precisely the asymmetry the dissenters are responding to. In the 1970s comparison, he distinguished current conditions from the first oil shock by noting Japan was already overheated then, but added the qualifier that matters now: *“as an initial condition, the current policy interest rate is at least below the neutral interest rate.”*

The labour market remains the pillar of the wage-price framework: unemployment at 2.6% in February, jobs-to-applicants ratio at 1.19. The 2026 shuntō — Japan’s annual spring wage round, the central data point for the BoJ’s normalisation thesis — produced wage gains above 5% for a third consecutive year. Rengo’s initial March tally showed an average increase of 5.26% (with base pay alone at 3.85%); the mid-April update, incorporating smaller firms, came in at 5.08%. Both readings were softer than 2025’s initial 5.46%, but well above the 2% baseline that prevailed for three decades before 2024. The wage-price mechanism that the BoJ requires for normalisation continues to operate.

*Reading of the Middle East shock*

The April Outlook Report is the most explicit of the three Committees on the real-economy production side. It states that “some material manufacturers have already started reducing the utilization rates of their plants” — the cascade in motion through production capacity rather than through price effects alone. It lists “raw materials, intermediate goods, and other items with a high share of imports from the Middle East” as exposed, and devotes a substantive paragraph to fertiliser supply chain implications for food prices. Ueda articulated the upstream-downstream cascade in production terms: “Crude oil is used widely as a raw material across many industries, from upstream to downstream processes. Therefore, persistently high crude oil prices would act to push up prices not only for energy and chemicals, but also for a wide range of goods.”

The acknowledgement is real but partial. The BoJ then routes the cascade’s consequences back through the price channel and the expectations anchor. The Outlook’s policy implication, that cascade-driven price effects matter only insofar as they *“affect underlying CPI inflation through changes in inflation expectations”*, preserves the expectations channel as the binding constraint. Among the three Committees, the BoJ shows the clearest tension between its empirical observations (real-economy cascade in motion) and its decision rule (look through unless expectations move).

*Risk assessment*

The Bank’s FY2026 risk balance is explicitly asymmetric: economic activity skewed to the downside; prices skewed to the upside. The conceptual frame is the classic look-through treatment: temporary supply shocks should be looked through unless they develop “secondary effects.” In Ueda’s framing: “ *if it has secondary effects — in our terminology, if it affects the underlying rate of inflation — then there is no question that we would need to respond appropriately in the direction of raising interest rates.*”

Three press conference exchanges show Ueda preserving room to act faster than the standard reaction function would suggest. Asked by Bloomberg whether upside price risks alone could justify a hike: *“Yes, that is correct. However, I do not think that this would become the only reason.”* Asked by TBS whether a hike is possible even if the baseline scenario remains a low-probability case: “ *if upside risks to prices become fairly evident… while downside risks to the economy… are to some extent limited, then there is a possibility of reaching a decision to raise interest rates.”* Asked by Nikkei what happens if inflation runs meaningfully above 2%: *“at least as a possibility, the terminal rate could exceed the neutral interest rate.”*

The cost of not yet having acted is now visible in the FX market. USD/JPY breached 160 in the days after the meeting, and Japanese authorities reportedly intervened on 30 April – 1 May to support the yen —following escalating verbal warnings from Finance Minister Katayama. The yen weakness has two drivers, both running through the BoJ’s hold: deteriorating terms of trade from higher oil prices, and a US-Japan rate spread that delayed normalisation has kept wider than it would otherwise be. The MoF can intervene without BoJ action, but the reported intervention itself signals that the costs of the holding pattern are being absorbed elsewhere in the policy framework. Asked at the press conference about FX, Ueda kept to the standard line: exchange-rate developments now feed through to domestic prices more readily than in the past, but FX policy is the MoF’s responsibility, not the Bank’s.

A separate political dimension was put on the record. Asked about reported government commentary on the policy rate ahead of the meeting under the new Takaichi administration, Ueda’s response was unambiguous: “regarding our policy decision this time, we discussed it ourselves and judged it to be appropriate.”

*Key themes*

- *Dissent has crossed from voice to faction***.** Two dissenters in January and March, three in April, with Nakagawa’s switch the most consequential. Combined with the proposed Outlook amendments, the hawkish minority is now contesting both the rate path and the official narrative. Another data print confirming upward inflation persistence could tip a fourth member.
- *Outlook revision and rate decision are pulling in different directions***.** Revising FY2026 CPI up nearly a full point while leaving the rate unchanged requires the market to trust the Bank’s read of underlying versus transitory. That trust is conditional on oil following the assumed path and on expectations remaining on the gradual trajectory the Bank has projected.
- *Forward guidance has been quietly tightened on two margins***.** First, the official forward guidance dropped its commitment to raise rates “ *in accordance with improvements in economic activity and prices”*; the Bank no longer needs growth to be strengthening to justify a hike, which matters given that FY2026 GDP has just been downgraded. Second, Ueda confirmed in the press conference that the Bank can hike on upside price risks alone and on the outlook rather than waiting for the data to confirm the central scenario. Together, the two changes preserve the option to hike even if growth weakens further and even if the inflation outlook remains uncertain.

**Policy reading**

The Bank is still pursuing gradual normalisation, but the cost of waiting is rising. The next meeting on 16–17 June will be limited by what data are observable: Ueda himself conceded that *“very clear, large price pressure may not necessarily appear in CPI data by then; it is more likely to appear a little further ahead.”* But the door to a June hike has been preserved on the outlook rather than the data: “ *if the risk of an even larger rise increases, it is possible that we would make judgments without waiting for that data.”* June also brings the interim assessment of the JGB purchase reduction plan; Ueda was clear that the two decisions will be judged separately even if announced on the same day.

**Current baseline:** hold at 0.75% in June, with a 25bps move to 1.0% increasingly likely in the second half of 2026 if oil prices stabilise or fall, the wage pass-through accelerates as projected, and underlying CPI continues to track toward target. A June move cannot be ruled out, as Ueda has explicitly preserved that option.

**Bank of England (BoE)**

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!z2zI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F69e003a2-39cb-4ce4-8c94-dc0ac76d1e5c_936x506.png)

*Policy assessment*

At its meeting ending on 29 April 2026 (announced 30 April), the MPC voted 8-1 to hold Bank Rate at 3.75%. Huw Pill dissented, preferring 4.00%. The 8-1 vote disguises the sharpest two-meeting pivot since the easing cycle began in August 2024. In February, the Committee was 5-4, with Breeden, Dhingra, Ramsden, and Taylor dissenting in favour of an immediate cut. The March meeting, held three weeks after the strikes on Iran began, produced a unanimous hold — the same four explicitly stating they would have voted for a cut absent the conflict. April delivers the second shift: the only dissent is now Pill’s, in the opposite direction. Two months ago, the active debate was about cutting; today it is about whether to hike. Bailey’s framing across the press conference captures what holds the centre: *“an active hold; it is not a passive wait-and-see hold.”* The April Monetary Policy Report’s three-scenario architecture is the analytical structure with policy paths conditioned on the duration of the energy-price shock and second-round effect strength, with the appropriate response made explicitly state-contingent.

Latest inflation data: ***[UK CPI – March 2026](https://gianlucabenigno.substack.com/p/uk-march-26-cpi-inflation-report)***

**Policy background**

*Policy decision*

The majority is split across several groupings. Bailey and Breeden place most weight on Scenario B, though Bailey assigns some weight to Scenario C. Dhingra keeps all three scenarios in play and preserves clear easing conditionality in the event of a swift resolution and materially lower energy prices. Ramsden places equal weight on Scenarios A and B (limited or no second-round effects), while Taylor is closest to the zone between Scenario A and the standard-treatment path. Their hold rationale rests on slack, weak demand, and the tightening already delivered by markets. Greene, Lombardelli, and Mann place most weight on Scenario B with a skew toward C, and explicitly contemplate that a Bank Rate increase “ *may be necessary in upcoming meetings”* (Greene). Mann is most direct: she “ *would expect to increase Bank Rate”* if inflation outturns and expectations continue rising. Pill is already there.

The dovish case has not gone away. Dhingra (vote rationale): “ *In the event of a swift resolution and materially lower energy prices, further reduction in Bank Rate would be warranted, and possibly quickly.*” The hold is therefore conditional in two directions: hawkish if Scenario B or C materialises, dovish if Scenario A holds.

*Inflation assessment*

CPI rose to 3.3% in March from 3.0%. Services inflation rose to 4.5% from 4.3%. The composition is energy-driven: transport up 4.7% YoY (fastest since December 2022), motor fuels up 4.9% year-on-year, domestic heating oil up 95.3% YoY. Food inflation rose to 3.7% from 3.3%, beginning to capture indirect effects. The Bank now projects CPI averaging 3.1% in 2026 Q2, rising back to 3.3% in Q3 (a 1.4pp upward revision from the February Report). Bailey’s headline (press conference): inflation will rise to “ *a little over 3.5% by the end of the year,*” compared with the pre-conflict expectation of a return to target from April.

The April Report’s three-scenario framework gives the policy stance analytical content. Scenario A: energy prices follow futures, no second-round effects, inflation falls below target by the end of 2027. Scenario B: higher and more persistent energy prices with moderate second-round effects, inflation returns to 2% gradually over years two and three. Scenario C: oil peaks at $130/barrel and gas at 211 pence/therm, then declines gradually but remains above Scenarios A and B through the projection, inflation rising above 6% and ending the horizon above target. Scenario C “ *would likely warrant a forceful tightening in monetary policy”*; Scenarios A and B justify a less restrictive stance, with A potentially warranting further easing once direct effects fade.

The Committee’s stated decision rule (Bailey, opening remarks) is a hierarchy: monetary policy *“generally looks through the direct effects”* because they fade before policy can affect them; the case for looking through indirect effects is “weaker” because they take longer to come through; and second-round effects *“should not look through.*” Lombardelli’s frame for the period ahead identifies two underlying dimensions: persistence of energy prices and strength of second-round effects. *“The persistence of energy prices will become more apparent as time passes. The extent of second-round effects will be much more of a judgment as the evidence emerges over time*.”

*Labour market and activity assessment*

Unemployment stood at 4.9% in December 2025 – February 2026 (latest release, published 21 April), down from 5.2% but on the rising trend that has characterised the loosening cycle. Vacancies fell to 711,000 in January-March. Regular pay grew 3.6% YoY in the three months to February, with private-sector pay at 3.2%.

Bailey’s structural framing of why this shock differs from 2022 is the analytical core (vote rationale, reinforced in press conference): *“the increase in energy prices has been smaller, monetary policy started more restrictive, and the labour market is weaker.”* Taylor goes further, citing 2011 as a better comparison than 2022, both in magnitude and starting position. With slack accumulating, demand subdued, and 2026 wage settlements largely agreed before the shock, the wage-side channel of second-round effects is judged substantially constrained. Bailey’s caveat on timing matters: “ *It will be some time before we really get a read on pay, because most pay settlements are done in the spring and we have passed through that period for this year.”* The wage channel will not show up clearly in data until 2027, meaning H2 2026 decisions will be made with limited visibility on the most important transmission channel for inflation persistence.

The price-side channel is where the Committee’s anxiety lies. The Minutes acknowledge that “ *any second-round effects were likely to materialise more quickly via pricing channels than wage-setting*.” Greene flags (vote rationale) that “ *household inflation expectations rose this March more than in March 2022 (following Russia’s invasion of Ukraine), suggesting household attentiveness to inflation is heightened after five years of above-target inflation.*” That implies faster transmission from energy to consumer prices than would otherwise be assumed.

*Reading of the Middle East shock*

The April Report is the most analytically developed of the three Committees on the price-channel architecture, and the most explicit on the wage-expectations definition of second-round effects. That being said, it is not the most concrete on real-economy production-side effects. The three-scenario framework is parameterised entirely by paths for oil and gas prices, with second-round effects modelled as a behavioural response that scales with energy persistence and labour-market tightness. The structural amplifier, production-network cascade independent of behavioural change, does not appear as a separate scenario.

The Committee addresses the channel through indirect effects discussion. Bailey returned to the fertiliser supply chain explicitly: *“lower supply and higher prices of fertilisers may also push up on food price inflation through higher costs and lower yields for UK farmers and across the world.”* Lombardelli supplied the staff projection: food inflation reaches 4.6% by September 2026 on staff forecast, with Agency intelligence suggesting 6-7% by year-end. The Minutes’ acknowledgement that price-side transmission may run faster than wage-side is a useful internal extension of the second-round category beyond pure wage dynamics, but the formal framework still treats those effects as behavioural, not structural. The BoE has the most articulated decision rule for responding to wage-expectations second-round effects, and the least developed framework for distinguishing those from cascade dynamics that would manifest in PPI before in expectations measures.

*Risk assessment*

Risks have flipped to the upside on inflation while remaining tilted to the downside on activity. Bailey’s framing in the opening remarks: monetary policy *“must manage a trade-off between the speed with which it brings inflation back to target and the consideration that should be placed on the variability of output.”*

The financial conditions argument does significant work in the hold rationale. Bailey laid this out: in February, markets priced roughly 40bps of cuts for 2026; the curve has now moved up by around 55bps, leaving the path well above where it was assumed. His conclusion: “ *for Scenarios A and B, the illustrative interest-rate responses would largely be accommodated by reversing out these assumed cuts without further increases in rates. But this would not be the case for Scenario C*.” The tightening Scenarios A and B require have, in large part, already happened in markets. Scenario C is the only path where active hikes become unavoidable.

On hike conditionality, Bailey declined to put numbers on what “ *forceful tightening* ” in Scenario C would look like but was unambiguous on timing: *“if we see that type of very difficult scenario emerging, it is important that we respond quickly.*” On the symmetric question — whether Scenarios A or B require a rate rise — Bailey: *“I cannot give you a cast-iron assurance that there will be no increase in any scenario. But… a good deal of space was available to accommodate that.*”

*Key themes*

- *The energy shock has reset the easing debate without resetting the easing cycle.*The four February doves have joined the hold consensus, but their underlying view of activity has not changed. Bailey’s “active hold” framing is the deliberate signal that this is not a stable equilibrium.
- *Several hold-camp members are openly contemplating hikes.*Greene, Lombardelli, and Mann set out the conditions; Pill is already there. The 8-1 understates the policy direction the Committee would move in if second-round effects begin to crystallise.
- *The state-contingent framework is a deliberate substitute for forward guidance.*Rather than committing to a single projection, the Report conditions the policy response on which scenario unfolds. It preserves optionality on both sides and makes the decision depend on incoming data.
- *Markets have already done much of the tightening work for Scenarios A and B.*The 55bps curve shift since February, combined with the disappearance of the previously priced 40bps of 2026 cuts, means the Committee can deliver the Scenarios A and B stance largely by accommodating markets rather than acting against them.

**Policy reading**

The Bank has entered an energy-shock-determined phase in which the easing trajectory of late 2025 is on indefinite hold, and the next move is genuinely two-sided. The 18 June meeting will not yet have decisive evidence. Bailey asked directly: *“Will we be in a position where we can say with certainty which scenario we are in? Very well may not.”* The 30 July meeting is likely to be the more natural strategic decision point, as it is the next Monetary Policy Report meeting. Taylor’s vote rationale: “ *We will know more by June or July about the likely conflict scenario*.”

*Current baseline***:** hold at 3.75% on 18 June. The 30 July meeting (with the Monetary Policy Report) is the more consequential decision point. A hike to 4.00% becomes a live possibility if services inflation continues to surprise upward, household expectations continue to drift higher, and the Middle East situation does not de-escalate.
