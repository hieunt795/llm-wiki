**Key takeaways:**

- The overall Consumer Price Index (CPI, n.s.a.) increased by 4.2% year-on-year (YoY) in May, above both the consensus expectation of 4.0% YoY and April’s 3.8% YoY increase.
- Core CPI in May was 2.9% YoY (n.s.a.), above April’s reading of 2.8% YoY and in line with the consensus forecast of 2.9% YoY.
- Inflationary pressures remain primarily concentrated in the core service sector, with an increase of 3.4% YoY in May, slightly above April’s 3.3% YoY reading. The core goods sector (ex-energy and food) experienced an increase of 1.1% YoY, in line with April’s 1.1% YoY increase.
- The rent of shelter increased by 3.3% YoY in May, unchanged from the 3.3% YoY increase recorded in April. The month-on-month increase stepped down to 0.4% MoM compared with the 0.6% MoM increase recorded in April.
- The longer-term easing in core services inflation, especially its housing component, remains a notable feature of the underlying inflation picture, and May data extended that improvement on the month. The monthly reading eased to 0.4% MoM (n.s.a.) in May, below April’s 0.5% MoM (n.s.a.) figure.
- Core goods inflation remained at 1.1% year-on-year, driven in part by a 4.8% increase in apparel. On a monthly basis, core goods inflation was little changed, at 0.0% MoM (n.s.a.) compared to a 0.1% MoM (n.s.a.) increase observed in April.
- Overall, the May CPI release suggested that inflation pressures remained elevated, but the underlying inflation impulse was, if anything, softer than the headline numbers alone imply. Headline CPI increased to 4.2% YoY, supported by higher gasoline prices and broader transportation-related costs as the Middle East energy shock continued to affect global oil markets. Core CPI edged up to 2.9% YoY from 2.8% in April on base effects, even as the monthly core pace eased to 0.2% (s.a.), with the remaining firmness concentrated in selected tariff-sensitive goods (notably apparel) and airline fares. Shelter and broader core services inflation remained well below their post-pandemic peaks and slowed further on the month, suggesting that the recent inflation impulse stayed concentrated in energy- and import-sensitive sectors rather than signaling a generalized reacceleration across the wider consumer basket.
- From a monetary policy perspective, the May inflation report arrived ahead of the June FOMC meeting and during the first month of Kevin Warsh’s tenure as Federal Reserve Chair. Recent Federal Reserve communication has consistently framed the inflation overshoot as primarily a supply-driven phenomenon, reflecting higher energy prices and tariff-related pass-through rather than a renewed demand-driven inflation cycle. The May data broadly remained consistent with that interpretation. While headline inflation continued to face upward pressure from fuel and transportation-related components and some tariff-sensitive goods categories showed further firming, evidence of broad-based inflation acceleration remained limited. As a result, the release is unlikely on its own to alter the Committee’s near-term policy stance materially. However, it reinforces the importance of monitoring inflation expectations and potential second-round effects ahead of the June meeting.

**Review of the Inflation Release**

In May 2026, the Consumer Price Index (CPI) rose by 4.2% (n.s.a.) year-on-year (YoY) (Chart 1), above both the market consensus at 4.0% YoY and April’s 3.8% YoY figure. On a month-on-month basis (MoM), CPI increased by 0.6% MoM (n.s.a.), compared with the 0.9% MoM value recorded in April. The seasonally adjusted monthly change was 0.5% MoM, down slightly from April’s 0.6% s.a. print and still elevated by historical standards. Energy remained the dominant headline driver, contributing over sixty percent of the monthly all-items increase. Gasoline rose 7.0% on a seasonally adjusted basis (8.6% on an unadjusted basis), a sharp deceleration from March’s record surge, though slightly firmer than April’s pace, and still leaving gasoline prices 40.5% higher than a year earlier.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!_bhk!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F28e987f7-ecb3-48e7-9eb9-ebf7147dbb4e_2499x1540.png)

Core CPI (excluding food and energy) increased by 2.9% year-on-year in May (*Chart 1*), above April’s 2.8% YoY figure and in line with the market consensus of 2.9% YoY. On a month-on-month basis, core CPI increased by 0.3% MoM (n.s.a.), lower than April’s 0.4% MoM value. On a seasonally adjusted basis, the increase was at 0.2% MoM, down from the 0.4% MoM increase recorded in April.

Core services (excluding energy) remained the primary driver of underlying inflationary pressures. In May, core services increased by 3.4% YoY, slightly above April’s 3.3% YoY reading, while core goods (goods excluding food and energy) experienced an increase of 1.1% YoY, in line with the 1.1% YoY rise observed in April.

*Chart 2* illustrates the contrast between core goods and core services inflation. After a prolonged period of restraint, goods inflation contributed positively to overall inflation in 2025, a shift likely linked to dollar weakness and recent tariff measures. Apparel inflation in particular continues to firm, consistent with tariff pass-through, rising 4.8% YoY in May. Core services inflation has eased substantially from its peak and remains broadly anchored in the low-3% range, with May showing a modest tick higher to 3.4%.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!VDSb!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5723d449-4696-4a3a-b551-45c6aea593f2_2534x1540.png)

As for past reports, CPI in May was primarily driven by the energy component, with the housing component playing a secondary role (*Tables 1 and 2*). The rent of shelter components rose by 3.3% YoY in May, in line with the 3.3% YoY increase recorded in April. The shelter component increased by 0.3% on MoM (s.a.), down from April’s 0.6% MoM reading.

Within this context, supercore services (core services excluding shelter – *Chart 3*) increased by 3.7% YoY, above April’s 3.5% YoY reading. On a monthly basis, the supercore component increased by 0.5% MoM in May, in line with the 0.5% MoM value observed in April.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!aO4Y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F58d62e40-42bd-4b68-be08-b1596d19d636_2499x1540.png)

The rent subcomponent of shelter rose 2.9% year-on-year, above the 2.8% YoY increase recorded in April, while owners’ equivalent rent increased 3.3% YoY, in line with April’s 3.3% YoY figure (see *Chart 4*). At the sub-index level, both measures continue to show gradual easing, which remains a supportive development for the medium-term inflation outlook. As noted in previous reports, this underlying improvement in rent components could help offset delayed inflationary effects on the goods sector from tariffs, though near-term uncertainty has risen materially given the energy shock.

**Summary & Policy Implications**

The May CPI release reinforced the distinction between headline inflation and underlying inflation dynamics. Headline CPI increased to 4.2% YoY from 3.8% YoY in April and rose by 0.5% MoM (s.a.), reflecting continued support from higher gasoline prices, transportation-related costs, and broader energy-sensitive components following the Middle East energy shock. Core CPI edged up to 2.9% YoY from 2.8% YoY in April—largely a base effect, as the monthly core pace slowed to 0.2% (s.a.)—with the residual firmness concentrated in a relatively narrow set of categories rather than becoming generalized across the consumer basket.

Within core inflation, tariff-sensitive goods categories continued to show signs of pass-through from higher import costs, particularly in apparel and selected consumer goods, while transportation services remained exposed to elevated fuel prices. By contrast, shelter inflation and broader core services inflation remained substantially below their post-pandemic highs and continued to provide an important offset to emerging goods-sector pressures. Taken together, the May inflation profile suggested that the transmission of higher energy and import costs into consumer prices remained visible but incomplete, with limited evidence that the recent inflation shock had yet broadened into a sustained economy-wide inflation cycle.

The May release also provided a cleaner read on underlying inflation trends than the April report. The temporary shelter-related distortions associated with the October 2025 government shutdown have now largely passed through the CPI calculation process, allowing policymakers to focus more directly on the evolution of core services inflation, tariff-sensitive goods prices, and transportation-related categories. While underlying inflation remains above the Federal Reserve’s target, the data continued to point toward a more selective and sector-specific inflation impulse than the headline figures alone might suggest.

From a monetary policy perspective, the May inflation report represents one of the final major data releases ahead of the June FOMC meeting and the first inflation report of Kevin Warsh’s tenure as Federal Reserve Chair. Recent Federal Reserve communication has emphasized that the current inflation overshoot reflects a combination of higher energy prices and tariff-related cost pressures rather than excessive domestic demand. The May data broadly support that interpretation. The key policy question going forward is therefore not whether inflation has risen, but whether energy and tariff-related shocks begin to generate broader second-round effects through wages, services inflation, and inflation expectations.

Looking ahead, incoming data on inflation expectations, labor-market conditions, shelter inflation, and tariff-sensitive goods categories will remain central to the policy outlook. If inflation pressures remain concentrated in energy-sensitive and import-sensitive sectors, policymakers may continue to view the current episode as predominantly supply-driven. A broader transmission into underlying inflation categories, however, would increase the likelihood that restrictive policy settings need to remain in place for longer than currently anticipated. The June FOMC meeting and updated Summary of Economic Projections will therefore be particularly important in assessing how the Committee, and its new Chair, intend to balance elevated inflation against signs of moderating economic momentum.
