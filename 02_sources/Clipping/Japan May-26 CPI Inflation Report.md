**Key takeaways**

- Japan Consumer Price Index (CPI) increased by 1.5% year-on-year (YoY) in May, higher than April’s 1.4% YoY reading but marginally below the market forecast of a 1.6% YoY increase.
- CPI excluding fresh food, the Bank of Japan’s preferred measure, held at 1.4% YoY in May, unchanged from April’s 1.4% YoY increase and in line with the consensus forecast of 1.4% YoY.
- Food inflation is still high at 3.5% in May, unchanged from the 3.5% YoY recorded in April. Fresh food inflation jumped to 3.5% YoY in May, a sharp acceleration from the 0.3% YoY increase recorded in April. By contrast, rice prices have swung into annual deflation, falling 4.9% YoY in May after a 0.6% rise in April, as last year’s extraordinary surge drops out of the base — even though price levels remain historically high.
- Goods inflation has pulled further ahead of services, widening the recent gap. The goods sector accelerated to 2.0% YoY in May (from 1.7% YoY in April), while services edged up to 1.0% YoY (from 0.9% in April). Energy base effects and firmer industrial product prices drove the goods pickup, while services remain the more stable underlying component.
- Service inflation excluding imputed rents edged up to 1.3% YoY in May, from April’s 1.2% YoY increase, still well below its peak above 3% in late 2023.
- Japan’s May CPI release sits against a more hawkish BoJ policy backdrop, with the Bank continuing to judge that underlying inflation is rising gradually toward 2% and that the wage–price mechanism has become increasingly established. The near-term inflation picture remains shaped by softer food-price momentum and contained energy pressures, while the BoJ is placing greater weight on broader pass-through risks from business-to-business prices, wages, and inflation expectations. The earlier rise in crude oil prices linked to Middle East tensions has already reached intermediate stages of production, and the key issue for the consumer basket is now the extent of pass-through into retail prices, partly offset by government energy-burden relief measures.
- At its 15–16 June meeting, the Bank of Japan raised the overnight call rate to 1.0%, marking its highest policy rate since 1995. The move passed by a 7–1 majority. The decision reflected the central scenario of underlying inflation moving toward 2%, the decline in downside risks from the Middle East, and the increased need to avoid falling behind the curve as upside inflation risks rise. In its Outlook scenario, the BoJ sees core CPI accelerating clearly above 2% on energy before easing back toward target. The next policy decision will be taken at the 30–31 July meeting, which includes the July Outlook Report.

**Review of the Inflation Release**

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!SPR2!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Ff24f173b-1d31-4685-a4ce-9f81511b82de_2534x1540.png)

In May 2026, consumer prices increased by 1.5% year-on-year (YoY), above the 1.4% YoY increase recorded in April. On a month-on-month basis (MoM), prices rose by 0.5% MoM in May, above April’s 0.3% MoM increase.

Regarding core measures, the Bank of Japan tracks two main core inflation metrics (*Chart 1*): The Consumer Price Index (CPI) excluding fresh food, and the CPI excluding fresh food and energy.

The first measure, which excludes fresh food, came in at 1.4% YoY in May, in line with market expectations of a 1.4% YoY rise, and unchanged from the 1.4% YoY increase recorded in April. On a month-on-month (MoM) basis, May’s figure rose by 0.5% MoM, above the 0.3% MoM increase recorded in April.

The second core measure, excluding fresh food and energy, eased to 1.8% YoY, just below the 1.9% YoY increase recorded in April. On a month-on-month basis, these core prices rose by 0.2% MoM, after a 0.1% MoM decline in April. Energy prices have been a key factor for the CPI in recent releases; energy inflation remained in negative territory, with prices down 2.5% YoY in May, a smaller decline than April’s 3.9% drop as the annual drag narrowed, even as energy prices rose 3.4% MoM, reflecting the pass-through of higher crude prices.

While goods price inflation has been the primary driver of overall inflation during Japan’s recent inflation surge, the latest release shows goods reasserting their lead over services (*Chart 2*). In May, goods CPI increased by 2.0% YoY, up from the 1.7% YoY increase observed in April. Services CPI also firmed, rising to 1.0% YoY from April’s 0.9% YoY figure.

On a month-on-month basis (MoM), goods inflation rose by 0.7% MoM in May, following April’s 0.9% MoM increase. By contrast, service prices rose by 0.2% MoM in May, rebounding from April’s 0.5% MoM decline.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!8gcD!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fbeeb0dd4-e80a-46b6-a271-f49c30e2625f_2534x1540.png)

Further emphasizing Japan’s unique position, the shelter component (“imputed rents”) of the CPI increased by 0.4% YoY in May, unchanged from the 0.4% YoY rise recorded in April. The “imputed rents” measure represents “the rent a person would have to pay to own and occupy a property”, which constitutes the largest part of the CPI’s housing component and weighs about 16% of the overall CPI. For a detailed breakdown of Japan’s housing and rent components, refer to Tables *1* and *2*.

In Tables *1* and *2*, we provide a more detailed breakdown of the components of CPI, including CPI inflation excluding imputed rents (*Chart 4*), which we consider the HICP-equivalent CPI. This measure facilitates international comparisons across countries and blocs, a methodology developed by the European Union Statistical Office. In May, HICP inflation rose by 1.7% YoY, above April’s 1.5% YoY figure. As *Chart 4* shows, HICP inflation peaked at the beginning of 2023, reaching just above 5%. In contrast, before the pandemic, HICP inflation fluctuated between 0% and 1% year-over-year.

**Summary & Policy Implications**

Headline CPI and core inflation in May sit against a more hawkish BoJ policy backdrop. The Bank continues to judge that underlying inflation is rising gradually toward 2%, with its central scenario still placing the achievement of a level broadly consistent with the price stability target from the second half of FY2026 into FY2027. At the same time, the policy discussion has shifted: the BoJ is no longer focused only on whether inflation reaches 2%, but also on the risk that underlying inflation overshoots the target as wage and price-setting behaviour becomes more entrenched.

The May release reinforces the tension between softer near-term CPI momentum and a stronger forward-looking inflation assessment at the BoJ. The Bank’s preferred interpretation rests less on the latest headline print and more on the persistence of underlying pressures. Wage formation remains central to that assessment: base-pay increases are running around the mid-2% range, scheduled wages are rising in the high-2% to around 3% range, and the mechanism through which wages and prices rise while influencing each other has become increasingly established amid labour shortages. This keeps the wage–price channel at the centre of the inflation outlook, even as some goods and energy components remain volatile.

The inflation outlook is shaped by three forces pulling in different directions. First, the food-price impulse that drove much of the 2024–25 cycle is moderating, reducing the contribution from earlier price shocks. Second, energy remains the main upside risk: the earlier rise in crude oil prices linked to Middle East tensions has already reached intermediate stages of production, and the BoJ is monitoring the extent of pass-through into consumer prices. Third, the wage–price channel remains operative, with firms now more active in setting wages and prices and with medium- to long-term inflation expectations continuing to rise. As inflation moves closer to 2%, the BoJ is placing greater weight on the second and third forces than on the fading food impulse.

The central risks to the inflation outlook are exchange-rate developments, the durability of the easing in Middle East tensions, the degree of crude-oil pass-through into consumer prices, and the pace of government energy-burden relief measures. Yen depreciation has become more important for the inflation outlook because firms are now more willing to pass higher costs into selling prices, and exchange-rate movements are more likely to affect inflation expectations than in the past. Lower oil prices and continued fiscal relief contain near-term headline pressure, while stronger pass-through from import costs, wholesale prices, and wages keeps the upside-risk channel active.

On the policy front, the June meeting marked a clear step in the BoJ’s normalisation process. The rate hike to 1.0%, with a 7–1 majority, was justified by the central scenario of underlying inflation moving toward 2%, the decline in downside risks from the Middle East, and the increased need to guard against falling behind the curve. The Bank is now actively adjusting the degree of monetary accommodation as inflation approaches target, and reiterated that it will continue to raise the policy rate and adjust the degree of accommodation in line with economic, price, and financial developments. Its Outlook scenario projects core CPI accelerating to a level clearly above 2% as higher crude feeds through to energy and goods prices, before declining back toward target as the oil impulse fades. The next key checkpoint is the July 30–31 Monetary Policy Meeting, followed by the July Outlook Report and the post-meeting communication, which will provide the next update on the balance between softer current CPI data and the BoJ’s stronger assessment of underlying inflation risks.
