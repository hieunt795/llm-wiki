The widening spread between the 10-year gilt and the Treasury counterpart has drawn much comment, with many in the market blaming Britain’s fragile fiscal position, and many concerned that the November 2025 Budget had front-loaded spending and back-loaded tax increases.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!5Piy!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fd7fccff3-4d0c-459b-94c3-542f8631b968_608x458.png)

However, the spread has been widening for six years, so we don’t need to attribute it to particular events. The era of QE drove yields to unusually low levels across the board, but especially in the UK, distorting relative rates. Part of the rise in the gilt-Treasury spread over recent years is due to normalization, as this note will explore more fully below. The spread is now back in the range seen before the GFC. We should be careful not to over-extrapolate the trend now that normalization may be more or less complete.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!oGCr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F958f8ed7-2d6e-4c8b-840b-40a2e90c0d24_1788x896.png)

**Sterling appreciation challenges the fiscal credibility story**  
A challenge to the fiscal-credibility (or lack thereof) explanation for the widening spread is the behavior of GBPUSD. The debacle over the Truss/Kwarteng “mini budget” in 2022 shows that a fiscal credibility event manifests as a jump in gilt spreads and a sharp fall in sterling’s value against the USD. However, since 2023, the widening gilt spread has accompanied a strengthening of sterling against the dollar, not a depreciation. That pattern suggests that other, non-fiscal, forces explain the recent widening of the spread, with a relative trend rise in UK real rates since 2022, as a process of normalization, being a likely candidate.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!4Xp3!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F547d1f66-b8e3-47b5-9531-59b24acbf9f2_1818x912.png)

**US budget projections are less favourable that the UK’s  
**Moreover, if we compare the debt and deficit projections from the CBO and the OBR, the UK’s fiscal prospects are in some ways less worrisome than those in the US, though they remain troubling; we should also remember that the OBR tends to be overly optimistic. The UK’s projections show a declining budget deficit and debt-to-GDP stabilizing, whereas the US has a deficit continuing at about 6% of GDP and a rising debt burden.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!g2ik!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F534f400c-a88e-4a43-b761-13639f689679_752x452.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tWnn!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F66e1982d-a6e7-437a-9f70-bea7d02cda86_752x452.png)

**The monetary explanation for the spread  
**The coincidence of recent upward trends in the gilt spread and GBPUSD, both symptoms of tighter conditions, points to relative monetary policy as the likely explanation for the spread.

The next two charts strongly suggest it is the case. They show the relationship between the 10-year spread and the 2-year spread; the 2-year reflects not just current monetary conditions but also the expected policy stance over the next 24 months. The first chart illustrates a persistent relationship between the two over twenty years, while the second focuses on the most recent period. The current 2-year spread reflects the market pricing in that the UK’s Bank Rate may rise over the balance of the year, whereas in the US, the expectation priced in is for unchanged fed funds rates, with a chance of a cut.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!LYzs!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F37e3e356-c5b5-450c-a5a1-b4b7359a85d4_1800x865.png)

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!1QWI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1b78caf1-b418-4892-b768-6d2ab18a02ac_1788x854.png)

**The short-end, heavily influenced by monetary policy, drives the 10-year spread  
**Using weekly data, regressions show that the 10-year spread moves strongly with the 2-year spread, both immediately and over the following few weeks. The front end appears to lead the long end, but not one-for-one; roughly speaking, a 1-point widening in the 2-year spread is associated with about a 0.7-point cumulative widening in the 10-year spread over the next 12 weeks, about 0.5 of this coming in the first week, and most of the rest in week 2.

**Inflation drives policy, drives the 2-year, drives the 10-year  
**The 2-year spread reacts to policy developments and to expected policy, with inflation as a key driver. UK core CPI inflation rose later and less sharply than in the US in 2021. Its plateau lasted longer, and its decline set in later than in the US; UK inflation is stickier downward than in the US. While the US and UK core rates declined over the last six months, the extent was greater in the US. Over the last six months, average core inflation in the UK has been 0.6pp higher than in the US; the most recent spread between UK gilts and US Treasuries is just over 50bp.

A nominal spread that broadly reflected expected inflation differences would be consistent with the established finding that there is a common factor behind real interst rate developments in advanced countries.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QkOU!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F90bfecfa-ad54-4e3e-abdf-a1d445af4336_752x452.png)

**Real rates and breakevens  
**Examining the split between real rates and breakevens in the gilt and Treasury markets helps explain why the nominal UK spread has been widening for years and, for a different reason, why it has widened recently.

**UK real rates were massively distorted downwards**  
The era of ultra-low rates in the UK drove real interst rates to as low as -3%, whereas Treasuries troughed close to zero in 2020. While the Bank of England’s QE was similar in size to the Federal Reserve’s relative to GDP, its impact on gilt yields, nominal and real, was more pronounced, reflecting the smaller market, heavier duration exposure, and the UK’s large stock of index-linked debt.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!QBSe!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F242a6856-45cb-4c2b-b070-0dbef2072b81_719x451.png)

When UK policy rates began to normalize in December 2021, and the US followed in March 2022, with both countries’ balance sheet operations shifting in 2022, the real rate in both countries began to rise. Since UK rates had been suppressed much more than in the US, this process of normalization has narrowed the UK-US real interest rate spread to basically zero, which is where one would expect it to be, given the gravitational pull of a common global real interest rate. As an aside, the chart helps us understand why the Truss/Kwarteng mini-budget had such an adverse effect on gilts: it came at a time when real yields were already in a sharp period of adjustment from massively distorted levels, akin to throwing fuel on a fire.

**The Iran war has spiked UK breakevens  
**The start of the Iran/Israel/US war has seen US breakeven rates rise slightly to around 2.4%, which remains slightly below last summer's levels. In the UK, however, the 10-year breakeven has risen from around 3% at the turn of the year to 3.5% at the end of March, close to the level in spring 2024, thereby unwinding the gradual decline in breakevens in the intervening period.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!atGI!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F5a4610b3-b654-40a8-8009-2f1d391c2f7c_752x452.png)

The behavior of the UK breakeven since late February tells us that the market views the shock to UK inflation as both significant and persistent, to a greater extent than in the US. Important reasons for this expected substantial effect are that: the structure of the UK energy market makes it particularly vulnerable to energy price shocks, pushing up not only oil and natural gas prices but also electricity prices; sharper rises in energy prices trannsmit more fully into broader prices; indexation of the minimum wage and welfare benefits fully transmit price shocks into the bottom end of the wage structure, with spill-overs into other wages; and the Bank of England lacks credibility, meanin that inflation expectations are not well-anchored.

**Conclusion  
**Commentators and investors are rightly concerned by the UK’s fiscal position. However, the US fiscal outlook is no better and, indeed, in some respects worse than in the UK. Moreover, the behavior of GBPUSD relative to gilt yields is not consistent with the hypothesis that fiscal worries are driving the gilt-Treasury spread wider. An important part of the widening spread over recent years is normalization, whereas the fiscal story would lead to UK real rates above a justifiable level. If that were the case, UK real rates would be well in excess of those in the US, not on a par with them.

The far more convincing argument is that, in addition to the normalization of real rates, the gilt-Treasury spread largely reflects differing expectations of relative UK and US short-term interest rates. Shifts in relative rate expectations reflect the market’s view that inflation in the US and UK will respond differently to a global price shock. This argument is supported by movements in breaks over recent months. The transmission of oil price shocks into domestic inflation is more substantial in the UK and is likely to last longer than in the US - that’s what the wider gilt spread tells are the inflation and rate consequences of the war. Thus, the gilt-Treasury spread is likely to remain wide given the likely inflation gap and could expand further, depending on how the price shock emanating from the war in the Gulf evolves.
