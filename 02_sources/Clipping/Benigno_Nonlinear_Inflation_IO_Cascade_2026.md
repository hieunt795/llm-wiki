# Benigno — Nonlinear Inflation / IO Cascade Series

Independent analyst substack, not peer-reviewed. Treated as RAW-CLIP (has citations and a defined methodology, unlike undocumented opinion) but confidence capped — single-author, self-described short-sample threshold, one uncited internal study. See caveats section below before citing any specific figure as established fact.

## Part I — "The Return of Nonlinear Inflation" (~April 2026)

**Core claim:** Large supply-chain disruptions (Middle East war → Strait of Hormuz closure) create nonlinear inflation risk beyond the initial price spike, via two amplifiers:
- **Structural (IO cascade):** energy costs sit upstream in multi-stage production networks; when buffer capacity (inventory, pricing flexibility) is exceeded, cost increases compound through each stage rather than being absorbed.
- **Behavioral (wages/expectations):** persistent, salient disruptions cause workers to demand compensation and firms to build cost expectations into pricing — conditional on tight labor markets and prior inflation experience (both present in 2026).

**Propagation pipeline (qualitative, no numbers given in this piece):** "PPI moves first, fastest, and most significantly... CPI follows [BLS/CPI]... core inflation responds last, most slowly, and most persistently." **No quantified lag estimate is given anywhere in the series** — this is asserted as a stylized fact.

**Historical illustration:** 2021 used-car CPI spike (>1/3 of monthly CPI increase from a 3.5%-weight category) reframed as the downstream terminus of a semiconductor-shortage IO cascade, not an idiosyncratic category shock — the "transitory" call missed the multi-node cascade.

**Backing claim — flagged as uncited:** references "research with my former FRBNY colleagues Ozge Akinci and Hunter Clark, and Marius Koechlin (Univ. Lausanne)" studying inflation response to supply shocks across 22 advanced economies, 2000-2024, concluding large shocks produce "qualitatively different dynamics," not just proportionally larger ones. **No paper title, journal, or working-paper link given anywhere in the piece — cannot be independently verified. Treat as an asserted, uncited claim of the author's own prior work.**

**GSCPI:** NY Fed's real Global Supply Chain Pressure Index — freight rates, airfreight costs, delivery times, order backlogs, PMI surveys across the 7 largest trading economies, extracted to a common signal (standard deviations from historical mean). March 2026 reading: 0.68σ, up from 0.54σ in Feb, highest since early 2023 — still well below pandemic peak (4.47σ in 2021).

**Why oil futures are an insufficient statistic:** markets treat crude price as sufficient for gauging inflation impact, missing (a) cumulative production-network costs (rerouted cargo, delayed inputs), (b) geographic price differentials (TTF gas, LNG re-routing) not in crude markets, (c) the cascade continuing to propagate even after crude normalizes.

**Policy view:** monetary tightening is a "blunt instrument" against a supply shock (compresses demand while economy absorbs a terms-of-trade hit); prefers fiscal support (household energy relief) to reduce wage-setter salience and second-round risk.

## Part II — "The Return of Nonlinear Inflation?" (~June 2026, discusses April 2026 data)

**Mechanism, more precise:** BLS PPI Table D "Intermediate Demand by Production Flow" — 4 stage indexes (Stage 1 raw/upstream → Stage 4 near-final), each tracking **inputs consumed** at that stage (not outputs). Stages are explicitly **not nested** — commodities like diesel, electricity, steel appear across multiple stages at different weights.

**Diagnostic metric:** Stage differential Δ(t) = Stage-1 YoY − Stage-4 YoY.
- **τ* ≈ +3pp** proposed threshold for "genuine IO cascade vs. energy noise" — author's own words: **"the sample is relatively short... indicative rather than a robust statistical test... a classification and confirmation device, not a forecasting tool."** Below τ*, "a positive gap typically reflects energy price volatility rather than a genuine supply-chain cascade."
- **r ≈ 0.81** correlation between the gap and GSCPI, **conditional on gap > τ*, over Nov 2010–Apr 2026 sample, 5-6 month lead/lag** — NOT an unconditional relationship across all regimes.

**April 2026 figures (his reported, YoY):** Stage1 +9.4% / Stage2 +7.1% / Stage3 +5.9% / Stage4 +5.4% → **gap +3.5pp** (crossed τ*). Key inputs: diesel +74%, crude petroleum +62%, jet fuel +104% (Stage-1-adjacent components).

**No CPI figures or CPI-PPI comparison given anywhere in this piece** — entirely a PPI-internal diagnostic.

**Framing point:** contrasts with ECB/BoE frameworks (per his read of March 2026 staff analysis) that "anchor analysis to wage dynamics" — argues supply-chain cascades can operate independently of wage-price spiral mechanics, deserving separate monitoring, not that markets are currently misreading CPI vs PPI specifically.

**5-point monthly monitoring protocol:** (1) compute stage differential from BLS Table D, (2) compare Stage 3 vs Stage 4, (3) isolate energy sub-components (diesel, crude), (4) track transportation/warehousing (airfreight), (5) monitor core-PPI-proxy acceleration above 3.5% y/y when gap exceeds threshold.

## Part II Update (~early July 2026, discusses May 2026 data)

**Update to the two diagnostic flags:**
1. Stage gap **jumped from +2.0pp to +5.8pp** (crossing τ*≈+3pp with margin) — his +2.0pp reference point corresponds to March 2026 per independent reconstruction (see companion clipping `US_CPI_PPI_PCE_H1_2026_BLS_BEA.md`).
2. **Stage 3 (7.2%) now exceeds Stage 4 (6.5%)** — "the first such reversal since 2022."

**Key point — price/physical decoupling:** Brent down ~18% from March peak, YET the cascade continues: **Hormuz Transit Stress Index at +3.7σ, an all-time high** — vessel transits collapsed from ~80/day to 4/day, tonnage from 4M to 0.1M tonnes/day, **flatlined for three months**. Physical disruption has not resolved even though the price signal has partially reversed.

**Explicit criticism of Fed/consensus forecasting:** "Conditioned on energy futures, they will mechanically build in disinflation as oil rolls back out of the twelve-month window — precisely as prices mean-revert while the chokepoint stays shut." States this repeats the 2021 "transitory" error — mistaking commodity price mean-reversion for supply-chain resolution.

**Sectoral diffusion (narrower than 2021):** diesel +106%, crude +78%, jet fuel +175%, plastic resins +19.5%, industrial chemicals +16.1%, truck freight +17.3%, freight forwarding +29.4%, processed materials ex-food-energy +7.8%, core final demand +5.1%. Concentrated in the energy-petrochemical-freight corridor — "core manufactured goods remain far cooler" — stage **depth** exceeds breadth, unlike 2021's broad-based +11.0pp stage gap and 4.47σ GSCPI.

**Author's own caveats, verbatim/near-verbatim:** magnitudes "well below COVID extremes" (+5.8pp vs +11.0pp; GSCPI <2σ vs 4.47σ); "the threshold rests on a short-sample estimate"; falsification conditions given as explicit: watch whether Stage 3 continues to outpace Stage 4, and whether Hormuz transits remain disrupted.

## Independent Verification (performed 2026-07-03, see companion clipping)

Pulled BLS Table D series directly (WPUID51/52/53/54, cross-checked BLS API vs FRED mirror after learning from an earlier FD-ID series-ID mis-fire). Reconstructed the full Jan-May 2026 gap trajectory: +0.16pp → +0.63pp → **+2.07pp (Mar)** → **+3.53pp (Apr)** → **+5.73pp (May)** — matches his cited reference points (+2.0, +3.5, +5.8) within ~0.1-0.3pp at every checkpoint he names. Stage 1 May MoM (+3.23% computed vs his "+3.2%") and Stage 3 May YoY (7.20% computed vs his "7.2%") match almost exactly. April Stage 2 diverges more (9.02% computed vs his 7.1%) — plausibly a revision-timing artifact (BLS revises PPI up to 4 months post-publication). **This upgrades the core stage-gap claim from single-source to independently-reproduced against primary data — but the τ*≈+3pp threshold's predictive value and the uncited 22-country study remain unverified author claims, not independently confirmed findings.**
