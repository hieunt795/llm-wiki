*The world’s three most important central banks – the Fed, the ECB, and the Bank of Japan – all left their policy interest rates unchanged this week.* *Their policy action going forward will be shaped to a significant extent by their respective credibility – or at least it should.*

The supply shock from the war in the Middle East presents a test of **credibility** for central banks and their policy frameworks. Supply shocks have the nasty property that they force central banks to trade off growth for inflation. As I told [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/ecb-set-to-hold-interest-rates-as-it-bides-time-on-iran-response?utm_medium=email&utm_source=author_alert&utm_term=260430&utm_campaign=author_23762579) last week, how much leeway central banks have to “look through” (not respond) to the surge in energy prices depends on the anchoring of inflation expectations. In turn, this depends on the accumulated credibility of the central bank.

## Fed: don’t lose it

Rather than the decision to leave rates on hold, the most important takeaways from the FOMC meeting were Powell’s announcement that he would stay on the Board, and the three hawkish dissenters who wanted to drop the easing bias (“additional adjustments to the … federal funds rate”). Given that already on the eve of the war US inflation was 3% (on the Fed’s preferred measure), the *risk of a loss of credibility* as inflation rises further looms large, and this will have driven both Powell’s desire to stay on and the dissenters’ views.

Christopher Waller – an FOMC member who didn’t dissent – has [framed it](https://open.substack.com/pub/thinicemacroeconomics/p/wallers-warning?r=1oa8fn&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) well: there’s only so many price level shocks (tariffs, energy) you can look through (while inflation remains above target). I noted [recently](https://thinicemacroeconomics.substack.com/p/wallers-warning) that the Fed’s stance going forward is difficult to read as economic uncertainty is compounded by uncertainty about its reaction function on account of the arrival of Kevin Warsh as Chair – a man who, it is reasonable to suppose, has made promises to the White House to lower interest rates. I find it difficult to escape the conclusion that the dissenters are putting the incoming chair on notice that they wouldn’t want the Fed’s credibility to be damaged.

Overall, then, the Fed’s posture appears to be “work in progress”: it’s on hold not just in terms of fed funds, but even more so because it awaits the arrival of the new chair. Many intelligent observers see in the hawkish dissent a prelude to the easing bias being dropped in June. I personally struggle to see Warsh coming in and removing it in his first meeting.

## ECB: use it

No such issues at the ECB, whose communication this week was as clear as it could be. The ECB is on a highway to hike (albeit with an offramp), validating market expectations of an interest rate increase in June: the posture was that the default outcome for the next meeting is a hike, unless in the meantime the conflict ends. While the communication in the March meeting and the Watchers conference suggested a preemptive attitude leaving many expecting an April move, the press conference sounded more reactive – albeit leaving no doubt about the ECB’s determination to stay on top of inflation rather than chasing it.

In a way, there is a paradox here. If anything, the ECB’s (anti-inflationary) credibility is the highest of the three central banks. Inflation was on target on the eve of the war, few doubt the ECB’s determination to return it to target now that it has jumped, and while the economy has been resilient recently, it is more vulnerable as the euro area is major importer of hydrocarbons.

A jump of inflation expectations such as the one we saw in March (see the Chart above) – and there’s likely more to come in April – probably means that the ECB will feel compelled to send a signal by hiking rates. As President Lagarde said in March: “the public may find it difficult to understand a reaction function that does not react”. There’s probably a fair amount of scarring from the late response of 2022 here.

Be that as it may, while I think there’s even a good chance of a second hike, I think that would be about it – even in the scenario where the Straight of Hormuz doesn’t reopen soon. What would make me change my mind? Evidence of second round effects. In the meantime: I still think one hike is more likely than three; and the ECB will have to reverse the hikes in the course of next year. The ECB has cred: it should use it.

## Bank of Japan: the price of lost credibility

… is $34.5 billion (or JPY 5.4 trillion) – that appears to have been the cost of Thursday’s [MoF intervention](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/japan-likely-spent-34-5-billion-in-fx-intervention-to-boost-yen?utm_medium=email&utm_source=author_alert&utm_term=260501&utm_campaign=author_15081083) to support the yen. This, to me, is the cost of Japan’s [policy](https://open.substack.com/pub/thinicemacroeconomics/p/takaichi-needs-to-restore-policy?r=1oa8fn&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) *[framework](https://open.substack.com/pub/thinicemacroeconomics/p/takaichi-needs-to-restore-policy?r=1oa8fn&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web)* – both monetary and fiscal policy – lacking credibility. The media made a big deal of the BoJ’s “hawkish hold” last week, and in particular of the remarkable three dissents in the 6-3 decision to hold rates. What I see is an attempt to substitute FX intervention for rate hikes.

Now, I did argue a while ago that Japan should [use its FX reserves](https://open.substack.com/pub/thinicemacroeconomics/p/the-honest-central-banks-guide-to?r=1oa8fn&utm_campaign=post&utm_medium=web) more actively – but what they’ve done is not what I meant. At around one trillion dollars, Japan has an enormous war chest, but FX intervention cannot be used to make up for the lack of credibility of the central bank or of the broader policy framework.

The bigger problem is that the BoJ remains [subject to pressure](https://bojwatchtower.substack.com/p/boj-fails-the-people-on-inflation) from the government. Contrary to the Fed, where the three dissents carry some hawkish information about future policy, that is not necessarily the case for the BoJ dissents for as long as the Takaichi administration doesn’t clear the BoJ’s path to hike rates. It is those political pressures that made me call the yen a **[palindrome](https://thinicemacroeconomics.substack.com/p/six-for-26)** (“back-and-forth”) in my year-ahead piece last-December: I thought that the chances are the BoJ’s stop-and-go normalization would continue, leaving the yen vulnerable and volatile.

While the Takaichi administration is forcing a dovish response to the oil shock on the BoJ, the ultimate result is likely to be the worst of both worlds: along with the inevitable hit to growth, inflation will be high. There’s more cost coming up from the loss of credibility.
