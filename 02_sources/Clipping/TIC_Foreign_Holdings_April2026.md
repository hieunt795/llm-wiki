## Key Structural Observations

**1. Euro Area overtook Japan as top holder**
Euro Area now at $2.0T vs Japan $1.24T. This shift reflects: European pension/insurance demand for UST as high-quality collateral; also may include ECB reserve management and EU sovereign wealth instruments.

**2. Japan still buying (+$14B MoM, +$113B YoY)**
Despite BoJ raising rates (end of ultra-easy policy), Japan life insurers and GPIF remain net buyers of US duration. Yen hedging costs factor: when JPY hedge expensive, unhedged UST demand rises.

**3. China continued structural reduction**
-$96B YoY, down >1/3 from 2015 peak (~$1.2T). China diversifying from UST: gold, other currencies, commodity-linked assets. Not a sudden "dump" but sustained strategic reduction. Impact on term premium: removes one source of captive long-duration demand.

**4. UK $897B — custody hub distortion**
UK figure inflated by London-based custodians holding UST on behalf of global investors. True UK sovereign demand much smaller.

**5. Official vs Private divergence (March)**
Foreign CBs net selling ($-14.9B) while private sector buying (+$111.4B). Pattern: official sector managing FX reserves (selling UST when USD strong or needing to intervene); private sector attracted by yield levels.

**6. Total foreign holdings $9.49T**
Out of total marketable debt ~$28.5T → foreigners hold ~33% of marketable UST. This is structural demand floor but not captive. Moody's downgrade (May 2025, to Aa1) has not triggered measurable selling in H1 2026 data.

## Basis Trade Footnote
WolfStreet cites Fed analysis that Cayman-domiciled hedge fund UST holdings undercounted by ~$1.4T due to custodial reporting. When adjusted, actual foreign holdings approach ~$11T. The basis trade (buy UST / short UST futures) is concentrated in Cayman-domiciled funds — this matters for systemic risk: if forced unwind (like March 2020), basis trade unwinding = sudden UST selling pressure not captured in TIC headline.
