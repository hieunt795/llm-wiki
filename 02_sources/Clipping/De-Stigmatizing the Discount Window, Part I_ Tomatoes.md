*The views…*

The matter of the discount window [^1] reform is receiving some attention from yours truly, though I have yet to find someone to thank me.

As I was parsing speechesnotespodcastsacademicarticles [^2], there is no question that the reluctance by banks to access the discount window for liquidity purposes is a key issue. Why?

Stigma, many say. Those who currently access the discount window do so because they have no other alternatives. Would I, suddenly transformed into a bank with abundant reserves, consider lending to a recent borrower from the discount window? Not at market rates, trust me.

Would I then, suddenly further transformed into a bank in need of reserves, borrow from the discount window? Maybe I can get by today by paying someone quite a bit above market—and above the discount window rate —thereby avoiding higher market rates for the foreseeable future.

This closed loop—we call it an equilibrium—perpetuates the stigma.

Abandoning my secret identity as a depository institution, I wondered how one would go about de-stigmatizing. And my mind decided to recall…

## In Jeopardy, circa XVIIth century…

“This plant is more pleasant to the sight than either to the taste or smell, because the fruit being eaten provoketh loathing and vomiting.” [^3]

“What is a *tomato?*”

Yep, the delicious red tomato that is the basis of some of the most popular dishes on the planet, pizza and pasta and everything your kid only eats if submerged in ketchup, as well as [the best way to dress bread on the planet](https://www.barcelonaturisme.com/wv3/en/enjoy/8/the-sacred-principles-of-pa-amb-tomaquet.html), period.

Yep, tomatoes were stigmatized in Europe for a long time.

Yep, they are not anymore. So, what happened?

## The Poison Apple

So it turns out the Evil Queen tricks SnowWhite with… a tomato?

The Spanish introduced tomatoes to Europe, which is to say, the *fun* part of Europe. Both in Spain and Italy, tomatoes were well received. The Italians still call it to this day, the golden apple— *pomodoro*. Spain has a [world-famous tomato fight](https://www.latomatinatours.com/) —as you may have heard, Spain is different.

Unfortunately, things up north went quickly south for the tomato. As horticulture was a survival skill, not a hobby, many noted that tomatoes are nightshade plants—and hence, poisonous.[^4] Nightshade berries can look like tomatoes, which were substantially smaller back then.

![NOT. A. TOMATO.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!GPZA!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F768551cc-4f73-4281-af88-50cbb285284d_560x436.jpeg)

NOT. A. TOMATO.

Unfortunately, we are still in the XVII and XVIII centuries, so JS Mill is not yet around to tell us not to assume that similar things have similar properties.

But then a barber turned botanist by the name John Gerard somehow managed to write the definitive botanical guide in Great Britain and call tomatoes “of ranke and stinking savour” and “corrupt.” Cue tomatoes surviving only as a decorative plant for centuries.

## De-Stigmatization

I would still advise against snacking on a tomato used for decorative purposes—it well could be an actual nightshade berry!

But clearly nobody is scared of tomatoes anymore. What happened to the stigma?

Quite discouragingly, de-stigmatizing took technological improvements in the production, storage, and use of the tomato, as well as the U.S. Civil War. Let me briefly document those before attempting to heroically and categorically map tomatoes back to the discount window:

- **Canning**, invented in the early 1800s, boomed to feed the Union army. Tomatoes grew quickly, survived the canning process beautifully, and became a massive military staple… as well as a now-acquired taste—demand for tomatoes would continue to grow after the war.
- **Better tomatoes**, meet the “Paragon,” which his creator, Alexander Livingston, sold as “the first perfectly and uniformly smooth tomato ever introduced to the American Public.”
- **Pizza**, partly as a placeholder for the plethora of delicious uses found for tomatoes, partly to make fun of my daughter, who does not like tomatoes but loves margaritha pizza. Also, pizza!

I am unsure whether the booming demand for the US Civil War transformed or simply accelerated the changing perception of tomatoes. In any case, enough time has been spent on the tomato.

## So, Roc, should we now serve pizza at the discount window?

It wouldn’t really hurt. You wouldn’t believe the events I went to as a hungry PhD student for a free slice of pizza. And I wasn’t alone.

More seriously, it took technologies, in plural, *and* only one of those innovations arguably with tomatoes in mind, and a large event, definitively without tomatoes in mind, to remove the stigma.

And, mind you, tomatoes *are* delicious.

So there is a mountain to climb to de-stigmatize the discount window.

But the first order of business **must** be to amplify the hard work the Fed has *already* put in to improve the discount window, of which the result is [Discount Window Direct](https://www.frbdiscountwindow.org/generalpages/dwd_main) ( this [post](https://www.frbservices.org/news/fed360/issues/070125/central-bank-discount-window-direct-quick-easy) is an easier read, even if some of my colleagues do not seem to know where the discount windows are).[^5] Some of the process improvements are too technical for me to explain; that is, I should understand them better—I am allergic to parroting. I also want to highlight the outreach, improved support, and assistance that my colleagues (who know where the discount windows are) have done in the last few years.

And then there are the terms. Since March 2020, the primary credit rate has been set at the top of the FOMC's target range for the federal funds rate. That is to say, strictly less than 25 basis points. Currently, 11 basis points. It has never been cheaper to access the discount window:

FRED: National Treasure to all but a nit.

Also, updated, generous collateral [margin tables](https://www.frbdiscountwindow.org/articles/2025/06/27/19/47/margins_announcement_20250701) since last summer.

And then there is more to come. The Fed posted a “Request for Information” in the Register (already closed, sorry), and a lot of excellent feedback has been received; it is being processed, and I am confident it will lead to further improvements.

I will leave aside the improvements that come with its integration in the liquidity framework more widely. They are indeed the most important, but they take us to the heart of the *reform* debate—and that will have to wait after the FOMC blackout, at the very least.

Will these or even the full wish list of technological improvements be ***sufficient*** to de-stigmatize? I doubt it, but they are ***necessary*** to do so. In the same way that the track better be downward-sloping if I am to run a mile under 10 minutes.

And, then, what about the large event—a transformative event that, accidentally, ends stigma? I am sorry to report that I was *there* in 2007-8. Banks were *begged* to use the discount window and lead by example. And to no avail, as [shown](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr483) by more colleagues who know where the discount windows are.[^6]

## Colimit

I hope you enjoyed learning about the history of tomatoes because, frankly, I came back empty-handed from the time machine.

De-stigmatizing the discount window will not be easy.

Which we already knew.
