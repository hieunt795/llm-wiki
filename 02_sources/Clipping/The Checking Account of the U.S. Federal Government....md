A long time ago in a job far, far away, I would occasionally be asked to explain the Fed balance sheet in one sentence. My go-to used to be that the Fed is the Bank of Banks, and the balance sheet is where the banks’ checking accounts live. Of course, I would rarely stick to the constraint; I do believe the Fed balance sheet merits more than a sentence. But my talking more is not equal to anyone listening more, so circa late 2010s, I switched to a more verbose but more accurate one-liner:

> The Fed is the Bank of banks, the Bank of the Treasury, and the Bank of anyone who has a buck.

And now I had a hook.[^1] In case it worked: Nope, you cannot have an account at the Fed as an individual. The last line is a reference to currency in circulation: Every U.S. dollar is a liability in the Fed balance sheet—and thusly a liability of the U.S. federal government.

Before the dance of balance sheet liabilities that is to come, here it is an attempt to offer an executive summary or TLDR:

- The Federal Reserve is the Treasury’s fiscal agent, and as such, it hosts the Treasury General Account (TGA) as a liability in the Fed’s balance sheet.
- The TGA competes 1:1 with reserves. The observed increases in both level and volatility in the TGA have contributed to balance sheet growth.
- This arrangement is just one of many possible; other arrangements have existed, and cooperation with the Treasury in this matter is well documented.

You are forgiven if you are not fascinated yet. As the last bullet point hints at, there are existing proposals to undo some of the Fed’s balance sheet growth by modifying or complementing the existing arrangement. This matter may require your attention.

## Fed Liabilities

The Fed’s balance sheet, like all other balance sheets since Renaissance Venice, has assets and liabilities, and they are always equal.

It has some semblance to a bank balance sheet: instead of deposits, it has reserves. And instead of depositors, it has depository institutions—let’s call them banks. Reserves are a liability of the Fed, and thus reside in the liability side of its balance sheet.

And there are assets on the asset side. That’s it, end of the semblances between balance sheets. In everything else, the Fed, like my native country, is different.

In addition to the reserves, the Fed’s liabilities also include the U.S. Treasury’s checking account—the TGA. I will return to this from the governance perspective below. For now, note that since reserves and TGA both sit on the liability side of the Fed’s balance sheet, banks or banks’ depositors can pay the Treasury easily, and vice versa. Indeed, both ends of the transaction must be dollars: **Changes in the TGA lead to an opposite change in reserves**. The TGA is one of the autonomous factors I mentioned in past articles, but did not explain. That is, until the Fed needs to maintain ample reserves. Then changes in the TGA are matched by changes in assets, and the Fed balance sheet grows.

The next liability is both the coolest and the dullest on the Fed balance sheet.[^2] Yep, every buck, currency, greenback, tender, moolah, bill, bone… is a liability of the Federal Reserve, and hence (1) lives in the Fed balance sheet, and (2) is also a liability of the U.S. government—indeed, it is *the* legal tender in the U.S.

Note that reserves and currency are a liability of the Fed and the U.S. federal government. To keep things confusing, the TGA is a liability of the Fed but an asset of the Treasury.

## The Fed is the Treasury’s Fiscal Agent

[The Fed (the Reserve Banks, to be precise) serves as the fiscal agent](https://www.federalreserve.gov/paymentsystems/fisagy_about.htm) for the Treasury, managing the TGA and processing daily financial transactions, facilitating the auction, issuance, and redemption of marketable securities and U.S. savings bonds, and much [more](https://www.frbservices.org/treasury-services).

Being the Treasury’s fiscal agent is not about monetary policy or Fed independence. The right mindset here is that the Treasury is the customer, and the customer is always right. Of course, we provide support and guidance, are happy to work together, and coordinate in many matters, with open channels for communication.

Let me hammer (the point home):

> The Federal Reserve Banks provide a range of services to the U.S. government, including financial and securities services, application development, and technology infrastructure support.
> 
> *Board of Governors of the Federal Reserve System*

> The Treasury Department authorizes Federal Reserve Banks, as fiscal agents of the United States, to perform all activities necessary to carry out the provisions of this part, any auction announcements, and applicable regulations.
> 
> *Treasury Auction Regulations (31 CFR Part 356.3)*

## dTGA = - dReserves and dTGA = dAssets

Either you knew, or you just learned that (1) both reserves and TGA are liabilities of the Fed balance sheet, and (2) the Treasury is the principal and the Fed is the agent in an operational relationship. Oh, and assets equal liabilities. Here it is, all the math needed in one line:

$A s s e t s = R e s e r v e s + T G A + C u r r e n c y$

Say a bank transfers someone’s tax payment to the Treasury, just now: a credit for the Treasury, dTGA>,0 and a debit for the bank, dReserves<0. The Fed’s assets continue their pre-set path, be that of QE, QT, or QN; but, critically, it is not responding to the transfer: dAssets = 0. Currency has no reason to move unless you are [Nick Stafford from Cedar Buff](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-us-canada-38603615), Virginia. Thusly, dCurrency = 0.

That is, every bank transaction with the Treasury obeys dTGA = - dReserves. The size of the Fed balance sheet remains unchanged.

That is, it does until it does not, and that is when the Fed needs to adjust its assets to “maintain reserves at ample levels” because the change in the TGA is large and persistent enough to induce a shortage (or an over-abundance) of reserves. Indeed, in the long run and abstracting from growth, reserves *must* stay ample, which means dTGA = dAssets. The size of the Fed balance sheet increases one-to-one with the TGA.

Recapping:

- Every transaction, every day, between Treasury and the private sector effectively involves dTGA = - dReserves.[^3]
- Over time, if dTGA /= 0, perdures, the Fed needs to maintain reserves at ample levels, and dTGA = dAssets.

Once this is understood, it will be a walk in the park to show that both an increase in the level and/or an increase in the volatility of the TGA map into a larger balance sheet. Click on the footnote for an additional analogy not involving a car.[^4]

## And contributed to the Fed’s balance sheet growth

Both the level and volatility increase in the TGA explain some of the growth in the Fed’s balance sheet.

**Level**. Taking point-to-point (in time) over at least a quinquennium, we can safely work with dTGA = dAssets. If only everything were so easy! As shown below, the increase in the TGA contributed about 3 percentage points of nominal GDP to the Fed balance sheet since 2007 (and a bit more than 1 pp since the end of 2019, not shown):

So clearly the TGA is not the gorilla in the room. But it is not a capuchin monkey either.

**Volatility**. A bit more subtle, as the effects on rate *control* are very asymmetric. With that said, it is not hard to see that the Fed needs a buffer of reserves so that, at all times, no increase in the TGA balance sends reserves into scarcity via dTGA = - dReserves—particularly, it doesn’t do it so fast that the Fed would need to hurry to deploy dAssets = - dReserves = dTGA to keep reserves ample. As it happened in September 2019.

And it is hard to **over** state the volatility in the TGA balance week-to-week, day-to-day, hour-to-hour. You can see it on the time series figure above—and that is a two-week average![^5] But you can also eyeball that some of the largest swings were *drops* in the TGA balance. Yep, those are periods of “emergency measures” by the Treasury as it tried to navigate the debt ceiling.

In San Francisco, I went with a guess-estimate of at least $100 bn. reduction in the buffer needed, if the TGA were to return to the volatility prior to 2020.[^6] I think that I was a bit too conservative; it could be more. In any case, that amount is not a game-changer.

## A changeable and changing arrangement

Hopefully, I have done a decent job explaining how the TGA and the Fed currently operate.[^7]

The last point of the day: this arrangement is a choice. It has operated differently in the past—very differently, actually. Prior to 2008, the Treasury kept its balances mostly in private banks. In 2015, it announced a minimum of $150 bn. at the TGA. It now forecasts $850 bn. for the next quarter, last time I checked.

Which is to say, the arrangement with the Fed can change, and it may revert some of the balance sheet growth. And indeed this is what [Annette Vissing-Jorgensen](https://sites.google.com/view/annette-vissing-jorgensen/home) is proposing: [paper](https://drive.google.com/file/d/1F3xs5AmgZjbxI_potdG7UFJpUwLWSsch/view?usp=sharing) and [shorter note](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/fluctuations-in-the-treasury-general-account-and-their-effect-on-the-feds-balance-sheet-20250806.html). There are other proposals, but Annette’s is the one yours truly discussed in San Francisco—that’s what I call synergies.[^8]

Further detail? Any detail. I do postpone the actual proposal content to another day and simply hammer, again: The TGA is the result of an operational relationship between Fed and Treasury, and it is a changeable and changing arrangement.
