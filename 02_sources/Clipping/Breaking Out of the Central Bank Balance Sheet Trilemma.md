*The views expressed here are mine and do not necessarily reflect the views of the Federal Reserve Bank of Philadelphia or elsewhere in the Federal Reserve System.*

My dear colleagues at the Board, [Burcu Duygan-Bump](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/burcu-duygan-bump.htm) and [R. Jay Kahn](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/r-jay-kahn.htm),[^1] recently offered, in their words,

> a framework for understanding the tradeoffs involved in determining the optimal size and behavior of a central bank’s balance sheet

in the Fed Note, ["The Central Bank Balance-Sheet Trilemma](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/the-central-bank-balance-sheet-trilemma-20260114.html#:~:text=The%20trilemma%20highlights%20that%20a,to%20lead%20to%20rate%20volatility)," which I urge you to read, even though I will eventually find quite a bit to disagree with.

In short, what they chose as the keystone of their proposed framework is a trilemma for the central bank, which

> can simultaneously achieve only two of the following—a small balance sheet, low short-term rate volatility, and limited intervention in money markets

In case words do not suffice, the authors made it starkly visual:

## Is This the Right Framework?

Yes, and no, no, and no.

## Yes

In its current configuration, with the tools and practices and regulations and systems in place now… Yes, the Fed balance sheet cannot shrink any further unless we are willing to tolerate more interest-rate volatility or heavy-handed market intervention.

I like to call it the *local* view, implicitly assuming that the system's global configuration remains unchanged. I agree with the authors that, in the local sense above, the trilemma is right. But I disagree that the trilemma is the right framework to advance the debate on the balance sheet.

(For a lengthy discussion on policy trilemmas and their logical problems, see [my other post.](https://rocarmenter.substack.com/p/false-trilemmas) )

---

Instead, the right framework should be open to changing the *global* variables and parameters that have us locked at the current balance sheet size.

Fortunately, these changes are possible and, wait, there is more, many changes come with benefits of their own—a windfall, in some cases—and do not require large (read, costly) endeavors. Now, those changes are not without risk, and it is uncertain how effective they will be in reducing the balance sheet.

## No

The first change to consider, and possibly the only (relatively) low-hanging fruit, is to change how the Treasury General Account (TGA) is managed.

[Annette](https://sites.google.com/view/annette-vissing-jorgensen) has the most completed [analysis and plan](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/fluctuations-in-the-treasury-general-account-and-their-effect-on-the-feds-balance-sheet-20250806.html), [Bill Nelson](https://bpi.com/wp-content/uploads/2026/01/Testimony-of-Bill-Nelson-Striking-the-Right-Balance-Sheet.pdf) has been testifying to Congress, [Governor Waller](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/waller20250710a.htm) has talked about it, and it is step 2 of the 4-step plan by [David Beckworth](https://www.barrons.com/articles/warsh-fed-balance-sheet-cutting-how-he-could-do-that-6f2cc6e5). I also happen to have (to be public soon) work on this, and will be discussing Annette’s paper in 48 hours.

The short of it is, there are possibilities to reduce the level and volatility of the Treasury’s general account, which currently brings opposite movements in reserves and thusly requires a large buffer to stay “ample” at all times. By my napkin math, the balance sheet could be reduced by up to 2 pp of Nominal GDP—far from the entirety of the surge in the balance sheet, but also more than charm-shrinking.

The authors view these proposals as more than “limited market intervention.” Now I have a bit of a beef with that corner, because at times it seems to refer to how often the Desk will need to operate, and at times to the amount of Fed lending.[^2] One corner of the trilemma cannot become “everything else you can think of.”

## No

While the contribution of the TGA is non-negligible, there is no question that the main driver of the Fed's balance sheet growth has been the demand for reserves.

![In both dates we are pretty certain that demand drove the supply chosen by the Fed.](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!fy_y!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F291ec1f1-0cba-4d6b-bd67-dfe0f3a33253_1593x1192.png)

In both dates we are pretty certain that demand drove the supply chosen by the Fed.

What’s to be done about the demand for reserves? Understanding how we got here will help us get back *there*. In my view, we can focus on two non-competing explanations for the surge in the demand for reserves: (1) supervision and regulatory changes, as for example articulated by former Governor Quarles in [2018](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/quarles20180504a.htm); and (2) the super-abundant supply of reserves by themselves, as most prominently argued by [Viral Acharya and coauthors](https://www.kansascityfed.org/Jackson/%20Hole/documents/9666/JH2022_Acharya.pdf).

The pathway here would leverage existing efforts on supervision and regulation, based on the first hypothesis above. We are in a good position, as there is now a lot of work understanding how regulation has shaped the demand for reserves. There are many options to temper the benefits of holding reserves and to improve the flow of reserves across banks. The latter should not be underestimated.

In my view, if reserves do not flow where they are needed, banks aim for self-insurance, at least at short horizons, and build large buffers that drive up the aggregate demand for reserves. Moreover, the Fed is left with only two bad options: supply reserves in such abundance that no pockets of scarcity form (\`\`ample for all’‘), or pursue targeted interventions. Thus, a primary or secondary objective of supervision and regulatory changes could be to enable banks to better redistribute their reserve balances.[^3]

An important reason for optimism is that many regulatory initiatives are warranted in their own right, with a reduction in the demand for reserves as a downstream benefit, albeit of uncertain magnitude. For example, improvements in the Treasury market could lead to reductions (again, likely modest) in the demand for reserves as banks are able to turn holdings into liquid funds faster.

As for hypothesis (2), and other versions of the “ratchet” theory of reserves, I have work to do before I pontificate. So, let me *hypothesize* that what went up can go down, that perhaps the demand for reserves should be put on a diet, and it may even work if we are afforded enough time.

## No

And, last but certainly not least, the implementation framework can change. Indeed, there are many dimensions to explore—and challenges in every direction. Starting with the distinction between rate volatility and rate control—that is a post by itself. Or the idea of using term repos (similar to the voluntary reserve targets from the Bank of England)—another post for another day.

Let me pull instead my usual brusque exit: My view is that rate control is paramount, but there are many valid and safe pathways to policy implementation. Enough options to subordinate the design of the operating framework to the desired balance sheet goals, not the other way around.
