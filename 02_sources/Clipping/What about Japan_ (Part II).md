## The hidden tax: who actually pays for Japan’s cheap money

In [Part I](https://substack.com/home/post/p-192908397), we looked at how Japan’s government pulled off a remarkable balance sheet trick: by using quantitative easing to borrow short at near-zero rates and invest long in risky assets, the consolidated public sector earned returns exceeding 6% of GDP above its funding costs for most of the past decade. The natural question is: who was on the other side of that trade?

The answer, it turns out, is Japanese households — and some more than others. During the low-rate era Japan has engineered a large wealth transfer: from the young to the old, from the financially unsophisticated to the wealthy, and from future taxpayers to current retirees. Understanding how requires a brief detour into balance sheet duration.

## A crash course in duration risk

Duration, in finance, measures how sensitive an asset’s value is to changes in interest rates. A bank deposit has near-zero duration — its value barely moves when rates shift. A government bond has moderate duration: a 10-year Treasury, for instance, has a duration of roughly 7 to 8 years, which is shorter than its maturity because coupon payments arrive before the final repayment date, pulling the weighted average of cash flows forward in time. Equities have by far the longest duration of the three — around 75 years in the Japanese case. The reason is that equity cash flows are not fixed: dividends are expected to grow over time. When you discount a stream of perpetually growing cash flows, the weight of the present value shifts far into the future, producing a very long effective duration. A small drop in real rates therefore causes a large rise in equity prices.

This ordering matters enormously for what follows. When real interest rates fall, the value of high-duration assets rises relative to low-duration ones. A depositor earns nothing extra. A bondholder gets a modest capital gain. An equity holder gets a windfall — because growing future cash flows, discounted at a lower rate, are worth far more today. Whoever holds equities wins; whoever holds deposits gets left behind.

Now look at where Japanese households actually keep their money.

Japanese households park the overwhelming majority of their financial wealth in bank deposits. The government, meanwhile, structured its consolidated balance sheet in the opposite direction: short-duration liabilities, long-duration assets. When rates fell, the government’s net position boomed in value. Households with their savings in deposits got nothing.

The contrast with the United States is striking. The two balance sheets are almost a mirror image of each other

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!xdfo!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F356b401f-7d4c-4e64-b075-0e7ce7b50a62_731x759.png)

QE itself sucks duration out of the market. When a central bank buys long-duration government bonds and pays for them by issuing short-duration reserves, it is extracting duration from the hands of private investors and replacing it with something that has almost none. It is not surprising, then, that Japanese households ended up holding so little duration: the BoJ’s decade-long asset purchase programme systematically stripped it out of the financial system, leaving households with a mountain of deposits.

## Who actually has duration — and who doesn’t

The aggregate picture masks enormous variation across the population. Using Japan’s 2019 National Survey of Family Income, Consumption and Wealth — around 90,000 households — we classify households into three groups based on whether they hold any securities beyond deposits.

The distinction matters because it determines everything about how a household fares when rates fall. 67% of Japanese households don’t participate in asset markets. They have no bond or stock holdings. Non-participants hold almost exclusively deposits and time deposits, which carry durations of zero and one year respectively. Participants hold equities — with an effective duration of around 76 years — and their portfolio duration is consequently an order of magnitude higher. The chart below makes this concrete.

Now consider what this means when rates fall. [Every household has a “required duration” — the duration of its future consumption needs, which must be financed out of savings.](https://thetwocents.substack.com/p/who-gains-and-who-loses-when-rates) For a non-participant under 35, that required duration is around 7 to 9 years. But their asset duration is a fraction of one year. The gap between the two is what financial repression exploits: rates fall, the cost of future consumption rises in present value, but deposits earn nothing extra. We calculate that a 1 percentage point decline in real rates costs a median non-participant under 55 between 6% and 9% of their total wealth. The same rate decline hands a participant aged 55 or older a gain of 17% to 25% of their wealth — because their long equity position appreciates far more than their remaining consumption needs increase.

*Two thirds of Japanese households hold near-zero duration at every stage of life. The same policy that stabilises public finances costs the typical saver a tenth of their wealth for every percentage point of financial repression.*

The divide tracks age and income in a compounding way. Participation in the stock market increases with both — only 16% of all households hold equities directly, rising to just 28% among the top income quartile. So older, wealthier households are doubly advantaged: they are more likely to be participants, and even if they are non-participants, they have less remaining consumption to finance. The youngest, lowest-income non-participants bear the largest losses. The welfare effects of financial repression are, in our assessment, regressive.

## Financial repression as policy, not accident

Japan’s government has a strong structural incentive to keep real rates low, because its consolidated balance sheet has negative net duration, because it borrows at floating rats invests in long-duration assets. Lower rates mechanically expand fiscal space. Higher rates erode it fast.

The same forces that created Japan’s aging demographic structure — the promises made to older generations, the accumulated pension obligations — created the government’s incentive to maintain low rates. Policy and demography reinforced each other. But the effect on younger households is no less real for being structural rather than deliberate.

We estimate that the return on government debt runs at least 197 basis points below what the risks on its balance sheet would warrant — a tax on depositors worth nearly 2% of GDP per year. This is the classic definition of financial repression: policies that channel savings into government debt at below-market rates, implicitly taxing savers to benefit the sovereign. Japan’s version runs through QE, reserve creation, and yield curve control rather than blunt capital controls — but the distributional outcome is the same. The fiscal math works because depositors — the non-participant majority — absorb the cost.

Japan is now slowly unwinding these policies. The BoJ has raised rates from effectively zero to 0.75%, and since December 2022 the 10-year JGB yield has risen more than 200 basis points — almost exactly the no-QE counterfactual we modelled. We conclude that the Japanese government will struggle to harvest net returns in excess of its funding costs going forward. Part III will be about what the unwind costs, and who — once again — ends up paying for it.
