**Key takeaways:**

- China’s Consumer Price Index (CPI, n.s.a.) increased by 1.2% year-on-year (YoY) in May, unchanged from the 1.2% YoY increase recorded in April, and below the consensus forecast of a 1.3% YoY change.
- Core CPI (excluding all food and energy) eased to 1.1% YoY in May, down from the 1.2% YoY increase recorded in April.
- Consumer goods inflation rose by 1.6% YoY in May, a higher increase than April’s 1.4% YoY increase. Service inflation eased to 0.8% YoY, slightly lower than the 0.9% YoY increase recorded in April.
- Food inflation remained in negative territory in May, with food prices falling by 1.7% YoY after the 1.6% YoY decline recorded in April. The continued weakness mainly reflected sharply lower pork prices, although higher egg prices and firmer vegetable prices provided a partial offset.
- Transport and communication costs remain in positive territory, recording a 5.4% year-on-year increase, following a 4.6% increase observed in April. The main driver within this category was fuels for transport, which rose by 21.1% YoY following a 17.4% YoY increase in April, reflecting the surge in global crude prices amid Middle East tensions, only partially cushioned by domestic fuel-price smoothing measures.
- Producer Price inflation (all items) strengthened further in May, rising by 3.9% YoY, following the 2.8% YoY increase in April, and slightly above the consensus forecast of a 3.8% YoY increase. The print marked the strongest PPI reading in 46 months, driven by higher upstream costs in energy, non-ferrous metals, and chemical raw materials. On a monthly basis, PPI rose by 0.5%, easing from the 1.7% MoM increase recorded in April, as the monthly impulse from oil and gas mining and refined-petroleum prices reversed even as non-ferrous metals and chemical raw materials continued to rise.
- Overall, the May data point to a sharper upstream inflation impulse alongside a flat headline for consumer prices, reinforcing the divergence between producer-side cost pressures and still-contained domestic consumer-price momentum. The headline held at 1.2% YoY mainly because a high year-on-year energy base and firmer consumer-goods prices offset deeper food deflation and a post-holiday pullback in services, rather than reflecting broad-based demand strength.
- Taken together, the May inflation profile underscores that China’s inflation impulse remained concentrated upstream, with PPI rising to its strongest level since July 2022 while the CPI held flat, supported narrowly by a high energy base rather than by holiday services, which fell back after the Labour Day holiday. On the consumer side, the post-Labour Day normalisation in travel pushed service prices down on the month, while the transport-energy component stayed firm in year-on-year terms on a high energy base even as domestic gasoline prices edged lower month-on-month. On the producer side, the Middle East energy shock and higher imported commodity costs continued to feed into petroleum, chemicals, plastics, metals, logistics, and other upstream industrial prices, underscoring that the May inflation impulse remained primarily cost-push rather than demand-led.

**Review of the Inflation Release**

In May 2026, consumer price inflation increased by 1.2% year-on-year (YoY), unchanged from the 1.2% YoY increase recorded in April, and below the consensus forecast of a 1.3% YoY increase (see Chart 1). On a month-to-month basis (MoM), consumer prices fell by 0.1% MoM, compared to April’s 0.3% MoM increase and slightly above the consensus forecast of a 0.2% MoM decline.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!INwQ!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F46754d48-8d4f-4d24-b443-b4bd0c06ff08_2573x1540.png)

Core inflation (excluding food and energy prices) eased to 1.1% YoY in May, lower than the 1.2% YoY increase observed in April. On a month-over-month basis, core inflation fell by 0.1%, compared with April’s 0.2% MoM increase.

A relevant split that is provided at the level of China’s release is the distinction between consumer goods and services. In May 2026, consumer goods rose by 1.6% YoY, a higher increase than the 1.4% YoY increase recorded in April. On a monthly basis, consumer goods fell by 0.2% MoM, compared to the 0.1% MoM increase observed in April.

Service inflation eased to 0.8% YoY in May, lower than April’s 0.9% YoY reading. On a monthly basis, service inflation fell by 0.1% MoM, following the 0.5% increase recorded in April, reflecting the seasonal pullback in travel after the Labour Day holiday.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!q3KT!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F1a5b2803-36b0-438e-a26d-81110b5a09a4_2534x1540.png)

In the case of China, we also report producer price indexes. Indeed, given China’s prominence in global manufacturing, producer prices provide a good indicator for thinking about global inflationary pressures at the goods level.

We report three main categories: All items, Means of production, and Consumer goods.

In May 2026, producer prices remained in positive territory. Producer prices for all items increased by 3.9% YoY in May, higher than April’s 2.8% YoY increase, and came in slightly above the consensus forecast of a 3.8% YoY increase. On a monthly basis, PPI rose by 0.5% MoM, marking a third consecutive monthly increase but easing by 1.2 percentage points relative to the previous month, as the monthly impulse from oil and refined-petroleum prices reversed.

Producer prices (Means of Production) rose by 5.2% YoY in May, compared with the 3.8% YoY increase reported in April, pointing to a pronounced strengthening in upstream price dynamics. Mining and quarrying prices rose by 15.8% YoY in May, compared with the 10.6% increase recorded in April.

Finally, in May 2026, producer prices (consumer goods) declined by 0.8% YoY, following the 1.0% YoY decline recorded in April, continuing to narrow gradually but remaining in negative territory, consistent with still-subdued domestic consumer demand. The contrast across the PPI’s by-use structure was stark: prices rose 15.8% YoY at the mining and quarrying stage and 9.2% in raw materials, but only 2.3% in processing and declined by 0.8% for consumer goods, illustrating how the upstream commodity and energy impulse attenuates — and ultimately reverses — as it moves downstream. Within consumer goods, food prices fell by 1.8% YoY, and both clothing and articles for daily use declined by 1.0%, while durable consumer-goods prices edged up to flat (0.0%) from a 0.3% decline in April, the first tentative sign of the AI- and metals-driven cost impulse reaching the consumer-durables stage.

**Summary & Policy Implications**

The May inflation release reinforced the split between a stalling consumer-price recovery and a stronger producer-side cost impulse. Headline CPI held at 1.2% YoY, unchanged from April, and fell by 0.1% MoM, compared with April’s 0.3% monthly increase. In year-on-year terms, the headline was held up by a high energy base, with gasoline up 23.5% YoY, and by firmer consumer-goods prices, while on the month, both gasoline prices and holiday-sensitive services reversed as travel normalised after the Labour Day holiday. Food prices, by contrast, remained a drag on the headline, deepening to a 1.7% YoY decline, while core inflation eased to 1.1% YoY, indicating that the flat headline continued to reflect energy base effects rather than a broad-based strengthening in household demand.

The underlying consumer-price profile remained soft. Core CPI eased to 1.1% YoY in May, compared with 1.2% YoY in April, and service inflation eased to 0.8% YoY. Although services activity was supported by the Labour Day holiday early in the month, the broader demand backdrop remained fragile, and services prices fell back on a monthly basis as the holiday boost faded. One narrow exception came through the AI channel, where prices for communication equipment, including smartphones, rose by 7% YoY, up from 4% in April, and home-appliance prices firmed, reflecting higher chip and metals costs rather than a broad pickup in consumer demand. This combination suggests that household demand improved at the margin but remained insufficient to generate sustained pass-through into core CPI or broad services prices.

By contrast, producer-price dynamics remained the more important signal from the May inflation release. PPI rose by 3.9% YoY, following the 2.8% YoY increase recorded in April and reaching its strongest reading since July 2022. The upstream impulse continued to reflect higher imported energy, petrochemical, metals, and raw-material costs, with the Middle East energy shock feeding into petroleum processing, chemicals, plastics, logistics, and broader industrial input prices. Two forces drove the upstream surge: the Iran-related energy shock and a domestic boom in AI-related investment, which lifted prices for non-ferrous metals, electrical machinery, and electronics, the latter rising at the fastest pace in the PPI’s by-industry data since 1996. Yet the same impulse failed to travel beyond the upstream stages of the production chain: producer prices for consumer goods remained in deflation, and the gap between producer and consumer inflation widened to its largest since June 2022, leaving downstream and consumer-facing firms absorbing higher input costs through compressed margins rather than passing them on. On a monthly basis, however, the impulse cooled, with PPI rising 0.5% MoM after April’s 1.7% gain as oil and gas mining and refined-petroleum prices reversed, indicating that the year-on-year acceleration increasingly reflected base effects rather than a fresh monthly surge. At the same time, the May PMI data suggest that the upstream price impulse remained present but was no longer accelerating at April’s pace. The official manufacturing PMI fell to 50.0 in May from 50.3 in April, while new orders declined to 49.9 from 50.6 and new export orders fell to 48.6 from 50.3, pointing to softer domestic and external demand. The raw-material purchase price index remained elevated at 60.5, down from 63.7 in April, and the ex-factory price index stayed above the 50 threshold at 51.9, down from 55.1. This indicates that input and output prices were still rising, but less rapidly than in April, pointing to a cost-push inflation impulse with limited demand-side pass-through.

From a policy perspective, the May inflation profile does not point to tightening, despite the further strengthening in producer prices. The PBoC left the 1-year LPR unchanged at 3.00% and the 5-year LPR unchanged at 3.50% for the 12th consecutive month in May, consistent with a cautious policy stance amid rising imported inflation risks. The PBoC’s first-quarter policy report explicitly flagged the need to monitor the impact of higher crude oil and commodity prices on domestic inflation, reinforcing the view that the current price impulse is mainly external and cost-push. At the same time, efforts to boost bank lending in May suggest that policymakers continued to prioritize credit support and domestic-demand stabilization. The yuan’s rise to a three-year high may have provided some marginal offset to imported price pressures, but the broader policy message remained one of targeted growth support alongside close monitoring of upstream inflation risks.
