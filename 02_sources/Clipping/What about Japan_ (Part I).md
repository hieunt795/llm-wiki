When I started working on fiscal sustainability in the US, and I started giving seminars on this topic, I noticed that there would always be an economist in the back of the room who’d raise his or her hand, occasionally with a big smile, and ask “What about Japan?” The undertone of the question was always: “what are you getting so worked up about?” In 2023, the Japanese central government had accumulated more than 2.0 times Japan’s GDP in debt. Total central and local government debt was 226% of GDP. “And Japan was doing just fine.” After a while, I started to get a bit annoyed with myself, because I didn’t know much about Japan, and I couldn’t come up with a sharp answer to this question. So I decided to work on this with my former UCLA colleague [Hal Cole](https://economics.sas.upenn.edu/people/harold-l-cole) and our former student, [Yi-Li Chien](https://www.stlouisfed.org/research/economists/chien), who’s at the St-Louis Fed. We wrote “ [What about Japan](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4620159)?” which we are about to present at the NBER Macro-annual in Cambridge, Ma. [^1]

We started by just looking at the central and local government debt. The Japanese central government’s cost of funding was actually higher than the growth rate of GDP over the past 3 decades. Its nominal growth rate was close to zero, while the government’s cost of funding was closer to 100 bps per annum. Japan was not just going to grow out of its high debt/GDP ratio. Japan was definitely not in [Blanchard’s](https://www.aeaweb.org/articles?id=10.1257/aer.109.4.1197) goldilocks region where government can run deficits indefinitely, and without any fiscal costs.

Instead, we decided to consolidate the central and local government with the public pension fund, the publicly owned financial institutions and the Bank of Japan. Ultimately, all of these entities are subject to a single budget constraint. To do so, we started to dig into the Japanese flow of funds. We quickly realized that the Japanese public sector was running a huge risky sovereign wealth fund. And Japan was funding these risky investments by borrowing at below-market rates.

The Japanese public sector was funding itself by issuing bonds, roughly 107% of GDP, but also by issuing reserves held at the BoJ by Japanese financial institutions, another 91% of GDP. When the BoJ buys back Japanese government bonds from banks as part of its large-scale asset purchases, it creates and wires the money into accounts held by the banks at the BoJ. Until 2024, the BoJ was operating a multi-tier system for bank reserves, but the bulk of bank reserves were earning zero interest.

As a result of QE, the Japanese public sector added a giant fixed for floating-swap with a notional of 91% of GDP to its balance sheet where its paying floating and receiving fixed. Total liabilities added up to 275% of GDP in 2023. So, the Japanese public sector has shortened the duration of its liabilities.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!iIxE!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F21e954a0-7373-48ff-8262-26952ad063d1_1440x1126.png)

The public sector then uses these borrowed funds to invest in long-duration risky assets. The asset side is dominated by loans (60.8%) — largely legacy lending from public financial institutions — but the strategically important part is the 95.3% of GDP sitting in risky assets: domestic equities and unhedged foreign securities. That's the sovereign wealth fund-like position that generates the excess returns over and above the government’s low cost of funding.

In 1997, the public sector was not taking on as much risk on the asset side. Domestic equities and foreign securities only added up to 18% of GDP. On the liability side, back in 1997, the government relied on cheap funding from FILP (47% of GDP in 1997). FILP stands for the Fiscal Investment and Loan Programme — essentially Japan’s “second budget,” a vast government-run financial intermediary that operated largely off the main fiscal accounts.

The basic mechanism was straightforward. Japanese households deposited their savings at the postal savings bank (Japan Post) and into public pension funds. Until 2001, those institutions were legally required to deposit those funds with the Ministry of Finance’s Trust Fund Bureau, which then channelled the money through FILP into government-directed lending. The postal savings bank was at its peak the largest deposit-taking institution in the world, and FILP’s loan portfolio exceeded 100% of GDP in 2001.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!dyi-!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F767021c0-aed8-49d1-aa78-c4cf4be5665f_1440x1126.png)

The 2001 reform broke this link. The postal savings bank, which uses to be the largest bank in the world, and public pension funds were no longer required to funnel deposits to the Trust Fund Bureau. FILP had to start raising money directly in bond markets instead — “FILP bonds” — like any other borrower. That’s why FILP deposits on the consolidated government balance sheet collapse from 46.4% of GDP in 1997 to just 1.6% by 2023, as you can see in the two balance sheet snapshots.

The BoJ’s QE programme — flooding the system with bank reserves from 2010 onwards — was in a structural sense the successor to FILP as a source of cheap government funding. The mechanism changed (reserves instead of postal deposits) but the logic was the same: the government found a way to borrow from the domestic financial system at below market rates. One form of more *market-friendly financial repression* replaced another.

The mechanics are elegant, if unconventional. Through quantitative easing and yield curve control, the Bank of Japan has flooded the financial system with bank reserves — essentially floating-rate, near-zero-cost liabilities. At end-2023, those reserves stood at 91% of GDP.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!tp83!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F961183ab-c2a3-499f-9abf-f97199225068_627x412.png)

Figure 2: Japanese Government Bond Holdings (excluding T-bills).

Meanwhile, the consolidated public sector has deployed the proceeds into long-duration, high-risk assets: domestic equities (39% of GDP) and unhedged foreign securities (57% of GDP). The result is a classic carry trade, just executed by a sovereign. Borrow at the short end of the curve at rates the BoJ controls. Invest at the long end — and in global equity markets, adding a currency carry trade, funded in yen. Pocket the spread. The government earned an annualised excess return of 4.66% above its funding costs over the decade from 2013 to 2023. This added up to 6.25% of GDP between 2013 and 2023. In a counterfactual exercise without QE and with currency hedging, we show that these excess returns disappear.

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!44R5!,w_1456,c_limit,f_webp,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F94bd9037-0c66-4a81-98ec-e7244b68f41e_856x1048.png)

The public sector has saddled Japanese taxpayers with lots of interest rate risk. Japan is funding risky, long-duration investments with short-term floating-rate debt, while government surpluses are far off in the distant future. As rates fall, this duration mismatch created lots of extra spending power for the government.

As bond yields have started to rise since 2024, the government’s funding costs are set to rise. For now, the BoJ has held its policy rate at 0.75%, which is also the interest on excess reserves. At the same the public sector will suffer capital losses on the asset side as valuations decline. I’ll write more about this in the second installment.
