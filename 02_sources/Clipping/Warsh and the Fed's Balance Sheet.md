Central bank **operating frameworks** – where the monetary policy rubber meets the financial markets road – are transitioning in many places as we are moving to a post-QE world. I will be writing a great deal in the coming weeks and months about these changes in the operating frameworks of the two most important central banks, the Fed and the ECB, so this piece is the inaugural one of an (irregular) series.

But in today’s post, I want to focus on a specific issue: the read-across from the size of the Fed’s balance sheet to the level of the fed funds rate.

If you haven’t been following, the ~~fracas~~ debate at the Fed (go to 52:00 of [this video](https://www.youtube.com/live/ARrKDX-CFlw)) about the size of the balance sheet is heating up – and will probably continue to do so the closer we come to Kevin Warsh’ arrival as chair. Warsh appears to have a strong opinion about shrinking the balance sheet – although what that means exactly remains to be seen.

In principle, there are a lot of legit arguments to shrink the balance sheet. They include:

- Reducing the Fed’s **footprint** in markets and the economy, not least with the aim of reducing price distortions in financial markets (since the aim of QE was to lower yields, it introduced “distortions” intentionally).
- **Monetary-fiscal interactions**: reduce the tight embrace between monetary and fiscal policy that large stocks of government bonds on the central bank balance sheet entail.
- Restoring “ **policy space** ” – i.e. if you accept that the central bank’s footprint shouldn’t be too large, then it makes sense to reduce it in case you need to expand the balance sheet again (in case of another large deflationary shock requiring renewed QE).

From reading reports about Warsh’s views, he seems to be partial to the first and second point above. But there seems to be an angle which is relevant for the level of the Fed funds rate. From a [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/business/finance/fed-chair-nominee-warsh-may-want-smaller-fed-holdings-thats-not-easy-do-2026-02-02/) article (paywall) referencing a Warsh WSJ op-ed:

> he wrote “the Fed’s bloated balance sheet.. can be reduced significantly”, with the proceeds redeployed “in the form of lower interest rates to support households and small and medium-sized businesses.”

I’m not sure what “the proceeds redeployed” means and it’s not a direct quote from Warsh. Taken literally I struggle to make any sense of it at all. Interpreted as a metaphor, it could mean that reducing the balance sheet would allow the Fed to cut rates, and from what I read out there that seems to be the prevailing interpretation.

The logic here would be as follows.

If balance sheet = assets bought, that refers to a certain amount of stimulus having been added to the economy (via QE). If you shrink the balance sheet, you take out stimulus – which you could then “put back in” via cuts in the fed funds rate. (An ancillary narrative goes that while the Fed purchases benefited the owners of the assets from whom the Fed bought, the reduction of fed funds would allow Main Street to benefit from the stimulus.)

There are a lot of loose ends to consider about whether this would actually work (yield curve segmentation and what happens to long vs short rates, what happens to bank lending, what happens to mortgages which are priced off the longer end of the Treasury curve etc.). But that’s not my point here.

One of the obstacles to shrinking the balance sheet is that there is *structural demand* for Fed liabilities. This shows up in market dislocations whenever Fed liabilities are reduced beyond a certain level ([Vítor Constâncio](https://open.substack.com/users/10211486-vitor-constancio?utm_source=mentions) has an excellent [primer](https://substack.com/home/post/p-180175157) on this). To be precise, whenever *reserve balances* (in many ways the most important of Fed liabilities) are reduced below 9% of GDP (or about $3 trillion currently), money market rates like SOFR spike.

Now, structural demand for Fed liabilities comes from many sources – and I’ll unpack these and what constraints they imply for the balance sheet in future posts. But the debate at the Fed has focused on two things ([David Beckworth](https://open.substack.com/users/8207880-david-beckworth?utm_source=mentions) has an excellent [overview](https://macroeconomicpolicynexus.substack.com/p/the-feds-overton-window-is-shifting?utm_source=post-email-title&publication_id=4309188&post_id=192349305&utm_campaign=email-post-title&isFreemail=true&r=1oa8fn&triedRedirect=true&utm_medium=email) in his latest post).

- that there are elements of **regulation** which force banks to hold more liquidity ( = reserve balances); and
- that the Fed’s *supply* of large amounts of reserves has contributed to liquidity *demand* on behalf of banks – or **[liquidity dependence](https://www.nber.org/papers/w31050)**.

The implication is that if regulation is relaxed, and if the Fed reduces its supply of reserves, then *structural demand for reserves is reduced*, and therefore the Fed can shrink the balance sheet without adverse effects for financial stability. So far, so good.

This would seem to set up Warsh for a slam dunk: with a smaller balance sheet, the Fed can cut fed funds. What’s not to like?

For one, whether relaxing regulation is itself desirable is debatable: regulation usually involves trade-offs between day-to-day dynamism and resilience when things go wrong. Reasonable people can disagree on the best place to come down in that trade-off.

But what I’m after here is the rate cut story. Remember that the idea the incoming Fed chair seems to espouse is that balance sheet reduction “frees up stimulus” which can be used to cut fed funds.

The problem with this line of argument in the present context is that it’s wrong. *When the Fed supplies reserves to satisfy **structural** demand, it isn’t stimulating the economy*. Hence *if the Fed shrinks the balance sheet because there is lower structural demand for reserves, it hasn’t reduced stimulus that it can then re-introduce by cutting fed funds*. That holds irrespective of whether the structural demand for reserves comes from regulation or the Fed’s supply of reserves as per the liquidity dependence argument.

There are reasons to worry about Kevin Warsh at the Fed: him likely having had to promise to the White House that he would [cut rates](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/trump-says-next-fed-chair-will-believe-lower-interest-rates-by-lot-2025-12-18/), and his apparent willingness to drop data dependence in favor of a wager on the productivity-enhancing effects of AI. If he really does espouse the view that balance sheet reduction is a way to deliver lower fed funds, then I’m even more worried.
