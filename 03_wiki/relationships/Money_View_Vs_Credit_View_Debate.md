## Thesis
The money view and credit view represent competing frameworks for understanding monetary transmission. Each generates different empirical predictions and policy implications.

## 1. Comparative Framework

### Money View (Neoclassical Channels)
| Dimension | Position |
|-----------|----------|
| **Financial Markets** | Perfectly efficient, no frictions |
| **Information** | Complete and symmetric |
| **Bank Role** | Passive conduit (transparent intermediary) |
| **Credit Supply** | Not a constraint; endogenously follows demand |
| **Interest Rate Pass-Through** | Mechanical: policy rate → retail rates → real economy |
| **Transmission Lag** | Determined solely by expectations and term structure |
| **Policy Distributive Effect** | None — uniform impact across sectors |
| **Amplification** | None — monetary policy is proportional |

### Credit View (Non-neoclassical / Post-Keynesian)
| Dimension | Position |
|-----------|----------|
| **Financial Markets** | Imperfect due to asymmetric information, moral hazard |
| **Information** | Incomplete; lender must screen and monitor |
| **Bank Role** | Active intermediary; credit supply is endogenous |
| **Credit Supply** | Constrained by bank capital, collateral, internal funds |
| **Interest Rate Pass-Through** | Non-linear: breaks down during stress or capital constraints |
| **Transmission Lag** | Depends on bank behavior, balance sheet cycles |
| **Policy Distributive Effect** | Yes — bank-dependent borrowers most vulnerable |
| **Amplification** | Yes — financial accelerator phenomenon |

## 2. Worked Contrast: Recession Scenario

### Money View Prediction
Policy rate cut → Long rates fall → Investment rises → Output recovers (smooth, proportional response)

### Credit View Prediction
Policy rate cut → But banks weak (low capital, high NPLs) → Credit supply rationed despite cheap funding → Investment remains depressed → Output falls more than predicted ("composition," "propagation," "amplification" anomalies)

## 3. Empirical Anomalies Driving the Debate

[RAW] The effect of financial frictions on the MPTM was observed by Bernanke and Gertler (1995), who found three anomalies in the aggregate demand response to changes in the interest rate:

1. **Composition Anomaly** — Short-term rate cuts affect long-term assets (houses) that *should* respond to long-term rates, not short-term rates. This violates money view expectations.

2. **Propagation Anomaly** — The real economy continues to respond after the short-term interest rate has been raised and even when the interest rate has been lowered. Lags are longer and more variable than the money view predicts.

3. **Amplification Anomaly** — Interest rate changes trigger *faster* and *larger* changes in output than warranted by the direct cost-of-capital effect. The financial accelerator amplifies shocks.

[RAW] In addition, there were indications of an asymmetric impact of monetary policy on real output and inflation between tightening and easing, which cannot be explained by the interest rate or exchange rate channels.

## 4. Policy Implications

**Under Money View (Policy is Simple):**
- Set policy rate based on inflation target and output gap
- Bank behavior, capital constraints irrelevant
- Quantitative easing (QE) only matters for term-structure expectations

**Under Credit View (Policy Must Account for Financial Cycles):**
- Monitor bank capital ratios, NPL trends, collateral values
- Distinguish between "supply-driven" and "demand-driven" credit slowdowns
- Macroprudential policy essential complement to interest rate policy
- QE transmission depends on which assets purchased and banks' balance sheets

## 5. Synthesis for Practice

[RAW] The implications for monetary policy are also different. The assumption of financial market efficiency per the money view implies that monetary policy does not have a distributive effect on the various segments of the real economy along with price setting. In contrast, financial frictions per the credit view cause the impact of monetary policy to vary by segment in the financial sector and real economy, depending on bank behavior and internal conditions, the lending relationship between lender and borrower, as well as risk-taking behavior among economic players.

**Modern Consensus:**
- Money view is useful for "normal times" baseline forecasting
- Credit view essential for crisis and structural break periods
- Most central banks now operate with *both* frameworks in parallel
- Macroprudential policy institutionalizes credit view insights
