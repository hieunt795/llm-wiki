## Thesis

Large-scale asset purchases (LSAPs) involve more than just the central bank and its direct bank counterparties. Because banks source assets from the private sector (funds, insurance), QE creates a mandatory expansion of the commercial banking system's balance sheet.

## Mechanism

### 1. The Intermediary Role
When the Central Bank (CB) buys a security:
1. The **Asset Manager (AM)** sells the security to the CB via a **Custodian Bank**.
2. The **CB** creates new **Outside Money** (reserves/CB deposits) for the Custodian Bank.
3. The **Custodian Bank** must create a matching **AM Deposit** (Inside Money) on its own liabilities side.

### 2. Mandatory Balance Sheet Lengthening
The banking system cannot "refuse" this expansion. Even though the bank now holds a risk-free CB deposit, it also carries a new liability to the asset manager. This forces the banking system to hold more reserves relative to its total size, potentially impacting leverage ratios and capital requirements.

### Transmission / Friction Costs

### The Repo Connection
If an asset manager is uncomfortable with the credit risk of a simple bank deposit (substituting a high-quality government bond), they may demand collateral. 
- **Effect:** This increases demand in the repo market. 
- **Consequence:** The "friction" of accommodating these massive new deposit claims explains why repo rates often decline (or even go negative) during QE programmes.
