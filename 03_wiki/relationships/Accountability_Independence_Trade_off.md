## Thesis

Can a central bank be "too independent"? If a bank is independent but not accountable, it lacks **Democratic Legitimacy**. De Haan et al. (1999) found that while legal independence and formal accountability often move in opposite directions, modern ITF regimes attempt to make them **Complements**.

## Empirical Findings (De Haan et al., 1999)

- **The Negative Correlation:** Countries with the highest legal independence (e.g., Germany, Switzerland) often have lower scores on formal, legal accountability indices.
- **The Ranking:**
    - **Highest Accountability:** Bank of England (UK).
    - **Lowest Legal Accountability:** ECB (though it practices higher "informal" accountability).
- **Rationale:** Highly independent banks feel less legal pressure to report, whereas banks with lower independence (like the UK pre-1997) used high accountability to "earn" their credibility.

## The Consensus: Democratic Accountability
Modern central banking theory argues that accountability is not a limit on independence, but its **Foundation**. 
1. **Independence** prevents time inconsistency (Episode 2 failure).
2. **Accountability** prevents "Incompetent Policymaking" and ensures the bank stays within its democratic mandate.
