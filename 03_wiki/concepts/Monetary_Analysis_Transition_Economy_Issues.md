# [[Monetary_Analysis_Transition_Economy_Issues]]

## Thesis

[RAW] Monetary analysts working with transition economies face special difficulties stemming from the structural and institutional characteristics of these economies. Standard monetary policy instruments and frameworks require significant adaptation.

## 1. Rapid and Jumpy Velocity Changes

[RAW] Many transition economies experienced **high and variable inflation** with accompanying large increases in the velocity of money. The increases occurred in **large, discrete jumps** rather than smooth trends. In extreme cases (Armenia, Georgia, late 1993), velocity rose as much as threefold in one quarter.

[RAW] Initial large velocity increases have generally taken a long time to reverse — real money balances remained at low levels for prolonged periods even after disinflation. Countries maintaining aggressive interest rate policy to ensure **positive real returns on domestic monetary assets** succeeded in restoring confidence and reducing velocity relatively quickly.

**Policy implication:** Money demand projections based on historical velocity averages are unreliable. Financial programming must use wide uncertainty bands around velocity assumptions and set intermediate targets that can be adjusted rapidly.

## 2. Lack of Competition Among Banks (Monobank Legacy)

[RAW] The shift to indirect monetary controls is hampered by lack of bank competition:
- Many banks are state-owned, with few branch networks
- Banks continue to depend on central bank funds, large shares of preferential credits, and credit ceilings
- Many banks rely heavily on **central bank refinancing** — a legacy of the surplus of credits over deposits transferred from the "monobank" of the centrally planned era
- Demand for bank reserves is **not sensitive to interest rates** — banks have not yet started behaving like profit-maximizing commercial entities

[RAW] **The monobank system:** Under central planning, a single institution (monobank) served as both central bank and monopoly commercial bank. Transition required separating the commercial banking functions into multiple banks — but these banks inherited the monobank's culture, balance sheets, and credit relationships. The lack of well-developed interbank markets to recycle funds compounds the problem.

[RAW] **Result:** Transition economies have found it necessary to rely on **nonprice rationing** of central bank credit (through auctions) rather than interest rate signals, with banks reallocating reserves among themselves in a nascent market for cash balances.

## 3. Interenterprise Arrears

[RAW] When countries tighten credit policies without getting enterprises to harden their individual budget constraints, the result is often a sharp increase in **disintermediation** — movement of funds from the banking system to informal financial instruments. In transition economies, enterprises use **payments arrears to each other** as a substitute for bank credit.

- Use of "interenterprise credits" (payment arrears) rose rapidly in many transition economies since 1990 (Romania, Russia, FSU states)
- Romania's experience: providing bank credit to clear arrears is not a lasting solution unless enterprises are convinced that a decisive hardening of budget constraints will follow

[RAW] **Preventing new arrears requires:**
- Positive real interest rates (imposing cost on arrears)
- Improved payments systems (reducing settlement delays)
- More managerial accountability
- Effective bankruptcy legislation
- Possibly enterprise restructuring and privatization

**Why arrears matter for monetary analysis:** Interenterprise arrears are a form of inside credit creation outside the banking system — they are not captured in the monetary survey, so the monetary survey understates total credit extension in the economy. The effective multiplier is higher than measured.

## 4. Absence of Money and Financial Markets

[RAW] The lack of money and financial markets in the initial stages of transition precludes the use of **open market operations** as an instrument of monetary control:
- No secondary market for government securities → no OMO
- No interbank clearing system
- Lack of confidence in the creditworthiness of interbank transactions

[RAW] **Alternative tools:** Reserve requirements and direct credit controls (credit ceilings) substitute for OMO in the early transition phase. As treasury bill markets develop (Poland: May 1991), OMO becomes feasible, but commercial banks may initially prefer T-bills (safe, liquid, high-yielding) over lending to the private sector — potentially crowding out nongovernment credit.

## 5. Interest Rate Complications

[RAW] Interest rate liberalization is complicated by:
- **Large stock of loans with long maturities at low/fixed interest rates** (especially housing loans) — these are impervious to rate changes
- **Weak financial discipline:** A systematic tendency for higher interest rates to feed into higher monetary growth if enterprises can borrow from banks to pay interest obligations. This turns the interest rate from a monetary tightening instrument into a monetary loosening mechanism.

[RAW] **The perverse channel:** Rising interest rates → enterprises with weak budget constraints borrow more (from banks) to service interest payments → bank credit expands → money supply expands → inflation rises. Breaking this perverse channel requires enterprise restructuring and hard budget constraints (effective bankruptcy enforcement), not just rate changes.

## 6. Large and Persistent Deposit-Lending Rate Spread

[RAW] The spread between deposit rates and lending rates has remained persistently large in transition economies. Causes:
1. **Large share of non-performing loans** — banks spread the cost of bad loans across performing borrowers
2. **Large share of preferential/directed credits** — banks must cross-subsidize below-market loans with above-market loans to others
3. **Heavy reliance on non-remunerated reserve requirements** — reduces banks' rate of return on assets, requiring higher lending margins
4. **High administrative costs** from inefficiencies in the banking system
5. **Absence of competition** — no pressure to abandon the old "cost-plus" pricing system inherited from central planning

[RAW] **Result:** Positive nominal lending rates do not imply positive real rates for borrowers who pay inflation-adjusted costs. And high lending spreads constrain credit access for creditworthy private sector borrowers, hampering real sector development.

## 7. High Government Financing Needs (Fiscal Dominance)

[RAW] Where monetary policy is not divorced from the political process, monetary authorities must frequently subordinate their objectives to accommodate the government's financing needs.

In countries with:
- High budget deficits
- Limited sources of nonbank domestic financing (no deep T-bill market)
- Limited access to external financing

This situation has typically been associated with a high inflation outcome. The CB is effectively forced to extend credit to the government (monetization), regardless of its inflation control mandate.

[RAW] **Fiscal dominance mechanism:** Government financing need → NCG expansion → RM expansion → M2 expansion → inflation. Breaking this requires: (1) independent central bank with legal authority to refuse government credit; (2) developing nonbank domestic financing markets (T-bills, bonds); (3) fiscal consolidation reducing the deficit itself.

## 8. Poland Institutional Background

[RAW] With the Banking Act of 1989, the National Bank of Poland (NBP) ceased to be the monobank — commercial functions were devolved to nine commercial banks. The NBP was granted considerable autonomy, required to submit draft monetary policy guidelines to parliament annually. Since October 1991, monetary policy has been conducted within a crawling peg framework.

Key Polish monetary developments:
- Inflation: fell from 249% (1990) to ~30% (1994) — but progress slowed in 1994
- Credit: large credit to government (crowding out private sector)
- Velocity: fell 1991–93 (money demand rose as inflation fell, deposits attracted by deregulated interest rates)
- Dollarization: peaked ~80% in 1989, fell sharply but remained elevated
- Money multiplier: rose from 3.0 (1992) to 3.9 (1994) as financial deepening progressed

## 9. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The eight transition-specific complications collectively explain why standard monetary programming frameworks fail without adaptation:

1. **The standard transmission mechanism is broken:** Normal channels — interest rate → investment → aggregate demand → prices — are compromised at every stage in early transition (interest-insensitive demand for bank reserves, enterprises not maximizing profits, arrears substituting for credit, absence of secondary markets). IMF programs compensate by using multiple instruments simultaneously and accepting higher uncertainty bands on outcomes.

2. **Monobank legacy creates correlated bank failures:** When all commercial banks inherited their portfolios from the same monobank, they have correlated exposures. Credit tightening causes correlated deterioration across all banks simultaneously — a systemic risk that requires CB LOLR support at the system level, not just for isolated institutions. See [[Monetary_Survey_Banking_System_Structure]] for the LOLR role.

3. **Arrears → invisible monetary expansion:** Interenterprise arrears are not captured in monetary surveys but function as credit. When a credit tightening forces enterprises to substitute arrears for bank credit, the CB observes "successful" monetary tightening in the NDA data while total credit (bank + interenterprise) may barely contract. Output falls but prices remain sticky — exactly the confusion observed in Russia 1992–93.

4. **The spread is the efficiency tax on credit:** The large deposit-lending spread functions as an efficiency tax on financial intermediation — channeling resources from productive borrowers to NPL recovery and bank overhead. Spread compression, through banking sector reforms (privatization, competition, provisioning discipline), is the key structural change that allows monetary policy transmission to improve over time.
