# Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs)

## 1. Thesis & Definition
[RAW][LLM] **Alternative Investment Funds (AIFs)** are investment vehicles that employ a diverse set of techniques to improve performance and reduce volatility. In the context of financial engineering, they are often synonymous with the [[Hedge_Fund_Industry]], acting as an "alternative asset class" that utilizes derivatives and engineering to design specific investment strategies.

## 2. Evolution & Scope
[RAW] The industry has undergone a fundamental transformation in recent decades:
- **1990s (Cottage Industry)**: Dominated by High Net Worth Individuals (HNWIs) as the primary investors; characterized by loose regulation and a multibillion-dollar scale.
- **Modern Era (Institutionalized Sector)**: A tightly regulated, multi-trillion dollar sector of the asset management industry. It has increasingly absorbed activities previously performed by commercial banks, leading to greater institutionalization.

## 3. Structural Differences (vs. Traditional Funds)
[RAW] AIFs (specifically hedge funds) differ from traditional investment vehicles (e.g., mutual funds) in three primary dimensions:

| Dimension | AIF / Hedge Funds | Traditional / Mutual Funds |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Regulation** | Historically light, now tightly regulated (AIFMD, Dodd-Frank). | Strictly regulated by securities acts (e.g., 1940 Act in US). |
| **Investment Mandate** | Broad, includes [[Short_Selling]], derivatives, and [[Financial_Leverage]]. | Typically long-only, limited use of derivatives. |
| **Legal Structure** | Private partnerships or limited liability vehicles (offshore/onshore). | Registered investment companies (open-ended/closed-ended). |

## 4. Transmission & Institutionalization
[LLM] The shift from a "cottage industry" to an institutional sector represents a **Transmission Mechanism** where systemic risk and activities have migrated from the banking sector to the [[Nonbank_Financial_Intermediation]] space.
