# Dodd-Frank and JOBS Act (Hedge Fund Impact)

## 1. Thesis & Overview
[WIKI][LLM] The **Dodd-Frank Act (2010)** and the **JOBS Act (2012)** collectively redefined the US hedge fund industry from a "loosely regulated multibillion dollar cottage industry" to a "tightly regulated institutionalized sector." Dodd-Frank focused on **systemic risk oversight** (registration and reporting), while the JOBS Act addressed **market communication** (advertising).

## 2. Dodd-Frank Act (2010)
[RAW][LLM]
### Key Provisions:
- **Mandatory Registration:** Requires all hedge fund advisers (AIFMs) with **$100 million or more** in Assets under Management (AuM) to register with the **SEC**.
- **Risk Reporting:** Instructed the SEC to collect confidential data from private fund advisers regarding their funds' **risk profiles** to monitor systemic risk.
- **Institutionalization:** Forced activities previously found predominantly in banks to move into a regulated asset management framework.

## 3. JOBS Act (2012)
[RAW]
- **General Solicitation:** Directed the SEC to remove the decades-old ban on **general solicitation** (advertising) for private securities offerings (Rule 506 of Regulation D).
- **Communication Impact:** Hedge fund managers are now free to communicate and advertise directly to the public, facilitating broader capital raising.

## 4. Transmission Mechanism: Registration to Institutionalization
[LLM]
  [Dodd-Frank Registration] ───> [Transparency/Oversight] ───┐
                                                            ├─> [Institutional Trust]
  [JOBS Act Advertising] ──────> [Public Visibility] ───────┘
                                                            |
                                                            v
                                            [Inflow of Institutional Capital]
                                            (Pension Funds, SWFs, Endowments)

## 5. Failure Conditions & Limitations
- **Registration Burden:** Increased compliance costs for mid-sized managers ($100M-$500M range).
- **Transparency vs. Alpha:** Concerns over confidential data leaks affecting proprietary strategies (though SEC reporting is confidential).
- **Adverse Selection:** Increased advertising might attract less sophisticated investors, though "Accredited Investor" rules still apply.
