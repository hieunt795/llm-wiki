# [[Inflation_Taxonomy]]

## Thesis

[RAW] A sustained and persistent increase in an economy's overall price level is generally called inflation. The increase in the average prices of all goods and services must be distinguished from a change in the relative prices of individual goods and services. Generally, an overall increase in the price level is accompanied by a change in the structure of relative prices, but it is only the overall increase — not the relative price change — that constitutes inflation.

## 1. Four Categories of Inflation

### Policy-Induced Inflation

[RAW] Often caused by expansionary monetary measures that reflect excessive fiscal deficits and their monetary financing. Classic hyperinflations (Austria and Germany in the 1920s) reflected excessive monetary expansion. Nonpolicy-induced inflation is caused by exogenous factors (e.g., drought).

### Cost-Push Inflation

[RAW] Caused by rising costs; may develop even when unemployment is high and resource utilization is low. Because wages are frequently the most important cost of production, a rise in wages out of line with productivity growth can initiate the inflation process. Cost-push inflation cannot persist if monetary policy refuses to accommodate it — in that case, wage increases lead to higher unemployment rather than higher inflation.

### Demand-Pull Inflation

[RAW] Caused by excess aggregate demand pushing up the overall price level. The boost to demand can come from internal or external shocks but frequently results from overly expansionary monetary and fiscal policies.

### Inertial Inflation

[RAW] Tends to persist at the same rate until economic events cause it to change. If inflation is steady, the prevailing rate is anticipated and embedded in wage and financial contracts, which further perpetuate it. Most modern inflation is classified as inertial. The inertial inflation rate is sometimes referred to as the core or underlying inflation rate.

## 2. Underlying (Core) vs. Measured Inflation

[RAW] An important distinction must be made between a one-time increase in the price level and underlying inflation.

- A rise in **administered prices** will raise the overall price level in the first instance but does not necessarily add to **underlying (core) inflation**, which reflects basic changes in the overall price level abstracting from unusual one-time increases caused by administered price changes, excise tax changes, or discrete devaluations.
- Underlying inflation is not always easy to measure precisely but can be estimated and may provide a better guide to policy than the measured rate.

[RAW] Especially in transition economies, analysts should focus on the underlying rate of inflation because structural changes and reforms inevitably result in discrete adjustments in the prices of many goods and services.

## 3. Cost-Push to Persistent Inflation: The Accommodation Mechanism

[RAW] It is possible for a one-time increase in the price of key goods (especially energy) to trigger a chain of price and wage increases throughout the economy. If monetary policy is not sufficiently firm, a one-time increase in the price level can become a sustained increase in inflation (the cost-push phenomenon).

**Historical example:** The worldwide oil price increase in the mid-1970s and early 1980s triggered bouts of inflation in oil-importing countries. Japan, despite being a large energy importer and experiencing a huge price shock, managed to keep inflation under control because the authorities followed **nonaccommodating financial policies** and the population accepted a temporary reduction in real incomes.

## 4. Supply and Demand Shocks

[RAW] Shocks to the economy from either the supply or demand side cause the actual inflation rate to move above or below the underlying inflation rate:
- **Major supply-side shocks:** oil price changes, poor harvests.
- **Major demand-side shocks:** rapid increases in aggregate demand due to expectations that incomes will rise.

## 5. Transition Economy Context

[RAW] Policy-induced, demand-pull inflation is found in many transition economies, as a result of insufficiently restrictive fiscal policies and monetary financing of the resulting fiscal deficits. External shocks — rising energy prices or contraction of trade with traditional partners — have tended to add to inflation pressures.

[RAW] In transition economies undergoing rapid price liberalization, a number of factors simultaneously influence the recorded inflation rate: financial policies, price liberalization, changes in taxation, and the exchange rate. The underlying rate of inflation can therefore diverge substantially from the measured rate.

## 6. Wages, Prices, and Productivity Relationship

[RAW] The relationship between inflation, wage growth, and productivity (Box 2.5):

$$\frac{\dot{P}}{P} = \frac{\dot{W}}{W} - \frac{\dot{Y/L}}{Y/L} \tag{2}$$

or equivalently:

$$\text{inflation} = \text{wage growth} - \text{productivity growth}$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{\dot{W/P}}{W/P} = \frac{\dot{Y/L}}{Y/L} \tag{4}$$

$$\text{real wage growth} = \text{productivity growth}$$

[RAW] If labor productivity grows at 2% per year, then wage growth of 6% per year is consistent with inflation of 4% per year. The productivity growth rate sets the upper boundary of wage growth compatible with price stability.

## 7. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The taxonomy matters for policy response calibration. Cost-push and supply-shock inflation that is not accommodated will reduce real output — the correct policy response depends on whether the shock is temporary (tolerate it) or persistent (tighten to prevent wage-price spiral). Inertial inflation is the hardest to reduce precisely because its persistence is self-reinforcing through contract indexation — this is why IMF-supported programs often combine financial tightening with incomes policies (wage controls) to break inertial dynamics simultaneously from both the demand and cost sides. See [[Incomes_Policy_Wage_Controls]].

For fixed income practitioners, the measured vs. underlying inflation distinction directly affects break-even inflation analysis. Periods of large administered price adjustments can send misleading signals about central bank credibility when actual underlying inflation remains anchored. See [[Breakeven_Inflation_Metrics]].
