# [[System_of_National_Accounts_SNA]]

## Thesis

[RAW] The SNA was developed as an accounting framework within which macroeconomic data can be compiled and presented for economic analysis. It provides an internationally recognized system for organizing a continuous flow of information indispensable to the analysis, evaluation, and monitoring of a country's economic performance.

## 1. Purpose and Uses

[RAW] The SNA serves three primary functions:

- **Macroeconomic analysis** — flexible enough to accommodate different economic theories and models, suitable for countries at various stages of development.
- **Intertemporal comparisons** — coherent system for tracking the performance of key macroeconomic aggregates (output, employment) over time.
- **International comparisons** — used for reporting to international organizations; allows comparisons of national accounts for determining eligibility for concessional assistance, calculating IMF member country quotas, and measuring relative economic power. Requires conversion to a common currency.

## 2. Account Structure

[RAW] The 1993 SNA structures flow accounts in two groups, supplemented by balance sheets:

### Current Accounts
- **Production accounts** — record output and intermediate inputs; balancing item is value added → GDP.
- **Income accounts** (sequential):
  - *Generation of Income Account* — how value added splits into compensation of employees (W), operating surplus (OS), and taxes less subsidies (TSP).
  - *Allocation of Primary Income Account* — balancing item is Gross National Income (GNI).
  - *Secondary Distribution of Income Account* — adds/subtracts current transfers; balancing item is Gross National Disposable Income (GNDI).
  - *Use of Income Account* — shows how GNDI is divided between consumption and saving.

### Accumulation Accounts
- **Capital account** — acquisition/disposal of nonfinancial assets, financed by saving.
- **Financial account** — changes in financial assets and liabilities.
- **Other changes in assets accounts** — holding gains/losses, other volume changes.

### Balance Sheets
- Record stocks of assets and liabilities at beginning and end of period.

## 3. Institutional Sectors

[RAW] All accounts are compiled for five mutually exclusive resident sectors:
1. Nonfinancial corporations
2. Financial corporations
3. Governmental units
4. Nonprofit institutions serving households
5. Households

Plus a **Rest of the World** sector capturing all transactions between residents and nonresidents.

## 4. Core Accounting Conventions

[RAW] Three fundamental conventions underpin the SNA:

- **Double-entry recording** — every transaction has two entries (credit and debit). For unrequited transfers (grants), a corresponding entry is made on the opposite side of the ledger.
- **Accrual basis timing** — transactions recorded when claims/obligations arise or are transformed, not at settlement.
- **Residency-based** — resident transactors must have been in a country for more than one year; enterprises are residents where they are engaged in business with at least one productive establishment planned for the long term.

## 5. Valuation Concepts

[RAW] Two broad systems of valuation in the SNA: **current prices** and **constant prices**.

| Price concept | Definition |
|---|---|
| Basic price | What the producer receives, minus taxes due plus subsidies on output; excludes transport charges |
| Producer's price | Price producer receives, minus VAT or similar deductible taxes; excludes transport |
| Purchaser's price | Price the buyer pays, excluding deductible VAT; includes transport charges |

Preferred method: **basic prices** (especially when VAT is involved). Purchaser's prices used for intermediate consumption valuation.

## 6. 1993 vs. 1968 SNA: Key Differences

[RAW] The 1993 SNA introduced five important changes from the 1968 version:

1. **Integrated sequence of accounts** — more integrated, institutional-sector-based sequence; includes current accounts, accumulation accounts, and (new in 1993) balance sheets with detailed sectoral transactions.
2. **Greater emphasis on income flows** — more emphasis on income beyond GDP; series of income accounts providing greater insight into generation, distribution, and uses of incomes (GNI, GNDI, sectoral disposable incomes).
3. **Financial instruments coverage** — more information on types of assets and liabilities, including sophisticated financial instruments and derivatives that each institutional sector employs.
4. **Consistency with other international standards** — aligned with IMF's Balance of Payments Manual 5th Edition (1993); future GFS and monetary statistics revisions designed to maximize consistency with 1993 SNA.
5. **Satellite accounts** — specifically recognizes development of satellite accounts that augment the SNA (e.g., environmental statistics).

[RAW] Countries with MPS-based national accounts (transition economies) began converting to 1993 SNA from the early 1990s. The 1993 SNA applies the same five mutually exclusive institutional sectors to each account: nonfinancial corporations, financial corporations, governmental units, nonprofit institutions serving households (NPISH), and households.

## 7. Difference from MPS

[WIKI] The SNA replaced the **Material Product System (MPS)** used in centrally planned economies. Key divergence: MPS excludes "nonmaterial services" (government, education, healthcare, personal services) from aggregate output. See: [[Material_Product_System_MPS]].

## 8. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For fixed income and macro analysts, the SNA's sequential account structure provides the accounting backbone for all flow-of-funds analysis. The chain from GDP → GNI → GNDI → Saving creates the accounting constraints that bound current account dynamics and sector financing gaps. Understanding which account a data item belongs to (production vs. income vs. capital) is prerequisite for identifying where shocks originate and how they propagate across sectors. The Rest of World sector being a residual closure means BOP imbalances are always the mirror image of domestic sector imbalances — a constraint that holds ex post regardless of behavioral assumptions.
