# [[Unemployment_Taxonomy]]

## Thesis

[RAW] The unemployment rate measures the percentage of those in the labor force who do not have regular employment and are seeking work.

## 1. Core Definitions

[RAW]

**Labor force:** All individuals of working age (usually 16 or older) who are either working or looking for work. Those in the labor force without regular employment who are looking for work are considered unemployed.

$$\text{Unemployment rate} = \frac{\text{Number of unemployed}}{\text{Labor force}}$$

**Labor force participation rate:**
$$\text{Participation rate} = \frac{\text{Labor force}}{\text{Working-age population}}$$

Changes in the participation rate (e.g., women's entry into the labor market) can significantly influence the unemployment rate.

**Discouraged workers:** People unemployed for long periods who stop looking and drop out of the labor force — not counted as unemployed. An unemployment rate excluding discouraged workers understates true joblessness.

**Full employment:** The optimum employment level of an economy. Does not refer to zero unemployment. In a market economy with constant shifts in demand, technology, and products, some unemployment always persists.

## 2. Five Categories of Unemployment

### Seasonal Unemployment

[RAW] Caused by shifts in the supply and demand for labor during the calendar year, typically in agriculture, construction, or tourism.

### Frictional Unemployment

[RAW] Emerges when job vacancies remain unfilled for significant periods because both employers and workers need time to explore the market. Generally does not last long; contributes to a better match between job and worker.

### Cyclical Unemployment

[RAW] Caused by falling output during recessions. Recessions are part of the business cycle in a market economy. Government policies to stimulate demand during a recession have a more positive impact on cyclical unemployment than on other kinds.

### Structural Unemployment

[RAW] Caused by a mismatch of skills or geographic locations. Changing patterns of demand and technology reduce the demand for certain types of skills. Structural unemployment can also occur when an industry in a region shuts down and is not replaced. Harder to eliminate than other kinds because it requires retraining unemployed workers or improving their mobility.

### Disguised Unemployment

[RAW] Develops when a worker's marginal contribution to output is zero or negative, so that the economy is better off by explicitly recognizing this and moving the worker to more productive employment. Pervasive in centrally planned economies ("labor hoarding").

## 3. NAIRU: Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment

[RAW] The NAIRU (Non-Accelerating Inflation Rate of Unemployment) is an equilibrium rate of unemployment defined as the rate of joblessness that is compatible with a stable rate of inflation.

[RAW] Many observers believe that shifts in the composition of the labor force and institutional changes in the labor market have caused the full employment rate (NAIRU) to rise in many countries in recent years, resulting in relatively high and persistent unemployment.

## 4. Transition Economy Context

[RAW] Because of the pervasiveness of "labor hoarding" by Polish firms prior to 1990 (disguised unemployment was estimated at 20 percent of the labor force), the economic reforms resulted in massive layoffs and a sharp rise in official unemployment:
- Joblessness climbed from practically zero in early 1989 to over 6 percent by end-1990, and over 11 percent by end-1991.
- Employment dropped significantly in the public sector in 1990, but by substantially less than production — indicating persistent labor hoarding.
- By 1993-94, the unemployment rate stabilized at about 15-16 percent of the labor force.

**Analytical implication:** During transition, measured unemployment likely overstates true involuntary joblessness because: (1) some "unemployed" are self-employed in the informal sector; (2) official unemployment registers may be used to access social benefits rather than reflecting genuine job search.

## 5. Wages, Unemployment, and Inflation

[WIKI] The NAIRU concept connects unemployment to inflation dynamics: if actual unemployment is below NAIRU, labor market tightness generates wage pressure that transmits into inflation (cost-push channel). Policy must maintain unemployment near the NAIRU to keep inflation stable. See [[Inflation_Taxonomy]] for the cost-push inflation mechanism and [[Incomes_Policy_Wage_Controls]] for wage control responses.

## 6. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For macro practitioners, the unemployment taxonomy shapes policy interpretation:

- **Cyclical unemployment** responds to demand management (monetary and fiscal stimulus) — this is the Keynesian policy lever.
- **Structural unemployment** requires supply-side policies (retraining, mobility subsidies, deregulation) — demand stimulus is mostly ineffective here.
- **Disguised unemployment** — when it surfaces as formal unemployment during market liberalization — creates a temporary statistical spike that does not reflect a genuine deterioration in economic welfare. Analysts must adjust headline unemployment figures accordingly during transition phases.
- **NAIRU** determines the inflation-neutral speed limit for demand expansion — pushing unemployment below NAIRU generates persistent inflation even without supply shocks.
