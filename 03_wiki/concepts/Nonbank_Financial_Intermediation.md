## Thesis

Nonbank Financial Intermediation (NBFI) comprises all credit intermediation, maturity transformation, and liquidity provision operating outside the regulated banking system. Holding over half of global financial assets, NBFI networks operate fundamentally differently from traditional banks: they fail not through traditional capital insolvency, but through systemic interconnectedness and acute liquidity gaps.

## Definition

**Nonbank Financial Intermediation (NBFI)**, historically dubbed **Shadow Banking**, refers to a broad spectrum of financial activities involving credit intermediation, liquidity provision, and maturity transformation conducted by institutions outside the heavily regulated perimeter of commercial banking.

By bypassing banking regulations (like Basel III capital requirements and deposit insurance constraints), NBFIs can operate with different leverage models and asset liability structures.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> NBFI is not a marginal sector. As of 2024, it accounted for **~51% of total global financial assets** ($256.8 trillion). Its credit provision branch—notably [[Private_Credit]]—now competes directly with the traditional banking system for corporate financing.

### Systemic Characteristics

- **Bypassing the Regulatory Boundary:** NBFI entities absorb the credit demand that traditional banks shed due to stringent capital constraints.
- **Different Failure Mechanics:** Unlike commercial banks that typically fail via insolvency (capital depletion) or retail bank runs, NBFIs predominantly fail due to **liquidity mismatches** and margin spirals.
- **Interconnectedness:** NBFIs do not operate in a vacuum. They are deeply reliant on the regulated banking system for leverage (e.g., via subscription lines, repo funding, and backup credit facilities). When an NBFI experiences stress, the shock is transmitted directly back to the regulated banking system through these credit lines.

### Contrast with Traditional Banking

| Feature | Traditional Bank | NBFI (Shadow Banking) |
|---|---|---|
| **Regulation** | Extremely strict (Basel III/IV, LCR, NSFR) | Relatively light (Securities & Investment laws) |
| **Funding Source** | Retail and corporate deposits | Equity, bonds, and bank-provided leverage |
| **Duration Risk** | Borrows short (deposits) to lend long | Models vary (typically matched-duration in private credit) |
| **Rate Sensitivity** | "Sticky" funding costs (deposits lag policy rates) | Highly sensitive to wholesale funding costs |
| **Safety Net** | Central bank discount window, Deposit Insurance | None explicitly |

### The Central Bank Dilemma

Because NBFIs lack direct access to the central bank's discount window (the classic [[Lender_Of_Last_Resort]] function), central banks possess limited tools to arrest an NBFI liquidity panic. Interventions often require indirect mechanisms or massive market-wide asset purchases (like [[Quantitative_Easing]]) that carry significant moral hazard implications.
