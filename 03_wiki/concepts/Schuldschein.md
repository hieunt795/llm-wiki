## Thesis

At the foundational layer of German corporate finance, the **Schuldschein** (literally "Promissory Note") serves as a bridge between a traditional bilateral loan and a tradable bond. Its unique legal structure explicitly restricts liquidity to achieve a specific regulatory benefit: avoiding the volatility of mark-to-market accounting. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch11.md#page=80]]

## Mechanism: The Hybrid Structure

A Schuldschein does not by itself constitute a debt; it is a physical and transferable certificate that serves as **evidence of a separate loan contract**.

1. **Bearer Feature:** It has a physical certificate that evidences ownership and can be transferred.
2. **Registered Feature:** Ownership is registered, and changes of registration are limited.
3. **Restricted Liquidity:** Schuldscheine are usually documented to allow only a limited number of transfers (typically three). These transfers are registered directly on the certificate itself. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch11.md#page=80]]

## Strategic Application: Accounting Arbitrage

The restriction on trading is a deliberate feature, not a flaw.
- **Mark-to-Market Avoidance:** Because the instrument has restricted transferability, it is not considered to have a meaningful liquid secondary market.
- **The Result:** Holders (typically banks or insurance companies) are legally permitted to carry the asset at **amortized cost** instead of its market value. This shields their balance sheets from daily price fluctuations while still allowing for liquidation if absolutely necessary. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch11.md#page=80]]
