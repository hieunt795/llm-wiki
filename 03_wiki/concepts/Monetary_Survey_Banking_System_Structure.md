# [[Monetary_Survey_Banking_System_Structure]]

## Thesis

[RAW] Among the main macroeconomic accounts, the monetary accounts play a special role: the monetary sector serves as a clearinghouse for all financial flows in a market economy, providing unique insight into the behavior of financial flows that mirror the flows of real resources among sectors.

## 1. Financial System Hierarchy (IFS Three Levels)

[RAW] In the IMF's International Financial Statistics (IFS) framework, monetary and financial data are presented at three levels:

| Level | Content |
|---|---|
| **Level 1** | Separate balance sheets of the monetary authorities (MAs) and deposit money banks (DMBs) |
| **Level 2** | Monetary survey = consolidated balance sheet of MAs + DMBs → statistical measure of money and credit |
| **Level 3** | Financial survey = monetary survey + balance sheets of other financial institutions (OFIs) |

[RAW] The banking system (MAs + DMBs) is distinguished from nonbank financial institutions (insurance companies, mutual funds, pension funds, money market funds). An unorganized financial sector also operates in many economies.

**Why focus on the banking system:**
1. Empirical evidence shows banking sector monetary liabilities strongly influence aggregate nominal spending
2. Banking sector data are readily available even in developing/transition economies
3. In countries with underdeveloped financial markets, the banking system accounts for the bulk of financial assets/liabilities

## 2. Accounting Principles Underlying Monetary Statistics

[RAW]

| Principle | Rule |
|---|---|
| **Stocks vs. flows** | Monetary statistics are balance-sheet stock data (outstanding at a point in time). Analyzed in terms of changes from one period to the next. |
| **Cash vs. accrual** | IFS records on a **cash basis** (when obligation settled). In practice, little difference since most bank transactions are immediately in cash. |
| **Currency denomination** | Accounts expressed in local currency. Foreign-currency items converted at **end-period** exchange rate (for stocks). Flow aggregates use period-average rate. |
| **Consolidation** | Interbank entries (items due to/from other banks) are **netted out**. Two levels: (1) all DMB balance sheets consolidated; (2) MA balance sheet consolidated with DMBs to get the monetary survey. |

## 3. Monetary Authorities (MA) Balance Sheet

[RAW] The monetary authorities (usually the central bank, but functionally defined to include any treasury agency performing central banking functions) perform: issuing currency; holding foreign exchange reserves; borrowing for BOP purposes; banker to government; overseeing the monetary system; lender of last resort.

**Analytical balance sheet (Box 5.2):**

| Assets | Liabilities |
|---|---|
| **Net foreign assets (NFA\*)** = gold + FX + IMF reserve position + SDRs − foreign liabilities (incl. IMF credit use, foreign CB deposits, swaps) | **Reserve money (RM)** = currency issued (held in banks + held outside banks) + deposits of DMBs + other domestic sector deposits |
| **Net claims on government (NCG\*)** = treasury bills + govt securities + loans − government deposits | — |
| **Claims on DMBs (CDMB\*)** = rediscounts + secured advances + loans (gross — not netted) | — |
| **Claims on private sector (CPS\*)** (generally small) | — |
| **Other items net (OIN\*)** = physical assets of CB; capital and reserves; CB profits/losses; FX valuation adjustments | — |

[RAW] **Key features:**
- Net claims on government are *net* because the government has overdraft access — it is the MA's *net* credit to government that corresponds to money creation.
- Claims on DMBs are *gross* — DMBs' deposits with the MA are not netted out because these deposits play a key role in monetary policy.
- Reserve money excludes government deposits (netted against NCG) and nonresident deposits (netted against NFA).

## 4. Deposit Money Banks (DMB) Balance Sheet

[RAW] DMBs are defined functionally: all banks and similar institutions with appreciable liabilities in the form of deposits payable on demand that can be transferred by check or otherwise used to make payments.

**Four roles of DMBs:**
1. Financial intermediation (maturity transformation: short-term deposits → long-term loans)
2. Primary creators of deposit money (extend credit under fractional reserve system)
3. Influence on money supply and liquidity through deposit-taking/lending policies
4. Transmission of monetary policy from MA to public

[RAW] Analytical balance sheet (Box 5.6):
- **Assets:** Net foreign assets; required + excess reserves; domestic credit (claims on government net + claims on other domestic sectors)
- **Liabilities:** Deposits — demand (DD), time and savings (TD), foreign currency (FC); liabilities to MA; other less liquid liabilities

**Three key points:**
1. DMBs hold foreign assets because of trade finance activities — inclusion in official reserves depends on degree of MA control
2. DMB liabilities are classified by instrument/liquidity (not by sector), because liquidity determines what is included in narrow money
3. Fractional reserve system: DMBs hold only a fraction of deposits as reserves; can borrow from MA at discount rate if reserves run low

## 5. Monetary Survey: Consolidated Result

[RAW] The monetary survey consolidates the MA balance sheet with the consolidated DMB balance sheets:
- **Liability side:** Total stock of money generated by the banking system (broad money M2)
- **Asset side:** Net foreign assets (NFA) + net domestic assets (NDA) = net domestic credit (NDC) + other items net (OINb)

[RAW] Valuation adjustments: When stocks change over two periods, the change reflects transaction flows **plus** revaluations from exchange rate changes and price fluctuations. Valuation adjustments should be reflected in "other items (net)" to maintain balance sheet integrity. See [[BOP_Framework_and_Conventions]] for the parallel BOP treatment.

## 6. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The monetary survey is the primary instrument for financial programming:

1. **IFS three-level hierarchy matters for policy analysis:** The monetary survey (Level 2) is what IMF programs target with NDA ceilings. The financial survey (Level 3) includes OFI intermediation that is increasingly important as capital markets develop — in more financially developed economies, targeting only the banking system's NDA understates total domestic credit growth.

2. **Cash vs. accrual distinction in transition economies:** Central banks in transition economies that retain large accrued but uncollected interest on government claims will have overstated NCG on a cash basis. This creates a gap between the measured money supply and the actual monetary stimulus — important for interpreting monetary survey data from countries with significant arrears to the banking system.

3. **Why claims on DMBs are gross (not net):** DMB reserve deposits at the MA are the transmission mechanism for reserve requirements and open market operations. If they were netted out, the MA balance sheet would lose its analytical connection to reserve money creation — the netting approach is used at the survey level, not the MA level.

4. **Lender of last resort and moral hazard:** The LOLR function (preventing systemwide credit freeze) creates a contingent liability on the MA's balance sheet not visible in standard accounts. In transition economies where banking crises have been common, the unrecorded LOLR exposure has frequently materialized as explicit QFO recapitalization costs. See [[Quasi_Fiscal_Operations]].
