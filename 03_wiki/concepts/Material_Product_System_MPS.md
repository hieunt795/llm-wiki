# [[Material_Product_System_MPS]]

## Thesis

[RAW] The centrally planned economies developed their own method of calculating national accounts. Several economies in transition still use the Material Product System (MPS) as the main economic accounting framework. Although international organizations have tried to improve the links between the SNA and the MPS, it will take some time for all countries to move to the SNA.

## 1. Core Difference: Scope of Economic Activity

[RAW] Unlike the SNA, the MPS **excludes "nonmaterial services"** from the measure of aggregate output and income. Under the MPS, only the activities of the "material sphere" contribute to the creation of national income.

- **Nonmaterial services (excluded from MPS):** government services, education, health care, personal services, and transportation not related to production of material goods.
- **Material products (included in MPS):** both material goods and material services — mostly transport of goods, trade, and maintenance and repair.

**Implication:** The MPS's key aggregate, **Net Material Product (NMP)**, is systematically lower than GDP because it excludes large segments of the service economy.

## 2. Depreciation Treatment

[RAW] The SNA and MPS differ in their treatment of the depreciation of fixed assets:
- **SNA:** estimates depreciation of fixed assets on the basis of **replacement values**.
- **MPS:** relies on **historical costs**, which are generally much lower than replacement values.

The NMP is expressed **net of the depreciation of all fixed assets**. Because historical costs understate actual depreciation, **the NMP generally overstates the value of output** — it uses acquisition prices rather than replacement values to measure depreciation.

## 3. Other Differences

[RAW] Additional divergences between MPS and SNA:
- **Inventories valuation:** MPS values inventories at acquisition prices. High inflation in transition economies creates large holding gains and losses on inventories that distort investment estimates.
- **Other items** treated differently: personal transportation, military expenditures, housing services, capital repairs, scrapped fixed assets.

## 4. Bridge Calculation: Deriving GDP from NMP

[RAW] Deriving GDP from NMP estimates requires the following adjustments:

| Step | Item |
|---|---|
| (1) | Net Material Product (NMP) |
| + (2) | Depreciation and losses on fixed assets |
| (3) = (1)+(2) | Gross Material Product (GMP) |
| + (4) | Value added in nonmaterial sphere |
| − (5) | Nonmaterial inputs used for material production |
| + (6) | Adjustments for differences in classification |
| (7) = (3)+(4)−(5)+(6) | Gross Domestic Product (GDP) |

## 5. Practical Consequence for Transition Economies

[LLM] In many cases, the GDP of transition economies is still scaled up from basic estimates derived from MPS concepts. This creates two analytical problems:

1. **Underestimation of output level** — Because nonmaterial services are excluded, MPS-based estimates understate the size of the economy, particularly as the service sector expands during transition.
2. **Distorted measurement of the output collapse** — When centrally planned economies collapsed, much of the apparent output decline reflected the contraction of the material sphere (state industry), while the growth of private services went unrecorded. Official statistics based on MPS data therefore overstated the depth of the recession and understated the subsequent recovery.

[RAW] One study suggested that Polish real GDP fell 5–8 percent from 1989 to 1990, with consumption falling substantially less than GDP; yet official estimates recorded a 12 percent decline. Another study concluded that the cumulative fall in real output from 1989 to 1992 was about 5–10 percent, in contrast to the official 18 percent decline.

## 6. Relation to GDP Measurement Problems

[WIKI] The MPS-related mismeasurement in transition economies is one instance of broader GDP measurement problems: nonmarket output undercounting, quality change, underground economy. See [[System_of_National_Accounts_SNA]] for the SNA framework and [[GDP_Three_Approaches]] for the standard GDP measurement methodology.

## 7. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For practitioners analyzing transition economies or EM countries with legacy MPS frameworks, the headline GDP figures may not be comparable to SNA-based international data without adjustment. The critical adjustments are: (1) adding nonmaterial services value added, (2) correcting depreciation to replacement cost basis. The direction of net bias is uncertain — MPS understates output by excluding services but overstates net output by understating depreciation — though in practice, the service exclusion typically dominates, causing net understatement of output level but overstatement of growth rates during the service-sector expansion phase of transition.
