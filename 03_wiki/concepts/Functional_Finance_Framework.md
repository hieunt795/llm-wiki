## Thesis
Functional finance reframes government spending as a tool to achieve macroeconomic outcomes (full employment, price stability) rather than treating budgets like households. The fiscal constraint is operational, not financial: a sovereign currency issuer can always meet its obligations.

## 1. Core Logic / Mechanism

[RAW] In functional finance (Abba Lerner, 1943), the government abandons the sound finance principle ("balance the budget") and instead pursues spending and tax policies that produce and maintain **full employment and price stability**.

The core insight: A sovereign government issuing its own currency has **no financial constraint** on spending—it can always print money. The real constraint is **real resources** (labor, materials, capacity).

**Three Functional Objectives:**
1. **Full Employment Target:** Fiscal stance adjusts to absorb idle labor via demand management.
2. **Price Stability:** Taxes act as a cooling mechanism when inflation threatens.
3. **Interest Rate Neutrality:** Government can set interest rates independently; deficits don't "crowd out" private investment.

[LLM] **Transmission Mechanism:**
- When unemployment rises → Government increases spending and/or cuts taxes → aggregate demand rises → firms hire → unemployment falls.
- When inflation rises → Government cuts spending and/or raises taxes → demand cools → inflation moderates.
- The deficit or surplus is a **lagging indicator** of the stance's success, not a target.

**Contrast with Sound Finance:**
| Principle | Sound Finance | Functional Finance |
|-----------|---------------|-------------------|
| Budget Target | Balanced (or surplus) | Whatever achieves full employment |
| Spending Limit | Revenue = Expenditure | Real resource availability |
| Inflation Control | Austerity (cut spending) | Tax increases or spending trim |
| Unemployment | Accept as natural | Unacceptable; use fiscal lever |
| Debt Worries | High debt = crisis risk | Operational reality only; no solvency risk |

## 2. Worked Example (Fiscal Adjustment in Demand Shortfall)

[RAW] Assume real GDP is growing at 1.5% but potential is 3%, unemployment is 6%, inflation is stable at 2%.

**Functional Finance Response:**
1. Diagnose: Demand-constrained economy (output gap = 1.5% of potential).
2. Policy: Increase government spending by ~1.5% of GDP (or equivalent tax cuts).
3. Mechanism: New spending ripples through multiplier → incomes rise → consumption rises → firms expand production → workers hired.
4. Outcome: Unemployment falls to 4%; inflation remains stable (unused capacity absorbs demand).

**Sound Finance Response:**
1. Problem: Budget deficit is "too high"; structural deficit seen as unsustainable.
2. Policy: Cut spending / raise taxes to shrink deficit.
3. Result: Aggregate demand falls further → unemployment rises → multiplier contraction amplifies austerity effect.
4. Paradox of Thrift: Individual prudence (balanced budget) becomes collective harm.

## 3. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

**When Functional Finance Breaks Down:**

1. **Inflation at Full Capacity:** When all labor and materials are fully deployed, demand-side stimulus cannot raise output—only prices. Functional finance requires **supply-side policies** (education, infrastructure, R&D) to expand productive capacity.

2. **Fixed Exchange Rates (Non-Sovereign Currency):** If a nation uses a foreign currency (Eurozone, currency peg), it loses monetary sovereignty. Functional finance cannot apply; such governments ARE financially constrained.

3. **High Dollarization / Foreign Debt in External Currency:** If most government debt is in USD (not home currency), fiscal dominance is weakened. Risk of currency crisis if confidence breaks.

4. **Hyperinflation Spiral:** If inflation expectations become unanchored (past 5–10%), fiscal multipliers collapse and functional finance loses traction.

5. **Political / Institutional Rigidity:** If legislative gridlock prevents rapid adjustment of spending/taxes in real time, functional finance becomes pro-cyclical (contracts when it should expand).
