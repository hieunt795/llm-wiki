## Thesis
[RAW] ITF implementation provides measurable macroeconomic performance improvements across inflation, output stability, and sacrifice ratios, though comparative studies with non-ITF countries show more nuanced results than initial success narratives suggested.

## 1. Core Logic - ITF Success Framework

[RAW] The Mishkin and Schmidt-Hebbel (2001) synthesis identified eight key success stories from ITF countries during the 1990s:

1. **Lower Inflation Achievement**: ITF countries achieved long-term inflation reductions beyond what would have been possible without the framework, though not systematically lower than advanced non-ITF countries
2. **Shock Resilience**: ITF countries successfully weathered the Asian Financial Crisis (1997-1999) and oil price shocks (1999-2000) with contained pass-through to inflation
3. **Sacrifice Ratio Reduction**: Evidence from Corbo et al. (2000) showed declining sacrifice ratios (disinflation costs) in EMEs after ITF adoption
4. **Inflation Expectations Anchoring**: ITF reduced inflation persistence and shifted formation mechanisms from backward-looking to forward-looking expectations
5. **Flexible Framework Application**: ITF countries demonstrated symmetric responses to shocks rather than strict "inflation nutter" behavior
6. **Clearer Monetary Policy Focus**: Central bank aversion to inflation shocks strengthened post-ITF implementation
7. **Central Bank Independence Enhancement**: ITF created institutional foundations for greater central bank autonomy
8. **Communication and Transparency**: ITF implementation accompanied systematic improvements in policy communication and reporting

[RAW] Ball and Sheridan (2003) challenged these findings through "differences in differences" methodology across 20 OECD countries:
- ITF countries improved macroeconomic performance, BUT non-ITF countries also improved during 1990s
- Key finding: Economic gains may reflect broader global trends rather than ITF causation
- ITF provides potential **political benefits** (transparency, democracy alignment) rather than guaranteed economic benefits

## 2. Worked Example - Indonesia ITF Performance vs. Theory

[RAW] BI's experience 2005-2015 (Table 9.1):
- **2005**: Target 6%±1%, Actual 17.1% (fuel price shocks)
- **2008**: Target 5%±1%, Actual 11.06% (fuel price hike + commodity shock)
- **2013-2014**: Target 4.5%±1%, Actual 8.4% (fuel reform + weather disruptions)

[LLM] Despite missing target corridor multiple times, BI demonstrated ITF institutional benefits:
- Shifted from inward-focused (base money targeting) to outward-focused, transparent organization
- Built policy signal clarity through BI Rate announcement mechanism
- Achieved credibility gains through public expectation formation shift
- Maintained transmission mechanism effectiveness even amid supply-side shocks

## 3. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

[RAW] Ball and Sheridan (2003) identified when ITF benefits may not materialize:
1. **Structural supply shocks** that monetary policy cannot control (stagflation scenarios)
2. **Emerging market vulnerabilities**: Exchange rate volatility, financial sector fragility
3. **Fiscal dominance**: Government budget constraints limit central bank independence
4. **Policy credibility gaps**: Especially in developing countries with political uncertainty
5. **Global economic shifts**: Crisis periods test ITF resilience (GFC exposed framework limitations)

[RAW] Post-GFC realization: ITF necessary but not sufficient—requires macroprudential framework and financial system stability for effectiveness
