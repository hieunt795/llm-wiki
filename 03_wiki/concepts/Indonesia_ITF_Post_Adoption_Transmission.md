## Essence

Upon official adoption of Inflation Targeting Framework in July 2005, Bank Indonesia shifted to an explicit, interest-rate-based operational framework centered on controlling the overnight (O/N) interbank rate through open market operations, with the BI Rate as the announced policy rate.

## Key Components of Post-ITF Framework

### 1. Policy Rate: The BI Rate
[RAW] Based on those considerations, BI chose the BI rate as the policy rate. In terms of OMO, the BI rate was used, until the middle of 2008, as the reference rate for auctions of 1-month SBI. Since 2008, however, the BI rate has been used as a reference rate for the O/N interbank rate.

**Characteristics:**
- **Announced by BI Board** — Monthly decision, published immediately
- **Operational Target** — O/N interbank rate controlled near BI Rate via OMO
- **Communication Vehicle** — Simplest form of transparent monetary policy signaling
- **Reference for Markets** — Banks and corporates use as pricing anchor

### 2. Selection of O/N Interbank Rate

[RAW] In terms of selecting a representative interest rate for the policy rate, there are several schools of thought, specifically concerning the criteria and essence of the policy rate itself. There are at least three salient criteria for interest rate indicators that could be used as the policy rate, namely that the interest rate indicator selected must be controllable by BI, can influence the cost of funds in the banking industry, and can effectively be used as a reference rate by economic players in their economic decision-making process.

**Why O/N?**
1. **Controllability** — BI can reliably steer via daily OMO and signaling
2. **Financial System Penetration** — Banks use for internal pricing of longer-term products
3. **Public Understanding** — Simple, daily observable, market-based (not administrative)

## Transmission Channels Under ITF

### Channel 1: Interest Rate Pass-Through
**BI Rate → O/N Rate → Lending Rates → Investment**

Path: BI announces rate → OMO keeps O/N near target → Banks repricing deposit/loan rates (with 3-6 month lag) → Corporate/household demand responds

**Dependency:** Bank internal conditions (LDR, CAR, NIM, NPLs) determine pass-through speed & magnitude

### Channel 2: Exchange Rate
**BI Rate → Interest Differential → Capital Flows → Rupiah**

Path: Tight policy (high BI Rate) → Higher returns in rupiah assets → Non-resident inflows → Rupiah appreciation → Export competitiveness

**Dependency:** Capital mobility, risk premium, global rates (Fed funds), commodity prices

### Channel 3: Asset Prices & Wealth
**BI Rate → Discount Rates → Stock/Property Valuations → Consumption**

Path: Lower BI Rate → Stock yields fall → Prices rise → Wealth effect on consumption

**Dependency:** Financial market development, household asset holdings, credit availability

### Channel 4: Credit/Bank Capital
**BI Rate → Bank NIM/Profitability → Bank Capital → Loan Supply**

Path: Lower BI Rate → Wider net interest margin (if deposit rates sticky) → Higher bank profits → Improved capital ratios → Expanded credit capacity

Inverse during crisis: Asset shocks → NPLs rise → Capital depleted → Credit rationing despite low rates

### Channel 5: Expectations
**BI Rate Announcement → Inflation Expectations → Wage/Price Setting**

Path: BI credibly targets inflation at announced level → Wage bargainers expect stable inflation → Pricing less aggressive → Actual inflation converges to target

**Dependency:** Central bank credibility, actual inflation track record, media coverage clarity

## Challenges & Complications for Indonesia

### 1. Floating Exchange Rate Volatility
[RAW] Under the floating exchange rate system, rupiah exchange rate fluctuations on the market became particularly vulnerable to market sentiment. To reduce excessive currency shocks, BI conducted intervention policy, at that time, by focusing on the lower bound of Net International Reserves (NIR).

**Implication:** Exchange rate channel can dominate, especially during external stress; BI faces policy trade-offs.

### 2. Open Economy with Volatile Capital Flows
[RAW] In an open economy, MPTM is also influenced by economic and financial developments in other countries, among others, through changes in the exchange rate, export and import volume, as well as the magnitude of capital inflows and outflows.

**Post-2005 reality:** Advanced country monetary divergence (US, ECB, BoJ) directly impacts rupiah cycles via carry trades, EM reallocation flows.

### 3. Bank-Dependent Credit System
[RAW] For economies which are still dominated by the banking industry, MPTM through the interest rate, money supply, and credit channels will play an important role.

**Implication:** Bank capital constraints binding; credit channel critical (non-bank financing limited); asymmetric transmission pronounced.

### 4. Structural Economic Changes
[RAW] The choice of policy strategy will depend on the characteristics of the respective economy and the monetary policy response. For example, Indonesia's economy is unique and has experienced economic gyrations over the past three decades. Paying due consideration to large macroeconomic fluctuations as a consequence of structural changes in Indonesia, particularly during the past decade, along with the reorientation of monetary policy strategy with the final target of price stability, monetary policy strategy formulation using state-contingent rules, which can accommodate rules and discretion, is incredibly relevant.

**Issue:** One-size-fits-all Taylor rule insufficient; BI must adapt to commodity cycles, credit dynamics.
