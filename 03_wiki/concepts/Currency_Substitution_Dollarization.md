# [[Currency_Substitution_Dollarization]]

## Thesis

[RAW] High and variable inflation seriously impairs a currency's ability to function as a store of value, unit of account, and means of exchange. As a result, the domestic currency in high-inflation countries tends to be abandoned in favor of a stable currency (often the U.S. dollar) — a phenomenon referred to as currency substitution or "dollarization."

## 1. Mechanism of Dollarization

[RAW] In high-inflation countries, residents find it less risky and often more profitable to keep assets in foreign currency:
- **Store of value function collapses:** Domestic currency loses real value rapidly
- **Portfolio shift:** Residents shift wealth from money to real assets (property, gold, commodities, foreign currencies) — all more stable in real terms
- **Chronification:** Even when inflation falls substantially, many economies remain highly dollarized (dollarization is sticky/hysteretic)

[RAW] Latin American economies provide the reference case: chronic inflation, sustained by accommodative monetary/exchange rate policies and formal/informal indexation, led to increasing dollarization that persisted well after disinflation programs.

## 2. Dollarization in Transition Economies

[RAW] Chronic inflation fueled by accommodative monetary/fiscal policies and sustained by indexation has led to pervasive dollarization in many transition economies. **The dollarization ratio** = foreign currency deposits / broad money.

| Phase | Dollarization ratio |
|---|---|
| Start of reform programs | 0–10% |
| Peak dollarization | 30–60% |
| Post-stabilization (gradual recovery) | Declining, but persistent |

[RAW] Poland: dollarization reached ~80% in 1989 (ratio of FX deposits to broad money) at peak of inflation. Since then, ratio fell sharply through the disinflation program, though it rose somewhat in 1993 before resuming its decline.

**Dollarization is frequently underestimated:** In the absence of data on foreign cash held by the public and on foreign exchange deposited abroad, estimates are based on domestic foreign currency deposits only. Dollarization ratios frequently understate the true phenomenon.

## 3. Consequences of Dollarization

### (a) Impaired Monetary Control
[RAW] Currency substitution substantially undermines the authorities' ability to conduct monetary policy — the foreign currency component of the total money supply **cannot be directly controlled**. The relevant money supply includes both domestic and foreign currency components, but the CB can only manage the domestic component.

### (b) Amplified Fiscal Inflation Effects
[RAW] To the extent that currency substitution reflects a shift away from domestic money, it will **exacerbate the inflationary consequences of a fiscal deficit**, making fiscal discipline all the more important. The inflation tax yield falls (the base of domestic money that is taxed shrinks), but the government's financing need remains — forcing higher monetary expansion rates to generate the same seigniorage, generating more inflation, generating more dollarization — a self-reinforcing spiral.

### (c) Positive Role of FX as Domestic Store of Value
[RAW] Dollarization provides citizens with a reliable store of value during high-inflation episodes — a positive result. Foreign currency holdings by residents are a close substitute for local currency and should therefore be included in a measure of money (broad money M2 should include FX deposits).

## 4. Anti-Dollarization Measures and Their Failure

[RAW] The Latin American experience suggests that combating dollarization with artificial measures backfires:
- **Issuing indexed domestic financial instruments** (to compete with FX on real return) → may magnify eventual inflation "explosion" if indexation spreads
- **Forcing conversion of foreign assets** (confiscating FX) → destroys confidence, triggers capital flight, worsens the very problem it targets

[RAW] **The correct approach:** Sound financial and fiscal policies increase holdings of domestic-currency assets without government mandates. Countries that maintained aggressive interest rate policies (keeping real deposit rates positive) restored confidence in the domestic currency relatively quickly.

## 5. Exchange Rate Regime Interaction

[RAW] Under a **fixed exchange rate**, the money supply becomes endogenous — the CB cannot control M2. Dollarization further erodes controllability:
- FX deposits are inside M2 but outside CB control
- Capital inflows (foreign residents purchasing domestic FX deposits) affect M2 without MA action

Under a **floating exchange rate**, the depreciation channel provides an additional transmission from money creation to prices — but in highly dollarized economies, depreciation raises the domestic-currency cost of FX liabilities, immediately increasing the financial stress on dollar-indebted residents (a "balance sheet effect").

## 6. IMF Exchange Rate and Capital Flow Responses

[RAW] In practice, most countries respond to capital flows (and associated dollarization dynamics) with a combination:
1. **Partial intervention** (buy some capital inflow, allow some nominal appreciation)
2. **Partial sterilization** (offset some NFA increase with OMO)
3. **Allow some monetary base expansion** (accept some inflation and real exchange rate appreciation)

[RAW] Currency boards (Estonia, Lithuania) created fixed exchange rate systems with no sterilization option — reserve money can only be created if fully backed by foreign exchange holdings. This eliminates the dollarization-fueled monetary policy dilemma but surrenders all monetary autonomy; adjustment requires nominal price/wage flexibility and real interest rate increases.

## 7. Treasury Insight

[LLM] Dollarization is one of the most persistent structural features of EM monetary systems:

1. **Dollarization hysteresis:** Even after successful disinflation, dollarization ratios decline slowly. Residents who switched to FX deposits during high inflation maintain the habit and the institutional infrastructure (bank FX accounts, FX payment mechanisms). This implies that the effective money multiplier on domestic currency remains lower than in non-dollarized economies, complicating monetary programming.

2. **Dollarization as fiscal signal:** When dollarization rises sharply (measured by FX deposit growth outpacing domestic deposit growth), this is a real-time signal that the public expects inflation to accelerate — often the earliest available market signal of fiscal sustainability concerns. Bond investors should monitor the dollarization ratio as a leading indicator, not just the headline CPI.

3. **Balance sheet dollarization risk:** In highly dollarized economies, a sharp depreciation of the domestic currency creates a balance sheet crisis for borrowers with FX liabilities and domestic-currency incomes. This is the mechanism behind several EM currency crises where the exchange rate depreciation triggered a banking crisis — the "original sin" (Eichengreen-Hausmann) of borrowing in foreign currency. Policymakers face a dilemma: defend the exchange rate (depleting reserves) or let it go (triggering balance sheet destruction).

4. **IMF program adjustment:** In highly dollarized economies, NDA ceilings need to account for the possibility that the public will shift between domestic and FX deposits — changing the M2 composition without changing its level. Tracking both domestic-currency M2 and total M2 (including FX deposits) simultaneously is required.
