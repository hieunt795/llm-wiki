# [[GFS_Government_Sector_Definition]]

## Thesis

[RAW] The GFS Manual identifies several layers of government: (i) the central government; (ii) the state or regional government; (iii) local government; and (iv) any supranational authority. Government finance statistics exclude from both definitions any banking or monetary transactions the government performs.

## 1. Hierarchical Structure

[RAW]

| Level | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Central government** | Formulates national budgets; minimum coverage for most fiscal analyses |
| **General government** | All levels: central, state, provincial, and local |
| **Public sector** | General government + nonfinancial public enterprises (railways, airlines, utilities) |

**Critical exclusion:** All functions of the monetary authorities — irrespective of the institution carrying them out — are treated as activities of the monetary sector, not the government sector.

## 2. Three Groups of Operations at Each Level

[RAW]

- **Budgetary operations** — covered in the budget by definition.
- **Extrabudgetary operations** — carried out outside the budget; raise resources through compulsory levies and provide nonmarket goods and services. Example: a fund that collects a fuel tax and uses the revenue to maintain roads.
- **Social security schemes** — a special category of extrabudgetary operations. In transition economies, typically comprise a pension fund, an employment fund, and a social insurance fund covering disability and welfare programs.

## 3. Consolidation

[RAW] Some central government revenues and expenditures reflect transfers to/from local governments and public enterprises. Such transfers must be **netted out** so that double counting is not an issue when aggregates for different levels of government are compiled. The process of netting out and combining accounts at different levels is called **consolidation**.

## 4. Fiscal Distortions in Practice

### Off-Budget / Extrabudgetary Accounts

[RAW] In some countries, particularly transition economies, governments set up off-budget or extrabudgetary accounts to conduct what would otherwise be budgetary operations. Problems arising from extensive use:
- Distorts the fiscal position.
- Reduces flexibility in fiscal management by earmarking revenues for specific purposes.
- Leads to loss of control over funds channeled through these accounts.

**Remedy:** Many countries have consolidated extrabudgetary accounts into the budgetary framework to maintain control, accountability, and parliamentary scrutiny.

### Counterpart Funds

[RAW] The domestic currency equivalent of foreign loans or grants (including commodity grants). These funds are often:
- A source of financing for the deficit.
- On-lent to institutions and enterprises.
- Often not recorded in the budget, escaping parliamentary scrutiny.
- Generating interest subsidies that distort the overall fiscal position.

[RAW] Not only must counterpart funds be properly reflected in any measure of the budget, but they must not become an indirect source of subsidized credit for ailing enterprises.

## 5. GFS Accounting Method: Cash vs. Accrual

[RAW] Government finance statistics compiled under GFS standards record transactions on a **cash (payment) basis** — as of the time of monetary settlement. This differs from the **accrual basis** used in the SNA (national income accounts, monetary accounts, BOP), where transactions are recorded when a claim or liability arises or becomes due.

**Why this matters:** The difference between cash and accrual deficits stems primarily from **payment arrears**. A government with a cash deficit smaller than the accrual deficit is behind on its interest payments and other obligations. When arrears are cleared, the accrual deficit will be smaller than the cash deficit.

**Operational implication:** Cash-basis GFS data allow direct comparison with monetary accounts, making it easier to measure the government's impact on monetary aggregates.

## 6. Revenue vs. Receipt Distinction

[RAW] Key GFS principle: **all revenues are receipts, but not all receipts are revenues**.

- **Revenues** = receipts that do not give rise to an obligation of repayment. Loan receipts from the government are not revenues.
- **Expenditures** = payments other than repayment of principal. Interest payments are expenditures; loan repayments are not.
- **Grants** = unrequited receipts from other governments or international institutions. GFS groups grants with revenues as reducing the deficit, but because they are not predictable or sustainable, analytical presentations often treat them as financing items.

## 7. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For fiscal and macro practitioners, the GFS coverage boundary determines what gets counted in program fiscal targets. The most common traps:

1. **Extrabudgetary funds excluded from headline deficit** — even though they may run large deficits of their own. In Poland, extrabudgetary funds (especially the Social Insurance Fund) accounted for over 60% of state budget expenditures.
2. **Counterpart fund on-lending not reflected above the line** — interest subsidies and quasi-grants embedded in below-market on-lending rates create hidden expenditures that inflate future deficits when recognized.
3. **Cash vs. accrual gap** — monitoring only the cash deficit during arrears accumulation understates the true fiscal deterioration. The accrual-to-cash deficit gap is the accumulation of payment arrears: monitoring this gap is essential for assessing whether fiscal adjustment is real or deferred.
