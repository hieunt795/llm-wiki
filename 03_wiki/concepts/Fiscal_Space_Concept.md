## Thesis
Fiscal space is operationally defined as the gap between **actual demand** and **potential demand at full employment**. A government can spend without inflation as long as idle labor and capital exist. The traditional balance-sheet constraint (debt limits) is irrelevant for sovereign currency issuers.

## 1. Core Logic / Mechanism

[RAW] The **Full Employment Fiscal Deficit Condition** states that fiscal policy has "space" to run deficits whenever the economy has **involuntary unemployment**.

**Core Principle:**
- At full employment (e.g., 4% unemployment, all capital utilized), additional fiscal spending will bid up prices—inflation emerges.
- Below full employment (e.g., 8% unemployment, slack in capital), the same fiscal spending creates jobs and real output growth—no inflation.

Therefore:
$$\text{Fiscal Space} = \text{Potential Output} - \text{Actual Output}$$

**Operationally:**
- If actual output is 5% below potential (a **negative output gap**), the government can inject ~5% of GDP in spending without inflation.
- Once actual output reaches potential, further spending capacity = 0 (unless productivity/supply increases).

[LLM] **Sectoral Balance Framework:**

In a closed economy with three sectors (Government, Private, Foreign), the sectoral balances must sum to zero:

$$\text{(G - T)} + (I - S) + (X - M) = 0$$

Where:
- $G - T$ = government deficit (-)
- $I - S$ = private sector deficit (-)
- $X - M$ = current account balance

**Fiscal Space Interpretation:**
- If the private sector is saving (wants $I < S$), the government must run a deficit ($G > T$) to absorb that saving.
- If the private sector is borrowing ($I > S$), the government can run a smaller deficit or even a surplus.
- The "right" deficit size depends on private behavior and trade balances—**not on arbitrary debt limits**.

**Example (USA, post-2008):**
- Households deleveraging ($I < S$, saving aggressively).
- Trade deficit (imports exceed exports).
- Government deficit must be large ($G > T$) to avoid demand collapse.
- In this context, reducing the deficit would worsen unemployment (pro-cyclical).

## 2. Worked Example (Fiscal Space Sizing)

[RAW] Consider an economy with:
- Potential annual output: $2,000 billion
- Actual output: $1,800 billion (output gap = 10%)
- Unemployment rate: 8%
- Inflation rate: 2.5% (stable)

**Fiscal Space Calculation:**
1. **Output Gap:** $2,000 - $1,800 = $200B (10% slack).
2. **Multiplier:** Assume government spending multiplier = 1.5x (typical mid-range estimate).
3. **Fiscal Injection Needed:** $200B ÷ 1.5 = ~$133B.
4. **As % of GDP:** $133B ÷ $1,800B ≈ 7.4% of actual GDP.

**Fiscal Space Summary:**
- Government can increase spending by 7.4% without inflation.
- This might mean $100–150B in new infrastructure, transfers, or hiring programs.
- As demand rises and unemployment falls, the multiplier weakens (people are already employed), and the injection needs shrink.

**Constraint Check:**
- Suppose government currently runs a balanced budget.
- Increasing spending by 7.4% → deficit rises to ~7.4% of GDP.
- Is this "sustainable"? Yes, under functional finance: as unemployment falls and tax revenue rises (from higher incomes), the deficit naturally shrinks.

## 3. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

**When Fiscal Space Disappears:**

1. **True Full Employment:** When unemployment reaches the NAIRU (non-accelerating inflation rate of unemployment, ~3–4% in most developed economies) and capacity utilization is high, fiscal space = 0. Further spending is inflationary.

2. **Supply-Side Constraints:** If productive capacity is rigid (insufficient skilled labor, shortage of intermediate goods), fiscal space is overstated. Supply-side policies (education, R&D) must expand capacity first.

3. **External Currency Debt:** If fiscal deficits are financed in foreign currency (not the home currency), external constraints bind. The government cannot "print" USD to pay USD-denominated debt; it must earn export revenue or attract foreign investment.

4. **Exchange Rate Passthrough:** In open economies with high import shares, fiscal expansion may weaken the currency and raise import prices → inflation faster than models predict.

5. **Credibility / Inflation Expectations:** If past deficits have triggered inflation and unanchored expectations, then even below-full-employment deficits will be inflationary (expectations become self-fulfilling).
