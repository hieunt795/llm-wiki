## Thesis

Free banking advocates argue that competition among private banks to issue banknotes would naturally enforce prudence, as customers would only hold notes from the most stable institutions. Central banking advocates argue that such a system is inherently fragile and leads to systemic collapses without a Lender of Last Resort.

## Comparison of Paradigms

### 1. Free Banking (Market Discipline)
- **Mechanism:** Banks issue their own non-standardized paper notes.
- **Pros:** High pressure on bank managers to maintain massive reserves to prove stability. No "Moral Hazard" from central bank bailouts.
- **Cons:** High transaction costs (checking the credit of every banknote) and high frequency of bank runs.
- **Historical Outcome:** Tended to end in "trouble" and consolidation into central systems.

### 2. Central Banking (Systemic Stability)
- **Mechanism:** A single, publicly chartered institution provides standardized legal tender.
- **Pros:** Eliminates interbank convertibility risk and provides an elastic money supply via LOLR.
- **Cons:** Risk of **Inflation Bias** and government manipulation. Creates "Moral Hazard" where private banks take excessive risks knowing the CB will save them.

## The Modern Compromise
Most modern economies use a hybrid: **Private creation of Inside Money** (deposits) managed under the **Regulatory Umbrella** of a central bank.
