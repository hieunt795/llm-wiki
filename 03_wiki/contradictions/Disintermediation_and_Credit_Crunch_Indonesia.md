## Thesis

The 1997 crisis left a "Scarring Effect" on Indonesia's transmission mechanism. Even when BI lowered rates aggressively (e.g., in the early 2000s), banks refused to lower lending rates or increase loan volumes. This **Decoupling** meant that liquidity remained trapped in the financial sector (buying SBIs) instead of reaching the real economy.

## Causes of the Credit Crunch

1. **Bank Internal Conditions:** High non-performing loans (NPLs) and the need to recapitalize made banks extremely risk-averse.
2. **Corporate Restructuring:** The "Demand Side" was broken. Many firms were too indebted to take on new loans, regardless of how low the interest rate was.
3. **Asymmetric Interest Rate Transmission:** Banks were quick to raise lending rates when BI tightened, but slow (or stagnant) when BI eased—a phenomenon of **Downward Inelasticity**.
4. **The SBI Trap:** Banks preferred the "Risk-Free" return of BI's own certificates (SBI) over the "Risky" return of lending to MSMEs, creating a perverse incentive against intermediation.

## Policy Response
This failure forced BI to move toward a **Policy Mix**: using macroprudential tools (like LTV and CCyB) to "push" banks to lend, recognizing that interest rates alone were being ignored by the market.
