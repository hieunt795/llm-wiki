## Thesis

Before the 2008 GFC, the dominant paradigm (the **Greenspan/Jackson Hole Consensus**) advocated for "Clean." Post-GFC, the consensus has shifted toward "Lean," recognizing that the costs of cleaning up a systemic collapse are far higher than the efficiency losses of leaning.

## The Two Positions

### 1. The "Clean" Position (Jackson Hole Consensus)
- **Proponents:** Alan Greenspan, Ben Bernanke (pre-2008).
- **Arguments:**
    - Bubbles are impossible to identify in real-time.
    - Interest rate hikes are a "blunt instrument" that could crash the real economy while failing to stop a bubble.
    - Central banks should focus only on price stability and provide liquidity *after* a bubble bursts to "clean up" the mess.

### 2. The "Lean" Position (Leaning Against the Wind)
- **Proponents:** BIS, Reserve Bank of Australia, Perry Warjiyo.
- **Arguments:**
    - Low interest rates for long periods foster "irrational exuberance" and excessive leverage.
    - Procyclicality means that by the time a bubble bursts, the financial system is too fragile to be easily cleaned.
    - Gradual rate hikes can signal the central bank's concern and dampen speculative credit growth.

## Boundaries / Conditions

### The Macroprudential Alternative
Perry Warjiyo notes that the debate is being redefined by **Macroprudential Policy**. Instead of using the blunt tool of interest rates to "lean," central banks can use surgical tools like **LTV (Loan-to-Value)** or **CCyB (Countercyclical Capital Buffers)**. This allows the policy rate to focus on inflation (Clean/ITF) while macroprudential tools focus on the bubble (Lean).
