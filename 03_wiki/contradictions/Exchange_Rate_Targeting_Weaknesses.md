## Thesis

While exchange rate targeting successfully lowered inflation in the 1980s (e.g., UK/France pegging to the Deutsche Mark), it creates a "Fragile Stability." The regime forces the domestic interest rate to mirror the anchor country's, regardless of domestic unemployment or growth conditions.

## Key Vulnerabilities

### 1. The Domestic-External Conflict
When the anchor country experiences a shock (e.g., German Reunification in 1990) and raises rates to fight inflation, all pegged countries must also raise rates. This can trigger recessions and soaring unemployment in the pegged countries (as seen in the UK and France in 1991).

### 2. Speculative Attacks
Once markets detect that a government's political will to maintain high interest rates is weakening (due to high unemployment), they launch speculative attacks.
- **The UK Case (Sept 1992):** The UK was forced to leave the ERM because the cost of defending the pound (high rates during a recession) became socially and politically intolerable.

### 3. Balance Sheet Trap in EMEs
In Emerging Markets, exchange rate stability often encourages massive unhedged foreign debt.
- **The 1997 Asian Crisis:** When structural problems forced a depreciation, the real value of USD-denominated debt spiked, causing a systemic collapse of corporate and bank balance sheets.
