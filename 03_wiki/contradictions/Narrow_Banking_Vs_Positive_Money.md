## Thesis

While **Banking Separation** aims to make private money creation safer, **Narrow Banking** views the process itself as inherently dangerous. These proposals argue that only the central government (or central bank) should have the right to issue money, stripping commercial banks of their power to create deposits through lending.

## Comparison of Paradigms

### 1. Modern Banking (Fractional/Credit-based)
- **Money Creation:** Commercial banks create **Inside Money**.
- **Risk:** Vulnerable to bank runs and procyclical bubbles.
- **Advantage:** Market-based allocation of credit.

### 2. Narrow Banking (Positive Money)
- **Money Creation:** Prohibited for commercial banks. All money is **Outside Money** issued by the state.
- **Mechanism:** Banks would essentially become "Mutual Funds," only lending out money that has already been saved. They cannot create new deposits.
- **Political Impact:** Results in total government control over the money supply and, by extension, the allocation of credit in society.

## Research Insight: The Trade-off
Perry Warjiyo and Alexander Düring both note that Narrow Banking ignores the **Fluctuations in Money Demand**. By fixing the money supply administratively, the government might fail to provide the necessary credit for economic growth, or misallocate it toward politically favored sectors rather than productive ones.
