## Thesis

The central bank's interpretation of the [[Quantity_Equation_Of_Money]] differential ($dP$) dictates its operational framework. While both schools agree that price stability is the goal, they diverge sharply on the causal precedence of the variables $M$ (money) and $Y$ (output). Alexander Düring argues that this theoretical conflict is not merely academic, but leads to fundamentally different communication strategies and legal challenges (particularly in the Eurozone).

## Mechanism

The conflict manifests in the logical precedence assigned to the terms in the differential equation:
$$dP = f(dV, dM, dY)$$

### 1. The Keynesian View (Focus on $dY$)
Keynesians view real variables as the central control mechanism for the economy.
- **The indicator:** The **Output Gap**—the difference between actual and potential output.
- **The mechanism:** Because the output gap is assumed to be mean-reverting, central banks use measures like unemployment and capacity utilization to set the policy rate relative to the **Natural Rate of Interest** ($r^*$).
- **Money's role:** The $dM$ term is seen as reacting *passively* to economic activity. In this view, money is an endogenous outcome of demand, not an exogenous policy tool. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=43]]

### 2. The Monetarist View (Focus on $dM$)
Monetarists reject the central bank's ability to intervene in "real" variables.
- **The indicator:** Money growth targets (e.g., targeting a specific growth rate for M3).
- **The rejection:** The output gap is rejected as a policy indicator because it is **unobservable in real-time**. Monetarists argue that the Taylor Rule works well as an ex-post explanation but is useless for real-time forecasting.
- **Money's role:** $dM$ is paramount. The central bank's sole duty is to provide a predictable volume of money; $dY$ will then take care of itself through market forces. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=44]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The ECB "Two-Pillar" Compromise
When the European Central Bank (ECB) was established in 1999, it faced the impossible task of choosing between these two mutually exclusive worldviews.
- **The Solution:** It adopted both. The ECB's framework consists of a **Monetary Analysis** (Monetarist M3 pillar) and an **Economic Analysis** (potentially Keynesian inflation/output gap pillar).
- **The Shift:** Over time, the ECB has shifted from a narrow focus on M3 toward Keynesian-style communication regarding the "slack in the economy" (the output gap). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=45]]

### The Legal Conflict
In Germany, legal challenges against the ECB often stem from this Monetarist tradition. Plaintiffs allege that by acting in ways that affect real variables (like $Y$), the ECB oversteps its mandate, which Monetarists believe should be limited strictly to nominal stability ($M$ and $P$). To a Keynesian, this separation is an absurdity. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=44]]
