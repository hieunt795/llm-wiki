## Case Study Overview

In September 1992, amid political turmoil surrounding the Maastricht Treaty ratification in France and a lack of coordinated European central bank efforts, George Soros and other speculators attacked the British Pound within the European Exchange Rate Mechanism (ERM). The Bank of England attempted a desperate defense and became the first central bank to be forced out of the ERM, exhausting approximately USD 15 billion in reserves on a single day. [RAW]

## Crisis Timeline

**System Design (1979):** The European Monetary System (EMS) linked member currencies to the ECU (European Currency Unit), requiring central bank intervention when exchange rates reached 75% of maximum-allowable divergence. The system held until 1992. [RAW]

**September 1992 Attack:** Political uncertainty regarding France's Maastricht ratification created market doubt about the sustainability of fixed parities. George Soros and others launched coordinated speculative attacks on the Pound and Italian Lira. [RAW]

**Black Wednesday (September 16, 1992):** In a single day, the Bank of England squandered approximately USD 15 billion worth of foreign exchange reserves defending the Pound. Despite these efforts, the reserve depletion forced the Bank to abandon the ERM, with the Pound and Italian Lira exiting simultaneously. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=240]]

## Evidence: The Transmission Channels

### 1. Credibility Collapse
The loss of coordinated efforts among EU central banks and public doubt about the Bank of England's ability to defend sterling created a self-fulfilling prophecy. Market participants anticipated the peg's collapse and moved to sell before devaluation. [RAW]

### 2. Reserve Depletion
On Black Wednesday alone, the Bank of England depleted USD 15 billion in forex reserves attempting to purchase Pounds and defend the fixed rate. This represents the mechanical limit of unilateral reserve defense—a finite resource exhausted in hours. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=240]]

### 3. Speculator Profits
George Soros's short Pound and short Italian Lira positions, combined with additional bets on Swedish Krona and other currency and equity markets, generated approximately USD 2 billion in profit on September 16 alone. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=240]]

### 4. Contagion and Exit
The failure to defend the Pound triggered immediate withdrawal of Italian Lira from the ERM. The system's symmetry (all currencies linked) meant that the collapse of one peg threatened the credibility of all others. [RAW]

## Evidence: Why Reserve Defense Failed

**Insufficient Reserves:** The Bank of England held approximately USD 40-50 billion in total reserves at the time. Sustained speculative selling of Pounds could create selling pressure exceeding the capacity to absorb (even billions per day). [RAW]

**Timing Constraint:** A 24-hour window limits the number of intervention operations a central bank can execute. If the daily selling exceeds daily intervention capacity, the peg becomes indefensible. [RAW]

**Credibility Threshold:** Once speculators believe a peg will break, the forward premium (expected devaluation) exceeds any interest rate differential the central bank can offer to discourage selling. The rational expectation of loss dominates carry considerations. [RAW]

## Evidence: Monetary Insights

Soros himself articulated the mechanics at the time: [RAW]

> "Fixed exchange rate systems are also flawed, because they eventually fall apart. In fact, any exchange rate system is flawed and the longer it exists the greater the flaws become... The only escape is to have no exchange rate system at all, but a single currency in Europe, as in the U.S. It would put speculators like me out of business, but I would be delighted to make the sacrifice." [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=240]]

This statement encapsulates the **reflexivity principle**: market participants' beliefs about the unsustainability of a peg become self-fulfilling, creating the very instability they anticipated. [RAW]

## Boundaries / Failure Conditions

**Exchange control:** Theoretically, the Bank of England could have imposed capital controls or FX restrictions. However, such measures typically cause greater long-term economic damage and were politically infeasible within the European framework. [RAW]

**Unlimited central bank lending:** The EMS/ERM lacked a supranational lender of last resort with unlimited access to foreign currency. Each central bank defended with its own reserves alone. [RAW]

**Coordination failure:** Unlike during the 1985 Plaza Accord (when G5 central banks coordinated to devalue), the 1992 ERM crisis saw lack of unified defense policy—some central banks raised interest rates (defense), others did not. [RAW]
