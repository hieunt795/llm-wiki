## Case Study Overview

The Russian Debt Crisis of 1998 was triggered by a **formal devaluation** of the Russian Ruble on August 17, 1998, followed immediately by **sovereign default** on Ruble-denominated debt. Unlike the ERM and Asian crises (which featured speculative attack against a defended peg), the Russian crisis featured the government itself unilaterally renouncing both its currency support and its debt obligations. The panic selling of remaining Russian securities cascaded to the Ruble's 80% depreciation and systemic contagion. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

## Crisis Timeline

**August 17, 1998 (Devaluation + Default):** The Russian government formally devalued the Ruble and defaulted on Ruble-denominated debt. Unlike a gradual currency depreciation, this was a **discrete shock**—a sudden recognition that the ruble peg could not be maintained and that liabilities would not be serviced. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

**August-September 1998 (Panic Sell-Off):** Following the government's default announcement, panic security sell-offs in Russia accelerated, exacerbating the Ruble's decline. As sellers rushed to liquidate positions before further losses, the Ruble fell without stabilization. [RAW]

**September 2, 1998 (Ruble Float):** The Ruble floated without support. Within a short period, it lost 80% of its value: [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

- **Pre-devaluation rate:** 6.29 Rubles per USD
- **Post-devaluation peak:** ~21 Rubles per USD
- **Loss:** 235% depreciation over ~2 weeks

## Evidence: The Transmission Channels

### 1. Sovereign Default as Direct Shock
Unlike speculative crises (where the central bank chooses to defend until reserves run out), the Russian crisis featured a **government decision** to default. This is distinct transmission mechanism: it is not depleted reserves that force the crisis, but willful default. [RAW]

**Mechanism:** Fiscal unsustainability + inability to finance → Default decision announced → Creditor panic → Currency collapse feedback

### 2. Panic Sell-Off Feedback Loop
Once default is announced, the **value of remaining Russian securities becomes uncertain**. Creditors seek to exit Russian positions before further deterioration. The selling creates the very depreciation that creditors feared. [RAW]

**Feedback:** Default announced → Existing Russian securities lose creditworthiness → Marginal holder sells to avoid being last → Cascade of sell orders → Currency accelerates downward → More securities distressed

### 3. Systemic Contagion: LTCM
The Russian crisis became a **systemic risk event** when Russia's unilateral debt renunciation triggered the collapse of **Long Term Capital Management (LTCM)**, one of the largest and most celebrated hedge funds of its era. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

**LTCM's exposure:** The fund had leveraged bets on Russian bonds and other emerging market spreads, assuming that arbitrage opportunities (e.g., buying higher-yielding Russian debt and hedging with lower-yielding USD assets) were low-risk. [RAW]

**The collapse mechanism:**
- Russian default eliminated the hedge (the Russian bond disappeared)
- LTCM's hedge fund positions were exposed (they had sold short high-yielding assets to finance long Russian bonds)
- Margin calls cascaded (as collateral values fell, LTCM faced forced liquidation)
- Liquidation created systemic risk (LTCM's large positions, once unwound, destabilized global repo and credit markets)

The Federal Reserve and major Wall Street banks coordinated a USD 3.6 billion private sector rescue of LTCM to prevent complete collapse. [LLM]

### 4. Contagion to Global Credit Markets
LTCM's distress revealed that *non-Russian* counterparties had absorbed significant counterparty risk from Russian default. As LTCM unwound positions:
- Bid-ask spreads widened across all fixed income markets
- Repo collateral haircuts increased (banks demanded greater safety margins)
- Credit spreads gapped out (lending became more expensive for non-sovereigns)

This temporary credit crunch affected non-Russian, unrelated borrowers. [RAW]

## Evidence: Why Sovereign Default Differs from Speculative Attack

**ERM 1992 / Asian 1997 Model:** Speculators attack a defended peg. Central bank depletes reserves. Peg breaks. Sovereign still has debt-servicing intention (credibility is restored once fundamentals adjust).

**Russian 1998 Model:** Government unilaterally announces it will not service liabilities AND will not defend currency. Both creditor confidence and currency support collapse simultaneously. This is a **coordination failure at the sovereign level**: the government signals that it is abandoning multiple commitments at once. [RAW]

## Evidence: The Magnitude of Depreciation

The 80% Ruble loss (6.29 → 21 Rubles/USD) was catastrophic for:
- **Importers:** USD 1 million import costs 6.29 million Rubles pre-crisis; 21 million Rubles post-crisis (235% increase)
- **Domestic borrowers with USD debt:** Ruble liability values soared
- **Savers:** Ruble savings lost 80% of purchasing power relative to USD

This **external balance-sheet effect** meant that even non-defaulting Russian entities became insolvent if they had FX liabilities. [RAW]

## Worked Example: Transmitting Default to LTCM

LTCM Position (simplified):
- Long: 10-year Russian ruble bond, 25% yield, USD 100 million notional
- Short: 10-year USD Treasury, 5% yield, USD 100 million notional

**Expected profit (annualized):** 20% spread on USD 100 million = USD 20 million/year

**August 17, 1998:** Russia defaults on ruble bonds. [RAW]

- Long position value → USD 0 (or deep restructuring haircut, estimated 50% loss)
- Loss: USD 50 million immediately
- Short position: Treasury still valuable (no credit risk)

**Margin call:** Collateral backing the short Treasury position is now USD 50 million in deficit. LTCM must post additional collateral or liquidate other positions to cover. As LTCM liquidates, it triggers broader market dislocations. [RAW]

## Boundaries / Why Official Rescue Was Necessary

**Size of LTCM:** The fund had USD 4-5 billion in capital but over USD 100 billion in notional positions (20x+ leverage). Uncoordinated liquidation would have created fire-sale conditions across emerging market debt, credit derivatives, and repo markets. [RAW]

**Interconnectedness:** LTCM had counterparty relationships with nearly every major global financial institution. Its collapse threatened cascading defaults among prime brokers and dealers. [RAW]

**No lender of last resort for private sector:** Unlike central banks (which can create base money), private financial institutions cannot create credit on their own. Once a large counterparty fails, creditor confidence can evaporate rapidly. [RAW]
