## Case Study Overview

The Asian Crisis of 1997 originated not primarily as a currency bubble but as an **equity bubble** with FX implications. The proximate cause (summer 1996) was a herd-mentality exodus from Thai equity markets, which triggered speculative attacks on the Thai Baht and subsequent capital flight from Baht-denominated assets. The crisis then **spread regionally** from Thailand to Malaysia, Indonesia, the Philippines, and South Korea through trade linkages and loss of confidence. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

## Crisis Timeline

**Summer 1996:** Thai equity bubble reverses. Western investors flee Thai stock markets amid signs of overvaluation and slowing growth. [RAW]

**1996-1997:** Contagion spreads throughout the region. As the Thai Baht depreciates under selling pressure, neighboring countries' competitive positions weaken, creating further uncertainty about their own currency pegs and asset values. [RAW]

**1997 Regional Impact:** Currency attacks spread to Malaysia, Indonesia, Korea, and the Philippines as investors flee regional assets and convert local currencies to dollars. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

## Evidence: The Transmission Channels

### 1. Equity-First Capital Flight (Not FX-First)
The crisis originated in stock markets, not currency markets. Western investors—particularly "herd" traders—exited Thai equities due to valuation concerns and growth slowing. As positions were liquidated, Thai Baht-denominated assets were sold, creating Baht selling pressure as a *consequence* of equity flight, not as a *cause*. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

**Mechanism:** Equity liquidation → Currency selling → FX crisis feedback loop → further equity declines

### 2. Trade-Linked Regional Contagion
The deterioration of the Thai Baht created negative implications for trade partners and neighbors. Countries dependent on Thai exports or in direct competition with Thai exporters faced:
- Loss of price competitiveness (as Baht depreciated, Thai exports became cheaper)
- Concern that their own pegs were unsustainable (if Thai pegs couldn't hold, why could theirs?)
- Speculative attacks on their own currencies

[RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

**Regional spread pathway:** Thailand → Malaysia → Indonesia → Philippines → South Korea

### 3. Confidence Collapse in Financial System
Stanley Fischer (IMF, 1998) identified the structural vulnerabilities that **enabled** the crisis to spread: [RAW]

> "They (Thailand, Indonesia, and Korea) have all suffered a loss of confidence, and their currencies are deeply depreciated. Moreover, in each country, weak financial systems, excessive unhedged foreign borrowing by the domestic private sector, and a lack of transparency about the ties between government, business, and banks have both contributed to the crisis and complicated efforts to diffuse it." [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

**Key vulnerabilities:**
- Weak financial systems (limited regulatory oversight, high NPLs)
- Excessive unhedged foreign borrowing (corporates borrowed in USD but earned in local currency)
- Lack of transparency (government-business-bank relationships obscured true leverage)

### 4. Sudden Stop and Liquidity Spiral
Once capital flight begins, international lenders abruptly cease rolling over short-term debt. Corporates and banks unable to service dollar-denominated liabilities become insolvent. Currency depreciation worsens their balance sheet position (liability increases in local currency terms). [RAW]

## Evidence: Why Contagion Spread

**Political Reaction (September 1997):** Malaysian Prime Minister Mahathir stated on September 20, 1997, that "currency trading is immoral and should be stopped." George Soros responded the next day: "Dr. Mahathir is a menace to his own country." [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=241]]

This exchange illustrates the **blame deflection:** policymakers viewed the crisis as speculative attack rather than reflection of their own financial system vulnerabilities. However, the speculative attack was *possible* precisely because of those vulnerabilities.

## Boundaries / Failure Conditions

**Limited Reserve Capacity:** Unlike developed countries, Asian central banks lacked sufficient USD reserves to defend pegs simultaneously across the region. Thailand's reserves were depleted defending the Baht. [RAW]

**Unhedged Liability Structure:** The private sector's short-term FX obligations (unhedged) meant that currency depreciation automatically created insolvencies. Central banks could not fix a balance sheet problem with monetary policy. [RAW]

**Coordination Failure:** IMF rescue packages were eventually deployed, but coordination among recipients and between official and private sector creditors was imperfect. [RAW]

## Worked Example: The Unhedged Borrower Trap

A Thai manufacturing corporation borrows USD 100 million at 6% from a U.S. bank. This debt services through Thai Baht export revenues.

**Pre-Crisis:** 1 USD = 25 Baht. Annual interest service = USD 6 million = 150 million Baht.

**Post-Crisis:** 1 USD = 50 Baht (50% depreciation). Annual interest service = USD 6 million = 300 million Baht.

**Result:** Baht-denominated operating cash flows do not double; they may fall due to recession. The corporation becomes insolvent despite no change in USD interest rates. The central bank cannot fix this by printing Baht. [LLM]
