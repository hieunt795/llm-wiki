## Overview

The efficacy of QE remains a subject of intense debate. While the expansion of central bank balance sheets is directly observable, the impact on broader economic indicators like money multipliers and market liquidity varies significantly across jurisdictions. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=67]]

## Evidence: The Money Multiplier Paradox

According to standard monetarist theory, an increase in base money should lead to a proportional increase in broader money aggregates (M2). However, empirical data from major QE episodes shows a significant decline in money multipliers.
- **The Case:** During the QE programs in the US, Japan, and the Eurozone, money multipliers (M2 / Base Money) dropped sharply.
- **The Inference:** This suggests that QE may have bypassed the standard private bank money creation mechanism, or that private money creation was independently declining during the economic shocks that triggered QE. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=67-68]]

## Evidence: Liquidity Velocity (Fedwire vs. Zengin)

Alexander Düring uses central bank reserve transfer volumes as a proxy for money velocity.

### 1. The US Fedwire System
- **Pre-2008:** A single reserve dollar moved approximately **6,500 times** per year through Fedwire.
- **Post-2012:** Velocity plummeted to an average of **28.9 times**.
- **The Insight:** This collapse in velocity supports the idea that the massive expansion of the money supply was offset by a corresponding decline in the frequency of its use, possibly due to lower trust in private bank money. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=69]]

### 2. The Japanese Zengin System
- **The Observation:** Small-value transfer volumes in the Zengin system remained extremely stable (1.7% annual growth) despite the massive expansion of current account holdings at the BoJ since 2012.
- **The Insight:** This confirms the monetarist fear that expanding the base money supply does not automatically translate into higher transactional volume in the real economy. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=69-70]]

## Evidence: The FX Impact

The correlation between QE and currency depreciation is sporadic and often overridden by fundamentals.
- **Japan's Experience:** The Yen depreciated 23% against the USD *before* the BoJ officially started its 2013 QE program, and moved only 7% after the announcement.
- **The Insight:** Fundamentals like trade balances and current account surpluses (persistent in Japan) can offset central bank attempts at currency devaluation. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=71]]
