## Case Study Overview

Argentina's 2002 crisis followed a decade of currency board stability (since April 1, 1991) that had initially brought confidence and eliminated inflation. However, external shocks—particularly Brazil's 30%+ devaluation in 1999 and the U.S. Dollar strength (1999-2001)—combined with mounting sovereign debt to create an unsustainable position. Capital flight and bank runs forced the government to abandon the currency board on January 6, 2002. The Peso lost 50%+ of its value within a month. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242-244]]

## Crisis Timeline

**April 1, 1991 (Currency Board Inception):** Argentina established a Currency Board pegging the Peso 1:1 to the U.S. Dollar. The Central Bank of Argentina was required to hold one Dollar for every outstanding Peso in circulation. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

**1991-1999 (Stability and Growth):** Through the 1990s, Argentina was one of Latin America's most prosperous economies. The currency board succeeded in controlling inflation and instilling fiscal discipline. [RAW]

**1999 (External Shock #1):** Brazil devalued by over 30%, eliminating Argentina's competitiveness in its largest regional trade market. Argentina's exports to Brazil became expensive relative to Brazilian competitors. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242-243]]

**1999-2001 (External Shock #2):** The U.S. Dollar strengthened significantly against the Euro and other major currencies. Because the Argentine Peso was pegged 1:1 to the Dollar, Argentina's currency strengthened alongside the Dollar. This made Argentine exports **more expensive** in non-Dollar markets. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243-244]]

**1999-2001 (Competitive Loss):** The combination of Brazilian devaluation and USD strength devastated Argentina's "open" (export-oriented) sectors. Competitiveness eroded while the currency board prevented peg adjustment. [RAW]

**Late 2001 (Debt Crisis):** Argentina threatened to stop servicing its USD 155 billion in external debt. International investor confidence **evaporated immediately**. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**Late 2001 (Capital Flight + Bank Run):** As confidence in Argentina's ability to service debt collapsed, external investors rushed to withdraw capital. Simultaneously, Argentine residents—fearing the government would freeze assets or inflate away savings—began withdrawing large amounts of Pesos from bank accounts and attempting to convert them into Dollars. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**Government Response (Late 2001):** The government responded to bank runs by **freezing bank accounts** (the "corralito"), restricting withdrawals. This only added to the panic. Residents realized their savings were trapped and increasingly worthless if a currency crisis occurred. [RAW]

**January 6, 2002 (Currency Board Abandoned):** The government formally abandoned the currency board. The Peso was allowed to float against the U.S. Dollar. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**January - February 2002 (Devaluation):** Within one month of floating, the Argentine Peso lost **more than half its value** versus the USD. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**Aftermath (2002):** The devaluation led to:
- **Double-digit inflation** returned (as import prices soared)
- **Widespread unemployment** (domestic demand collapsed)
- **Other economic ills** (output contraction, financial system stress)

[RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**November 14, 2002 (Sovereign Default):** Argentina failed to repay a debt obligation to the World Bank, triggering "credit events" that activated Credit Default Swaps (CDS). [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

## Evidence: The Transmission Channels

### 1. Currency Board as Commitment Device That Becomes a Straitjacket
The currency board initially succeeded in controlling inflation (1991-1999) by removing the government's ability to inflate the currency supply. However, once external shocks hit, the board prevented *necessary adjustment*. [RAW]

**Why adjustment was necessary:**
- Brazil devalued 30% → Argentine exports became relatively expensive
- USD strengthened 1999-2001 → Argentine exports (if priced in non-USD currencies) became more expensive
- These were **real shocks** to competitiveness, requiring either wage deflation or currency depreciation

The currency board allowed only deflation. [RAW]

### 2. Deflation Creates Debt Servicing Impossibility
Wage and price deflation reduces the economic output from which debt is serviced. If the government's Peso-denominated tax revenue is contracting due to deflation, but the dollar-denominated debt is constant, the debt-to-revenue ratio soars. [RAW]

**Example:**
- Government revenue: 1,000 Pesos → Converts to USD 1,000 at 1:1 rate
- External debt: USD 155 billion (constant)
- After deflation: Economic output falls 20%, revenue becomes 800 Pesos → USD 800
- Debt burden *increased* in real terms even though currency board is "maintained"

[RAW]

### 3. Confidence Collapse Triggers Capital Flight
Once investors recognized that the government would struggle to service debt under deflation, they rushed to exit Argentine assets. Foreign direct investment halted. International creditors stopped rolling over short-term debt. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**Capital flight transmission:**
- External investors sell Peso assets → Convert Pesos to Dollars → Peso supply exceeds demand
- Central bank's Dollar reserves deplete (if defending the peg)
- Currency board becomes indefensible (insufficient Dollars to back all Pesos)

### 4. Bank Run as Internal Manifestation
While external capital fled, Argentines themselves withdrew deposits. The **corralito** (account freeze) was the government's desperate attempt to prevent reserve depletion. However, the freeze itself signaled that the Peso peg was doomed—why else freeze accounts unless redenomination to a weaker currency was imminent? [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243]]

**Bank run mechanism:**
- Residents fear Peso redenomination
- Attempt to withdraw Pesos and convert to Dollars
- Government freezes accounts to prevent Peso drain
- Freeze confirms fears (why freeze unless default is near?)
- Those who managed to withdraw before freeze succeed; others trapped

This distributional impact created social unrest (famously, Argentines filmed themselves "cacerolazo"—banging pots and pans—outside banks and government buildings). [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=244]]

### 5. Depreciation as Redenomination Shock
The 50%+ devaluation within a month (Jan-Feb 2002) meant: [RAW]

- Dollar-denominated debts doubled in Peso value instantly
- Peso savings halved in Dollar value instantly
- Importers faced 100%+ import cost increases (Peso depreciation + import price volatility)

## Evidence: Credit Default Swaps Settlement (Novel at the Time)

The November 2002 default on the World Bank obligation was historically significant because it triggered **Credit Default Swaps** settlement in an orderly fashion:

- **Buyers of CDS protection** (those who had hedged Argentine debt exposure) delivered their defaulted securities to CDS sellers
- **Sellers** paid par value (face value) of delivered bonds in exchange
- **Settlement occurred in 2-day T+2 timeframe** in normal capital markets fashion

Unlike traditional bond defaults (which often required months of legal workouts), CDS settlement was **rapid and orderly**. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=243-244]]

This demonstrated that CDS could function as genuine insurance—and that markets could settle default risk without requiring court intervention. [RAW]

## Evidence: Post-Crisis Recovery

Counterintuitively, Argentina rebounded strongly after the crisis: [RAW]

- **Peso weakness was beneficial:** The 50%+ depreciation made Argentine exports highly competitive
- **Commodity boom (2002-2008):** Rising global commodity prices helped Argentina's agricultural and mining exports
- **Real economic growth:** Growth exceeded 10% annually for several years post-crisis
- **Inflation management:** Despite initial double-digit inflation, the government eventually managed price stability

By 2005+, the government was actively managing the **appreciation** of the Peso to prevent excessive strength that would harm competitiveness. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=244]]

This contrasts sharply with many currency crises (1990s Asian, 1998 Russia) where recovery was far slower. The key difference: Argentina had **real assets** (agricultural/mining land) and weak currency eventually made those assets globally competitive. [RAW]

## Worked Example: The Peso Problem

A Buenos Aires import-export business had: [RAW]

**Assets (October 2001):**
- USD 1 million in dollar accounts
- 100 million Pesos in Peso accounts
- Net position: USD 2 million (Pesos + Dollars = USD 2MM equivalent at 1:1 peg)

**December 2001 (Corralito Freeze):**
- Cannot withdraw Peso accounts (government frozen)
- Dollar account remains accessible (for now)
- Real position: USD 1 million + 100M Pesos (frozen, value uncertain)

**February 2002 (After 50% Devaluation):**
- Frozen Peso account: Still 100 million Pesos, but now worth only USD 500K (at 2:1 peg)
- Dollar account: USD 1 million (unchanged)
- Net position: USD 1.5 million (vs. USD 2MM before)

**Reputational/Trust Impact:** The business lost 25% of its net worth through no fault of its own—merely by holding Pesos. This lesson ensured Argentines became strong Dollar-holders for a generation. [RAW]

## Boundaries / Why This Crisis Was Structural

**Currency Board Rigidity:** A floating currency or a peg with adjustment capacity could have devalued gradually as Brazil weakened. The currency board eliminated this flexibility. [RAW]

**Original Sin / Unhedged Liability Structure:** The government's dollar-denominated debt, combined with Peso-denominated tax revenue, meant the government itself was a net short-Peso, long-Dollar position. Once the Peso weakened, the government's debt burden increased. [RAW]

**Lack of International Lender of Last Resort:** Unlike euro members (who could theoretically call on ECB), Argentina stood alone. An international reserve currency facility might have extended liquidity, preventing the full cascade. [RAW]
