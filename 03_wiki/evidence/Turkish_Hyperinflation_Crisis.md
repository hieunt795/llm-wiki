## Case Study Overview

The Turkish Lira crisis of 2001 differed fundamentally from the ERM, Asian, and Russian crises: the currency collapse stemmed primarily from **political controversy and loss of confidence in the banking system**, rather than market pressure or reserve depletion. The crisis was initiated by a political clash, not by an economic fundamental triggering speculative attack. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

## Crisis Timeline

**February 19, 2001 (Political Shock):** President Ahmet Necdet Sezer and Prime Minister Bulent Ecevit clashed in a National Security Council meeting regarding Turkey's prospects for EU membership. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

**February 19-20, 2001 (Confidence Collapse):** Following the political clash, personal attacks and allegations of political corruption emerged. Investigations began into the "graft ridden," "corruption-plagued" state-owned banking system. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

**February 2001 - October 2001 (Hyperinflation):** The Lira entered a cascading depreciation that lasted months, not days (unlike 1992 or 1998). [RAW]

- **February 2001:** 1 USD = 650,000 Turkish Lira
- **October 21, 2001:** 1 USD = 1,678,822 Turkish Lira
- **Depreciation:** 158% in ~8 months

[RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

**November 2001 onwards:** The IMF dispatched a "swat team" to stabilize the situation. [RAW]

**January 1, 2005:** Turkey redenominated, introducing the New Turkish Lira (TRY) at 1 million old TRL = 1 TRY, eliminating the psychological impact of hyperinflation-denominated currency. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

## Evidence: The Transmission Channels

### 1. Political Shock to Institutional Confidence
Unlike speculative currency attacks (which respond to perceived fundamental imbalance), the Turkish crisis originated in **loss of confidence in political leadership**. The public clash between the president and prime minister signaled potential instability in economic policy decisions. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

**Mechanism:** Political conflict → Doubt about policy credibility → Expectation that CBT (Central Bank of Turkey) may be pressured to abandon price stability → Currency selling

### 2. Banking System Corruption as Trigger
Allegations of corruption in the "graft ridden" state-owned banking system created immediate concern about:
- Who owns the exposed bad assets?
- What is the true capital position of Turkish banks?
- Will the government bail them out (inflation)?
- Are Turkish deposits safe?

This rapidly morphed into a **banking crisis within a currency crisis**. [RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

### 3. Pre-Existing Macroeconomic Weaknesses
Turkey already suffered from:
- Particularly **high inflation** (reaching double digits annually)
- **Massive government debt** (unsustainable fiscal position)
- **Negative real economic growth** (recession)
- **Serious unemployment** (labor market weakness)

[RAW] [[Weithers_Foreign_Exchange_Guide.md#page=242]]

These weaknesses meant that once confidence eroded, there was no underlying economic strength to support the currency. A speculative attack on the ERM faced credible central bank defenses (until reserves ran out); a speculative attack on the Lira faced a government with no credible solution.

### 4. Feedback Loop: Depreciation → Inflation Acceleration
As the Lira depreciated, **import prices soared**. Turkey imports oil and many industrial goods. Import-driven inflation accelerated, validating the original fear that the government would use monetary expansion. [RAW]

**Vicious cycle:** Currency depreciates → Import inflation rises → Inflation expectations rise → Currency sells off further → Further import price increases

## Evidence: Dollarization as Informal De-Facto Policy

As Lira confidence collapsed, Turkish residents and businesses **shifted to holding and transacting in US Dollars**. This informal dollarization meant: [RAW]

- Savers moved Lira bank deposits into Dollar accounts or physical dollars
- Businesses began pricing in Dollars (instead of Lira)
- Banks faced deposit flight (Lira deposits withdrew, fewer Dollar deposits came in)
- The money supply effectively contracted in Lira terms

The government could not prevent this without capital controls. By accepting informal dollarization, the state ceded monetary control to the market. [RAW]

## Evidence: The IMF "Swat Team" and Policy Reversal

The IMF intervention (November 2001) involved a comprehensive restructuring: [RAW]

- **Banking sector recapitalization:** Closing insolvent banks, recapitalizing others
- **Fiscal consolidation:** Reducing government spending to reduce inflation pressures
- **Monetary policy tightening:** Real interest rates pushed into deeply positive territory to attract and retain Lira holders

The healing took years. The creation of the New Turkish Lira (January 2005) was partly a psychological reset, removing the "hyperinflation currency" label. [RAW]

## Worked Example: The Pensioner's Dilemma

A Turkish pensioner in February 2001 held 650 million Lira in bank savings (equivalent to USD 1,000 at 650,000 TRL/USD). If holding Lira: [RAW]

- **October 2001:** Same USD 1,000 equivalent is now 1.68 billion Lira (if the pensioner had kept the Lira)
- **Purchasing power loss:** If Turkish inflation (import-driven) reached 50%, then the Lira's real value fell even faster

**Rational response:** Withdraw all Lira and convert to Dollars. This is what millions of Turks did, creating bank runs and further banking system stress. [RAW]

## Boundaries / Conditions for Severity

**No currency reserves to deplete:** Unlike the ERM (where the BOE had USD 40+ billion), the Turkish crisis involved political institutions, not reserve levels. Policies could theoretically have prevented it, but the political conflict prevented credible policy commitment. [RAW]

**Informal dollarization as safety valve:** While dollarization meant loss of monetary control, it also prevented complete currency collapse into uselessness. The Dollar became the store of value. [RAW]

**IMF conditionality as forcing function:** The formal IMF program provided a *commitment device*—the government agreed to external monitoring, making it harder to reverse course and return to soft-money policies. [RAW]
