## Thesis
The New Keynesian Monetary Model is the standard theoretical framework for inflation targeting, consisting of three core equations: a supply equation (Phillips Curve), a demand curve (IS), and a monetary policy rule. [extracted]

## Mechanism
The baseline model consists of the following structure:
1. **Supply Equation (NKPC):** $\pi_t = E \pi_{t+1} + \alpha x_t + \epsilon_t$
   (Inflation is a function of future expectations, the output gap $x_t$, and supply shocks).
2. **Demand Curve (IS):** $x_t = E x_{t+1} - \delta (i_t - E \pi_{t+1}) + u_t$
   (The output gap depends on future expectations and the real interest rate).
3. **Policy Rule:** $i_t = \phi_{\pi} \pi_t + \phi_x x_t + \phi_i i_{t-1} + \upsilon_t$
   (The central bank adjusts the nominal rate $i_t$ in response to inflation and output). [extracted]

### Key Features
- **Microfoundations:** Derived from the optimal behavior of representative agents (consumers, producers, and the central bank).
- **Expectations:** The model is forward-looking; expectations about the future directly influence current variables. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Indeterminacy:** If the policy response is weak ($\phi_{\pi} < 1$), the model can lead to multiple equilibria. An active rule ($\phi_{\pi} > 1$) is required for stability. [extracted]
- **Rigidity:** Assumes staggered price setting (Calvo pricing).
