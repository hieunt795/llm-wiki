## Thesis

The **Currency Substitution Model** goes beyond the traditional monetary model (which assumes only one currency is held in the domestic economy) by recognizing that investors can hold multiple currencies simultaneously. The demand for domestic money is no longer purely a function of domestic income and interest rates but is also highly sensitive to the returns on foreign currency.

## Mechanism: Competitive Money Holding

In this framework, the demand for domestic money ($M^d$) is expressed as:
$$M^d/P = L(Y, r, r^*, E[\Delta S])$$

Key factors driving the choice of currency include:
1.  **Inflation Differentials:** High domestic inflation incentivizes holding foreign currency as a store of value.
2.  **Exchange Rate Expectations:** If investors expect the domestic currency to depreciate ($E[\Delta S] > 0$), they will preemptively shift toward foreign currency.
3.  **Interest Rate Parity (IRP):** The choice depends on the return differential between domestic and international financial markets.

## Implications for Monetary Policy

- **Reduced Autonomy:** When currency substitution is high, the central bank’s control over the domestic money supply is weakened because investors can easily switch to foreign money.
- **Enhanced Volatility:** Shocks to inflation expectations lead to rapid portfolio shifts, which can trigger sharp exchange rate fluctuations.
- **Dollarization:** In extreme cases (like some EMEs), currency substitution can lead to "dollarization," where a foreign currency effectively takes over the roles of domestic money as a store of value and unit of account.
