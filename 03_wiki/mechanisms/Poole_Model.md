## Thesis
The Poole Model identifies the optimal monetary policy instrument (money supply vs. interest rate) based on the relative volatility of shocks to the goods market (IS) and the money market (LM). [extracted]

## Mechanism
The model compares the expected loss (output deviation) under two policy regimes:
1. **Interest Rate Targeting ($r$):** The central bank accommodates all shifts in money demand.
2. **Money Supply Targeting ($M$):** The central bank holds the quantity of money constant, letting the interest rate fluctuate. [extracted]

### Decision Logic
- **Monetary Shocks ($\sigma_v^2$):** If uncertainty originates in the money market (LM curve instability), **Interest Rate Targeting** is superior as it insulates the real economy from these shocks. [extracted]
- **Spending Shocks ($\sigma_u^2$):** If uncertainty originates in the goods market (IS curve instability), **Money Supply Targeting** is superior as interest rate movements provide an automatic stabilizer. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- Assumes the objective is output stabilization. [extracted]
- Rational expectations models suggest that interest rate targeting may be more prone to larger output responses to supply-side shocks if the aggregate demand curve is flat. [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Poole (1970):** Original formulation based on Keynesian IS-LM.
- **Trend:** Most modern central banks favor interest rate targeting, implying they perceive monetary shocks (instability in money demand) to be more prevalent than goods market shocks. [extracted]
