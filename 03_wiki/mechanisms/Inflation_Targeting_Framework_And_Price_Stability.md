## Thesis

Inflation targeting frameworks operationalize the principle that **price stability is the only variable central banks can influence in the long run** and the only final policy target for which they can be held accountable. By making explicit quantitative commitments to inflation levels or ranges, central banks reduce inflation expectations, provide forward guidance, and resist political pressure to use monetary expansion for short-term stimulus. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

## 1. Core Mechanism: Inflation Target Framework

### Definition and Structure

An **inflation target** expresses a specific inflation target or target range of inflation in quantitative terms, designed to be straightforward and transparent to the public. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Key Design Features:**
- **Target format:** Either a specific point target (e.g., 2% inflation) or a range (e.g., 1%-3%)
- **Time horizon:** Medium to long-term achievement (not month-by-month or even year-by-year)
- **Flexibility:** Allows for year-by-year variation while anchoring long-run expectations
- **Transparency:** Publicly announced and monitored against actual outcomes

[RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

### Historical Adoption

**First post-WWII formal inflation target:** New Zealand (1989), following a long period of inflation and economic instability. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Pre-modern precedent:** Swedish Riksbank (1931) targeted price level in an industrial country context, but this remains rare. [RAW]

**Global adoption by 2012:** 29 central banks had adopted formal and explicit inflation targets. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Notable exception:** The U.S. Federal Reserve operates with an **implicit** rather than explicit inflation target (at the time of Cargill's writing, pre-2012 explicit target adoption). [RAW]

## 2. Mechanism: How Inflation Targeting Transmits to Outcomes

### A. Public Commitment and Credibility Anchoring

The central bank **publicly commits** to price stability, making it difficult for government to pressure the bank to accommodate fiscal spending or use easy monetary policy to postpone structural reforms. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Transmission:** Public commitment → Market belief in central bank resolve → Inflation expectations anchored → Actual inflation stabilizes at target → Central bank credibility reinforced

[RAW]

### B. Phillips Curve Disenchantment

An explicit inflation target signals that the central bank does **not** attempt to exploit the Phillips curve trade-off between employment and inflation. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Historical context:** The 1970s-80s Great Inflation demonstrated that Phillips curve trade-offs can be unstable. Attempts to use monetary expansion to reduce unemployment (moving along the Phillips curve) instead generated stagflation and financial instability. [RAW]

**Modern understanding:** Price stability is a prerequisite for sustainable employment and growth; inflation targeting accepts that the long-run Phillips curve is vertical and attempts to generate employment through real fundamentals, not nominal illusions. [RAW]

### C. Forward-Looking Anchoring

The inflation target provides a **transparent forward perspective** of monetary policy. Public understanding that short-term decision-making is anchored to the long-run goal of price stability reduces uncertainty and inflation volatility. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Example:** If a central bank targets 2% inflation and faces a temporary supply shock (e.g., oil price spike), the public expects the central bank to tolerate short-term inflation above 2% without accommodating it through loose money. This reduces inflation expectations anchoring and speeds return to target. [LLM]

## 3. Explicit vs. Implicit Inflation Targets

### Explicit Inflation Targets

**Definition:** Formal, public, quantitative commitment to an inflation level or range, with regular reporting and accountability. [RAW]

**Advantages:** [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]
- Credibility signal to markets and public
- Transparency for accountability
- Reduces discretion (and discretionary inflation bias)
- Particularly effective for developing economies under political pressure for loose money

**Examples:** New Zealand, Canada (2% midpoint), Australia (2-3% range), UK (2.0%), Sweden (2%), etc. [RAW]

### Implicit Inflation Targets

**Definition:** Central bank focuses on price stability but is not formally committed only to price stability; targets are internal or communicated qualitatively. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Advantages:** [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]
- Greater flexibility in pursuit of other objectives (employment, financial stability)
- "Have cake and eat it too"—can claim commitment to price stability without formal constraint
- Avoids commitment that might prevent crisis-time accommodation

**Disadvantages:**
- Less credible to public (if crisis time comes and the central bank deviates, inflation expectations rise)
- More discretion available, increasing risk of inflation bias

**Example:** U.S. Federal Reserve (pre-2012 explicit target adoption). [RAW]

## 4. Empirical Evidence: Has Inflation Targeting Worked?

### 1970s-1990s Experience

By the late 20th century, inflation had been brought under control in most countries, whether inflation-targeting or not. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Interpretation problem:** Did inflation targeting cause the disinflation, or did central banks adopt inflation targeting *because* they had committed to price stability anyway? [RAW]

**Careful analysis suggests:** Inflation targeting provided a **marginal difference**, especially for developing economies under intense government pressure to expand money supply. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

### 2000s Global Financial Crisis Evidence

During the 2007-2010 period (commodity price shocks + global financial crisis), **inflation-targeting countries outperformed non-inflation-targeting countries**: [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

- **Better inflation control:** Inflation-targeting countries kept inflation in check despite sharp commodity price increases
- **Better crisis absorption:** Inflation-targeting countries absorbed financial shocks more effectively
- **Mechanism:** Anchored inflation expectations remained stable despite short-run shocks, reducing pass-through from commodity prices to wage and price setting

[RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

**Implication:** Inflation expectations anchoring appears to reduce the *costs* of commodity and financial shocks. [RAW]

## 5. Why Did Inflation Targeting Become Dominant?

Cargill identifies five reasons central banks adopted inflation targeting: [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

1. **Long-run constraint on central bank:** Inflation is the only variable the central bank can influence in the long run; it is the only final policy target for which accountability is appropriate. [RAW]

2. **Consequences of inflation:** High inflation (e.g., Great Inflation 1970s-80s) generates stagflation and contributed to financial system collapse (S&L crisis in the U.S.). [RAW]

3. **Credibility through commitment:** Public commitment to price stability, combined with announcement that government pressure will not interfere, strengthens central bank independence and reduces inflation bias. [RAW]

4. **Forward anchoring:** Transparent announcement of price stability objective allows public to understand that short-term decisions serve long-term fundamentals. [RAW]

5. **Constraint on fiscal accommodation:** Inflation targeting framework makes it difficult for government to pressure the central bank to finance spending or postpone structural reforms. [RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]

## 6. Boundaries / Failure Conditions

### Crisis-Time Trade-offs

During financial crises, strict inflation targeting can conflict with financial stability or liquidity provision. A central bank committed to an explicit inflation target may face political pressure to provide emergency lending (which could exceed the target). [RAW]

**Example:** During the GFC, central banks with explicit ITF frameworks often invoked "financial stability" as secondary objective, justifying QE as necessary despite temporary inflation concerns. [RAW]

### Regime-Dependent Effectiveness

Inflation targeting's effectiveness depends on underlying institutional credibility. In countries with **low institutional trust**, an explicit target may lack credibility and fail to anchor expectations. [RAW]

### Zero Lower Bound Constraint

Once nominal interest rates hit zero, inflation targets below zero become mechanically difficult to achieve through conventional policy. Some countries (Japan, Switzerland, ECB) have experimented with negative rates, but there are limits. [RAW]

## 7. Worked Example: How Inflation Target Anchors Expectations

**Scenario:** Central bank targets 2% inflation explicitly.

**Year 1:** Oil prices spike 20%. Headline inflation rises to 4%.

**Without inflation target:** 
- Public expects central bank will accommodate inflation (or at least accept it)
- Wage bargainers demand 4%+ raises (expecting higher inflation to persist)
- Wage-price spiral develops
- Central bank faces pressure to raise rates sharply to control expectations
- Potential for persistent inflation bias

[LLM]

**With explicit 2% inflation target:**
- Public understands central bank is committed to 2% medium-term
- Public believes central bank will tolerate 4% now but will tighten to return to 2%
- Wage bargainers demand smaller raises (expecting reversion to 2%)
- Core inflation remains closer to 2%
- Central bank needs less rate hikes to maintain credibility

[RAW] [[Cargill_Financial_System_Policy.md#page=330]]
