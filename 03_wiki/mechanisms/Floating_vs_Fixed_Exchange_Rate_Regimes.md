## Thesis
A nation's exchange rate regime determines whether it is a **currency issuer** (floating) or **currency user** (fixed). Under floating rates, government can conduct countercyclical fiscal and monetary policy without running out of its own currency. Under fixed rates, government must accumulate foreign reserves, constraining policy autonomy and reducing to currency-user status for foreign obligations.

## 1. The Bretton Woods Era: Fixed Rates & Gold Constraint

[RAW] "Many people are unaware that a major historical event occurred in 1971, when US President Nixon abandoned gold convertibility and ended the system of fixed exchange rates that had existed in the Bretton Woods international monetary system since the Second World War."

[RAW] "Under that system (as well as under the gold standard system that had existed since the late 19th century, with breaks for both world wars), currencies were convertible into gold, and exchange rates were fixed to the US dollar. As such, they had to operate their economies in such a manner as to accumulate gold or dollars."

**Bretton Woods constraint:**

```
Fixed exchange rate to gold/USD
    ↓
Government must defend peg (promise to buy/sell at fixed price)
    ↓
Requires accumulation of gold/foreign reserves
    ↓
If reserves fall, must tighten policy (deflationary adjustment)
    → Cut government spending
    → Raise taxes
    → Raise interest rates (attract capital inflows, discourage imports)
    ↓
Result: Procyclical, deflationary bias
```

[RAW] "This usually meant adopting contractionary fiscal policy as well as maintaining high interest rates to ensure trade surpluses and strong currencies."

[LLM] Example: UK under Bretton Woods faced persistent balance-of-payments deficits (more imports than exports) → gold reserves declining → forced to raise interest rates and cut spending to shrink imports and attract capital inflows → recession deepened. This persisted until 1971 when UK floated the pound.

## 2. The 1971 Transition: From Fixed to Floating

[RAW] "However, after 1971, most governments floated their currencies and traded them freely on foreign currency markets. Occasionally, central banks would conduct what became known as a 'managed' float where they tried to limit the amplitude of movements that the free float would generate."

**Post-1971 landscape:**

```
Pre-1971: Bretton Woods (Fixed)
├─ Exchange rate pegged to USD
├─ USD pegged to gold ($35/oz)
├─ Government must defend peg
├─ Policy constrained by reserve accumulation requirement
└─ Deflationary bias (must run surpluses)

Post-1971: Floating/Managed Float (Flexible)
├─ Exchange rate determined by supply/demand in FX market
├─ Government floats currency; does NOT defend peg
├─ Government retains full policy autonomy
├─ Inflation emerges as new constraint (not reserves)
└─ Policy can be countercyclical
```

[LLM] The shift from fixed to floating fundamentally altered the **nature of the constraint** on government fiscal policy:
- **Fixed rates:** Constraint = foreign reserve availability
- **Floating rates:** Constraint = inflation / real resources

## 3. Spectrum of Exchange Rate Regimes

[RAW] "It is thus essential to understand the notion of a currency regime, which can range through a continuum from a fixed exchange rate system to a floating exchange rate system with varying degrees of exchange rate management in between."

**Regime continuum:**

| Regime Type | Government Role | Policy Autonomy | Reserve Requirement | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Hard peg** | Defend rate rigidly | Minimal (currency board) | High (24/7 intervention) | Hong Kong (HKD/USD), Argentina (1991-2001) |
| **Soft peg/crawl** | Adjust rate gradually, defend within band | Limited | Moderate | Managed crawling peg (Indonesia pre-1997) |
| **Managed float** | Intervene to smooth volatility, not target rate | Substantial | Low (tactical only) | Most central banks post-2000 |
| **Free float** | No intervention; market determines rate | Maximum | None | UK, Japan, US (in practice) |

[LLM] Each step toward floating gives government more fiscal/monetary autonomy but exposes currency to exchange rate volatility.

## 4. Policy Space Under Floating Rates

**Under floating, sovereign currency issuer regains full autonomy:**

[RAW] "Understanding the way the" [continuation would discuss how floating rates enable policy autonomy]

[LLM] **Government fiscal autonomy under floating:**

```
Government wants to stimulate recession
    ↓
Announces fiscal expansion (spending ↑, taxes ↓)
    ↓
Private sector spending rises; imports ↑
    → Trade deficit widens
    → FX market: demand for foreign currency ↑
    → Supply of home currency ↑ (exporters holding home currency)
    ↓
Exchange rate depreciates (home currency weakens)
    ↓
Exports become cheaper; imports become expensive
    → Net exports decline as trade deficit narrows
    → Depreciation offsets fiscal deficit's import effect
    ↓
Domestic employment ↑ (from fiscal stimulus)
Economic recovery without running out of currency
```

Key insight: The exchange rate **equilibrates** the current account (imports/exports). No need to accumulate reserves.

## 5. Policy Constraints Under Fixed Rates

**Under fixed rates, government loses fiscal autonomy:**

```
Fixed exchange rate regime
    ↓
Government wants to stimulate (spending ↑)
    ↓
Private sector spending rises; imports ↑
    → Trade deficit widens
    → FX market: demand for foreign currency ↑
    → Cannot depreciate (must defend peg)
    ↓
Central bank must sell foreign reserves to meet FX demand
    → Reserves fall
    ↓
If reserves approach zero:
    → Government must raise interest rates (attract capital inflows)
    → Cut spending (shrink imports)
    → Raise taxes
    ↓
Contractionary policy forces recession
Result: Cannot conduct independent fiscal stimulus
```

[LLM] **Example — Thailand 1997 Asia Crisis:**
- Thailand pegged baht to USD
- Private sector borrowed heavily in USD (expecting peg to hold)
- Current account deficit widened (imports ↑)
- FX reserves fell as exporters panicked and converted baht to USD
- Central bank sold reserves but could not sustain peg indefinitely
- June 1997: Bank of Thailand abandoned peg
- Baht collapsed (from 24/USD to 56/USD); banks insolvent in USD debt → financial crisis

## 6. Worked Example: Fiscal Stimulus Under Different Regimes

**Scenario: Government increases spending by 1% of GDP to fight recession**

### Under Floating Rate (UK)
- Government borrows and spends £10 billion
- Economy improves; wages ↑, imports ↑
- Demand for euros (to buy German goods) ↑
- Supply of pounds ↑ (UK importers holding pounds to convert)
- Pound depreciates (£1 = €0.95 instead of €1.00)
- British exports cheaper; foreign demand ↑
- Unemployment ↓; inflation stable
- **Outcome: Stimulus works; exchange rate absorbs trade deficit**

### Under Fixed Rate Peg (Hypothetically UK pegged to euro)
- Government borrows and spends £10 billion
- Economy improves; wages ↑, imports ↑
- Demand for euros ↑; supply of pounds ↑
- **Cannot depreciate** (must maintain peg at £1 = €1.00)
- Central bank sells euro reserves to buy up pounds
- Reserves fall; confidence in peg erodes
- Interest rates rise (defend peg, attract inflows)
- Spending must be cut (high rates kill investment)
- Stimulus fails; recession deepens
- **Outcome: Stimulus fails; forced austerity**

## 7. The "Fear of Floating" Problem

[LLM] Many emerging markets have historically resisted floating, despite its autonomy benefits:

**Reasons for fear of floating:**
1. **Currency mismatch:** Private firms borrowed in USD; fear depreciation increases debt burden in local currency
2. **Inflation bias:** Central bank lacks credibility; fear depreciation will trigger high inflation
3. **Capital flight:** Worry that floating signals loss of discipline
4. **Terms of trade shock:** Export-dependent economies fear depreciation reduces income (if demand is inelastic)

**Modern understanding:** Floating is preferable for policy autonomy, but requires:
- Credible central bank (low inflation history)
- Long-term debt in local currency (not USD)
- Diversified export base (not commodity-dependent)

## 8. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

**When do floating rates create problems?**

1. **Hyperinflation:** If government uses fiscal autonomy to overspend beyond real capacity → inflation accelerates → currency collapses → imports become unaffordable → poverty
2. **High dollarization:** If private sector is dollarized (borrows in foreign currency), depreciation creates insolvency (liabilities in USD, revenues in home currency)
3. **Commodity-dependent economy:** If exports are commodity-priced in USD (oil, copper), depreciation doesn't help much (quantity demanded doesn't rise)
