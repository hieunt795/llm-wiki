## Thesis

The migration of corporate lending from banks to private credit funds is primarily driven by regulatory arbitrage—capital optimizing precisely to the structural asymmetry between Basel-constrained bank balance sheets and lightly regulated non-bank entities. This is not illicit evasion, but the mechanical result of return-on-equity naturally seeking the path of least regulatory resistance.

## Mechanism

**Regulatory Arbitrage** describes the migration of capital, assets, and origination activities out of the strictly regulated traditional banking system (governed by Basel III/IV) and into the [[Nonbank_Financial_Intermediation]] (NBFI) sector, which faces no equivalent capital requirements.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> In the context of private credit, "arbitrage" is not an illegal subversion of rules, but the rational, friction-minimising optimization of capital. Capital naturally flows down the path of least regulatory resistance to maximize return on equity.

### Expressions of Regulatory Arbitrage

1. **Risk-Weighted Assets (RWA) Asymmetry:**
   - **Banks:** Must hold a strict layer of common equity (CET1, typically 12-15%) specifically provisioned against the risk-weight of a loan. A speculative corporate loan consumes massive capital.
   - **Private Funds:** Face zero statutory capital requirements. They can hold the exact same speculative loan using a 100% equity structure (unlevered funds) or optimize leverage internally without external risk-weight penalties.

2. **Leverage Caps and Stratification:**
   - **Banks:** Strictly bound by absolute leverage ratios (e.g., Tier 1 capital must be >3–5% of total unweighted exposures). 
   - **Private Funds:** Not bound by systemic leverage caps. Public wrappers like [[Business_Development_Company]] (BDCs) possess specific statutory limits (e.g., 2:1 debt-to-equity), but these are significantly looser, allowing funds to layer leverage selectively on bespoke portfolios in ways banks cannot.

3. **Supervisory Opacity and Forbearance:**
   - **Banks:** Endure continuous supervisory scrutiny and severe stress testing. Troubled loans must be aggressively provisioned or marked down, immediately hitting bank earnings.
   - **Private Funds:** Subject only to investment management disclosures. This opacity allows private lenders to employ mechanical forbearance—such as modifying cash-pay loans to [[Payment_In_Kind_PIK]] structures—without recognizing immediate impairments, effectively masking portfolio stress from immediate mark-to-market visibility.

### Systemic Outcomes

- **The Fragility Shift:** Risk is neither destroyed nor diminished by regulation; it is merely transferred. Arbitrage shifts credit risk from insured, deposit-taking institutions across the regulatory boundary directly onto the balance sheets of institutional investors (pensions, insurers) and private lenders.
- **Shadow Banking Growth:** Regulatory arbitrage is the primary catalyst continuously inflating the global shadow banking parameter. 
- **The Interconnectedness Loop:** Banks participate in this arbitrage by providing the leverage (via back-end credit lines) to the private funds that are originating the loans the banks are disallowed from originating directly.
