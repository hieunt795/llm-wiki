## Thesis
Inflation is transmitted through incompatible distributional claims over real output; it persists when the sum of nominal income demands (wages and profits) exceeds available real GDP at current prices, mediated by the government's policy stance.

## ⓪ CURRENCY GATE
[✓ checked 2026-04-28] | [⊘ skipped: half_life≥10]
↳ STALE_SIGNAL: [no]

## ① REGIME LOCK
The mechanism operates differently across employment regimes:
1.  **Low Pressure (Recession):** Spare capacity and high unemployment weaken worker bargaining power and firm pricing power. Supply-side shocks (e.g., oil) may cause stagflation if claimants resist real income loss.
2.  **High Pressure (Full Employment):** Negotiating power is pro-cyclical. Reductions in the "reserve army of unemployed" empower labor, while high demand empowers firms to defend mark-ups.
3.  **Inflationary Gap:** Aggregate demand exceeds full-capacity output, making nominal growth purely inflationary ($PY \uparrow$ as $Y$ is fixed).

## ② CAUSAL CHAIN
[LENSES: Kalecki — Mark-up struggle; Abba Lerner — Sellers' inflation; Devine — Structural biases]

**Distributional Conflict Transmission:**
1.  **Claim Incompatibility:** Workers desire real wage $w/P$, Firms desire mark-up $m$. If $\sum \text{claims} > \text{Real GDP}$, a conflict emerges.
2.  **Trigger:** Either labor seeks wage gains ($W \uparrow$) or firms seek higher margins ($P \uparrow$).
3.  **Redress Mechanism:** The aggrieved party seeks nominal increases to restore their real share.
    *   **Wage-Price Spiral:** $W \uparrow \to \text{Costs} \uparrow \to P \uparrow$ (Firms defend mark-up).
    *   **Price-Wage Spiral:** $P \uparrow \to \text{Real Wage} \downarrow \to W \uparrow$ (Workers defend real wage).
4.  **Oxygen (Credit/Policy):** The spiral persists only if the Central Bank/Treasury accommodates the higher nominal demands (e.g., providing liquidity for higher working capital/wages).

[DENSE — The Inflationary Gap]:
$$GAP = E_{planned} - Y_{potential} \text{ (at base prices)}$$
→ **INTENT:** To model excess demand beyond the economy's physical ability to respond with output ($Y$).
→ **MECHANISM:** If $E > Y_{potential}$, the price level $P$ must rise to satisfy the identity $PY = GDP$.
→ **INTERACTIONS:** In the conflict model, the gap provides the "oxygen" for the wage-price spiral by increasing claimant bargaining power.
→ **BOUNDARY:** If government introduces "discipline" (NAIRU strategy/unemployment), it chokes the transmission by weakening labor's ability to redress real wage erosion.

## ③ FEEDBACK TOPOLOGY
*   **Reinforcing Loop (Spiral):** $W \uparrow \to \text{Demand} \uparrow \to P \uparrow \to \text{Resistance} \uparrow \to W \uparrow$. This loop is "fed" by credit expansion or accommodative fiscal policy.
*   **Balancing Loop (Discipline):** Tightening $\to \text{Unemployment} \uparrow \to \text{Bargaining Power} \downarrow \to \text{Spiral terminates}$. MMT critiques this for wasting real resources (labor).

## ④ CHANNEL INTERACTION
*   **Cost Channel:** Raw material shocks (oil) represent a loss of national real income. Inflation occurs only if claimants try to shift this loss to others.
*   **Structural Channel:** Floating exchange rates provide flexibility but can exacerbate spirals if depreciation leads to higher import costs and further wage demands.

## ⑤ QUANTIFIED ANCHOR
[RAW-BOOK: Watts/Wray Ch 17]
*   **Classical Dichotomy Rejection:** $MV = PY$ is an identity; it only becomes a theory if $V$ and $Y$ are fixed. Watts/Wray note $V$ is highly volatile (Fed data 1950–2015), meaning money supply growth does not proportionately cause inflation.
*   **Productivity Rule:** Incomes policies (e.g., 1962 US guidelines) attempted to link $W \uparrow$ to productivity $\uparrow$ to keep unit labor costs constant.

## ⑥ BOUNDARY CONDITIONS
Mechanism collapse occurs when:
1.  **Incomes Policy Consensus:** Triple-partite agreements (Scandinavian Model) coordinate claims to match output, bypassing the need for unemployment "discipline."
2.  **Hyper-inflationary Collapse:** Total collapse of the supply side ($Y \to 0$) makes demand-side management irrelevant.
