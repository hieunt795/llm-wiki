## Thesis

Banks are rarely generic institutions; most are founded for a specific purpose that dictates their asset-liability structure. When specialist banks are set up to conduct **Promotional Lending** (e.g., development banks like KfW or the EIB), their ability to offer lower interest rates than commercial competitors is a structural result of their architectural design. Alexander Düring argues that understanding this "Cost of Capital Advantage" is essential for correcting the common misconception that these banks operate outside the normal laws of finance.

## Mechanism

Every bank must earn its cost of capital to remain solvent. Promotional lenders achieve a significantly lower hurdle rate through two primary mechanisms:

1. **Non-Profit Nature (Equity Risk Premium Removal):** 
   - Commercial banks must generate a profit for shareholders, which requires an **Equity Risk Premium** on their paid-up capital. 
   - Specialist promotional banks typically operate as non-profit entities. By removing the need to pay out shareholder profits, they can pass this "saved" premium directly to their borrowers in the form of lower interest rates. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]
2. **Funding Guarantees (Credit Risk Premium Reduction):** 
   - Most development and specialist banks carry explicit or implicit **Sovereign Guarantees** on their debt.
   - This makes their bonds (SSA - Sovereigns, Supranationals, and Agencies) significantly safer for investors, resulting in a much lower credit risk premium on their liabilities. The bank can borrow from the market at rates close to the government yield, and then lend that cheap capital to targeted sectors. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]

### Categories of Specialist Purpose
Alexander Düring identifies four primary categories of specialist bank mandates:

| Mandate Type | Examples / Specific Institutions | Primary Focus |
|---|---|---|
| **Government Funding** | Bank of England, John Law’s Banque de France | Financing the sovereign's expenditure and war debts. |
| **Real Estate** | *Schlesische Landschaften*, *Deutsche Pfandbriefbank*, *Kreditkassen* (DK) | Collecting long-term funds to finance infrastructure and housing. |
| **Community / Regional** | US Community Banks, Japan Regional Banks, European Savings Banks | Managing the liquid assets and credit needs of a specific local community. |
| **Infrastructure Providers** | **CLS Bank** (FX settlement), **State Street**, **Bank of New York** | Serving other financial institutions through settlement and custody. |

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The Brecht Critique
Düring uses the "cost of capital" logic to refute Bertolt Brecht’s famous pseudo-koan: *“What is the robbing of a bank compared to the setting up of a bank?”*
- **The Flaw:** Brecht’s quote suggests setting up a bank is an inherently exploitative act of "free money" creation.
- **The Financial Reality:** Setting up a bank requires significant capital that must be serviced at market rates. Even the most "advantaged" specialist bank must still cover its operational and capital costs to exist indefinitely. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]
