## Thesis

The boundary between Quantitative Easing (QE) and **Helicopter Money** is not determined by the type of asset purchased, but by the **intended duration** of the central bank's involvement. While QE is technically a temporary injection of liquidity meant to be unwound, Helicopter Money represents a permanent expansion of the monetary base to fund sovereign deficits, effectively subordinating monetary policy to fiscal aims. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=63]]

## Mechanism: The "Hidden" Helicopter

Alexander Düring argues that explicit debt cancellation (burning the bonds) is not required for helicopter money to exist. It can be engineered through three technical mechanics that achieve the same economic result:

### 1. Perpetual Rollover (Roll-over Commitment)
Instead of canceling debt, the central bank simply commits (implicitly or explicitly) to reinvesting all maturing government bond principals into new bonds indefinitely. If a 10-year bond is rolled over for 50 or 100 years, it is economically indistinguishable from a perpetual asset or canceled debt. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=64]]

### 2. Economic Neutrality of Profit Remittance
Most central banks are legally required to remit their profits (including the interest/coupons paid by the government on bonds held by the central bank) back to the government.
- **The Result:** The government's interest payments are "handed right back" to the treasury at the end of the fiscal year. The funding cost of the debt held by the central bank mathematically drops to zero. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=64]]

### 3. Historical Proxy: The Bank of Japan (BoJ)
The BoJ has held Japanese Government Bonds (JGBs) persistently since 1967. Because its total holdings have never fallen to zero, these purchases are functionally permanent. A debt rolled over for over half a century is "helicopter money" in all but name. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=64]]

## Boundaries / Conditions

### 1. From Monetary to Fiscal Realm
Helicopter money subordinates the central bank's mission of preserving the "store of value" property of money to other aims, such as dealing with a sovereign debt overhang. According to the quantity theory of money, a permanent expansion of the money supply must eventually lead to a discrete jump in the price level. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=63]]

### 2. The Seigniorage Limit (Banknote Rule)
To limit the inflationary impact, some central banks historically applied a "banknote rule" (e.g., BoJ until 2013), limiting their government bond holdings to the total volume of banknotes in circulation. This capped the fiscal boost to the amount of seigniorage derived from physical cash. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=64]]
