# [[Twin_Deficits_Sectoral_S_I_Balances]]

## Thesis

[RAW] Although the identity $S - I = CAB$ is central to macroeconomic analysis, it does not make explicit the respective roles of the government and private (or nongovernment) sectors. By decomposing saving and investment into their private and government sector components, the link between the fiscal aggregates and the balance of payments can be derived.

## 1. The Three-Sector Identity (Equations 6.1–6.3)

[RAW] Starting from the economy-wide saving-investment identity (eq. 6.1):

$$(S - I) = CAB \tag{6.1}$$

Decomposing S and I into private and government components (eq. 6.2):

$$(S_p + S_g) - (I_p + I_g) = CAB \tag{6.2}$$

Rearranging into sectoral balances (eq. 6.3):

$$(S_p - I_p) + (S_g - I_g) = CAB \tag{6.3}$$

where subscripts $p$ and $g$ refer to private and government sectors.

This identity shows that:
$$\underbrace{(S_p - I_p)}_{\text{private sector}} + \underbrace{(S_g - I_g)}_{\text{government sector}} = \underbrace{CAB}_{\text{current account}}$$

[RAW] The significance of this identity cannot be overemphasized. It suggests that there are important relationships among (i) the saving-investment gap of the private sector, (ii) the overall fiscal position of the government sector, and (iii) the current account of the balance of payments. It focuses on the separate roles that the private and public sectors play in a current account imbalance.

## 2. Three Configurations of Sectoral Balances (Box 6.1)

[RAW]

| Situation | Private balance | Government balance | Current account | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1 — Twin deficits** | $(S_p - I_p) > 0$ | $(S_g - I_g) < 0$ | **CAB < 0** if \|government deficit\| > \|private surplus\| | Government deficit is the primary source of the current account deficit; CAB improvement requires fiscal adjustment |
| **2 — Double deficit** | $(S_p - I_p) < 0$ | $(S_g - I_g) < 0$ | **CAB < 0** necessarily | Both sectors in deficit; requires simultaneous fiscal consolidation AND private saving stimulus |
| **3 — Fiscal surplus + private shortfall** | $(S_p - I_p) < 0$ | $(S_g - I_g) > 0$ | **CAB < 0** if \|private deficit\| > \|fiscal surplus\| | A private investment boom (financed by capital inflow) OR private consumption boom (saving shortfall); policy implication differs sharply |

[RAW] **Situation 1 is typical of many countries undertaking adjustment programs:** the fiscal deficit is the main source of the current account deficit. In many economies, however, a large fiscal deficit is more than offset by an excess of private saving over investment. Italy example: high private saving financed a high government deficit with only a modest current account deficit.

[RAW] In **Situation 3**, the distinction between a private **investment** boom vs. a private **consumption** boom is critical:
- Private investment boom financed by capital inflow → current account deficit is sustainable (capital flowing toward higher returns); different policy implication from fiscal contraction
- Private consumption boom (saving shortfall) → current account deficit reflects excess absorption; may require macro tightening on the private side

[RAW] **Key insight:** The accounting identity provides only a useful conceptual framework for analyzing different behavioral and policy outcomes and deriving appropriate policy implications. Accounting identities must be complemented by behavioral relationships to determine causality.

## 3. Behavioral Interpretation (Not Just Accounting)

[RAW] The identities reflect accounting constraints — they do not imply causality:

| If... | Then the identity accommodates via... |
|---|---|
| Government raises taxes to finance spending | Private sector reduces saving or spending; private S-I balance worsens; may worsen CAB |
| Government finances deficit with CB credit | Higher nominal GDP → higher tax revenues (partial self-financing) + private sector holds more money (monetary counterpart to credit expansion) |
| Private investment rises (productivity shock) | Either government adjusts (reducing its surplus), or CAB worsens; external borrowing accommodates |

[RAW] Complemented by behavioral relationships about the private and government sectors, the identity can be extremely useful in macroeconomic analysis and financial program design.

## 4. Flow of Funds Embodiment

The three-sector S-I decomposition is the nonfinancial block of the [[Flow_of_Funds_Framework]] matrix. In Table 6.1, the "Nonfinancial balances (S−I)" row records:
- Column 1 (overall economy): = 0 (closed-system identity)
- Column 3 (government): = $S_g - I_g$ (government saving-investment gap)
- Column 4 (private): = $S_p - I_p$ (private sector saving-investment gap)
- Column 5 (banking): = 0 (by convention)
- Column 6 (rest of world): = −CAB (foreign sector mirror)
- Column 7 (horizontal check): = 0 ✓

The financial transactions block then shows how each sectoral balance is financed — connecting the nonfinancial diagnostic to monetary and BOP channels. See [[Monetary_Survey_Identity]] (NDA ceiling), [[BOP_Identities_and_Current_Account_Analysis]] (CAB decomposition), and [[GFS_Fiscal_Deficit_Measures]] (government balance).

## 5. Poland Data (1993) — Illustrative Flow of Funds (Table 6.6)

[RAW] From Table 6.6 (Poland 1993, in trillions of zlotys):

| Sector | Nonfinancial balance (S−I) | Financing |
|---|---|---|
| General government | −45 | Bank credit: +35; nonbank: +11; foreign: −6 |
| Private sector | +49 | Net lending to banking (−ΔM2): −109; FDI: +11; NFB: −3 |
| Banking system | 0 (by convention) | ΔNFA: −12; ΔNDC: +99; ΔOIN: +85 |

Interpretation: Poland in 1993 ran a government deficit (−45 Tz) more than offset by a large private surplus (+49 Tz), resulting in a small current account deficit (4 Tz = ~0.3% GDP). The private surplus was held primarily in money balances (falling velocity in the disinflation context).

## 6. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The three-sector identity is the canonical tool for distinguishing fiscal from private sector drivers of current account imbalances:

1. **"Fiscal" vs. "Lawson" current account deficits:** A Situation 1 current account deficit (driven by fiscal imbalance) requires fiscal consolidation as the primary adjustment instrument — there is no Lawson argument that the market is optimally allocating. A Situation 3 deficit (fiscal surplus, private investment boom) is the Lawson doctrine — market-efficient capital allocation from rest-of-world savers to domestic investors. Confusing the two leads to the wrong prescription: tightening fiscal policy when the private sector is over-investing does not fix the underlying saving-investment gap.

2. **Italy / Germany counterexample:** High fiscal deficits coexisting with modest or no current account deficits (Situation 1 with large private surplus) show that fiscal deficits alone do not mechanically generate external deficits. Germany runs fiscal deficits while maintaining current account surpluses because private savings are structurally high. This means fiscal multipliers on the current account depend on private sector behavioral responses, not just accounting identities.

3. **The decomposition as financial programming check:** When building an IMF program, the three-sector identity provides a consistency check: if the program projects (a) fiscal deficit improvement of 3% of GDP and (b) no change in private S-I balance, then it must project CAB improvement of 3% of GDP. If the program simultaneously projects no improvement in the CAB (for external reasons), one of (a) or (b) must give — either the private S-I balance must worsen (private crowding-in of government saving) or the fiscal improvement target is aspirational. The identity forces transparency about which behavioral assumption is doing the work.

4. **Sovereign bond read-across:** When reading a country's current account, bond investors should decompose into government and private sector S-I balances before forming a view. A 5% of GDP current account deficit financed by a 6% private investment boom (Situation 3) and partially offset by a 1% fiscal surplus is entirely different from a 5% deficit driven by a 5% fiscal deficit (Situation 1). The first may be sustainable; the second requires fiscal adjustment to be sustainable.
