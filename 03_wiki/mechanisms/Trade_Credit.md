## Thesis

While macroeconomic analyses often focus on bank-intermediated credit, **Trade Credit** represents a significant portion of the total debt in an economy. Every time a company delivers a good and issues an invoice for later payment, it is effectively granting a loan to its customer. Alexander Düring argues that terms of payment are not merely a matter of convention, but are active tools of balance sheet management that can replicate complex financial positions.

## Mechanism

Trade credit exists primarily in two forms: **Trading on Account** (asymmetric relationships between traders and non-traders) and **Negotiable Instruments** (like bills of exchange).

### 1. The Collection Period Formula
The extent of trade credit on a corporate balance sheet is measured by the relationship between sales and receivables:

$$\text{Collection Period} = \frac{\text{Accounts Receivable}}{\text{Total Sales}}$$

- **Interpretation:** This ratio calculates the average time between finalizing a sale and receiving the eventual payment. 
- **Management Incentive:** Companies manage this ratio aggressively around reporting dates. A company might accelerate payments before December 31st to reduce receivables and shorten its collection period for published accounts. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=28]]

### 2. Active Balance Sheet Management
Treasuries use payment terms to optimize returns or manage risk:
- **Returns:** Leaving an outstanding balance with a customer may offer a better return than depositing the cash at a low-interest bank account.
- **Position Replication:** A company can replicate a foreign exchange position via payment timing. For example, by paying invoices quickly in Currency A but allowing invoices in Currency B to be paid to itself over long periods, the firm creates a **Short A / Long B** position. Such positions are frequently "invisible" to international balance-of-payments statistics. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=29]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### 1. Non-Cash Trading Instruments
Trade credit often evolves from a simple ledger entry into a transferable instrument:
- **Bills of Exchange:** Unconditional obligations of the party it is drawn on (the drawer). These are used as a means of payment between traders via **Endorsement**.
- **Banker's Acceptance (BA):** When a bank accepts a bill on behalf of the payee, it acts as a guarantor, transforming commercial risk into bank risk. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=29]]

### 2. The Conversion to Money
While trade credit is "credit," it is not "money" in the sense of monetary aggregates. However, when a bill becomes payable, it represents a claim on money. This distinction is maintained by the fact that trade instruments lack **Legal Tender** status—a payee can refuse to accept a bill as payment for a debt. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=30]]
