# [[Policy_Credibility_Improvement_Mechanisms]]

## Thesis
[WIKI/LLM] Policy credibility is not inherent but must be earned through institutional design and consistent behavior. Warjiyo identifies five primary mechanisms: rule-based policy, reputation building, delegation of authority, optimal contracts, and political/legal support.

## Mechanism

### 1. Rule-Based Policy
[RAW] Using specific policy rules (e.g., fixed exchange rates, monetary aggregates like the Friedman or McCallum rules, or interest rate rules like the Taylor rule) prevents authorities from "deceiving" markets. Rules reduce uncertainty, stabilize expectations, and alleviate inflation bias by making the consequences of policy actions transparent to the public. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=295]]

### 2. Reputation Building
[RAW] Based on Barro and Gordon (1983), reputation is built when a central bank consistently meets its commitments over a protracted period. If a central bank shocks the public with high inflation to stimulate growth, it damages its reputation. Conversely, bold policy measures ("shock therapy") and appropriate policy sequencing signal seriousness and anchor low inflation expectations. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=296]]

### 3. Delegation of Authority
[RAW] Rogoff (1985) proposed delegating central bank leadership to "inflation-averse" or "conservative" individuals. This principal-agent solution ensures monetary policy implementation remains consistent with public hopes for low inflation, protecting the process from political cycles. Joining a monetary union or adopting a fixed exchange rate with a credible anchor are also forms of delegation. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=296]] [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=297]]

### 4. Optimal Contracts
[RAW] Walsh (1995) suggested that inflation bias stems from a lack of incentives. Optimal contracts, such as performance-based salaries for central bankers tied to inflation targets, provide an incentive structure that penalizes failure and rewards accountability, aligning central bank actions with price stability. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=297]]

### 5. Political and Legal Foundations
[RAW] Credibility depends on support from the political party in power and legitimacy in the eyes of the public. Formal laws (constitutions) and unwritten social contracts/norms provide the legal foundation for policy discipline. Inflation targeting frameworks (ITF) are cited as a modern method to build this discipline and governance. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=297]] [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=298]]
