## Thesis

The turn premium is one of the most visible "market quirks" in the fixed income world. It represents the extra cost an investor must pay to borrow money over a reporting date (the "Turn"). Alexander Düring emphasizes that this premium is **unrelated to economic fundamentals** or central bank policy shifts; it is a pure artifact of regulatory accounting and institutional behavior. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch16.md#page=133-134]]

## Mechanism: The Reporting Date Logic

Banks are subject to periodic regulatory reporting, with the most critical date usually being the calendar year-end (December 31st).

1.  **Balance Sheet Window Dressing:** To report higher capital ratios and lower risk profiles, banks aggressively attempt to shrink their balance sheets towards year-end.
2.  **Lending Reluctance:** Banks become extremely reluctant to lend cash or provide liquidity if the transaction opens before year-end and closes after it, as this requires carrying the asset on the reporting date.
3.  **The Resulting Jump:** Any interbank lending transaction that "spans the turn" (e.g., a 3-month loan made in late September) will see an immediate jump in its interest rate to compensate for the balance sheet commitment. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch16.md#page=133-134]]

## Strategic Dynamics: Drivers and Magnitudes

### 1. Historical Spikes
Exceptionally large turn premia occur when a reporting date coincides with major systemic changes. Examples include:
- **1998/1999:** Introduction of the Euro.
- **1999/2000:** Y2K technical worries.
- **2000/2001:** Introduction of Euro physical cash. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch16.md#page=134]]

### 2. The Leverage Ratio Effect
In modern times, the introduction of the **Leverage Ratio** has led to significant spikes in the repo market around turndates. Because the leverage ratio treats all assets (even low-risk ones) equally, the cost of providing repo liquidity on reporting dates has become prohibitively expensive for many dealers. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch16.md#page=134]]

### 3. Normalization
In recent years, turn premia have often declined because market participants have learned to "fund earlier"—securing their year-end liquidity needs months in advance to avoid the December scramble. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch16.md#page=134]]
