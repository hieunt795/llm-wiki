## Thesis

Proposed by Paul Samuelson (1958), the OLG model provides a micro-foundation for money that does not rely on its physical properties. In this model, money functions as a "link" between generations, enabling the young to save for their old age by providing resources to the current old.

## Mechanism

### 1. Basic Structure
- Population consists of two generations: **Young** (work and earn endowment $w$) and **Old** (consume savings).
- In each period $t$, the young consume $c_{1,t}$ and save $s_t$.
- In period $t+1$, as old, they consume $c_{2,t+1}$ using their savings.

### 2. The Budget Constraint
- **Young:** $c_{1,t} + m_t/p_t = w$
- **Old:** $c_{2,t+1} = m_t/p_{t+1}$
- **Combined:** $c_{1,t} + \frac{p_{t+1}}{p_t} c_{2,t+1} = w$

### 3. The Role of Money
Money ($m_t$) allows the transfer of value. For the system to be stable, the real money balance ($m/p$) must be positive. This requires the "Biological Interest Rate" (population growth $n$) to be greater than or equal to the real interest rate.

## Research Insight: Non-Superneutrality
Perry Warjiyo notes that in OLG models, money is often **non-superneutral**. An increase in the growth rate of money (inflation) changes the relative price of consumption between youth and old age, altering the real saving rate and thus real output.
