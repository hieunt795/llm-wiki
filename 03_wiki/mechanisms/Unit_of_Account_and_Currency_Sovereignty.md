## Thesis
The unit of account is the government's most fundamental monetary power: the monetary standard in which taxes are imposed, contracts are written, and all financial stocks flow are measured. This creates a self-reinforcing system where economic actors demand the currency to pay taxes, making the unit of account the economy's permanent scorekeeping standard.

## 1. Unit of Account vs. Medium of Exchange vs. Store of Value

[RAW] "Let us look at money as the unit of account in which stocks and flows are denominated."

[LLM] Money has three potential roles; unit of account is **distinct and pre-requisite** to the others:

| Function | Definition | Binding Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| **Unit of Account** | Numeraire in which prices, debts, wages are quoted | Government acceptance + legal enforcement |
| **Medium of Exchange** | Accepted in transactions (reduces barter friction) | Network effects + confidence |
| **Store of Value** | Maintains purchasing power over time | Price stability + low inflation |

In MMT framework:
- Unit of account is **mandatory** (government-enforced)
- Medium of exchange is **endogenous** (follows from unit of account + acceptability in taxes)
- Store of value is **contingent** (requires low inflation, not guaranteed)

A currency can fail as store of value (high inflation) while remaining the unit of account and medium of exchange. Examples: Venezuela (bolívar), Argentina (peso) — currency used for transactions but loses value rapidly.

## 2. One Nation, One Currency Rule

[RAW] "One nation, one currency. There have been a few exceptions to this rule, including the modern euro, which is the money of account adopted by several countries that have joined the Economic and Monetary Union of the European Union (EMU)."

**Standard case:** Nation issues and monopolizes one unit of account.
- Australian dollar (Australia)
- US dollar (United States)
- Japanese yen (Japan)
- British pound (United Kingdom)

[RAW] "The government of the nation issues a currency (usually consisting of metal coins and paper notes of various denominations) denominated in its money of account."

**Exception: Multi-nation currency (EMU):**
- Euro is money of account for 20 countries
- But **no single nation** issues the euro
- ECB (supra-national institution) holds issuance monopoly
- Individual governments (France, Germany, etc.) become "currency users" with respect to euro, not issuers
- This creates policy constraints for member governments (cannot create base money unilaterally)

[RAW] "When we address the exceptional cases, such as the EMU, we will carefully identify the differences that arise when a currency is used, but not issued, by a nation."

## 3. How Unit of Account Enforces Currency Demand

[RAW] "Spending by the government as well as tax liabilities, fees, and fines owed to the government are denominated in the same money of account. These payments are enforceable by law."

**Enforcement chain:**

```
Government designates unit of account (e.g., US dollar)
    ↓
Imposes tax in dollars: "You owe $X in US dollars"
    ↓
Enforces collection via IRS (threat of penalties, liens, asset seizure)
    ↓
Households/firms must obtain dollars to discharge tax obligation
    ↓
Broad demand for dollars in economy
    ↓
Merchants accept dollars in transactions
    ↓
Dollars become primary medium of exchange
    ↓
All contracts (wages, rents, prices) denominated in dollars
    ↓
Dollar is now universal unit of account and medium of exchange
```

[RAW] "More generally, broad use of a nation's money of account is ensured by enforcing monetary contracts in a court of law, such as the payment of wages."

[LLM] Example: If employer offers wage in foreign currency and worker sues for payment in home currency, courts enforce contracts in home currency. This legal enforcement sustains the unit of account monopoly.

## 4. Private Dollarization and Underground Economies

[RAW] "In many nations there are private contracts that are written in foreign monies of account. For example, in some Latin American countries it is common to write some kinds of contracts in terms of the US dollar. It is also common in many nations to use US currency in payment."

[RAW] "According to some estimates, the total value of US paper currency circulating outside America exceeds the value of US paper currency used at home. Much of this is thought to be involved in illegal activities, including the drug trade."

**Private dollarization reflects:**
1. **Loss of confidence:** Domestic currency inflation makes foreign currency preferable for store of value
2. **Cross-border convenience:** US dollar ubiquity in international trade
3. **Tax evasion/illegality:** Foreign currency avoids domestic financial reporting

[RAW] "While we recognise these deviations from the 'one currency, one nation' rule, they generally account for a relatively small proportion of transactions and contracts, most of which will be denominated in the nation's own money of account."

[LLM] Key point: Private use of foreign currency does **not** undermine sovereignty of domestic unit of account because:
- Government still collects taxes in domestic currency
- Government still makes payments in domestic currency
- Vast majority of legal contracts denominated in domestic currency
- Foreign currency use remains marginal (underground economy, high-inflation scenarios)

## 5. The Sovereignty-Currency Nexus

[RAW] "Sovereignty and the currency. The national currency is often referred to as a sovereign currency, that is, the currency issued by the sovereign government. The sovereign government retains a variety of powers for itself that are not given to private individuals or institutions. Here, we are only concerned with those powers associated with money."

**Three sovereign powers over unit of account:**

1. **Designation:** "Government alone has the power to determine which money of account it will recognise for official accounts."
   - Can change unit (redenomination) — rare but happened: Yugoslavia → dinar → no currency; Argentina → peso → no currency

2. **Monopoly issuance:** "Modern sovereign governments alone are invested with the power to issue the currency denominated in each nation's money of account."
   - Counterfeiting laws enforce this

3. **Tax acceptance:** "The sovereign government...decides how these liabilities can be paid: that is, it decides what it will accept in payment so that taxpayers can fulfil their obligations."
   - Government accepts own currency in tax payment
   - May accept foreign currency or bonds, but core acceptance is domestic currency

[RAW] "For example, if any entity other than the US government tried to issue US currency it would be prosecuted as a counterfeiter, with severe penalties being imposed. (Enemy nations do sometimes try to undermine a nation's economy by counterfeiting its currency, hoping to cause inflation and destroy trust in the currency.)"

## 6. Failure Conditions: When Unit of Account Loses Anchor

**Hyperinflation:** If government spends excessively beyond real productive capacity, inflation accelerates and unit of account function degrades:
- Prices change daily (prices quoted in foreign currency instead)
- Medium of exchange function collapses (people use barter)
- Unit of account remains nominally enforced (contracts still written in home currency) but loses store-of-value function

**Currency redenomination:** In extreme hyperinflation, government may:
- Drop zeros from currency (1 million old → 1 new)
- Switch to foreign currency (official dollarization)
- Example: Zimbabwe (2009) abandoned Zimbabwean dollar entirely, adopted US dollar

**State collapse:** If government loses monopoly on force (civil war, state failure), unit of account enforcement collapses:
- Competing currencies emerge
- International currency (USD) may become de facto unit of account

## 7. The Financial System as Scoreboard

[RAW] "In this context the financial system can be viewed as a record of transactions, that is, a scoreboard."

[LLM] This is a crucial insight: the unit of account is the **metadata** of financial transactions. All accounting entries are simply positions in the unit of account:

- Bank deposit: position in unit of account (IOU of bank)
- Government bond: position in unit of account (IOU of government)
- Stock certificate: derivative position (claim on future cash flows in unit of account)
- Wage contract: position that will accrue in unit of account

The unit of account is the **single coordinate system** in which all economic activity is tracked. Changing it would require redenominating the entire financial system — a massive collective action problem (why nations almost never do it).
