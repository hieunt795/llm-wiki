## Thesis
[RAW] The credibility problem in monetary policy results from five analytically distinct causes, which in practice combine and reinforce each other, creating feedback loops that erode central bank reputation and public expectations alignment.

## 1. Core Logic - Five Credibility Problem Causes

### Cause 1: Time Inconsistency (Kydland-Prescott Framework)

[RAW] **Definition**: Policy authority faces time preference dilemma in determining policy preferences relative to time dimension.

**Mechanism**:
1. Period t: Policy authority pledges to implement disinflationary policy
2. Economic agents respond: Set nominal wages based on expectation of low inflation
3. Period t+1: Policy authority becomes tempted to tolerate higher inflation to increase output and absorb labor
4. Dilemma source: Central bank faces trade-off between controlling inflation OR reducing unemployment
5. **Result**: Policy authority deviates from announced commitment

**Credibility Destruction Process**:
- Early deviation: Quickly erodes credibility
- Future period: Economic agents less inclined to trust authority
- Expectation formation: Based on previous experience, agents anticipate higher inflation
- Wage-setting: Nominal wages set higher, anticipating inflation
- **Self-fulfilling prophecy**: Central bank must work harder to achieve same inflation target with larger output sacrifice

[LLM] This is the fundamental time-inconsistency problem that ITF and central bank independence attempt to resolve through institutional commitment devices.

### Cause 2: Internal Inconsistency (Sectoral Policy Coordination Failure)

[RAW] **Definition**: Policy authority faces policy dilemma related to sectoral interests or multiple policy authorities.

**Manifestations**:
1. **Intra-government inconsistency**: Lack of policy coordination within central bank (different departments pulling differently) or across government entities
2. **Fiscal-monetary contradiction**: Example—Deflationary monetary policy combined with unrestricted budget deficit expansion
   - Monetary authority tightening (BI raises policy rate)
   - Fiscal authority expanding (Government increases spending)
   - Result: Contradictory impulses confuse expectations

3. **Asymmetrical program implementation**: Program design is sound but execution inconsistent across sectors or time periods

**Credibility Impact**: Contradictory signals prevent public from understanding true policy direction, creating uncertainty that undermines credibility.

### Cause 3: Asymmetric Information (Incomplete Transparency Problem)

[RAW] **Definition**: Economic agents lack symmetric information about policy authority's true commitment and capabilities.

**Problem Manifestation**:
- Economic agents may not be aware that policy authority is seriously pursuing disinflationary policy
- Announcement of policy not believed because agents unconvinced of commitment
- Agents require time to "learn and convince themselves" that authority will implement promised policy
- **Especially acute in developing countries** with history of inconsistent policymaking

**Credibility Mechanism**:
- Without symmetric information, agents cannot monitor what central bank actually trying to achieve
- Difficult to build solid reputation that authority pursuing particular policy
- Information asymmetry prevents reputation-building, which is credibility mechanism

[LLM] This is why ITF includes transparency requirement—to reduce asymmetric information and allow public to verify central bank commitment.

### Cause 4: Stochastic Economic Shocks (Unpredictable External Shocks)

[RAW] **Definition**: Economic shocks difficult to predict could cause policy program to fail despite sound design and consistent implementation.

**Mechanism**:
1. Central bank designs and implements sound disinflationary policy
2. Unpredictable economic shock occurs (oil price spike, financial crisis, terms-of-trade shock)
3. Shock creates off-target public expectations regarding economic indicators
4. Public assumes central bank's stabilization program is inaccurate/failure
5. Low expectations → policy may fail even with consistent implementation

**Example from Text**:
- 1997/1998 Asian Financial Crisis undermined ITF preconditions in multiple countries
- Oil price shocks 1999-2000 tested ITF credibility in energy-importing countries
- Supply shocks create inflationary pressures independent of monetary policy control

[RAW] "Economic shocks that are difficult to predict (stochastic) could cause a policy program to fail in the achievement of its target despite sound design and consistent implementation because such shocks could create off-target public expectations concerning an economic indicator set by the policy authority."

### Cause 5: Political Uncertainty (Institutional Instability)

[RAW] **Definition**: Public presumption that policymakers unable to implement policy package due to lacking domestic political support.

**Triggers**:
1. Government is coalition of multiple political parties without clear majority
2. Government legitimacy questioned by public
3. Vested political interests prevent program implementation
4. Parliament fails to support central bank's policy decisions

**Credibility Effect**:
- Even if economic stabilization policy package is sound, lack of political support triggers credibility problem
- Vested interests create expectation that policy will be reversed
- Public assigns low credibility to announced policy because political viability seems weak

[RAW] "The presumption among economic agents that the policymakers are unable to implement an economic policy package due to a lack of domestic political support could also exacerbate the credibility problem, which could occur if the government is a coalition of various political parties or if the government's legitimacy is questioned by the public."

## 2. Worked Example - Multiple Causes Combining: Indonesia 2000-2005

[RAW] ITF transition period in Indonesia exhibited combinations of credibility problems:

**Time Inconsistency Risk**:
- BI pledged inflation control but government debt sustainability concerns created temptation to tolerate higher inflation
- Energy price adjustments forced inflation overshoots (2005, 2008) despite disinflationary commitment

**Internal Inconsistency**:
- Weak government-BI coordination on inflation target determination (pre-2004 amendment)
- Government budget pressures conflicted with monetary tightening needs
- Banking sector recapitalization bonds (variable-rate) created fiscal-monetary tension

**Asymmetric Information**:
- Public skeptical of BI's commitment after crisis period instability
- Frequent policy reversals during transition created skepticism about BI consistency
- Limited communication infrastructure compared to advanced central banks

**Political Uncertainty**:
- Democratic transition in Indonesia created political instability
- People's Representative Council support for inflation control variable and weak
- Coalition government structure reduced policy implementation certainty

**Result**: BI required 5-year transition period (2000-2005) to build credibility sufficient for full ITF implementation—demonstrating that credibility problem resolution requires addressing multiple causes simultaneously.

## 3. Failure Conditions / Boundary Cases

[RAW] Credibility problem becomes unresolvable when:

1. **Persistent policy deviations**: Central bank repeatedly announces one policy but implements another
2. **Fundamental goal ambiguity**: Central bank lacks clear legal mandate or government undermines stated goals
3. **Fiscal dominance entrenchment**: Government financing requirements prevent monetary independence achievement
4. **Regime uncertainty**: Frequent legal/institutional changes prevent credibility building
5. **Structural crisis**: Economic crisis so severe that even consistent policy cannot prevent inflation overshoots

## 4. Policy Implications - Credibility Building Requirements

[RAW] To overcome credibility problems, central bank must:

1. **Prevent time inconsistency**: Use rules-based policy, long-term governance structures, independent leadership
2. **Ensure internal consistency**: Coordinate across government, establish monetary-fiscal coordination frameworks
3. **Reduce asymmetric information**: Enhance transparency, communication, and public education
4. **Prepare for shocks**: Build policy space (low inflation baseline, strong reserves) to accommodate shocks
5. **Obtain political support**: Establish broad political consensus on monetary policy independence and inflation control goals
