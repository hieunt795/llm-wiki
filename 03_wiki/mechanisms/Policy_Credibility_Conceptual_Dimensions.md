# [[Policy_Credibility_Conceptual_Dimensions]]

## Thesis
[WIKI] The "credibility problem" arises when public expectations of inflation deviate from the central bank's announced targets, often due to the perceived incentive for the central bank to "surprise" the market to stimulate short-term output.

## Determinants and Causes
[RAW] Policy and target credibility are fundamentally determined by at least three aspects: (1) public response reflected in the formation of expectations; (2) central bank capacity to maintain and commit to the policy strategy; and (3) the structure and development of the economy. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=293]]

[RAW] Time inconsistency occurs when the policy authority faces a dilemma... pledges to implement policy to subdue inflationary pressures... but subsequently could be tempted to tolerate higher inflation... to increase output. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=294]]

[LLM] Other significant causes include internal inconsistency (lack of policy coordination), asymmetric information (agents unable to monitor CB commitment), and stochastic economic shocks that render even well-designed programs off-target.

## Efforts to Improve Credibility
[RAW] Several efforts can be applied to improve policy credibility: (1) Using a rule (e.g., Taylor rule); (2) Building reputation (Barro and Gordon); (3) Delegation of authority (Rogoff); (4) Contract determination (Walsh); and (5) Political support for the rule of law. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=295]]
