## Thesis

A central macroeconomic disconnect is the public belief that central banks directly dial the inflation rate up or down on command. In reality, a central bank lacks any direct operative mechanism to enforce terminal consumer goods pricing. Instead, it must rely on the highly indirect, notoriously lagged, and frequently unreliable **Monetary Transmission Mechanism**—a profound systemic hierarchy mapping how manipulating base money eventually distorts private credit appetite to alter terminal inflation.

## Mechanism

The transmission mechanism is bifurcated into two conceptual frameworks as articulated by Warjiyo & Juhro (2019):

### 1. The Money View (Market Channels)
Focuses on how policy rates influence the cost of capital and asset prices in the broader financial markets.
- **Interest Rate Channel:** The traditional Keynesian view where a lower real rate reduces the cost of borrowing, stimulating investment and consumption.
- **Exchange Rate Channel:** Especially critical in open economies (EMEs). Policy rates affect capital flows, which alter the exchange rate and, consequently, the price of imports and competitiveness of exports.
- **Asset Price Channel:** Rates influence the valuation of stocks and real estate, affecting household wealth (Wealth Effect) and corporate financing costs (Tobin's q).
- **Expectations Channel:** Modern ITF reliance on "Forward Guidance" to anchor long-term inflation expectations, which directly colors current wage and price-setting behavior.

### 2. The Credit View (Intermediary Channels)
Focuses on market imperfections (asymmetric information) and the role of the banking system as a non-neutral conduit.
- **Bank Lending Channel:** Policy changes directly alter the supply of loans by affecting bank reserves and their ability to issue deposits.
- **Balance Sheet Channel:** Focuses on the "Net Worth" of borrowers. Higher interest rates reduce asset values used as collateral, increasing the external finance premium due to adverse selection and moral hazard.
- **Bank Capital Channel:** Recognition that bank equity levels (Basel III constraints) affect their capacity to absorb risk and extend credit, regardless of the base rate level.
- **Risk-Taking Channel:** Prolonged low rates can induce banks and investors to search for yield, shifting their risk tolerance and potentially creating financial instability.

## Boundaries / Conditions

### The Long and Variable Lags
A central bank rate hike or cut typically requires **12 to 18 months** to fully wash through the sequential hierarchy and physically alter Consumer Price Index (CPI) metrics.

### Transmission Failure (The Credit Crunch)
If the Credit View channels are blocked (e.g., due to high bank non-performing loans or extreme risk aversion), the Money View channels become severely neutered. Injected liquidity remains trapped as excess reserves, a state often described as "pushing on a string."
