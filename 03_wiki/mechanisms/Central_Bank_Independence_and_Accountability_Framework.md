# [[Central_Bank_Independence_and_Accountability_Framework]]

## Thesis
[WIKI/LLM] Institutional credibility at a central bank is built on two pillars: independence to execute mandates without political interference, and accountability to justify actions to the public. This framework ensures that the delegation of authority to "conservative central bankers" remains consistent with democratic expectations.

## Mechanism

### 1. The Delegation Logic
[RAW] Building on the credibility problems discussed in Chapter 10, one solution is to provide a clear mandate to conservative central bankers with full independence. By delegating authority from the principal (public) to the agent (central bank), the focus shifts to achieving low and stable inflation. However, this delegation necessitates mechanisms—accountability and transparency—to guarantee that implementation aligns with public hopes. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=319]]

### 2. Dimensions of Independence
[RAW] Independence is not monolithic but categorized into several dimensions:
- **Goal vs. Instrument Independence:** Goal independence allows the central bank to set its own targets (e.g., the Fed), while instrument independence grants autonomy only over the tools to achieve goals set by the government (e.g., the ECB). [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]]
- **Political vs. Economic Independence:** Political independence refers to freedom from government interference in goal-setting and appointments. Economic independence refers to the unrestricted use of policy instruments. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]]
- **Financial Independence:** Access to adequate financial resources and full control over the budget, which correlates positively with policy credibility. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]]
- **Personal Independence:** The decision-making body exists without formal or informal pressure from the government. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=321]]

### 3. Synthesis of Governance
[WIKI/LLM] Warjiyo (citing Bofinger, 2001) synthesizes these into three core categories: (1) Goal independence, (2) Instrument independence, and (3) Personal independence. The degree of independence varies significantly across jurisdictions, with the Federal Reserve owning a high degree of goal independence compared to many other central banks. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=321]]
