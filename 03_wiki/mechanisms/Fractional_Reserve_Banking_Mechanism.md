## Thesis

The fractional reserve system is the engine of modern credit expansion. It allows the money supply to grow beyond the physical base of reserves, but it also introduces inherent fragility in the form of potential bank runs.

## Mechanism

The mechanism operates through a cycle of deposit taking and lending:
1. **Initial Deposit:** A bank receives a deposit of "Outside Money" (Central Bank reserves or cash).
2. **Reserve Retention:** The bank retains a portion of the deposit as reserves, determined by the [[Reserve_Ratio_Constraint]].
3. **Lending:** The remainder is lent to a borrower, appearing as a new deposit in the system.
4. **Multiplier Effect:** This process repeats across the banking system, expanding the total money supply (M2) relative to the monetary base (M0).

### Structural Fragility
Because banks issue demand liabilities (deposits) that exceed their liquid reserves, they are structurally illiquid. 
- **Bank Runs:** If a significant portion of depositors demand withdrawal simultaneously, the bank cannot fulfill these requests without the intervention of a [[Lender_Of_Last_Resort]].
- **Inside vs. Outside Money:** The system creates "Inside Money" (private bank debt) backed by "Outside Money" (public sovereign debt/cash). [[Inside_Vs_Outside_Money]]

## Boundaries / Conditions

- **Reserve Requirements:** Regulatory limits on the minimum reserves a bank must hold.
- **Liquidity Coverage:** Modern regulations like [[Liquidity_Coverage_Ratio]] (LCR) supplement simple reserve ratios.
- **Solvency vs. Liquidity:** The mechanism assumes the bank remains solvent (assets > liabilities), even if temporarily illiquid.
