## Thesis

The primary obstacle to a speedy debt restructuring is the **Holdout Problem**: a minority of investors (vultures) refuse a deal and sue the debtor for 100% repayment, knowing that if others take a loss, the debtor's capacity to pay the holdouts improves. Alexander Düring explains that **CACs** are the primary tool for circumventing this Nash equilibrium. By agreeing to be bound by a majority vote ahead of time, bondholders eliminate the risk of a small minority hijacking the default process. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch25.md#page=261]]

## Mechanism: The Voting Paradigms

CACs differ in how they aggregate votes across different bond series:

### 1. Dual-Limb CAC (Double Majority)
- **Requirement:** Requires a majority both (a) within each individual bond series and (b) across the aggregate nominal value of all affected series.
- **The Risk:** A holdout investor can "block" the restructuring by acquiring a sufficient blocking minority in a single, small bond series. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch25.md#page=261]]

### 2. Single-Limb CAC (Aggregate Majority)
- **Requirement:** Requires only a single majority across the entire aggregate nominal of all series.
- **The Advantage:** Much harder for holdouts to block, as they would need to own a massive percentage of the total national or corporate debt rather than just one series. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch25.md#page=261]]

## Case Study: The Eurozone Model CACs

Since January 2013, all Eurozone government bonds with maturities over 1 year must include **Model CACs** as mandated by the ESM (European Stability Mechanism) Treaty.
- **Objective:** To streamline debt adjustments before a country can receive ESM support.
- **The Market Signal:** Interestingly, markets do not consistently price a yield difference between CAC and non-CAC bonds. Düring notes this may be because investors expect a sovereign to retroactively apply CACs to all domestic-law bonds anyway (as happened in Greece in 2012). [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch25.md#page=261-262]]

## Boundaries / Conditions: CACs are not a Cure
It is critical to understand that CACs do not *prevent* or *cause* a default. They only govern the **procedure** to be followed *after* a default decision has been made. If bondholders reject an issuer's proposal even under a CAC framework, the default proceeds to a messy, court-ordered resolution. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch25.md#page=261]]
