# [[GFS_Fiscal_Deficit_Measures]]

## Thesis

[RAW] Although the GFS concept of the overall deficit is widely used, there is no single or best measure of the fiscal deficit. Alternative concepts of the deficit defined according to different analytical criteria can also be useful, depending on the purpose at hand.

## 1. Conventional Fiscal Deficit

[RAW] Defined as the difference between total revenues (including grants) and total expenditures (including net lending):

$$\text{Conventional deficit} = \text{Total revenue and grants} - \text{Total expenditures and net lending}$$

[RAW] A government deficit represents the portion of expenditure and net lending that exceeds revenue and grants; the government covers it by borrowing and/or running down liquidity holdings.

**Shortcomings of the conventional deficit:**
1. The same deficit level may have substantially different macroeconomic effects depending on the structure of taxation and expenditure.
2. Different financing methods for the same deficit have different macroeconomic effects (central bank vs. nonbank vs. foreign financing).

## 2. Government Saving-Investment Identity

[RAW] Government saving ($S_g$) and the overall fiscal balance are linked:

$$S_g = R_g - C_g \tag{3.1}$$

$$(S_g - I_g) = R_g - E_g \tag{3.2}$$

where $R_g$ = total revenues, $C_g$ = current expenditures, $I_g$ = government investment, $E_g$ = total expenditures.

The government financing gap is covered ex post:

$$(S_g - I_g) + F_g = 0 \tag{3.4}$$

where financing sources are:
$$F_g = NFB_g + \Delta NDC_g + NB \tag{3.3}$$

($NFB_g$ = net foreign borrowing, $\Delta NDC_g$ = net credit from banking system, $NB$ = nonbank borrowing).

## 3. Public Sector Borrowing Requirement (PSBR)

[RAW] The most comprehensive standard measure of the fiscal deficit — defined for the entire public sector (general government + public enterprises). Can be derived from the general government and public enterprise sector borrowing requirements, with appropriate netting of intra-sector transfers.

## 4. Current Fiscal Deficit (Government Saving)

[RAW] Defined as:

$$\text{Current fiscal balance} = \text{Government saving} = \text{Total current revenues} - \text{Total current expenditures}$$

Used as a measure of the government's contribution to the economy's total saving.

**Limitations:**
- Hinges on the inherently arbitrary distinction between capital and current expenditures.
- The underlying assumption that all government investment contributes to growth is questionable — many public investment projects are wasteful.
- Spending on investment at the expense of maintenance may misallocate resources.

[RAW] The current fiscal deficit is not a very useful indicator and is not helpful in analyzing the impact of fiscal developments on either the external or domestic macroeconomic balance.

## 5. Primary (Noninterest) Deficit

[RAW] Accurately measures the effects of current discretionary budgetary policy by **excluding interest payments** from the conventional deficit:

$$\text{Primary deficit} = \text{Conventional deficit} - \text{Interest payments}$$

Or equivalently:
$$\text{Primary surplus} = \text{Revenue} - \text{Noninterest expenditure}$$

**Purpose:** Indicates how current fiscal actions affect the government's net debt and is therefore important for assessing **debt sustainability**. Interest payments reflect the legacy of past borrowing and are not within the control of current fiscal policy decisions.

## 6. Operational Deficit

[RAW] The conventional deficit less the **inflation-induced portion of interest payments** (the "monetary correction"):

$$\text{Operational deficit} = \text{Conventional deficit} - \text{Inflation component of interest payments}$$

Or equivalently:
$$\text{Operational deficit} = \text{Primary deficit} + \text{Real component of interest payments}$$

**Rationale:** Inflation reduces the real value of the outstanding nominal stock of public debt, but creditors are compensated through higher nominal interest rates. This compensation — the "monetary correction" — represents a **return of capital** (amortization), not a return on capital. If not removed from interest payments above the line, the deficit is overstated.

[RAW] This concept is particularly important in countries with high inflation and large public debt, because it measures the extent to which fiscal policy in a given year affects the **real stock of public debt**.

[RAW] In countries with high inflation, the conventional and operational deficit measures show large differences, and trends in the two measures may diverge markedly.

## 7. Summary: Choosing the Right Measure

| Measure | Excludes | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Conventional deficit | Nothing | Overall fiscal position, monetary impact |
| PSBR | Nothing (broadest) | Full public sector resource claim |
| Current deficit | Capital expenditures | Government saving contribution |
| Primary deficit | Interest payments | Discretionary policy assessment, debt sustainability |
| Operational deficit | Inflation component of interest | Real deficit in high-inflation environments |

[RAW] The choice of an appropriate concept of the deficit is based on: (i) the type of imbalance involved; (ii) coverage (central government, general government, public sector); (iii) accounting method (cash or accrual); and (iv) the status of any contingent liability.

## 8. Treasury Insight

[LLM] In IMF program negotiations and fiscal surveillance, the **primary balance** is the operational target because it isolates what the government can actually control (revenues and noninterest spending) from the inherited debt stock legacy (interest payments). A country can run a conventional deficit while achieving a primary surplus — the latter being the relevant signal for debt trajectory.

The **operational deficit** matters most in high-inflation EM contexts: a country showing a large conventional deficit may in fact be reducing its real debt burden rapidly through inflation erosion. Failure to use the operational deficit in such environments leads to procyclical policy tightening that is analytically unjustified.

For sovereign credit analysis, the progression from conventional → primary → operational deficit narrows down to the structural fiscal stance: primary balance relative to the real interest-growth differential determines whether debt dynamics are self-stabilizing or self-reinforcing. See [[Fiscal_Sustainability_Debt_Dynamics]].
