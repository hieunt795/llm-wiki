## Thesis

Seigniorage is the "profit of the press." Alexander Düring identifies the micro-economic mechanism where money functions as a 0% interest-bearing liability for the issuer. Perry Warjiyo expands this into the macro-economic realm, demonstrating how seigniorage acts as a **lump-sum transfer ($x$)** in general equilibrium models (like MIU), effectively acting as an implicit tax on money holders. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch02.md#page=15]] [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=56]]

## Mechanism: The Cost-Value Gap

Seigniorage exists because the **Purchasing Power** of a currency unit is significantly higher than its **Marginal Cost of Production**.

### 1. Currency Seigniorage (Banknotes)
- **The Value:** A $100 bill can buy $100 worth of goods.
- **The Cost:** The paper, ink, and printing security cost only a few cents.
- **The Result:** The difference is an immediate real gain for the sovereign. In a fiat system, this is a 0% interest loan from the public to the state. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch02.md#page=15]]

### 2. Monetary Base Seigniorage (Reserves)
In the modern digital system, seigniorage is derived from the spread between:
- The assets held by the central bank (e.g., government bonds earning interest).
- The liabilities issued by the central bank (e.g., bank reserves, which often earn a lower rate or zero interest). [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=56-57]]

## Strategic Implications: The Inflation Tax

Perry Warjiyo highlights that seigniorage is linked to the **Inflation Tax**.
- **The Logic:** As inflation rises, the real value of the public's cash holdings declines. To maintain the same "real cash balance" ($m$), the public must hold more nominal units. 
- **The Capture:** The government captures this extra demand by printing more units, effectively transferring purchasing power from the public's pockets to the state's budget.
- **The MIU Transfer ($x$):** In the Sidrauski model, the government remits this profit back to agents as a lump-sum transfer, but in reality, it is used to fund fiscal deficits. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=56]]

---

## Boundaries / Conditions: Central Bank Remittance
In most modern jurisdictions (including the ECB and US Fed), the central bank covers its own operational expenses from seigniorage and then **remits the remaining profit** to the National Treasury. 
- **The Distortion:** If a government becomes overly reliant on seigniorage, it may pressure the central bank to maintain higher-than-optimal inflation, a state known as **Fiscal Dominance**. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch02.md#page=15]]
