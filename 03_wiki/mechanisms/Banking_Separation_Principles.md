## Thesis

The primary rationale for separation is the policing of **Conflicts of Interest**. A bank that is both a lender and a securities underwriter might be tempted to lend its own funds to a failing firm or, worse, invite its unsuspecting retail customers to assume that firm's risk through securities sales.

## Key Regulatory Models

### 1. The Glass-Stegall Act (US, 1933)
- **Mechanism:** Strict prohibition on securities underwriters conducting regular commercial banking.
- **Outcome:** Forced a split between "Commercial Banks" (deposits/loans) and "Investment Banks" (securities/M&A). Repealed in 1999, though many argued its practical effect had already faded.

### 2. Ring-fencing (UK, Post-2008)
- **Mechanism:** Requires the assets and liabilities of retail banking (supporting households/SMEs) to be isolated from the group's wholesale or investment bank.
- **Goal:** Ensures that a collapse in the trading arm does not freeze the retail payment system or require a depositor bailout.

### 3. Living Wills (Global Standard)
- **Mechanism:** Continuously updated plans for an **Orderly Wind-down**.
- **Rationale:** Prevents "Too Big to Fail" by ensuring that complex entities can be liquidated without destroying systemic infrastructure.

## Boundaries / Conditions: Separation vs. Narrow Banking
Banking separation (isolating risk types) should not be confused with **Narrow Banking**. Separation allows the creation of private credit money (Inside Money), whereas Narrow Banking seeks to prohibit it entirely.
