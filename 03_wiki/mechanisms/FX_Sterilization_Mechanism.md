## Thesis

Central bank interventions in the foreign exchange market create a fundamental conflict between external value management and internal price stability. When a central bank buys foreign currency to prevent appreciation, it inadvertently expands the domestic [[Base_Money]] supply. **Sterilization** is the necessary "second step" that allows a central bank to manage the exchange rate without losing control of its primary inflation mandate. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=52]]

## Mechanism

An FX intervention is "Sterilized" when its effect on the central bank's short-term liabilities (reserves/cash) is offset by an opposite domestic market operation. This process transforms a highly liquid asset (cash) into a less liquid asset (bills or bonds) on the private sector's balance sheet.

### 1. The Liquidity Creation (Step 1)
To manage its currency downwards, a central bank buys foreign currency (e.g., USD) from domestic entities.
- **Action:** The central bank pays for the USD by creating new units of domestic currency.
- **Result:** Domestic base money increases. If left unsterilized, this could lead to inflationary pressure.

### 2. The Sterilization (Step 2)
The central bank removes the newly created liquidity by exchanging it for a less liquid asset.
- **Direct Way:** The central bank sells domestic long-term assets (bonds) from its portfolio to the public, pulling the cash back.
- **Indirect Way (Financing Bills):** The government or central bank issues specialized short-term bills to "mop up" the surplus cash.
- **Result:** The total base money supply returns to its pre-intervention level, but the private sector now holds less-liquid debt instruments instead of cash. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=52-53]]

### Case Study: Japan's MoF and Financing Bills
In Japan, the Ministry of Finance (MoF) is responsible for FX intervention rather than the Bank of Japan.
- **The Process:** To buy USD, the MoF must first obtain Yen. Since it cannot create money *ex nihilo*, it issues **Foreign Exchange Fund Financing Bills**.
- **The Result:** The intervention is "automatically sterilized" because the Yen used to buy USD was first pulled from the market through the bill issuance. This prevents the intervention from flooding the domestic market with Yen cash. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=52]]

## Strategic Dynamics

### 1. Global Coordination (The Symmetry Problem)
FX interventions are symmetric: selling EUR to buy USD increases Euro liquidity while reducing Dollar liquidity. 
- **The Coordination Fix:** If the ECB intervenes, the liquidity impact on the Dollar must be managed by the US Federal Reserve. Major central banks coordinate these actions to prevent global liquidity shocks and manage the simultaneous sterilization required in both markets. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=53]]

### 2. Non-Monetary Policy (NMP) Portfolios
In the Eurozone, sterilization has contributed to the growth of **NMP portfolios**. These consist of domestic long-term assets held by national central banks to offset the liquidity impact of their historical or ongoing FX reserve management. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=53]]

### 3. Market Interconnection
In economies with persistent current account surpluses (like Japan), domestic interest rates can be depressed by the abundance of foreign-earned funding seeking domestic assets. Alexander Düring notes that this can lead to negative interest rates on short-dated government debt even before the official policy rate is cut below zero. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch08.md#page=53]]
