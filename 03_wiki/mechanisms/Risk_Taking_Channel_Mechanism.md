## Thesis

Defined by Borio & Zhu (2008), the **Risk-taking Channel** explains how "stability breeds instability." Prolonged periods of low interest rates (Great Moderation) change the psychology of the financial system, leading to a dangerous expansion of risk exposure that eventually culminates in a crisis (like the GFC).

## Mechanism: Three Main Channels

### 1. Valuation and Cash Flow Effects
Similar to the balance sheet channel but focused on **Perception**. Lower rates boost asset values, making borrowers *look* safer than they are, leading banks to ease lending standards (LTV, CCyB).

### 2. Search for Yield (Benchmark Disparity)
Many institutional investors (pension funds, insurance) have fixed nominal return targets. 
- **The Gap:** When policy rates fall, the gap between safe returns and target returns grows.
- **Response:** Investors move into "riskier" assets (junk bonds, EME debt) to meet their benchmarks, often ignoring the underlying fragility.

### 3. Monetary Communication (Signalling)
Clear, transparent **Forward Guidance** reduces uncertainty. If the market believes the central bank will keep rates "lower for longer," risk premia collapse as investors "lever up" on the promise of future liquidity.

## The Role of Liquidity (Feedback Loop)
- **Funding Liquidity:** Ability to get cash for assets.
- **Market Liquidity:** Ability to trade without shifting prices.
Low rates stimulate both, creating a **Liquidity Multiplier** that reinforces risk-taking behavior until a shock triggers a "Fire Sale" (Cash is King).

## Research Insight: Indonesia Contrarian Finding
In Indonesia (Satria & Juhro, 2011), findings show that banks might be **Countercyclical** during easing: they become *more* prudent when rates are cut if they detect signs of future economic fragility, weakening the standard risk-taking channel. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=156]]
