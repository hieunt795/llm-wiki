# [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_in_Transmission]]

## Thesis
[WIKI] Monetary policy transmission is not just an interest rate effect; it operates through "credit rationing" and "funding constraints" within the banking sector, influenced by bank capital and liquidity ratios. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=131]]

## Mechanism
### Bank Lending Channel
[RAW] Banks face adverse selection and moral hazard. When central banks tighten policy, banks may resort to credit rationing, especially if they rely on retail funding which is less sensitive to interest rates. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=131]]

[RAW] The bank supply function is determined by: $K_i = f(y, r_i, \rho_i, LDR, CAR)$. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=132]]
- $LDR$: Loan-to-Deposit Ratio (Liquidity constraint)
- $CAR$: Capital Adequacy Ratio (Capital constraint)

### Bank Capital Channel
[RAW] Capital constraints become binding when a bank's capital falls toward requirements. A decline in asset values or increase in NPLs (Non-Performing Loans) undermines capital, compelling banks to reduce lending regardless of interest rate levels. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=132]]

[LLM] There is an interaction with the demand for reserves: during downswings, increased credit risk elevates the "need for reserves" (precautionary or mandated for risk mitigation), which can further tighten the capital channel. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=132]]

[RAW] In economic downswings, central bank interest rate policy may become "less effective" due to financial sector deleveraging and binding capital/liquidity constraints. [[Bank_Lending_and_Capital_Channels_Perry#page=132]]
