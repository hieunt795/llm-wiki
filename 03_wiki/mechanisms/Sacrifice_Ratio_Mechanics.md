## Thesis

Why don't central banks kill inflation instantly? Because doing so would trigger a catastrophic recession. The sacrifice ratio is the "price tag" of a central bank's credibility.

## Estimations across History

| Source | Ratio | Context |
|---|---|---|
| **Okun (1978)** | **10 : 1** | A 1% drop in inflation costs 10% of GDP. (Extreme case). |
| **Gordon & King (1982)** | **3 : 1** | More moderate estimate using complex modelling. |
| **Ball (1994)** | **2.3 : 1** | Based on direct observation of disinflation episodes. |

## Variables Affecting the Ratio

1. **Expectations (Anticipation):** If a central bank clearly announces a disinflation plan and is *trusted*, firms adjust prices early, and the sacrifice ratio can drop to near zero (Sargent, 1983).
2. **Nominal Flexibility:** Fast price/wage adjustment reduces output loss.
3. **Regime Path:** The ratio is higher during a downturn (5.0) than during an overheating period (2.1), due to the **Asymmetric Phillips Curve**.

## Decision Rule: The Speed of Disinflation
- **Cold Turkey:** Rapid disinflation. High immediate cost but shorter duration of pain.
- **Gradualism:** Slow reduction. Lower annual output loss but risk of losing credibility over time.
