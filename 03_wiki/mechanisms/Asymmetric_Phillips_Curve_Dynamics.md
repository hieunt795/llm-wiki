## Thesis

The traditional linear Phillips curve assumes a constant trade-off. However, research by Laxton et al. (1995) proves that the curve is often **Convex**. This has profound implications for central banks: they must react faster to an overheating economy than to a slowing one.

## Mechanism

### 1. The Asymmetry Principle
Inflation responds more aggressively to a positive output gap ($y > y^*$) than to a negative one.
- **Why?** Capacity constraints. When a factory hits 100% capacity, prices must rise; when it is at 50%, prices don't necessarily fall proportionally due to "downward wage/price rigidity."

### 2. The Expectations Shift (Post-Crisis)
In Indonesia (Solikin, 2004), the Phillips curve transitioned from linear to convex after the 1997 crisis:
- **Pre-Crisis:** Moderate output gap effect (0.2–0.3), linear relationship.
- **Post-Crisis:** High output gap effect (0.4–0.6), non-linear/convex relationship.
- **Implication:** External shocks now pass through to domestic inflation much more violently.

## Decision Rule: Pre-emptive Striking
Because of this asymmetry, to maintain a long-term inflation target, the central bank must keep average real growth slightly below potential ($y < y^*$) if it wants to avoid a sudden, non-linear spike in prices during booms.
