## Thesis

For a small open economy like Indonesia, monetary policy is never fully "Autonomous." Even with a floating rate, the central bank's efforts to tighten liquidity are often "leaked" through the capital account. High **Offset** combined with low **Sterilization** leads to monetary impotency.

## The Metrics (Indonesia Case study)

Based on Juhro (2010) using the Kouri-Porter (1974) reaction function:

### 1. Offset Coefficient ($\approx 0.7$)
- **Definition:** The percentage of domestic liquidity contraction (by the CB) that is replaced by capital inflows.
- **Result:** **70%**. This means if Bank Indonesia tries to reduce the money supply by 100 trillion Rupiah, 70 trillion Rupiah of new foreign capital flows into the country to take advantage of the higher interest rates, neutralizing most of the tightening.

### 2. Sterilization Coefficient ($\approx 0.5$)
- **Definition:** The percentage of liquidity expansion (caused by the CB buying foreign exchange to stop appreciation) that is successfully reabsorbed by OMOs.
- **Result:** **50%**. This means BI only successfully "neutralizes" half of the inflationary pressure created by its own FX interventions.

## Policy Implications
The relatively high offset (0.7) vs. low sterilization (0.5) proves that Indonesia has a **low degree of monetary policy autonomy**. This necessitates the use of non-interest rate instruments, such as **Reserve Requirements (GWM)** or **CFM**, to control liquidity without further attracting "Hot Money."
