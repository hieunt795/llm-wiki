## Thesis

In the modern **Baseline ITF Model**, expectations are the "Switch." If expectations are unanchored, the central bank must use massive interest rate shifts to move the economy. If expectations are anchored, a simple "Speech" from the Governor can achieve the same result.

## Mechanism: The Influence Path

### 1. Anchoring Inflation Expectations
By announcing a credible target (e.g., 2%), the central bank ensures that when an oil shock occurs, firms don't immediately raise prices long-term, believing the bank will bring inflation back down.

### 2. Influencing the Yield Curve
Communication regarding the future policy path (**Forward Guidance**) directly affects the pricing of long-term bonds.
- **Formula:** Long-term rate $\approx$ Average of expected short-term rates + Risk Premium.
- **Impact:** Communication lowers the risk premium and guides the "Average expected rate."

### 3. Price and Wage Setting
If workers expect inflation to be 2%, they demand a 2% wage increase. If the CB is not transparent, they might demand 5% to "play it safe," creating a self-fulfilling inflationary spiral.

## Boundaries / Conditions

### The Credibility Prerequisite
The expectations channel *only* works if the central bank is credible. If the bank has a history of missing targets, its communications become "Noise," and markets will only react to physical rate changes (the Money/Credit views).
