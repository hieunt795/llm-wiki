# [[Narrative_Approach_to_Policy_Credibility_Assessment]]

## Thesis
[WIKI/LLM] Beyond mathematical models, the narrative approach (qualitative) uses historical evidence, such as recorded committee discussions, to evaluate how policymakers weigh trade-offs. This method, exemplified by Chappel and McGregor's (2004) study of the FOMC, reveals the practical impact of political pressure and short-termism on inflation persistence.

## Mechanism

### 1. Methodology: Historical Discourse Analysis
[RAW] The narrative approach utilizes transcripts and official documentations (e.g., FOMC "Memorandum of Discussion") to explore the rationality of policymakers. Its primary advantage is the ability to uncover original motives and arguments that influenced specific policy directions, though it lacks the ability to test alternative hypotheses rigorously. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=308]]

### 2. Case Study: FOMC under Arthur Burns (1970-1978)
[RAW] Analysis of the 1970s US experience identifies four critical aspects of time inconsistency in practice:
- **Political Pressure:** The FOMC faced constant pressure to achieve unemployment levels below the natural rate ($k < 1$), leading to inflation performing beyond desired levels. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=308]]
- **Phillips Curve Adherence:** Policymakers recognized short-term trade-offs. However, as inflation expectations rose, the cost of controlling inflation was treated as an exogenous factor in each meeting, perpetuating high inflation. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=309]]
- **Short-Termism:** Members struggled with short planning horizons. Despite desiring contractionary policy to curb inflation, political and public opinion often blocked these moves to avoid recession, forcing a focus on short-term goals. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=309]]
- **Market Reaction Caution:** The FOMC viewed public expectations as inertial but feared excessive financial market reactions. This led to over-prudence and a belief that stimuli were only effective if they did not trigger immediate increases in expectations. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=309]]
