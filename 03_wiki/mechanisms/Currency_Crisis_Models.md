## Thesis
The Currency Crisis Models categorize theoretical understandings of exchange rate collapses into three distinct "generations": the first generation driven by policy inconsistencies, the second generation driven by self-fulfilling market expectations, and the third generation driven by structural vulnerabilities in the private sector and financial system. [extracted]

## Mechanism
### First-Generation Models (Krugman, 1979)
Focus on **policy inconsistency**. A fixed exchange rate is doomed if the government runs excessive fiscal deficits financed by money creation. Speculators, seeing reserves deplete, launch a preemptive attack. [extracted]

### Second-Generation Models (Obstfeld, 1986)
Focus on **market perception** and **self-fulfilling crises**. A crisis can occur even without fundamental imbalances if investors believe the government will abandon a peg due to high unemployment or other trade-offs. The expectation of collapse forces the collapse. [extracted]

### Third-Generation Models (1997 Asian Crisis)
Focus on **private sector behavior** and **financial fragilities**. High levels of short-term external debt, currency/maturity mismatches, and "noise traders" create systemic vulnerability. Market panic and contagion trigger the crisis. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Costs:** Restructuring after a crisis can exceed 20% of GDP, with output losses around 14%. [extracted]
- **Prevention:** Requires strengthening fundamentals, transparency, and macroprudential safety nets. [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **México 1994:** Example of second-generation dynamics (short-term debt and banking system weakness).
- **Asia 1997:** Example of third-generation dynamics (market panic, currency mismatch). [extracted]
