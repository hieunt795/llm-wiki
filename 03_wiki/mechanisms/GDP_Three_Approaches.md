# [[GDP_Three_Approaches]]

## Thesis

[RAW] GDP — the sum of value added across all sectors in the economy — can be determined using three basic approaches: the production approach, the income approach, and the expenditure approach. These alternative approaches yield equivalent results.

## 1. Production Approach

[RAW] GDP equals the sum of gross value added across all sectors:

$$GDP = \sum VA \tag{2.1}$$

where $\sum VA$ = sum of value added across all sectors.

Value added (VA) = gross output minus intermediate consumption (inputs used up in production).

**NDP (Net Domestic Product):**

$$NDP = GDP - D \tag{2.2}$$

where $D$ = depreciation (consumption of fixed capital). NDP is a more accurate measure of net productive capacity but harder to measure precisely and available with lag, hence GDP is preferred in practice.

**Key note:** GDP is residency-based (only production by residents) and gross (includes depreciation).

## 2. Income Approach

[RAW] GDP equals the sum of incomes generated by resident producers:

$$GDP = W + OS + TSP \tag{2.3}$$

where:
- $W$ = compensation of employees (wages, salaries, employers' + employees' social security contributions)
- $OS$ = gross operating surplus of enterprises (profits, rents, interest, depreciation; includes statistical discrepancies)
- $TSP$ = taxes less subsidies on products

## 3. Expenditure Approach

[RAW] GDP equals the sum of final uses:

$$GDP = C + I + (X - M) \tag{2.4}$$

where:
- $C$ = final consumption (government + nongovernment sectors)
- $I$ = gross investment (fixed capital formation + changes in inventories)
- $X$ = exports of goods and nonfactor services
- $M$ = imports of goods and nonfactor services

**Absorption** ($A$) = $C + I$ (aggregate domestic demand).

Therefore: $GDP = A + (X - M)$

## 4. Summary Reconciliation (Box 2.1)

[RAW] The three approaches yield equivalent GDP at market prices:

| Production | Income | Expenditure |
|---|---|---|
| $\sum VA$ (by sector) | $W + OS$ (basic prices) | $C + I + X - M$ |
| + Taxes less subsidies on products (TSP) | + TSP | |
| = GDP at market prices | = GDP at market prices | = GDP at market prices |

## 5. Beyond GDP: GNI and GNDI

[RAW] GDP measures only income from domestic production. Two additional aggregates extend to national basis:

**Gross National Income (GNI):**
$$GNI = GDP + Y_f \tag{2.5}$$
where $Y_f$ = net factor income from abroad (capital income, labor income of migrants, royalties). Unlike GDP (both production and income concept), GNI is solely an income concept. In previous SNA versions, GNI was called GNP.

**Gross National Disposable Income (GNDI):**
$$GNDI = GNI + TR_f \tag{2.6}$$
where $TR_f$ = net current transfers from abroad (workers' remittances, government grants, etc.).

**Gross National Saving (S):**
$$S = GNDI - C \tag{2.7}$$

## 6. Nominal vs. Real GDP

[RAW] Nominal GDP measures output at current prices. Real GDP uses prices of a fixed base year to capture only changes in physical output.

$$\text{Real GDP} = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{GDP\ Deflator} \times 100 \tag{2.17}$$

$$GDP\ Deflator = \frac{\text{Nominal GDP}}{\text{Real GDP}} \times 100 \tag{2.18}$$

In terms of growth rates:
$$(1 + v) \approx (1 + g) \times (1 + p) \tag{2.19}$$

where $v$ = nominal GDP growth, $g$ = real GDP growth, $p$ = inflation (GDP deflator).

## 7. GDP Measurement Problems

[RAW] Four sources of GDP mismeasurement:
1. Nonmarket output (government services, volunteer work, subsistence farming, owner-occupied housing) is inaccurately measured.
2. Quality improvements in goods are not adequately reflected (e.g., computers).
3. Output of "bads" (crime defense, pollution cleanup) adds to GDP without improving welfare — not netted out.
4. Environmental degradation and natural resource depletion are excluded.

Additionally: the **underground/black economy** — agents conceal transactions to avoid taxes, evade regulations, or hide illegal activity. Currency holdings are often used to estimate its size. Growth in the black market causes measured GDP growth to understate true growth.

## 8. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For practitioners, the equivalence of the three GDP approaches is a consistency check: if national accounts data show divergence between production-side and expenditure-side estimates, the residual is a statistical discrepancy that may signal data quality issues or unrecorded activity. In transition economies and EM contexts, the expenditure approach (C+I+X-M) is often the most timely indicator available, while the income approach (W+OS+TSP) requires enterprise survey data with longer lags. The production approach sectoral breakdowns provide early signals of structural shifts (e.g., a falling share of industry versus services). For BOP analysis, the key identity is GDP = A + (X-M), so current account deficits directly indicate that absorption exceeds domestic output. See: [[Macroeconomic_Accounting_Identities]].
