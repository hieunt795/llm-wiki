## Thesis
The MMT framework pivots on a critical operational distinction: sovereign governments as currency issuers face no inherent financial constraint (can never run out of own currency), while households and firms as currency users must obtain currency to meet obligations. This reframes fiscal policy as a tool for real resource allocation, not financial survival.

## 1. Core Logic / Mechanism

[RAW] "By placing government, as the currency issuer, at the centre of the monetary system, the MMT approach immediately focuses on how a government spends, and how that spending influences those aforementioned macroeconomic aggregates that we seek to explain."

[RAW] "The most important conclusion reached by MMT is that the issuer of a currency faces no financial constraints. Put simply, a country that issues its own currency can never run out and can never become insolvent in its own currency. It can make all payments as they come due."

[RAW] "Households and firms are users of the currency; they must obtain the currency in order to make payments as they come due. They must either earn income, or borrow, or sell assets to obtain the currency. They can be forced to default. But the sovereign currency issuer can never run out of its own currency."

**Core operational mechanism:**
```
CURRENCY ISSUER (Gov)          CURRENCY USERS (Households/Firms)
├─ Issues currency             ├─ Earns income
├─ Cannot run out              ├─ Can borrow
├─ Cannot be insolvent         ├─ Can sell assets
├─ Real constraint = inflation  ├─ Real constraint = solvency
└─ Fiscal space defined by     └─ Fiscal space defined by
   real resources & inflation     income, credit, asset sales
```

[LLM] The transmission mechanism is operational: government spending creates bank reserves (electronic entries), which constitute liabilities of the central bank and assets of the banking system. Tax collection destroys reserves. The sequence is **spending first, taxing second**, making tax revenue not a pre-condition for spending but a post-hoc reconciliation.

## 2. Worked Example

**Scenario: Government Spending $1 Billion into Infrastructure**

1. Treasury instructs central bank to credit contractor bank accounts: +$1B reserves created (central bank liability)
2. Contractors receive deposits, spend on materials & labor: reserves shift between banks via interbank system
3. Tax collection: Treasury debits taxing entity's accounts: -$0.5B reserves destroyed
4. Net result: +$0.5B reserves in banking system, financed entirely by government operations, not taxation or borrowing

[RAW] This contrasts with household logic: "For this reason, it makes no sense to compare a sovereign government's finances with those of a household or a firm."

## 3. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

**When does currency issuer status fail?**

[RAW] "Even a sovereign currency issuer can tie its own hands. This occurs if the government promises to deliver precious metal (such as gold) or a foreign currency in payment."

- **Gold standard era (pre-1971):** Government tied to gold convertibility → forced to accumulate gold reserves → operates like currency user
- **Foreign currency debt:** Developing nations issuing debt in USD must earn or borrow USD → not truly operational issuer for those obligations
- **Fixed exchange rate regimes:** Gov must maintain peg → constrained by need to accumulate foreign reserves → reduced policy space vs float

[RAW] "In the past, many governments promised to exchange their own currencies for gold or silver so, again, they had to obtain the gold or silver to meet these promises. Thus while these governments cannot run out of their own currencies, they can certainly run out of precious metal or foreign currencies and then be forced to default."

**Inflation constraint remains:** Even with currency issuance, real resource availability (labor, materials, productive capacity) remains the binding constraint. Excessive spending beyond real productive capacity → inflation.
