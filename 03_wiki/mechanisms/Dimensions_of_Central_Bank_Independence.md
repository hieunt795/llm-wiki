# [[Dimensions_of_Central_Bank_Independence]]

## Thesis
[WIKI/LLM] The concept of independence evolved to solve the inflation bias inherent in political cycles. It is broadly divided into the "what" (Goal Independence) and the "how" (Instrument Independence), alongside protections for personnel and finances.

## Definition

### 1. Goal vs. Instrument Independence
[RAW] Goal independence (Debelle and Fischer, 1995) exists when the central bank itself sets the policy targets to be achieved. Instrument independence implies the central bank has the jurisdiction and autonomy to implement duties and select tools to achieve goals, regardless of who set them. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]]

### 2. Political vs. Economic Independence
[RAW] Political independence (Grilli, Masciandaro, and Tabellini, 1991) is the freedom to set goals or policy decisions without government interference, including nomination and dismissal procedures. Economic independence is the freedom to use policy instruments (e.g., interest rates, credit controls) without government-imposed restrictions or the requirement to finance budget deficits. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]]

### 3. Financial and Personal Independence
[RAW] Financial independence (Baka, 1994) requires access to adequate financial resources and full control over the central bank's budget, which is linked to higher policy credibility. Personal independence (Bofinger, 2001) ensures the decision-making body (e.g., Board of Governors) can act without formal direction or informal pressure from the government. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=320]] [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P4.md#page=321]]
