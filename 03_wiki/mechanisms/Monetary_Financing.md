## Thesis

Monetary financing represents the ultimate "conflict of interest" in a monetary system: when the entity that issues the currency is also the economy's largest borrower (the sovereign). Alexander Düring argues that while modern central banks are legally shielded from this practice, the advent of unconventional monetary policies has blurred the distinction between "policy implementation" and "fiscal support," creating new risks for long-term currency stability.

## Mechanism

Monetary financing is older than central banking itself and has manifested in various forms across history.

### 1. Historical Forms (Specie Systems)
Under a metal standard, a government did not need a central bank to conduct monetary financing. It achieved the same economic effect through:
- **Debasement:** Diluting the precious metal content of coins while mandating they be accepted at face value.
- **Seizure:** Forcibly taking the specie reserves of private money-issuing banks.
- **Effect:** Both methods reduce the "conversion value" of the currency, effectively taxing the holders of the money to fund the sovereign. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=39]]

### 2. Modern Fiat Form
In a fiat system, monetary financing occurs when the central bank creates new money to purchase government debt directly from the treasury (Primary Market).
- **The Stability Problem:** Because this overrides the public interest in stable money, it destroys the **Store of Value** property. 
- **Historical Sheltering:** In medieval times, sovereigns sometimes "sheltered" the money-issuing function within religious institutions to protect it from their own fiscal temptations. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=39]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### 1. The Legal Shield (Independence)
Today’s approach is to rule out monetary financing by law:
- **Europe:** Central banks are strictly, constitutionally forbidden from lending to their sovereigns.
- **US and Japan:** Lending is permitted only to a very limited degree.
- **Rationale:** To underpin the separation between money creation and state debt, ensuring that "Outside Money" remains a neutral asset. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=39]]

### 2. The Quantitative Easing Paradox
The primary technical question in modern finance is how to reconcile the prohibition of monetary financing with large-scale bond purchases (QE).
- **The Rationale:** Central banks argue that buying bonds on the **Secondary Market** is a tool for managing the yield curve and inflation, not for funding the state.
- **The Reality:** Since a holder of a debt instrument acts as a lender to the issuer, large-scale holdings of sovereign debt by the central bank functionally lower the state's borrowing costs, regardless of the stated policy intent. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=39]]
