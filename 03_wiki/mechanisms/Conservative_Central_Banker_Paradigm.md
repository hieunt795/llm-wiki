## Thesis

Kenneth Rogoff (1985) provided a structural solution to the **Barro-Gordon** dilemma. If politicians (who care about short-term votes/employment) control the central bank, inflation bias is inevitable. Delegation to a "Conservative" Governor acts as a binding commitment device.

## Mechanism: The Weighting Shift

- **The Public Preference ($b$):** Society cares significantly about employment.
- **The Governor's Preference ($b_{cb}$):** The appointee is chosen such that $b_{cb} < b$.
- **Result:** Because the Governor hates inflation more, the temptation to create "Surprise Inflation" is smaller. This lowers the public's inflation expectations ($\pi^e$), leading to a lower actual equilibrium inflation.

## Boundaries / Conditions

### The Second-Best Problem
There is a limit to "Conservatism." If a central banker is *too* conservative ($b_{cb} \approx 0$), they will ignore output stabilization entirely during a crisis, potentially leading to unnecessary economic suffering. The optimal choice is someone moderately more conservative than the median voter.
