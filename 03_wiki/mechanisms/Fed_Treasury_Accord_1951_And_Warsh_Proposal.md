## Thesis

The 1951 Accord broke the Federal Reserve's post-WWII obligation to purchase unlimited Treasury bonds at a fixed rate (2.5% cap on long-term yields). This mechanism reestablished Fed independence by giving the central bank discretion over bond purchases, allowing long-term rates to float. Modern invocations (Warsh 2026) use the Accord's precedent not to replicate WWII-era financing but to propose a **three-pillar framework** strengthening Fed autonomy: (1) Treasury controls debt maturity, (2) Fed exits credit allocation, (3) Fed and Treasury coordinate on crisis protocols. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane (2026)]

## Mechanism (RCL Framework)

### 1. Regime Lock (Historical Context)

**WWII–1951 Period:** The Federal Reserve was contractually bound to purchase Treasury bonds to hold long-term yields below 2.5%. This created **Fiscal Dominance** — the Fed's balance sheet became a monetary financing tool rather than a price-stability instrument. By 1946–1951, the Fed recognized this was driving inflation and loss of policy autonomy.

**1951 Accord Outcome:** The Fed negotiated independence (instrument independence) to refuse new Treasury bond purchases unless warranted by monetary conditions, not fiscal financing needs. This broke the mechanical link between fiscal deficits and monetary expansion. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane (2026)]

### 2. Causal Chain (Three Modern Pillars)

**[LENSES: John H. Cochrane — institutional boundaries between central banks and fiscal authorities; Perry Warjiyo (Central Bank Policy, Ch. 8) — independence as a prerequisite for price stability; Ulrich Bindseil — monetary operations architecture]**

The mechanism operates through **three explicit bargains**:

#### Pillar I: Maturity Structure Ownership
- **Problem:** In 2008–2020 QE episodes, the Fed purchased long-term Treasury bonds and issued overnight reserves in exchange. This **swaps the interest-rate risk** originally purchased by the Treasury (e.g., when the Treasury issues 30-year fixed debt, it hedges against rising rates). The Fed undid this hedge, converting back to floating-rate risk.
  - Transmission: Treasury issues long-term bond → Fed buys it (undoing maturity hedge) → Fed issues overnight reserves (short-duration, floating rate) → Interest rate shock → Fed's losses compound, transferred to Treasury/taxpayers via central bank profits remittance.
  - Condition: Applies when Fed holds QE duration >2 years while issuing <3-month maturity reserves. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]
  
- **Warsh Proposal:** Treasury retains exclusive control of debt issuance maturity mix. If Fed must purchase long-term bonds (for "market functioning"), it should immediately **swap interest-rate risk back to Treasury** or purchase only short-term debt. This clarifies accountability: Treasury owns fiscal duration risk, Fed owns price-stability risk.

#### Pillar II: Exit from Credit Allocation
- **Problem:** Fed purchases of mortgage-backed securities (MBS) funnel subsidized credit to homeowners rather than businesses. This is a **political/distributional decision** masked as monetary policy. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]
- **Warsh Proposal:** If credit allocation is necessary, Treasury should purchase MBS directly (as it now does with $200B programs). This places distributional choices where they belong — with elected officials accountable to voters.
- **Condition:** Applies to multi-year programs, not temporary crisis liquidity provision. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]

#### Pillar III: Crisis Protocols
- **Problem:** In 2020, the Fed treated COVID-19 as license to monetize ~$3 trillion in new Treasury issuance (roughly 15% of GDP). This reprised WWII-era financing and contributed (in some estimates) to 2021–2023 inflation. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]
- **Warsh Proposal:** Congress should declare a **crisis exception** (similar to TARP 2008) that temporarily authorizes the Fed to ease, rather than the Fed unilaterally deciding "dislocation = unlimited financing." This transfers political accountability to legislators while preserving Fed discretion during true emergencies.
- **Boundary Condition:** Without this clarity, the Fed cannot credibly commit to non-financing in normal times, weakening its independence signal.

### 3. Feedback Topology

**Reinforcing Loop (Credibility):**
- Fed clearly refuses to finance deficits → Market expects higher Treasury yields on issuance → Treasury faces fiscal discipline pressure → Fiscal consolidation improves → Inflation expectations anchor → Fed credibility strengthens → Fed can act more effectively with smaller moves.

**Balancing Loop (Flexibility):**
- If Fed binds itself too tightly (e.g., "never purchase long-term debt"), it loses flexibility to stabilize financial markets during genuine dislocations. A **constrained discretion** model (Fed can act in emergencies, but Congress owns the decision) preserves both autonomy and practical effectiveness.

### 4. Interactions with Other Mechanisms

- **Fiscal Dominance:** The entire framework rests on breaking fiscal dominance. If Treasury runs massive deficits and the Fed feels compelled to finance them, all three pillars collapse. Prerequisite: fiscal discipline (manageable debt-to-GDP ratio). [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]
- **Bond Market Pricing:** If the Fed exits maturity management, Treasury must develop deeper, more liquid bond markets to absorb long-term issuance without distortion. Interaction: Healthy bond markets ↔ Fed credibility on non-financing.
- **Financial Stability Mandate:** Modern central banks (ECB, BOJ) use QE partly to stabilize markets. The Accord framework does not forbid this but clarifies boundaries: temporary market interventions ≠ permanent fiscal financing. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]

### 5. Quantified Anchors

- **1951 Accord Context:** Fed was forced to purchase Treasury bonds until long-term yield hit the 2.5% ceiling — unlimited commitment, no discretion. [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane]
- **2020 QE Scale:** Fed purchased ~$3 trillion in Treasuries/MBS in 2020–2021, roughly equivalent to ~15% of GDP stimulus. Compare: 2008 QE1 was ~$1.7 trillion (9% GDP). [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane — historical reference]
- **Debt Maturity Mismatch (Recent):** Fed's balance sheet holds ~$1.5 trillion in long-term Treasuries (duration ~6 years) funded by overnight reserves. If rates rise 200 bps, notional loss ~$180 billion (12% of balance sheet equity). [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane implicit]

### 6. Boundary Conditions & Failure Modes

1. **Fiscal Dominance (Primary Failure Mode):** If Treasury debt-to-GDP exceeds 120% and rolls over in <3-year average maturity, Fed will face pressure to finance regardless of Accord language. Venezuela, Japan (pre-1990s) examples.
2. **Genuine Financial Dislocations:** In 2008–2009 (Lehman collapse), markets froze; some emergency Fed action was necessary. The Accord does not forbid this but requires it be **temporary, crisis-specific, and with clear exit criteria.**
3. **Regime Shift (Fiscal Consolidation Failure):** If Accord is signed but fiscal discipline collapses (e.g., massive new entitlements), the framework loses credibility within 5–10 years.

## Worked Example: Interpreting Warsh's 2026 Proposal

**Context:** Kevin Warsh published commentary in Feb 2026 invoking a "new Fed-Treasury Accord" to resolve tensions over Fed bond holdings and maturity structure. **Update (2026-07-03):** Warsh was confirmed and sworn in as Federal Reserve Chairman, holding his first FOMC press conference as Chair on 2026-06-17 [RAW-OFFICIAL: Warsh_FOMC_PressConf_20260617_And_Sintra.md] — the Feb 2026 commentary below should now be read as the sitting Chair's own prior position, not an external proposal he might later be pressured to adopt. His first press conference announced a balance-sheet task force to "review the benefits and risks of the current ample-reserves regime and the composition of the Fed's balance sheet," directly consistent with the maturity-structure-ownership pillar described below — this is no longer speculative pressure but an active internal review under his own chairmanship.

**Market Interpretation (Naive):** "Warsh wants Treasury to control Fed policy" → Fear that Fed will print money to finance deficits.

**Cochrane's Reinterpretation:**
- Warsh is NOT proposing Fed subservience; he's proposing **Fed say "no" more often**.
- Current state (post-QE): Fed holds long-term bonds, issues short reserves → This compounds fiscal risk for taxpayers.
- Warsh's proposal: Restore the 1951 logic — if government wants long-term debt, it stays at the Treasury. Fed buys only short-term paper or refuses the maturity mismatch entirely.
- **Result:** More fiscal pressure on Treasury (higher yields), but stronger Fed independence signal and clearer accountability lines.

**Evidence from Source:** Cochrane explicitly states: "The invocation of the Accord, in which the Fed won the right *not* to buy bonds tells you what's up. Otherwise, it would be 'repeal the accord.'" [RAW-CLIP: Cochrane (2026)]

## Failure Conditions (Detailed)

| Failure Mode | Trigger | Consequence |
|---|---|---|
| **Fiscal Dominance Re-Emergence** | Debt > 150% GDP, <3yr avg maturity | Fed cannot credibly refuse financing; Accord collapses within 2–3 years |
| **Liquidity Crisis Override** | Banking/FX crisis hits; Treasury unable to issue | Fed pressured to breach Accord; credibility damage permanent |
| **Political Whipsaw** | Congress changes Fed leadership when Accord inconvenient | Fed independence undermined; next crisis = automatic financing |
| **Instrument Instability** | If short-term rates become volatile (ZLB, supply shocks), Fed may "need" to manage long-term curve | Fed creep back into maturity management under guise of "market function" |
