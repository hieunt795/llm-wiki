# [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply]]

## Thesis
[WIKI] Central banks must adjust the scale of their monetary policy operations to offset fluctuations in autonomous factors, ensuring that the supply of reserves matches the total demand generated by reserve requirements. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=42]]

## Definition
[RAW] Autonomous factors are defined as all central bank balance sheet items which are neither monetary policy operations nor the deposits of banks. They are not under the direct control of the monetary policy implementation function but impact the money market. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=42]]

[RAW] Net autonomous factors = Banknotes + Government deposits – Foreign reserves – Investment portfolios. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=42]]

## Mechanism
[RAW] The balance sheet identity can be expressed as: $Deposits\ of\ banks = Monetary\ policy\ operations - Net\ autonomous\ factors$. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=42]]

[RAW] To maintain stability, the central bank must adjust operations such that: $Monetary\ policy\ operations = Reserve\ requirements + Net\ autonomous\ factors$. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=43]]

[LLM] In this framework, the right-hand side represents the "demand" for reserves (RR + Net AF as a liability), while the left-hand side represents the "supply" through central bank credit or asset purchases.

[RAW] "Liquidity absorbing" items include liabilities (banknotes, government deposits), while "liquidity providing" items include assets (foreign reserves, investment portfolios). Any increase in an autonomous liability leads to a decrease in bank deposits unless offset by the central bank. [[Autonomous_Factors_and_Liquidity_Supply#page=43]]
