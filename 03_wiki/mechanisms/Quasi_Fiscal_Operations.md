# [[Quasi_Fiscal_Operations]]

## Thesis

[RAW] In many transition economies, central banks and other public financial institutions are involved in activities that give rise to financial transactions affecting the effective size of the fiscal deficit.

## 1. Definition and Scope

[RAW] Quasi-fiscal operations (QFOs) are activities of the central bank and public financial institutions that create fiscal-equivalent effects — subsidies, taxes, or financing flows — but are not recorded in the conventional government budget.

**Three main types of QFOs:**

| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Exchange rate subsidies** | Administered through the exchange system — government or state enterprises receive FX at below-market rates |
| **Subsidized lending** | Below-market-rate credit to government, public enterprises, or private entities |
| **Unfunded/contingent liabilities** | Exchange rate guarantees, deposit insurance, implicit bail-out commitments |

## 2. Why QFOs Matter

[RAW] Where QFOs are significant, their costs need to be included in any comprehensive measure of the public sector deficit for several reasons:

1. **Financial instability:** In many countries, central and public sector bank losses are so large that they contribute to financial instability.
2. **Misleading headline deficit:** Their existence means that conventional measures of the government's fiscal balance are misleading indicators of the true role of fiscal operations in the economy.
3. **Distortionary effects:** The taxes and subsidies associated with QFOs may have distortionary effects on resource allocation — below-market energy lending subsidizes energy-intensive sectors; FX subsidies penalize exporters while subsidizing importers.

## 3. Treatment and Reform

[RAW] Although QFOs are not always easy to quantify precisely, they need to be extracted from the accounts of the central bank and public financial institutions.

**Three-step reform sequence:**

1. **Quantify and extract:** Extract QFOs from central bank/public financial institution accounts for inclusion in a comprehensive public sector deficit measure.
2. **Convert to explicit:** To the extent possible, transform quasi-fiscal activities into normal budgetary operations — replace quasi-fiscal taxes and subsidies with explicit taxes and subsidies.
3. **Address root causes:** The long-term objective is to address structural causes of QFOs:
   - Lending activities that are essentially substitutes for explicit budget subsidies.
   - Lack of a unified exchange system (multiple exchange rate regimes that embed FX subsidies).
   - Revise the central bank's legal authority to limit its scope to carry out QFOs.

[RAW] Such structural reforms are essential to minimize and eventually eliminate the need for quasi-fiscal operations.

## 4. Impact on Monetary Analysis

[LLM] QFOs distort the standard monetary programming framework in two ways:

1. **NDA contamination:** When the central bank extends subsidized credit (directed credit programs), Net Domestic Assets (NDA) expand beyond what is required for the government financing gap — the excess represents a QFO-driven monetary expansion. The conventional NDA ceiling set by the IMF does not capture this if QFO lending is recorded off-budget.

2. **Monetary policy effectiveness:** Below-market directed lending rates insulate borrowers from monetary policy rate changes. When the central bank tightens by raising policy rates, the subsidized borrowers face no additional cost — the transmission is broken. This forces more aggressive tightening than would be needed in an economy without QFOs to achieve the same disinflation.

## 5. Fiscal Impact of Government Recapitalizations

[RAW] A related issue: when governments inject capital into undercapitalized banks or assume their debts:
- The GFS cash-basis framework does not treat the government's **noncash assumption of debt** as an expenditure at the time the debt is assumed.
- However, **future cash interest payments** on assumed debt are included as expenditures, and **cash amortization payments** as negative financing.
- According to GFS methodology, only the interest payments on assumed debt affect the size of the fiscal balance.

[RAW] This means that bank recapitalization through debt assumption understates the fiscal cost in the period of assumption and spreads it forward through higher interest expenditures. The true fiscal impact — the present value of all future debt service on assumed liabilities — is only visible through balance sheet analysis, not cash-flow analysis.

## 6. Central Bank Profits and Revenue Recognition

[RAW] The profits of the central bank that are actually transferred to the government are treated as revenue. However:
- These profits should **not** include unrealized profits from revaluation of foreign exchange or gold reserves.
- They should cover the **entire** operations of the central bank, not just selected operations (such as sales of foreign exchange or gold).

## 7. Treasury Insight

[LLM] For analysts monitoring EM sovereigns with large state-owned banking sectors:

1. **QFO detection checklist:** Look for (a) multiple/dual FX windows, (b) directed credit mandates in the central bank law, (c) state bank lending rates significantly below policy rates, (d) growing NPL stock in state banks with no provisioning. Each is a QFO signal.

2. **True fiscal deficit = conventional deficit + QFO cost:** The market-relevant fiscal position is wider than the headline. IMF staff typically publish a "augmented fiscal deficit" that includes quasi-fiscal activities of the central bank; this is the relevant metric for fiscal sustainability analysis.

3. **Bank recapitalization as fiscal event:** A government that recapitalizes a state bank creates a future stream of interest expenditures that does not appear in the current-year fiscal accounts. For bond investors, this is a forward liability that worsens the medium-term debt dynamics even if the current-year deficit looks manageable. See [[Fiscal_Sustainability_Debt_Dynamics]].
