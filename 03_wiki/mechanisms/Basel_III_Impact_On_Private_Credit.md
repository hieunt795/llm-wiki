## Thesis

Basel III intervenes not by dictating what banks can lend, but by redefining the **cost of holding assets** through a multi-layered capital barrier system. By making corporate credit artificially expensive to hold on the balance sheet, capital regulation incentivizes asset shedding—stimulating the growth of private credit not through bans, but through structural return-on-equity (ROE) optimization.

## Mechanism

### The Multi-layered Capital Barrier

1. **CET1 Ratio:** Raises actual common equity requirements to 10–13%.
   > ⚠️ **[UPDATED 2026-03-20]:** Bản Re-proposal (Basel III Endgame) tại Mỹ đã đảo ngược dự phóng tăng vốn 19% trước đây, giảm yêu cầu CET1 xuống trung bình **-4.8% cho G-SIBs**. Dù vậy, vốn tự có cho các khoản vay rủi ro vẫn bị áp lực do LCR và AOCI phase-in.
2. **RWA (Risk-Weighted Assets) Inflation:** A $100M leveraged loan carries a risk weight of **75% to 150%**, generating $75–150M in RWA and requiring $9–18M in hard capital.
3. **Supplementary Leverage Ratio (SLR):** A non-risk-based backstop (~5% for G-SIBs) that turns balance sheet space into a scarce commodity, even for low-risk assets.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **Allocation by ROE:** When an asset's capital charge increases so much that it can no longer clear the bank's target ROE (e.g., 12–15%) under competitive loan pricing, the bank's internal Asset Liability Management (ALM) engine eliminates it. The retreat is an output of internal capital optimisation, not external regulatory prohibition.

### The ALM Transmission Channels

1. **ROE Compression:**
   CET1 increases the denominator of the ROE equation. If competition limits loan rate hikes, the ROE of speculative corporate lending falls below the bank's hurdle rate. Banks retreat because lending is no longer economically viable under the new capital constraints.

2. **Output Floors & RWA Standardization (Basel 3.1):**
   The imposition of a 72.5% "output floor" restricts banks from using internal models to artificially lower risk weights on middle-market and leveraged lending. This forces previously profitable assets out of the banking envelope.

3. **Liquidity Coverage Ratio (LCR) Constraints:**
   Private credit consists of illiquid, long-term loans. LCR forces banks to hold High-Quality Liquid Assets (HQLA) against potential outflows. Tying up capital in illiquid private debt degrades the bank's LCR, adding pressure to shift these assets to private funds with locked-up capital structures.

### Structural Result: An Adaptive System

- **Risk Migration:** Basel does not reduce aggregate risk; it merely transfers it from the regulated banking sector to the shadow banking (NBFI) sector.
- **Structural Fragmentation:** The credit market bifurcates: Banks focus on high-grade, flow-based, and advisory businesses, while private credit absorbs bespoke, high-yield, and middle-market lending.

### Boundaries / Conditions

### Heterogeneous Implementation

While Basel is a unified framework, local implementation dictates the speed and form of migration:

| Region | Primary Binding Constraint | Result |
|---|---|---|
| **USA** | Leverage Ratio (SLR) & G-SIB capital surcharges | Rapid shift to an "Originate-to-distribute" model; aggressive growth of private credit. |
| **Europe (EU)** | **Output Floor 72.5%** (CRR3/CRD6) | Massive RWA inflation for banks historically reliant on internal models, accelerating European private credit adoption. |
| **Asia (APAC)** | More flexible implementation; banks remain central | Slower migration, but pressure is building as governance standards converge globally. |

> [!NOTE]
> Basel is unified in principle but heterogeneous in execution. This differential implementation dictates the trajectory of regional private credit markets.
