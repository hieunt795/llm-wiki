## Thesis
The Taylor rule is an interest rate feedback rule that adjusts the short-term nominal interest rate in response to changes in inflation (relative to its target) and the output gap (relative to potential output). [extracted]

## Mechanism
The rule provides a benchmark for the nominal policy rate ($i_t$):
$$i_t = i^* + \pi_t + \alpha(y_t - y^*) + \beta(\pi_t - \pi^*)$$
where:
- $i^*$: Long-term equilibrium real interest rate.
- $\pi_t$: Current average inflation.
- $\pi^*$: Target inflation rate.
- $y_t - y^*$: Output gap (actual vs. potential GDP).
- $\alpha, \beta$: Sensitivity coefficients for output and inflation deviations. [extracted]

### The Taylor Principle
Requires $\beta > 0$, meaning that if inflation rises by 1%, the central bank should raise the nominal rate by *more* than 1% to increase the **real** interest rate and cool the economy. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Contemporaneous:** The original rule is based on current data, whereas some variants use forward-looking forecasts to account for policy lags. [extracted]
- **Judgment:** Taylor (1993) conceded that rules should be used as a guideline, allowing for discretionary departures in exceptional circumstances. [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Taylor (1993):** Showed that this simple rule accurately described the Federal Reserve's interest rate behavior. [extracted]
- **Indonesia:** The "BI Rate" implementation follows the logic of interest rate rules within an ITF framework. [extracted]
