## Thesis

The "Fed Put" (named after the protective put option) is an implicit guarantee that the central bank will provide a floor for asset prices. While not a formal mandate, central banks are mindful of equity valuations because of the **Wealth Effect**: in economies where a large portion of household assets is invested in equities (like the US), a market crash leads to reduced consumer expenditure, which is disinflationary. Thus, to fulfill its mandate for price stability, the central bank may feel compelled to stabilize asset prices. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch09.md#page=54]]

## Mechanism: The Wealth Effect Transmission

1.  **Market Shock:** Equity prices fall sharply.
2.  **Wealth Destruction:** Household net worth (especially in pension funds and 401ks) contracts.
3.  **Consumption Drop:** Economic agents reduce spending to rebuild savings (the wealth effect), leading to a contraction in aggregate demand.
4.  **Central Bank Response:** The central bank eases monetary policy (rate cuts or outright asset purchases) to support valuations, restore household wealth, and stimulate demand. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch09.md#page=54]]

## Strategic Implications: The Volatility Paradox

### 1. Volatility as a Brake
Alexander Düring argues that volatility is a necessary "natural brake" on excessive risk-taking. It forces market participants to develop risk management skills and stay mindful of potential losses. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch09.md#page=54]]

### 2. The Moral Hazard Risk
By providing a perceived floor (the Put), the central bank removes this natural brake. This encourages **irrational exuberance** and excessive leverage, as investors believe the downside risk has been eliminated. 

### 3. Leaning Against the Wind
Ideally, central banks should "lean against" both higher and lower asset prices. However, while they are quick to intervene on the downside (the Put), they are often slower to lean against excessive valuations on the upside, which can store up future systemic risks. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch09.md#page=54]]
