## Thesis

ITF represents a shift from mechanical monetary/exchange rate anchors to a governance-based framework where the **Inflation Forecast** acts as the de facto intermediate target. By anchoring public expectations to a transparent, medium-term numerical target, the central bank breaks the **Time-Inconsistency** trap, decoupling long-term price stability from short-term political or interventionist pressures. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 224-225]

## Mechanism (RCL Framework)

### 1. Regime Lock
Historically, the transition to ITF began in the 1980s-1990s (New Zealand 1990, Canada 1991) as a response to the failure of monetary targeting. In EMEs (Chile, Brazil, Indonesia), it emerged as a mechanism to build credibility post-crises. The regime operates under **Instrument Independence** (Central Bank controls tools) but **Goal Dependence/Consultation** (Government often sets/agrees on the target). A critical prerequisite is the absence of **Fiscal Dominance**; the central bank must not be a source of government financing. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 223, 235]

### 2. Causal Chain
The transmission logic follows:
**Mandate Codification** →[Legal Basis (e.g., BI Act 1999)]→ **Numerical Target Announcement** →[Anchoring Expectations]→ **Policy Rate Adjustment** →[Transmission Channels]→ **Inflation Forecast Realignment**.
- **Intent:** To eliminate the **Asymmetric Information** between the central bank and economic players.
- **Mechanism:** The central bank commits to a medium-long term target. If the 2-year inflation forecast deviates from this target, the central bank adjusts the policy rate *today*. This creates a "constrained discretion" where rules (like Taylor-type) guide but do not dictate policy.
- **Interaction:** Interacts with fiscal policy through accountability mechanisms. ITF requires fiscal discipline to prevent the "overprinting of money" to finance deficits, which would otherwise undermine price stability. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 225-228]

### 3. Feedback Topology
The core of ITF is the **Credibility-Expectations Loop**. 
- **Reinforcing:** Success in hitting targets → Increased Credibility → Better Anchored Expectations → Reduced [[Sacrifice_Ratio_Mechanism|Sacrifice Ratio]] (lower output cost to control inflation) → Higher Policy Effectiveness.
- **Balancing:** Flexible ITF (λ ≠ 0) allows the central bank to respond to output shocks, preventing the "inflation nutter" syndrome. This flexibility preserves social support for the central bank mandate. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 229, 231]

## Dense Unpacking (4-Step)

- **INTENT:** To anchor inflation expectations and provide a nominal anchor that is more stable and controllable than monetary aggregates or fixed exchange rates.
- **MECHANISM:** A governance framework combining: (1) Price stability mandate, (2) Numerical target, (3) Instrument independence, (4) Transparency/Accountability.
- **INTERACTIONS:** It interacts with market behavior through the **Rational Expectations Hypothesis**, where only anticipated policy affects real output in the long term, making transparency a tool for effectiveness.
- **BOUNDARY:** ITF breaks down under **Fiscal Dominance** (high debt/deficit), extreme supply-side shocks (where inflation and output gap move in opposite directions), or the **Zero Lower Bound (ZLB)**.

## Worked Example: Indonesia (Transition)
Indonesia implicitly introduced ITF in 2000 (Act No. 23/1999) following the Asian Financial Crisis to stabilize the Rupiah. The transition was gradual, with full implementation in 2005. The framework allowed BI to shift focus from broad money targeting to a price-stability-centric model, crucial for rebuilding trust in the Rupiah. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 223]

## Failure Conditions (Boundary Conditions)
1. **Instrument Instability:** If applied too strictly in a small open economy with high exchange rate volatility.
2. **Fiscal Dominance:** If the government relies on the central bank to purchase debt in the primary market.
3. **Low Credibility (ITL Regime):** Where targets are announced but never hit due to structural vulnerabilities or lack of central bank independence. [RAW-BOOK: Perry Warjiyo & Solikin M. Juhro (2019), p. 229, 247-248]
