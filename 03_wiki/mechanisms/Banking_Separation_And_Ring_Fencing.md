## Thesis

Banking separation is a structural defense mechanism designed to protect the "utility" function of the economy (retail payments and savings) from the "casino" function (wholesale trading and securities underwriting). By creating legal firewalls between these business lines, regulators aim to ensure that federally insured retail deposits do not subsidize speculative market-making and that the failure of a trading desk does not freeze the national payment system.

## Mechanism

Alexander Düring outlines four primary historical and modern models for achieving this separation:

### 1. The Glass-Steagall Model (USA)
The **Glass-Steagall Act of 1933** established the most famous separation principle in financial history.
- **The Rule:** It strictly prohibited securities underwriters and investment banks from engaging in regular commercial (deposit-taking) banking.
- **Conflict of Interest Rationale:** The primary motivation was to prevent banks from deciding whether to lend to a firm at their own risk versus offloading that firm's risk onto customers through securities underwriting (dumping "bad" loans into "good" bonds). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]

### 2. The UK Ring-Fencing Model (Post-2008)
Following the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, the United Kingdom mandated a specific form of internal segregation.
- **The Rule:** Retail banking assets and liabilities must be physically and legally isolated from the wholesale/investment banking operations within the same banking group.
- **Goal:** To ensure that the retail bank remains solvent and operational even if its investment banking parent or sibling suffers catastrophic losses in the capital markets. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]

### 3. The Japanese Explicit Separation
Japan maintains a clear legal divide between **Commercial Banks**, **Trust Banks**, and **Securities Firms**.
- **Naming Requirement:** This separation must be made explicit in the names of the legal entities. While different types can be held within a single holding company, they operate as distinct silos. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]

### 4. The "Living Will" (Orderly Resolution)
A modern, post-crisis mechanism where regulators require systemically important banks (GSIBs) to maintain updated plans for an **orderly wind-down**.
- **Mechanism:** The "living will" serves as a technical blueprint for disassembling a complex bank during a crisis. It identifies which parts can be saved, which can be sold, and which should be allowed to fail without requiring a government bailout. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18-19]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The Universal Banking Exception (Germany/France)
Unlike the US or Japan, Germany and France traditionally allow **Universal Banking**, where a single entity performs all functions. However, even these systems utilize "specialist bank designations" (e.g., *Sparkassen* for community savings, *Sociétés de crédit foncier* for real estate) to segment risks and markets. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]

### Separation vs. Narrow Banking
Banking separation must not be confused with **Narrow Banking**. Separation allows commercial banks to continue creating "Inside Money" via lending; it merely restricts *where* and *how* that money can be deployed. Narrow banking seeks to abolish private money creation entirely. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=19]]
