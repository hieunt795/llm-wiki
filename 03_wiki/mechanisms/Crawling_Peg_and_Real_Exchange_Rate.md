# [[Crawling_Peg_and_Real_Exchange_Rate]]

## Thesis

[RAW] Because inflation in transition economies was typically higher than among trading partners, a fixed exchange rate would severely erode competitive positions and place intolerable pressures on the balance of payments. The preannounced crawling peg was designed to prevent this erosion while preserving the discipline of a fixed rate.

## 1. Exchange Rate Regime Sequence (Poland Evidence)

[RAW] The transition from fixed rate to managed float followed a sequence driven by the inflation-competitiveness tradeoff:

| Period | Regime | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| January 1990 | **Fixed rate** (USD peg) | Nominal anchor against hyperinflation; rate set at 31.6% devaluation from prior level to unify official and parallel markets |
| May 1991 | **Fixed rate** (basket peg: 45% USD, 35% DEM, 10% GBP, 5% FRF, 5% CHF) | European trade rising; 14.4% devaluation against basket to restore competitiveness |
| October 1991 | **Preannounced crawling peg** (1.8%/month, Zl 9/day) | Inflation differential with trading partners made fixed rate unsustainable |
| Feb 1992 | Crawl + 10.7% devaluation | Additional competitiveness adjustment; daily rate: Zl 11 |
| Aug 1993 | Crawl slowed to 1.6%/month | Reduce crawl as disinflation progresses; 7.4% devaluation against basket |
| Sep 1994–Nov 1994 | Crawl further reduced: 1.5% → 1.4%/month | Active crawl as disinflation tool |
| May 1995 | **Float within crawling band** (±7% around crawling midrate, crawl = 1.2%/month) | Large capital inflows forced exchange rate management flexibility |

## 2. Crawling Peg Mechanics

[RAW] Under the crawling peg, the monetary authorities fix a **preannounced path** for the exchange rate involving periodic and predictable depreciations. This system is suited to a country that:
- Does not wish to abandon fixed-rate discipline entirely; but
- Needs to prevent competitiveness erosion when domestic inflation exceeds trading partner inflation.

**The critical design parameter is the rate of the crawl:**

| Crawl Type | Rate of depreciation | Effect |
|---|---|---|
| **Passive crawl** | = Projected inflation differential | Targets the real exchange rate — neutralizes competitiveness erosion |
| **Active crawl** | < Projected inflation differential | Imposes additional disinflation pressure — concedes some competitiveness but uses exchange rate as anti-inflation lever |

[RAW] Poland adopted an **active crawl** in October 1991, setting the rate at 1.8%/month — significantly below the projected inflation differential. Daily corrections rather than monthly ones avoided sharp rate movements.

## 3. Real Exchange Rate Assessment (Four Indicators)

[RAW] Assessing the appropriateness of a country's exchange rate requires considering multiple factors:

### Indicator 1: Real Exchange Rate (RER)
The most appropriate RER measure = **ratio of unit labor costs at home to unit labor costs in trading partner countries** (wage costs adjusted for productivity). Where unit labor cost data are unavailable, **relative consumer prices** serve as proxy.

Comparison baseline: prevailing RER vs. RER in a period when the current account was considered to be in broad balance (though note: the equilibrium rate changes over time with productivity, terms of trade, trade barriers).

### Indicator 2: Foreign Exchange Reserves
Falling official reserves → signal of inappropriate exchange rate (current account deficit or capital outflow due to lack of confidence). **Caveat:** reserves can be augmented by borrowing, so reserve movements alone may not be reliable indicators.

### Indicator 3: Current Account Balance
Size of current and prospective current account deficit is a major criterion:
- Under fixed rate: if projected CAB deficit cannot be financed, devaluation will eventually be necessary — even if not currently in deficit.
- But a temporary CAB deficit may not require devaluation.
- Current account reacts slowly to RER changes (J-curve), reducing its timeliness as a signal.

### Indicator 4: Parallel Market Exchange Rate
The parallel/black market rate provides a reasonable indication of the degree of disequilibrium in the official rate. Caution: parallel markets can be thin and subject to a few dealers' influence. However, a **very large discount** on domestic currency in the parallel market is often a reliable indicator of an overvalued official exchange rate.

## 4. Effective Rate of Protection and Trade Bias

[RAW] A widely used measure of trade regime bias is based on **effective protection**:

$$ERP = \frac{v' - v}{v} \tag{Box 4.7, eq. 2}$$

where $v'$ = value added at domestic prices (including tariffs/subsidies); $v$ = value added at world prices.

The **trade bias (tb)**:

$$t_b = \frac{\text{Average ERP for importables}}{\text{Average ERP for exportables}} \tag{Box 4.7, eq. 1}$$

| tb value | Implies |
|---|---|
| tb = 1 | Neutral regime — no discrimination between importables and exportables |
| tb > 1 | Bias toward import substitution (importables more protected) |
| tb < 1 | Bias toward export promotion |

[RAW] Full neutrality requires **no significant variation in effective protection rates across tradable goods industries** — an average of zero ERP can mask high dispersion.

Four principal factors determining the overall incentive structure for exportables:
1. Degree of exchange rate overvaluation
2. Direct trade controls (quotas, import licenses)
3. Effective rate of protection
4. Export incentives (direct and indirect subsidies)

## 5. Trade Policy Strategies

[RAW] Trade policy strategies divide into two broad groups:
- **Outward-oriented (export-promotion):** Does not discriminate between domestic-market production and exports — "neutral" strategy. Evidence from successful exporters (East Asia) supports this approach.
- **Inward-oriented (import substitution):** Uses trade/industrial incentives that favor domestic-market production over exports.

[RAW] Trade flows are affected by: exchange rate policy; domestic/foreign demand; tariffs, quotas, and subsidies; domestic price liberalization; partner country policy changes; regional trade agreements.

## 6. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The crawling peg episode provides the canonical case study of the inflation-competitiveness tradeoff in disinflation:

1. **Active crawl as implicit interest rate policy:** By setting the crawl below the inflation differential, policymakers effectively penalized domestic-currency holders and rewarded foreign-currency holders — providing an incentive to hold foreign currency (carry) and a constraint on domestic wage/price inflation (exporters facing competitiveness erosion demand real wage moderation). This makes the active crawl an incomes policy complement to monetary tightening.

2. **The nominal anchor versus competitiveness dilemma:** No exchange rate regime resolves this simultaneously — the "impossible trinity" (Mundell-Fleming) requires choosing between fixed rate, free capital flows, and monetary autonomy. The crawling peg attempts to split the difference, maintaining a form of anchor predictability while allowing gradual depreciation. It works until capital account liberalization makes the crawl a one-way bet for speculators (as happened in Mexico 1994, Czech Republic 1997, Turkey 2001).

3. **ERP vs. nominal tariff:** The effective rate of protection diverges significantly from nominal tariff rates when inputs are taxed differently from outputs. A high nominal tariff on final goods with zero tariff on inputs creates very high ERPs; a modest nominal tariff with high input tariffs can produce negative ERPs (penalizing domestic value-added). For transition economy analysis, constructing ERP maps is essential to identify which sectors are genuinely competitive at world prices.

4. **Four-indicator RER assessment in practice:** None of the four indicators dominates. Practitioners typically weight them by context: in a capital-account-liberalized economy, capital flight (indicator 2) and parallel market premium (indicator 4) are more informative than the current account (slow-reacting, indicator 3). In a controlled economy with multiple exchange rates, the parallel premium is the primary signal.
