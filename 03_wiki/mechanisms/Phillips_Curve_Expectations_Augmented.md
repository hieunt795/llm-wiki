## Thesis

Milton Friedman's "Money Matters" proposition revolutionized central banking by proving that money is non-neutral in the short run but neutral in the long run. ## 1. The Monetarist Proposition: "Money Matters"

Friedman (1958) argued that changes in the money supply influence nominal spending and output in the long term, but influence **real output** in the short term during the adjustment phase.

### The St. Louis Model
Monetarists used this empirical procedure to assess the link between national income ($Y$) and money supply ($M$):
$$Y = \alpha_0 + \sum a_i M_{t-1} + \sum b_j G_{t-j} + \dots$$
- **Findings:** Monetary aggregates have a stronger, faster effect on income than fiscal policy ($G$).
- **The Lag Implication:** The existence of a lag (demonstrated in Friedman & Schwartz, 1963) provides the "adequate space" for central banks to stabilize short-term real output. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=62]]

---

## 2. The Expectations-Augmented Mechanism

Friedman's contribution to the Phillips Curve was the **Natural Rate Hypothesis**.

### The Logic
- **Short Run:** A trade-off exists because of **unanticipated price shocks**. If inflation is higher than expected, real wages fall, and employment rises.
- **Long Run:** Economic agents adjust their expectations. The curve shifts upwards, and unemployment returns to its **Natural Rate**, regardless of the inflation level.
- **Inference:** Inflation targeting (ITF) is necessary to anchor these expectations and prevent the "Accelerationist" trap. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=62]]

---

## 3. The Keynesian-Neoclassical Synthesis (Gali, 1992)

Modern consensus identifies four distinct shocks that drive the curve's dynamics:

| Shock Type | Impact on Output | Impact on Prices |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Aggregate Demand** | Positive (Short-run) | Positive |
| **Aggregate Supply** | Positive | **Negative** |
| **Money Demand** | Negative | Negative |
| **Money Supply** | Positive | Positive |

**Transmission:** Monetary shocks are transmitted to the real sector through changes in the **Real Interest Rate**, validating the use of the interest rate as an operational target in the modern era. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=63]]
