## Thesis

Monetary financing—the creation of money to pay for government spending—is older than central banking itself. While it provides immediate fiscal relief, it historically leads to the destruction of the currency's value (debasement). The modern prohibition is the institutional response to protect the social contract of money.

## The Problem: Erosion of Value

In a metal standard, governments financed themselves by seizing reserves or diluting coin purity. In fiat systems, the temptation is to "print" money to cover deficits.
- **Economic Impact:** This overrides the public interest in stable money, making the currency's conversion value unpredictable or zero.
- **Result:** Loss of the **Store of Value** property, leading to hyperinflation or a collapse in credit markets.

## Modern Institutional Safeguards

1. **Central Bank Independence:** Sheltering the money-issuer from political pressure.
2. **Explicit Legal Bans:** 
    - **Europe:** Absolute prohibition on direct lending to sovereigns (TFEU Art. 123).
    - **US/Japan:** Extremely limited facilities for government funding.
3. **Primary Market Restriction:** Central banks are generally authorized to buy government bonds in the **Secondary Market** (for monetary policy) but forbidden from the **Primary Market** (direct funding).

## Boundaries / Conditions: Large-Scale Asset Purchases (QE)
The line between "Monetary Policy" and "Monetary Financing" blurs during Quantitative Easing. While the CB buys bonds to manage interest rates, it effectively acts as a lender to the state. The legal distinction rests on the **Intention** and the **Secondary market** nature of the trades.
