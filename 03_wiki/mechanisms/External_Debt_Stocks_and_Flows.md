# [[External_Debt_Stocks_and_Flows]]

## Thesis

[RAW] The stock of gross external debt is defined as the amount of outstanding contractual liabilities to nonresidents. Only the disbursed portion of a loan is part of gross debt — undisbursed commitments are not recorded until disbursement occurs.

## 1. Debt Stock Equation (eq. 4.5)

$$D_t = D_{t-1} + B_t - A_t \tag{4.5}$$

where:
- $D_t$ = stock of outstanding and disbursed debt at end of period $t$
- $B_t$ = disbursements of new loans during period $t$
- $A_t$ = amortization (repayment of principal) during period $t$

[RAW] Debt at the end of a given year is broadly equal to debt at end of the previous year, augmented by **net debt-creating flows** (new disbursements minus amortization).

**Reasons eq. 4.5 is only a rough approximation:**
1. **Valuation adjustments:** Debt contracted in multiple currencies — the dollar value of euro-denominated debt changes when the dollar depreciates/appreciates.
2. **Interest capitalization:** Unpaid interest obligations (interest arrears) are sometimes added to the debt stock.
3. **Debt cancellation:** Outstanding debt is sometimes written off.

## 2. Debt-Creating vs. Non-Debt-Creating Flows

[RAW] Within the capital and financial account, most flows are **debt-creating**. The exception is direct foreign investment (FDI), which involves equity acquisition with no contractual obligation — classified as **non-debt-creating**. All other financial account liabilities add to gross debt (on inflows) or subtract from it (on amortization repayments).

| Flow Type | Debt-creating? | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Long-term loans | Yes | Bilateral/multilateral disbursements |
| Bonds | Yes | Sovereign Eurobonds |
| Short-term trade credits | Yes | Supplier credits |
| IMF credits | Yes | Stand-By purchase |
| Portfolio equity | Yes (for debt component) | Debt securities |
| **Direct investment** | **No** | FDI equity stake |

**Why FDI is preferred:** No contractual obligations; transfers technical and managerial skills; profit repatriation depends on investment profitability (not contractually fixed unlike debt service).

## 3. Debt Burden Indicators

[RAW] Three conventional ratios for analyzing debt burden:

| Indicator | Formula | Measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Debt service/exports** | Scheduled debt service ÷ exports of goods & services | Impact of debt service obligations on foreign exchange cash flow |
| **Interest/exports** | Scheduled (or actual) interest payments ÷ exports of G&S | Current cost of the debt stock |
| **Debt/GDP** (or Debt/exports) | Total outstanding debt ÷ GDP (or exports) | Long-term sustainability of the debt burden |

[RAW] Conceptually, analysts should compare the **present value of future debt servicing obligations** with the **present value of future export receipts** — this approach requires a discount rate assumption and is sensitive to it. In practice, the three ratios above are used as proxies.

## 4. World Bank Debt Classification Thresholds

[RAW] The World Bank's debt reporting system classifies countries by the severity of indebtedness using two indicators based on present value (to account for differences in loan terms):

| Threshold | Classification |
|---|---|
| PV of total debt service / GNP > 80% | Severely indebted |
| PV of total debt service / exports > 220% | Severely indebted |

[RAW] These ratios are starting points only — economic circumstances of countries with similar ratios may differ significantly. A full assessment must take into account the overall macroeconomic situation and BOP prospects.

## 5. External Debt Sustainability

[RAW] A country's external position is considered **sustainable** if the government can be expected to meet its external obligations in full without:
- Rescheduling debt
- Seeking debt relief
- Accumulating arrears over the medium or long term

**Sustainability thresholds (Box 4.9):**
- Scheduled debt service declines to **20–25% or less** of exports of goods and services
- Financing gaps are eliminated
- NPV of debt-to-exports declines below **200–250%** of exports

[RAW] **Sustainability differs from viability:** Medium-term external viability precludes recourse to exceptional financing (including IMF resources). If exceptional financing is necessary, a country's external position cannot be regarded as viable.

## 6. Fiscal Burden of External Debt

[RAW] In many countries with external debt difficulties, scheduled debt service payments absorb a major proportion of government revenues, reducing fiscal room to maneuver. External debt sustainability is therefore closely linked to **fiscal sustainability** — see [[Fiscal_Sustainability_Debt_Dynamics]].

## 7. Gross vs. Net External Debt

[RAW] Gross debt = outstanding liabilities only. Net debt = gross debt minus stock of external assets. While net debt can be useful for countries that are both debtors and creditors (e.g., Russia), several practical difficulties arise:
1. Unclear which assets should offset liabilities (official only? or bank and nonbank assets?)
2. Net debt masks differences in maturity structure, currency composition, and risk features
3. Debt-reporting systems may not record all gross flows
4. Quality of credit owed by a country may differ fundamentally from quality of credit owed to it

For these reasons, international practice uses **gross debt** as the primary concept.

## 8. Treasury Insight

[LLM] The debt stock dynamics framework is foundational to EM debt analysis:

1. **Valuation effects can dominate:** For countries with large USD-denominated debts, a 10% dollar appreciation mechanically increases the gross external debt stock (in domestic currency or in relation to GDP) by approximately 10% times the foreign-currency share. This valuation component is often larger than annual net debt-creating flows — which is why equation 4.5 is a "rough approximation" and why IIP stock data is required alongside flow data.

2. **Debt service/exports as the operational constraint:** The interest/GDP ratio captures the fiscal cost, but debt service/exports captures the foreign exchange constraint. Countries can run primary surpluses in local currency but still face external crises when export earnings are insufficient to service external debt — the two constraints are related but distinct. A country with a large domestic-currency debt but small external debt faces the fiscal constraint; a large commodity exporter with high external debt primarily faces the external constraint.

3. **FDI vs. debt financing:** The non-debt-creating character of FDI is not just accounting — it means no contractual call on foreign exchange regardless of economic conditions. Debt service becomes due regardless of whether the investment produces returns; FDI profit repatriation shrinks automatically in a bad year. This is why multilateral frameworks (WTO, bilateral investment treaties) and IMF programs have historically preferred FDI-heavy financing mixes for transition economies.

4. **Sustainability thresholds are calibrations, not laws:** The 20–25% debt service/exports and 200–250% NPV/exports thresholds were derived empirically from countries that experienced debt distress. They are useful first-pass filters. A country breaching these thresholds in an otherwise improving macroeconomic environment (rising exports, falling interest rates) may remain viable; one meeting the thresholds in a deteriorating environment may not.
