## Thesis

The efficacy of QE depends on which transmission channels are active in a specific economic environment. Alexander Düring identifies eight primary paths through which large-scale asset purchases influence the price level and real activity. While monetarists focus on direct money supply increases, modern central banking relies on more indirect structural channels. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=59]]

## The 8-Channel Matrix

| Channel | Label | Mechanism |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Quantity Effect** | (A) | More [[Base_Money]] ($M$) in the system should, ceteris paribus, increase the price level ($P$). |
| **FX Market** | (B) | Increasing the supply of domestic currency devalues it, raising import prices and supporting export demand. |
| **Signaling** | (C) | Using a theoretically unlimited balance sheet signals the central bank's absolute commitment to its inflation target. |
| **Relative Pricing** | (D) | Intervening in specific asset markets (e.g., mortgage bonds) directly lowers financing costs in those sectors. |
| **Portfolio Effect** | (E) | Removing safe assets from private portfolios forces investors to reallocate their cash to riskier alternatives (spillover). |
| **Bank Capital Formation** | (F) | Raising asset prices allows banks to sell assets above amortized cost, creating permanent new equity capital. |
| **Risk-Weighted Assets** | (G) | Removing assets with non-zero risk weights from bank balance sheets frees up regulatory capital for new lending. |
| **Lower Volatility** | (H) | Dampening price volatility flattens the yield curve (lower term risk premia) and stimulates investment. |

[[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=59-60]]

## Critical Transmission Mechanisms

### 1. Portfolio Rebalancing (Risk-Forcing)
The central bank removes high-quality sovereign debt from the market. Private asset managers, now holding excess cash but lacking risk-free bonds, are coerced into reallocating capital into corporate bonds and equities. This structural spillover lowers funding costs for the private sector. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=60]]

### 2. Bank Capital and RWA Channels
In economies where bank lending is the primary source of credit, channels (F) and (G) are critical. QE effectively "recapitalizes" the banking system by engineering higher asset values and freeing up risk-weighted capital, enabling banks to lend even if they were previously constrained by a lack of equity. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=60]]

### 3. Balance Sheet Lengthening
Large-scale asset purchases force the banking system to lengthen its aggregate balance sheet. When the central bank buys assets from non-banks (like insurance companies), the banks must accommodate the new deposits created. Thus, an increase in **Outside Money** (CB liquidity) is matched by an increase in **Inside Money** (bank deposits). [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=61]]

## Boundaries / Conditions: The Volatility Paradox

While dampening volatility (Channel H) stimulates investment in the short term, prolonged suppression of volatility is detrimental to financial stability.
- **Moral Hazard:** Investors increase leverage as the "natural brake" of volatility is removed.
- **Liquidity Erosion:** Market-makers disinvest from their operations during low-volatility periods.
- **The Crash:** If volatility returns abruptly, the resulting deleveraging occurs in an environment with depleted market-maker capacity, leading to amplified systemic shocks. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=60]]
