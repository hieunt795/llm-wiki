## Thesis

The **Shar'iah** framework rules out traditional Western fixed income instruments because they are based on unconditional interest (usury). Instead, Islamic Finance requires a shared risk-reward mechanism, transforming "creditors" into quasi-"equity partners."

## Convention

### 1. Prohibition of Riba (Interest)
Lending money to earn more money (interest) is strictly forbidden. Money is viewed as a medium of exchange, not a commodity that can generate wealth on its own.
- **Fixed Income Impact:** Conventional bonds, which pay coupons based on a principal loan, are disallowed.

### 2. Prohibition of Gharar (Uncertainty)
Contracts with excessive ambiguity or where the outcome is purely speculative are forbidden. Both parties must have clear knowledge of the subject matter and price.
- **Fixed Income Impact:** Rules out complex derivatives where the underlying or payoff is highly uncertain or deceptive.

### 3. Prohibition of Maysir (Gambling)
Acquiring wealth by chance rather than productive effort is prohibited.
- **Fixed Income Impact:** Rules out "pure options" or speculative bets on market direction without underlying economic substance.

### Variants / Sukuk
To interact with global markets, Islamic Finance uses **Sukuk** (Islamic Bonds). These are not debt obligations but certificates representing ownership in an underlying asset or project. The "coupon" is a share of the profit generated by that asset, rather than interest.
