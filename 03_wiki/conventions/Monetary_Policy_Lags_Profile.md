## Thesis

Milton Friedman famously stated that monetary policy operates with **"Long and Variable Lags."** Understanding this profile is why central banks must be **Pre-emptive** (Forward-looking): raising rates today to fix inflation that will happen in 18 months.

## Lag Structure (Perry Warjiyo's Timeline)

### 1. Stage 1: Financial Sector Response (Short Lag)
- **Duration:** **3 to 6 months**.
- **Mechanics:**
    - **Fast:** FX market and Stock market respond almost instantly through portfolio rebalancing.
    - **Slow:** Bank lending and deposit rates lag because they must wait for existing term contracts to mature.

### 2. Stage 2: Real Economy Response (Long Lag)
- **Duration:** **12 to 18 months** (up to 2 years for peak effect).
- **Mechanics:** Households and firms take time to adjust their consumption and investment plans. Decisions to build a factory or buy a house are based on long-term outlooks, not overnight rate shifts.

## Variables for Indonesia
Empirical evidence in Indonesia (Solikin, 2005) confirms an average lag of **1.5 years**.
- **Peak effect on Output:** Around 1 year.
- **Peak effect on Prices:** Persistent, up to 2 years.

## Boundaries / Conditions

### Asymmetry of Lags
Lags are not constant. They tend to be:
- **Shorter during upswings** (financial accelerator speeds up transmission).
- **Longer and less effective during downswings** (banks' reluctance to lend creates a "Credit Crunch" blockage).
