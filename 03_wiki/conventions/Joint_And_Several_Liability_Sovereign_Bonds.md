## Thesis

To maximize liquidity and achieve extremely cheap sovereign funding, smaller entities frequently pool their borrowing requirements into massive "Joint Bond" issues. The fundamental stability of these joint macro-bonds relies entirely on the legal parameters describing what happens if one member of the consortium goes bankrupt. The legal framework branches violently into two distinct protocols: pure fractionalization ("Several Liability") and absolute collective defense ("Joint and Several Liability"). The implementation of these protocols serves as the ultimate boundary testing the limits of fiscal sovereignty within macro-economic blocks like the EU and Japan.

## Convention

### 1. Several (But Not Joint) Liability
The fractional model.
- **Mechanics:** Each member is liable solely for the exact fraction of the debt they physically received.
- **Systemic Impact:** If the consortium issues a $\$10$B bond, and Entity A takes $\$1$B, Entity A's liability is capped at $\$1$B. If Entity B defaults entirely on its share, Entity A has zero legal obligation to cover the shortfall. The bond investors take the loss.
- **Example:** The joint issues of the German regional states (Länder). The market prices these bonds effectively ignoring the structure, treating the constituent German states as virtually risk-free.

### 2. Joint and Several Liability
The cross-mutualized model.
- **Mechanics:** Every single member of the consortium is legally liable for the *entire* amount of the bond.
- **Systemic Impact:** If Entity B defaults, the bond investors can legally force Entity A to repay Entity B's share. This represents the ultimate systemic guarantee, allowing the weakest members to flawlessly borrow at the exact same ultra-low interest rates as the strongest members.
- **Example:** Japanese Joint Municipal Bonds utilize this structure, underpinned by the overarching fiscal integration and strict oversight policies of the Japanese central government.

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The European Union Constitutional Blockade
In the aftermath of the Eurozone Crisis, analysts aggressively demanded the EU issue "Stability Bonds" (Eurobonds) utilizing Joint and Several Liability to permanently eradicate sovereign spread panics (e.g., Greece vs Germany).
The mechanism was fiercely rejected due to absolute constitutional blockades:
- **Article 125 TFEU (The No-Bailout Clause):** A member state cannot legally assume the commitments of a fellow member state. Implementing a Joint and Several bond allows a bankrupt state to legally extort ex-post fiscal transfers from solvent states simply by defaulting on the joint bond.
- **Fiscal Sovereignty:** The German Constitutional Court explicitly ruled in 2011 that transferring fiscal liability without the prior, explicit democratic mandate of the local parliament fundamentally breaches constitutional sovereignty.

### The Supranational Workaround
To still issue AAA-rated common debt, the market utilizes **Supranationals** (e.g., EIB, ESM).
Instead of issuing debt jointly guaranteed by member states, a distinct, isolated corporate entity is created. The member states inject "Paid-Up Capital" and promise massive "Callable Capital." The Supranational issues debt under its own name. If a borrower defaults, the Supranational asks its sovereign members for capital injections to patch the equity hole, completely bypassing the illegal "Joint and Several" debt guarantee prohibition.
