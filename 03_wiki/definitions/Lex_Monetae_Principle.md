## Thesis

Lex monetae gives sovereigns the power to "discharge debts" through assets of their choosing. While trivial in peacetime, this principle becomes a significant risk factor during crises, as it can be used to unilaterally devalue domestic currency claims without being technically in default.

## Scope and Power

1. **Legislating Legal Tender:** Sovereigns command large payment flows (taxes) and can therefore lend significance to any asset by prescribing it as "Forced Money."
2. **Historical Precedent:** The **Pax Westphalica (1648)** set the precedent that international restitution payments could be specified in the local currency of the sovereign.
3. **Internal vs. International:** Lex monetae applies strictly to domestic law. In cross-border funding or international treaties, sovereigns typically cannot unilaterally change the value of monetary claims without violating international law.

## Boundaries / Limits

### The Dollarization Constraint
If a legislated money becomes unsuitable for daily transactions (due to hyperinflation), society may choose to ignore *lex monetae* and use foreign currencies (e.g., USD) for trade. This "Market-based Veto" proves that sovereign power over money is not absolute.

### The Euro Area Paradox
Member states of the Euro Area have effectively "signed away" their *lex monetae* rights regarding domestic currency. Article 118 of the TFEU states that the Euro is the currency of the EU, making it illegal for a member state to unilaterally revert to a devalued national currency for existing debt.
