## Thesis

As a system developed to provide Muslim investors with profitable alternatives to interest-bearing Western instruments, **Islamic Finance** replaces fixed-interest lending with equity-based models. It is rooted in the prohibition of *riba* (interest), *gharar* (uncertainty), and *maysir* (gambling), requiring that capital providers share in the risks of the enterprise.

## Definition

Islamic Finance refers to financial services and products that comply with the principles of Shar’iah law. These principles strictly forbid the standard fixed income concept of "money for more money" (interest).

### The Three Core Prohibitions

1. **Riba (Interest):** The prohibition of lending money at interest or the issuance of unconditional repayment obligations. This is the primary hurdle for traditional fixed income instruments in Islamic jurisdictions.
2. **Gharar (Uncertainty):** The prohibition of contracts with excessive uncertainty or lack of clarity regarding the terms of exchange. This generally rules out pure options and speculative derivatives.
3. **Maysir (Gambling):** The prohibition of activities where wealth is gained through pure speculation or chance.

### Comparison to Western Finance

While the legal structures differ, the economic substance of Islamic products is often translatable to Western terms.
- **Profit-Sharing:** Instead of earning interest, investors earn a share of the profits from equity investments.
- **Risk-Sharing:** Lenders and borrowers form "equitable partnerships" where risks are shared, rather than the borrower being solely liable for repayment regardless of business performance.
- **Regulatory Convergence:** As Muslim nations grow economically, Islamic finance practices have begun to influence Western notions, particularly within the **Ecological and Social Responsibility (ESR)** investment trend, which shares similar ethical concerns.

### Boundaries / Conditions

Islamic finance presents a distinct legal relationship between lenders and borrowers:
- **Discretion:** In Western fixed income, the obligor usually has limited discretion over payments. In Islamic finance, the relationship is more equitable, with payment timing and amounts often linked to business success.
- **Pricing:** Despite the differing legal mechanisms, the pricing of Islamic banking products can be translated into rates and yields familiar to Western practitioners for comparison. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=7]]
