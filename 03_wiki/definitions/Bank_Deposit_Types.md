## Thesis

The liabilities of a banking entity are primarily structured as various forms of deposits. These instruments are distinguished by their legal claim on immediate liquidity, creating a taxonomy that essentially maps the bank's **liquidity risk profile**. Alexander Düring notes that while the modern interface for these deposits is digital, they retain their historical character as claims on physical cash.

## Definition

Bank deposits are broadly divided into three main categories based on the conditions under which a customer can reclaim their value:

1. **Demand Deposits (or Sight Deposits):**
   - **Legal Obligation:** These represent funds that the customer can reclaim at **any time**. The bank is legally bound to return the value in cash or central bank money immediately upon request.
   - **Usage:** These serve as the primary means of payment in the modern economy, functioning as "Inside Money." [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]
2. **Checking Deposits:**
   - **Context:** A sub-category of demand deposits particularly prominent in the United States.
   - **Mechanism:** Specifically designed to be drawn on by writing cheques (checkbook-accessible funds). They combine the liquidity of a sight deposit with a specific legal instrument for transfer. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]
3. **Time Deposits:**
   - **Requirement:** Deposits that are only repaid after a **set period** of time (e.g., certificates of deposit or fixed-term savings accounts).
   - **Trade-off:** Because these provide the bank with more stable, predictable funding, they usually command a higher interest rate than demand deposits.
   - **Maturity Mismatch:** These allow the bank to better match its long-term assets (loans) with its liabilities. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]

### Related Concepts: The Certification of Value
Historically, some banks offered to "certify" deposited amounts through physical pieces of paper called **Bank Notes**.
- **The Receipt Model:** Originally, these were receipts for 100% of the metal deposited.
- **The Banking Shift:** They became modern banknotes (and thus a form of "Inside Money") the moment the bank began to lend the physical money to third parties, leaving the "notes" in circulation as transferable claims on the bank's ability to provide cash. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### Par Value Liquidity
The defining feature of all these deposit types is that they are intended to be liquid at **par value**. This distinguishes them from investment fund units, where the value fluctuates according to the market price of the underlying assets. The bank absorbs the market risk of its assets to maintain the stability of these deposits.

### Systemic Functions
Banks also utilize these deposits to offer ancillary services:
- **Securities Dealing:** Requiring immediate cash payments for purchases.
- **Custody:** Crediting interest and dividends directly to these deposit accounts. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]
