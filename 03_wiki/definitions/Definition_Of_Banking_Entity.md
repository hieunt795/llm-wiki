## Thesis

Providing a singular definition for a "bank" is complex due to the significant overlap between modern banking functions and those performed by non-bank financial institutions. However, from a structural and legal perspective, a bank is defined by a narrow and essential hallwark: the standing obligation to pay **cash on demand** to its customers. This distinguishing feature separates banks from other asset holders by guaranteeing immediate, par-value liquidity for the liabilities they issue.

## Definition

In the narrowest financial sense, a bank is an institution that holds itself out as offering the service of providing immediate liquidity. Its functional identity rests on three pillars:

1. **Cash on Demand:** The institutional hallmark of a bank is the unconditional promise to return deposited value in the form of physical or central bank cash immediately upon a customer's request.
2. **Deposit Acceptance:** The bank functions as a **depositary institution**, collecting liquid funds from the public which are then recorded as liabilities on its ledger.
3. **Dual Sources of Liquidity:** This "cash on demand" can be derived from two sources:
   - **Pre-existing Deposits:** Money previously placed at the bank by other customers.
   - **Credit Provision:** Money that is created *ex nihilo* through the bank's ability to issue loans, which then manifest as new deposit entries. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]

### Historical Evolution: Functions vs. Institutions
The functions of banking pre-date the formal institution of the "bank" as a legal species.
- **The Knights Templar:** Operated a sophisticated trans-European financial service network comparable to modern-day traveler's cheques. Pilgrims could deposit funds in one location and withdraw them in another, demonstrating that "banking" is a service of value transfer and liquidity management.
- **The Merchant Shift:** Banking as a business grew from merchants employing their surplus capital to finance the ventures of *other* merchants (e.g., chartering ships or purchasing trade goods) rather than risking it solely in their own direct operations. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### Banks vs. Non-Bank Financial Institutions
A crucial distinction exists between banks and other financial entities like insurance companies or asset managers:
- **Banking Entity:** Provides immediate, par-value access to cash. The risk of the bank's assets (loans) is absorbed by the bank's capital, not directly by the depositor.
- **Non-Banks:** While they also hold client assets, access is often conditional.
  - **Asset Managers:** Turning an investment into cash is subject to market valuation (NAV). The client bears the market risk.
  - **Insurance:** Access is restricted by specific policy terms and triggers (e.g., a death or accident), rather than being available on simple demand. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=17]]
