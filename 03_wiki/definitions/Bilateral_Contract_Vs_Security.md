## Thesis

The fixed income market bifurcates all instruments into two categories based on their legal structure and liquidity: **Bilateral Contracts** and **Securities**. This distinction is critical because it dictates how a lender can exit their position—either through a complex legal assignment (contracts) or a rapid market trade (securities).

## Definition

Fixed income instruments can be broadly divided into these two categories based on the consent required for transfer:

### 1. Bilateral Contracts
A **Bilateral Contract** is a direct agreement between two parties.
- **Transferability:** These are generally **not transferable without the consent of all parties**. To move a loan or a swap, the existing counterparty must typically agree to the new one.
- **Direct Relationship:** The contract is personalized and often reflects specific negotiation between the borrower and the lender.
- **Examples:** Deposits, Loans, Swaps, Futures. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=3]]

### 2. Securities
A **Security** is a standardized financial asset that represents a claim on the issuer.
- **Transferability:** Securities differ in that they are **transferable without the consent of all parties**. A holder of a bond can sell it to a new buyer without asking the issuer for permission.
- **Standardization:** To be freely tradable, securities follow strict, standardized formats (e.g., specific coupon dates, legal boilerplate).
- **Examples:** Bonds, Commercial Paper, Certificates of Deposit, Asset-Backed Securities. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=3]]

### The Hybrid Reality (Linked Features)
While the legal category is distinct, some modern instruments blur the lines. For instance, the features of many **securities** (like ABS) may be inextricably linked to the underlying **bilateral contracts** (the original loans being securitized). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=3]]

### Summary Table 1.1

| Bilateral Contracts (Consent Required) | Securities (Transferable) |
|---|---|
| Deposits | Bonds |
| Loans | Commercial paper |
| Swaps | Certificates of deposit |
| Futures | Asset-backed securities |

### Legal Implications
- **Bilateral:** Typically involves more complex legal documentation (e.g., ISDA for swaps, LMA for loans).
- **Security:** Typically involves a "Prospectus" and is governed by securities laws (e.g., the 1933 Act in the US).
