## Thesis

The term "Central Bank" implies a fundamental role in the economy, yet there is no uniform definition of what specific function makes a bank "central." Alexander Düring highlights that central banks are distinguished not by their ownership structure or the physical appearance of currency, but by their position at the apex of the monetary hierarchy—providing the foundational settlement asset that other banks use to clear their private obligations.

## Definition

Defining a central bank requires choosing between two primary intellectual perspectives:

### 1. The Bindseil View (Liability Focus)
Bindseil defines a central bank through three identifying characteristics:
- **Ultimate Quality:** It issues "financial money of ultimate quality"—money that serves as the final settlement asset for all other financial claims.
- **Public Charter:** It is established via a legislative act or public mandate (though historically, charters were issued by monarchs to city-states, mirroring joint-stock models).
- **Public Policy Aims:** Its primary objective is the stability of the economy or the currency, rather than profit maximization. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=35]]

### 2. The Goodhart View (Functional/Aims Focus)
Goodhart links the definition less to the mechanics of issuance and more to the **Lender of Last Resort (LOLR)** function.
- **The Argument:** A bank becomes a central bank when it recognizes its systemic role in providing liquidity to the market during times of stress.
- **Historical Implication:** This view creates a later starting date for central banking (coinciding with the creation of the Swedish Riksbank) and views central banking as an evolutionary rather than revolutionary step. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=36]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### 1. The Ownership Illusion
Public perception often assumes central banks must be state-owned. In reality, ownership is a poor guide to central bank status:
- **Private Ownership:** The US **Federal Reserve Banks** are privately owned by their member banks. The **Banca d'Italia** is also privately owned.
- **Public Listing:** The **Central Bank of Greece** is a listed entity on the Athens Stock Exchange.
- **Irrelevance of Nationalization:** The 1946 nationalization of the Bank of England did not fundamentally alter its operational position in the UK financial system. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=34-35]]

### 2. The Issuance Paradox
A "Bank of Issue" does not always physically issue all currency:
- **Hong Kong:** The central bank (HKMA) does not issue notes; instead, private banks like **HSBC** issue Hong Kong Dollar notes under HKMA governance. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=35]] (Figure 5.1)
- **United States:** US Dollar notes bear the Federal Reserve seal but are legally issued by the **US Treasury** and signed by the Treasury Secretary. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=35]] (Figure 5.2)

### 3. Electronic vs. Physical
While the public focuses on paper notes, the vast majority of "central bank money" consists of electronic book-entry (giro) money—the reserves held by commercial banks on the central bank's ledger. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=36]]
