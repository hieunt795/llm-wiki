## Thesis

Not all "inflation targeters" are equal. The classification highlights the relationship between **Macroeconomic Development** and **Policy Clarity**. As a country stabilizes its fiscal position and deepens its financial markets, it typically migrates from ITL to FFIT.

## The Three Regimes

### 1. Full-fledged Inflation Targeting (FFIT)
- **Characteristics:** Medium-high credibility, clear public commitment, transparent institutional framework.
- **Goal:** Maintaining consistency between inflation control and economic growth.
- **Examples:** Australia, New Zealand, Canada, United Kingdom, and (post-2005) Indonesia.

### 2. Eclectic Inflation Targeting (EIT)
- **Characteristics:** High credibility and long-term price stability established *without* the need for formal rules.
- **Flexibility:** High. The bank can pursue multiple objectives (growth, exchange rates) because the inflation anchor is already established.
- **Examples:** US Federal Reserve, ECB, Japan, Singapore.

### 3. Inflation Targeting Lite (ITL)
- **Characteristics:** Low credibility, weak institutional framework, high vulnerability to shocks.
- **Nature:** A **Transitional Regime**. The bank announces a target to signal direction while implementing structural reforms.
- **Examples:** Philippines (early 2000s), Indonesia (pre-2005), Russia.

## Key Differentiator: Credibility
Credibility is measured by (1) average inflation performance and (2) the **Sovereign Debt Rating**. FFIT countries typically have higher ratings and lower debt-to-GDP ratios than ITL countries.
