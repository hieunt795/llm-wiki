## Thesis

Narrow banking represents the absolute extreme of banking regulation. While models like **Banking Separation** seek to isolate retail deposits from market risks, **Narrow Banking** seeks to destroy the commercial bank's ability to create money altogether. By forcing a 100% reserve requirement against all demand deposits, this model transforms banks into secure payment vaults and shifts the entire burden of economic expansion and credit allocation onto the state.

## Definition

In the current financial system, commercial banks create "Inside Money" whenever they issue a loan (the loan creates a deposit). The Narrow Banking Concept, often marketed under the political banner of **"Positive Money"**, proposes to outlaw this process:

1. **Elimination of Private Credit Money:** Commercial banks would no longer be permitted to expand the money supply through the fractional reserve process. Every unit of currency in circulation would be "Outside Money" (Sovereign Money).
2. **100% Reserves:** Any institution acting as a deposit-taker must hold 100% liquid reserves (cash or central bank deposits) against all customer funds. They cannot lend out deposited money.
3. **Total Government Control:** Because private sector banks cannot create money to meet economic demand, the central government (or central bank) assumes absolute, sole control over the total volume of money in the economy. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=19]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The Critical Trade-off: Credit Allocation
Alexander Düring highlights the primary economic danger of the Narrow Banking model:

- **From Markets to State:** In a traditional system, the allocation of credit is decentralized—thousands of individual bank managers decide who gets a loan based on perceived risk and return.
- **The State Monopoly:** Under Narrow Banking, because the government controls the expansion of the money supply, it inevitably assumes control over the **allocation of credit**.
- **Consequence:** This risks transforming a market economy into a command economy, where the state dictates which sectors or political favorites receive funding, potentially stifling innovation and private enterprise. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=19]]

### Distinction from Separation
It is vital to distinguish these two principles which are often conflated in public debate:
- **Banking Separation (e.g., Ring-fencing):** A risk-management model. It isolates deposits from *securities* trading, but still allows banks to create money by lending to the "real" economy. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=18]]
- **Narrow Banking:** A systemic architecture model. It prohibits *all* private money creation, regardless of whether the loan is for a mortgage or a speculative hedge fund. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=19]]
