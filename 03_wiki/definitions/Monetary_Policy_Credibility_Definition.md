## What is Monetary Policy Credibility?

[RAW] **Two-Level Definition Framework:**

### Policy Credibility vs. Target Credibility
[RAW] Pragmatically, credibility operates at two dimensions:

1. **Policy Credibility**:
   - Consistency between public expectations and actual policy implementation by central bank
   - Depends on: Whether policy implementation deviates from previous statements due to external interference or economic changes
   - Core question: Did the central bank do what it said it would do?

2. **Target Credibility**:
   - Consistency between public expectations and actual achievement of announced future policy targets
   - Depends on: Level of policy credibility AND whether economic structure facilitates target achievement
   - Core question: Can the central bank actually achieve the announced target?

[RAW] Both dimensions are fundamentally determined by three aspects:
1. Public response reflected in expectations formation
2. Central bank capacity to maintain and commit to policy strategy over time
3. Economic structure and development that facilitates sound policy transmission mechanisms

## Operational Definition: Central Bank Reputation Function

[RAW] **Simple Formulation**: For a central bank mandated with controlling inflation:

$$\text{Credibility} = f(\text{Actual Inflation} - \text{Inflation Target})$$

Higher credibility = smaller and more stable deviation between actual and target inflation over time.

## Why Credibility Matters: Blinder (2000) Framework

[RAW] Four key benefits of higher central bank credibility:

1. **Confidence Reassurance**: Credible central bank reassures public that policy measures will achieve stated goals
2. **Expectation Formation Efficiency**: With credibility, public forms expectations consistent with central bank targets
3. **Output Preservation**: Credible central bank can achieve price stability without sacrificing economic growth in the medium term
   - Because: Public believes inflation targets and sets nominal wages accordingly
   - Result: Disinflation achieved with smaller output sacrifice
4. **Economic Capacity Judgment**: Credible central bank can make more accurate assessments of economy's sustainable growth capacity without inflation risks

[RAW] Perrier and Amano (2000): "A credible monetary authority could form more accurate judgments on economic capacity in terms of producing goods and services, thereby creating job opportunities without stoking concerns regarding the emergence of inflationary pressures."

## Core Mechanism: Rational Expectations + Policy Announcement

[RAW] **Credibility's Transmission Through Expectations:**

If central bank has credibility AND public has rational expectations:
1. Central bank announces disinflationary policy commitment
2. Public believes the announcement (credibility-based trust)
3. Public forms expectations consistent with low inflation target
4. Wage setters and price setters moderate nominal wage/price growth
5. Actual inflation declines toward target with minimal output sacrifice

[LLM] Without credibility, same announcement triggers:
1. Public skepticism of central bank ability/willingness to follow through
2. Expectations remain high ("based on previous experience")
3. Wage/price setters maintain high nominal growth
4. Central bank must tighten more aggressively to hit target
5. Large output sacrifice required (high sacrifice ratio)

## Dimensions of Credibility Assessment

[RAW] **Blinder (2000) emphasizes**: Credibility assessment depends on:
1. **Institutional design**: Does legal framework support policy independence?
2. **Leadership quality**: Does central bank governor demonstrate commitment and competence?
3. **Track record**: What is recent history of inflation achievement?
4. **Consistency**: Are policies consistent with announced targets?
5. **Transparency**: Can public understand and evaluate policy decisions?
