## Thesis
Sovereign currency derives value not from commodity backing but from state acceptance (taxation obligation) + legal tender power. Government monopoly over currency issuance and tax collection creates demand and enforces use in a closed economy.

## 1. Core Definition & Boundary

[RAW] "The national currency is often referred to as a sovereign currency, that is, the currency issued by the sovereign government. The sovereign government retains a variety of powers for itself that are not given to private individuals or institutions."

**Three essential characteristics:**

1. **Unit of Account (Money of Account):** [RAW] "All financial stocks and flows are denominated in the national money of account. The Australian dollar, the US dollar, the Japanese yen, the British pound, and the European euro are all examples of a money of account."

2. **Monopoly Issuance:** [RAW] "Modern sovereign governments alone are invested with the power to issue the currency denominated in each nation's money of account. For example, if any entity other than the US government tried to issue US currency it would be prosecuted as a counterfeiter, with severe penalties being imposed."

3. **Tax Enforcement:** [RAW] "The sovereign government imposes tax liabilities (as well as fines and fees) in its money of account, and decides how these liabilities can be paid: that is, it decides what it will accept in payment so that taxpayers can fulfil their obligations."

## 2. Basis of Acceptance: The Tax Foundation

[RAW] "Spending by the government as well as tax liabilities, fees, and fines owed to the government are denominated in the same money of account. These payments are enforceable by law."

[LLM] The transmission chain of valuation:
```
Government mandates tax in sovereign currency
    ↓
Households/firms must obtain sovereign currency
    ↓
Broad willingness to accept in transactions
    ↓
Sovereign currency becomes medium of exchange
    ↓
Becomes natural unit of account for contracts
```

[RAW] "More generally, broad use of a nation's money of account is ensured by enforcing monetary contracts in a court of law, such as the payment of wages."

## 3. The One Nation, One Currency Rule (& Exceptions)

[RAW] "It has been the usual situation: 'one nation, one currency'. There have been a few exceptions to this rule, including the modern euro, which is the money of account adopted by several countries that have joined the Economic and Monetary Union of the European Union (EMU)."

**Euro exception:** When multiple governments adopt a single currency (EMU), none of them is the **sovereign issuer** of the euro. Instead, the ECB holds that monopoly, constrained by the political compromise of the EMU treaty. This creates "currency user" behavior for member governments despite retaining tax power.

[RAW] "When we address the exceptional cases, such as the EMU, we will carefully identify the differences that arise when a currency is used, but not issued, by a nation."

## 4. Foreign Currency Contracts Within Sovereign Currency Domain

[RAW] "In many nations there are private contracts that are written in foreign monies of account. For example, in some Latin American countries it is common to write some kinds of contracts in terms of the US dollar. It is also common in many nations to use US currency in payment."

[RAW] "According to some estimates, the total value of US paper currency circulating outside America exceeds the value of US paper currency used at home. Much of this is thought to be involved in illegal activities, including the drug trade."

[LLM] Private dollarization (use of foreign currency within domestic transactions) does not undermine sovereignty of the domestic currency but reflects:
- Loss of confidence in domestic currency (hyperinflation scenarios)
- Convenience for cross-border commerce
- Tax evasion / underground economy

Remains marginal for most developed sovereigns with stable currencies.

## 5. Failure Conditions / Boundaries

**When does sovereign currency lose purchasing power?**

1. **Hyperinflation:** Excessive government spending beyond real resource capacity → currency value collapses → loss of medium-of-exchange function
2. **Loss of tax enforcement:** If government cannot credibly enforce tax collection (state collapse, civil war), currency demand evaporates
3. **Foreign currency debt:** If government borrows heavily in foreign currency (emerging markets), it becomes "currency user" for servicing those obligations

[RAW] "Occasionally, central banks would conduct what became known as a 'managed' float where they tried to limit the amplitude of movements that the free float would generate."

## 6. Distinction: Sovereign Currency vs Commodity Money vs Fiat

| Attribute | Commodity (Gold) | Fiat (Backed by Tax) | Crypto |
|---|---|---|---|
| Basis of value | Intrinsic scarcity | Tax obligation + legal tender | Belief in protocol + scarcity |
| Issuer control | Limited (mining) | Complete monopoly | None (distributed protocol) |
| Price stability | Volatile (supply shocks) | Managed by monetary policy | Highly volatile |
| Tax acceptance | No | Yes (mandatory) | No |
