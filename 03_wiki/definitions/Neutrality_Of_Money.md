## Thesis

The neutrality of money debate forms the foundational split in modern macroeconomics. The consensus (the Keynesian-Neoclassical synthesis) posits that **money is non-neutral in the short run but neutral in the long run**. This distinction grants central banks the theoretical "mandate" to stabilize the economy through short-term interventions while remaining anchored by long-term price stability. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=50]]

---

## 1. The Theoretical Spectrum

### A. The Classical View (Neutrality)
- **Principle:** "Money is a veil" (Pigou). It only masks real activity.
- **Logic:** Based on **Say's Law** ("Supply creates its own demand") and **Walras' Law**.
- **Outcome:** Doubling the money supply simply doubles all prices and wages; the real economy ($W/P$) remains unchanged. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=49-50]]

### B. The Keynesian View (Non-Neutrality)
- **Principle:** "Nominal wages are rigid."
- **Mechanism:** If money supply increases but nominal wages stay fixed, real wages ($W/P$) drop as prices rise. This induces firms to hire more, increasing real output.
- **Outcome:** Monetary policy can effectively stimulate the real economy during recessions. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=50]]

### C. The Monetarist View (Money Matters)
Friedman argued that while money is neutral in the long run, it is the primary driver of nominal spending and can influence real output during the adjustment phase. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=51]]

## 2. Advanced Classifications

Alexander Düring and Perry Warjiyo identify three levels of neutrality based on the nature of the monetary change:

| Level | Condition | Real Effect? |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Neutrality** | A once-for-all change in the *level* of money supply. | **No** (in long run). |
| **Superneutrality** | A change in the *growth rate* of money supply. | **Depends** on the model. |
| **Non-Neutrality** | Short-term adjustments or structural rigidities. | **Yes**. |

### 4 Conditions for Neutrality (Handa, 2000)
1. All prices rise proportionally.
2. The real value of the endowment does not change.
3. Interest rates are paid on all components of money.
4. No further price changes are anticipated. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=51]]

## 3. Modeling Non-Superneutrality

### OLG Model (Overlapping Generations)
Shows **non-superneutrality** based on the distribution effect.
- **Mechanism:** Additional money is transferred as a lump sum to the "old" generation. This redistributes wealth from the "young" (savers) to the "old" (spenders), altering real resource allocation in the steady state. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=53]]

### MIU Model (Money-in-the-Utility)
Shows **superneutrality** in the steady state.
- **Mechanism:** By including money directly in the utility function (Sidrauski, 1967), the model proves that money growth only affects inflation and government transfers (seigniorage), but does not change real interest rates, capital formation, or consumption. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=55]]
