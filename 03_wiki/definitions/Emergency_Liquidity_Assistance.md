## Thesis
ELA operates as extraordinary LOLR provision when normal liquidity facilities (open market operations, standing facilities) are insufficient or stigmatized; it signals central bank judgment that underlying solvency is intact despite temporary funding market dysfunction.

## 1. Definition and Operational Status

[RAW] Per Bindseil (p.242):

"Emergency Liquidity Assistance as Special Type of LOLR" represents central bank lending outside the normal corridor framework when:
- Counterparty faces acute liquidity stress despite solvency
- Normal funding markets non-functional (repo spreads at extremes, wholesale funding frozen)
- Institution's collateral acceptable under broad LOLR standards but excluded from normal operations
- Crisis conditions justify departure from Bagehot's lending rule

## 2. Historical Examples (2007-2009)

[RAW] Per Bindseil (p.159-160):

"Central banks around the world had to act as lenders of last resort starting in 2007 to help out banks who had funded themselves too short-term or who had provided liquidity lines to vehicles with a considerable maturity mismatch (like ABCPs or other special investment vehicles). While Northern Rock (see Shin, 2009) was the only example of a bank in the industrialized world during the current crisis which faced a physical retail depositor bank run, there was a multitude of banks that were under acute liquidity stress and needed central bank rescue through Emergency Liquidity Assistance (ELA)."

**Specific cases:**
- Northern Rock (2007): ECB provided ELA to offset retail deposit run
- Bear Stearns (Mar 2008): Fed provided liquidity via primary dealer credit facility
- AIG (Sep 2008+): Fed provided multi-hundred-billion ELA for derivative counterparty obligations
- European banks (2010-2012): ECB's ELA for Greek, Irish, Portuguese banks facing funding market closure

## 3. Distinguishing Features from Standing Facilities

| Feature | Standing Facility | ELA |
|---------|------------------|-----|
| Availability | Always open | Crisis-triggered |
| Collateral scope | Narrow eligible list | Broad, including distressed assets |
| Lending rate | Fixed (penalty rate) | Often higher, negotiated |
| Counterparty assessment | Credit risk check | Solvency judgment required |
| Stigma | Routine use expected | Signals distress |
| Duration | Overnight to term | Extended periods |
| Conditions | Minimal | May include behavioral covenants |

## 4. Collateral Framework Under ELA

ELA typically expands collateral acceptance to:
- Asset-backed securities (ABSs) with limited market liquidity
- Corporate bonds below investment grade
- Bank loans and syndicated loans
- Equities (in extreme crises)
- Illiquid sovereign debt (Eurozone periphery)

**Rationale:** Solvent institutions may hold illiquid assets suitable as collateral; excluding them from LOLR penalizes liquidity-constrained but fundamentally sound actors.

## 5. Moral Hazard and ELA Conditionality

[RAW] Implicit in Bindseil's treatment of moral hazard (Ch.16):

ELA provision raises moral hazard concerns:
- **Ex-ante:** Banks may deliberately rely on short-term funding, knowing ELA available
- **Ex-post:** Availability of ELA may prevent necessary balance sheet adjustments

**Typical central bank responses:**
- Require liquidity coverage improvements going forward
- Impose time limits on ELA tenure
- Signal commitment to withdrawal after crisis resolves
- Coordinate with supervisors on institution-specific remediation

## 6. Stigma and Take-Up Dynamics

Central bank ELA effectiveness hindered by:

**Stigma problem:** Publicly accessing ELA signals distress → markets infer solvency concerns → funding conditions worsen → need for larger ELA

**Examples:**
- Irish banks (2010-2011): Heavy ECB ELA reliance became public; funding costs spiked
- Greek banks (2015): ELA invoked amid capital control fears; deposits fled

**Mitigation strategies:**
- Broad-based emergency lending (applies to multiple counterparties)
- Opacity about which institutions use ELA (confidential disclosure)
- Rapid market normalization (removing stigma rationale)

## 7. Central Bank Risk-Taking in ELA

ELA exposes central bank balance sheet to:
- **Credit risk:** Collateral valuation uncertainty; counterparty insolvency discovered post-lending
- **Liquidity risk:** Collateral unmarketable; central bank recovery value uncertain
- **Foreign currency risk:** Cross-border ELA in foreign currencies (ECB to US banks in 2008)

**Examples of realized losses:**
- AIG ELA: Counterparty quasi-solvency questionable; ultimate cost to Fed ~$85B
- Irish bank ELA: Eventual bank failures realized as losses
- Greek bank ELA (2015): Institutions ultimately unable to repay

## 8. Legal and Institutional Constraints

Central bank ELA authority varies:

**Broad authority:** Federal Reserve (13(3) for "unusual and exigent circumstances"), Bank of England
**Constrained authority:** ECB (Article 123 EU Treaty prohibits monetary financing; ELA rationale is illiquidity not insolvency)
**Questionable authority:** Some EME central banks (statutory restrictions on collateral types)

**Eurozone constraint:** ECB ELA subject to:
- Eurosystem member approval requirement
- Threshold interest rate (typically above marginal lending facility)
- Fiscal backing (national Treasury indemnity for losses)
- Solvency certification (national supervisor role)

## 9. Conditional vs Unconditional ELA

**Unconditional ELA:** Central bank provides lending against eligible collateral; counterparty bears all conditions (market rate, collateral risk)

**Conditional ELA:** Lending tied to institution behavior:
- Dividend restrictions
- Executive compensation limits
- Deleveraging requirements
- Merger/acquisition mandates

**Post-2008 practice:** Most ELA conditional; supervisory coordination essential
