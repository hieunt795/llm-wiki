## Thesis

Commodity money represents the historical anchoring of financial value in physical assets. While it provides a "straightjacket" against arbitrary money creation, it also subjects the money supply to the volatility of physical commodity markets.

## Definition

Money predicated on the perceived intrinsic material value of the underlying asset. Common examples include gold, silver, or highly durable consumables.
- **The Consumable Anchor:** In regions where mining was late (e.g., Central Europe), salt served as money because it was scarce, durable, and possessed alternative utility as food.
- **Islamic Law Precedent:** Shar’iah law explicitly recognizes **six commodities** that must be traded "equal for equal, and hand to hand": gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, and salt. These rules govern modern FX transactions. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=11]]
- **The Stability Paradox:** It is common to believe that a gold standard anchors the value of other goods. Düring argues the reverse is also true: the monetary use of gold anchors the value of gold itself. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=11-12]]

### Debasement and Forgery
Historically, commodity systems were plagued by manipulation:
- **Debasement:** Governments issuing coins with lower purity to clandestinely reduce their own debt burdens.
- **Clipping:** Private forgers cutting the edges of coins to steal metal. This was effectively ended by **Sir Isaac Newton** through the invention of the milled edges. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]
