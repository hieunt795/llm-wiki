## Thesis
A nominal anchor is a constraint on the value of domestic money used by the central bank to achieve long-term price stability and anchor public expectations. [extracted]

## Definition
Regime selection depends on the choice and clarity of the nominal anchor, categorized by the trade-off between commitment and flexibility (Stone & Bhundia, 2004). [extracted]

### Types of Regimes
1. **Exchange Rate Targeting:** Pegging domestic currency to commodities (Gold Standard) or a hard currency (Currency Board). [extracted]
2. **Monetary Targeting:** Stipulating growth targets for monetary aggregates (M1, M2). [extracted]
3. **Inflation Targeting (ITF):** Public commitment to a medium-term numerical inflation target. [extracted]
4. **No Explicit Anchor (Implicit Targeting):** Flexible, forward-looking policy without a formal target (e.g., US Fed "just-do-it" regime). [extracted]
5. **Nominal Income Targeting:** (Hypothetical) Targeting the growth of nominal GDP ($P \times Y$). [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Independence:** Exchange rate targeting sacrifices monetary autonomy in open economies.
- **Stability:** Monetary targeting relies on a stable relationship between money supply and inflation (stable velocity). [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Stone & Bhundia Classification:** Gradation from "Monetary Non-autonomy" to "Full-fledged Inflation Targeting." [extracted]
- **Bipolarization:** Trend toward either hard pegs or free floating, though "intermediate" systems persist in many EMEs. [extracted]
