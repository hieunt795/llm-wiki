## Thesis

PPP is fundamentally a "flow concept" based on the international trade of goods and services. While it rarely holds in the short term due to transaction costs and sticky prices, it remains the primary tool for central banks to measure **Currency Misalignment** via the REER index.

## Variants

### 1. Absolute PPP
The simplest form, based on the **Law of One Price**:
$$P = S + P^*$$
(In log terms, where $S$ is the nominal exchange rate, $P$ is domestic price, and $P^*$ is international price).
- **Assumption:** No transport costs, no taxes, homogeneous products.

### 2. Relative PPP
Focuses on changes over time, as proposed by Cassel (1918):
$$\Delta S = \pi - \pi^*$$
- **Logic:** The exchange rate adjusts to mirror the inflation differential between two countries.

### 3. Expectations-based PPP
Proposed by Roll (1979) to account for speculative behavior:
$$E(\pi - \pi^*) = E(e_{t+1} - e_t)$$
- **Logic:** Expected inflation differentials drive expected exchange rate movements.

## Applications: REER and Misalignment
The Real Effective Exchange Rate (REER) is the weighted version of PPP:
$$\text{REER} = \sum \omega_j \frac{\pi^*_j}{\pi}$$
Central banks use the REER index to detect **Misalignment**—deviations from the fundamental "normal" level—to analyze export competitiveness and the need for intervention.
