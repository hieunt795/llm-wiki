## Thesis

The core of fixed income is the **contractual certainty** of the payment stream. Unlike equity, where the timing and amount of payments (dividends) are subject to the board's discretion, fixed income obligates the payer to meet specific terms regardless of their current profitability, provided they are solvent.

## Definition

According to Alexander Düring, a fixed income instrument must fulfill three criteria:
1. **Contractual Specification:** The timing and amount of payments are predefined.
2. **Economic Independence:** Payment obligations are not linked to the obligor's specific economic situation (unlike dividends).
3. **Financial Asset Basis:** Excludes contracts linked to physical asset performance (e.g., commodity derivatives or weather bonds).

## Functional Categories

Düring classifies the market into two distinct legal structures:
- **Bilateral Contracts:** Private agreements (Deposits, Loans, Swaps) where transfer typically requires the consent of all original parties.
- **Securities:** Standardized, mass-distributed instruments (Bonds, CP, CD, ABS) defined by their **transferability without the consent** of the obligor.

## Research Insight: Etymological Divergence
The German term **"Renten"** encompasses both fixed income instruments and pensions, highlighting their shared nature as annuity-like income streams. In contrast, the English term **"Rent"** has narrowed over time to refer specifically to physical asset usage, while **"Pension"** refers exclusively to post-employment income.
