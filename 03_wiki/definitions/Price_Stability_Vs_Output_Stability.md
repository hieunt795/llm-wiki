## Thesis
The duality between price stability and output stability represents the fundamental trade-off in monetary policy, where the central bank must balance the minimization of inflation deviation with output gap stabilization. [extracted]

## Definition
The central bank's objective is typically represented by a **Macro-Welfare Function** or **Quadratic Loss Function** ($L$) that must be minimized:
$$L = \frac{1}{2} [\omega_p (\pi_t - \pi^*)^2 + \omega_y (y_t - y^*)^2]$$
where:
- $\pi_t - \pi^*$: Inflation deviation from target.
- $y_t - y^*$: Output deviation from potential (output gap).
- $\omega_p, \omega_y$: Relative weights assigned to price and output stability. [extracted]

### The Long-Term Perspective
In the long term, consensus (Walsh, 2001) suggests that money is neutral; monetary policy only affects inflation and not real output. Thus, price stability is considered the most appropriate long-term target. [extracted]

### The Short-Term Perspective
In the near term, shocks (demand or supply side) create a trade-off.
- **Demand Shocks:** Policy can stabilize both inflation and output simultaneously.
- **Supply Shocks:** Policy face a direct conflict; stabilizing prices requires contractionary measures that further reduce output. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Quadratic Nature:** Large deviations are penalized more heavily than small ones; positive and negative deviations are treated as equally harmful. [extracted]
- **Time Horizon:** The prioritization often shifts from output in the short run to prices in the long run.

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Great Moderation:** Era in the US where both inflation and output volatility were successfully minimized. [extracted]
- **Indonesia History:** Prioritized output in the 1970s (oil boom) before shifting to price stability in the 1990s. [extracted]
