## Thesis
In the Indonesian context, inflation is bifurcated into components manageable by monetary policy (Core) and those driven by government policy (Administered Prices) or supply shocks (Volatile Food). [extracted]

## Definition
### 1. Administered Prices (AP)
Prices directly determined or heavily influenced by government policy, such as fuel, electricity, and transportation tariffs. [extracted]
- **Impact:** Shocks to AP (e.g., fuel subsidy cuts) often drive headline inflation beyond the target corridor.

### 2. Volatile Food (VF)
Price fluctuations caused by supply-side shocks, seasonal harvests, or distribution disruptions (inclement weather, infrastructure bottlenecks). [extracted]
- **Impact:** Contributes to high inflation volatility in Indonesia.

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Monetary Policy Limits:** Interest rate changes are less effective at controlling AP and VF; these require coordination with fiscal and sectoral authorities. [extracted]
- **Inertia:** Shocks to AP and VF can bleed into inflation expectations, creating "second-round effects" that core inflation measures attempt to isolate. [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Fuel Hikes:** Major contributors to target misses in 2005, 2008, 2013, and 2014. [extracted]
- **Regional TPI/TPID:** Formed specifically to address the supply-side bottlenecks driving VF. [extracted]
