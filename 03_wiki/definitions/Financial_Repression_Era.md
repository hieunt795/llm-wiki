## Thesis

Post-World War II, central banks were widely blamed for the Great Depression. As a result, monetary policy was subordinated to fiscal needs. This era prioritised economic growth and full employment at the cost of soaring inflation and market distortions.

## Key Mechanisms of Repression

1. **Interest Rate Caps:** Governments kept interest rates artificially low to reduce the cost of servicing massive post-war sovereign debt.
2. **Credit Rationing:** Instead of using price (interest rates) to allocate capital, governments used quantitative limits to direct loans to "preferred" sectors.
3. **Directed Lending:** Forcing banks to purchase government bonds or lend to state-owned enterprises.
4. **Deposit Insurance and Safety Nets:** Established to stabilize the system after the banking collapses of the 1930s, but also served to tie the banking system closer to government control.

## Consequences

- **The Inflation Spike:** Subordinating the central bank to the government's printing press led to hyperinflation in several countries and "The Great Inflation" of the 1970s in the West.
- **Inception of Offshore Markets:** To escape domestic repression, banks moved operations to London, creating the **Eurodollar** market and the global bond markets.
- **Loss of Credibility:** Central banks became viewed as political tools rather than neutral arbiters of value, eventually necessitating the "Independence Revolution" of the 1980s.
