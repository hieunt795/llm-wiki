## Thesis
FSS is not merely institutional soundness but a system-wide capacity to resist shocks through sound macrofinancial linkages and prevention of contagion via interconnectedness and financial networks.

## 1. Definition and Core Concepts

[RAW] Per Perry Warjiyo (p.1714-1724), FSS has multiple definitions:

**Borio-Drehmann (2009):** "Financial instability is a set of conditions sufficient to result in emergence of financial distress/crises in response to normal-sized shocks... Financial stability is then defined as the converse of financial instability."

**Schinasi (2004):** "A financial system is in a range of stability whenever it is capable of facilitating the performance of an economy, and dissipating financial imbalances arising endogenously or from significant and unanticipated events."

**ECB Definition:** "Financial stability as a condition in which the financial system – intermediaries, markets and market infrastructures – can withstand shocks without major disruptions in financial intermediation and in the general supply of financial services."

**Indonesia Act (2016):** "Financial stability as a condition in which the financial system functions effectively and efficiently and is resilient to domestic and global shocks."

## 2. Five Operative Dimensions

[RAW] Per Perry Warjiyo (p.1743-1761), FSS requires management across five critical dimensions:

### 2.1 Institutional Soundness (Necessary but Insufficient)
"The soundness of individual financial institutions is necessary but not sufficient. FSS is linked to how the financial system functions to resist and survive shocks in the economy."

### 2.2 Macrofinancial Linkages (Primary Focus)
"The key lies in the macrofinancial linkages of the financial system with economic activities rather than the soundness of individual financial institutions."

Four macrofinancial linkage types trigger crises:
- Asset and property price bubbles
- Credit booms and excessive leverage
- Sudden-stop capital reversals
- Spillover contagion from external shocks

### 2.3 Macroeconomic Stability (Shock Prevention)
Policy must dampen "demand-side pressures that outstrip economic capacity, avoiding overheating marked by high inflation, current account deficits, and fiscal deficits."

### 2.4 Contagion and Interconnectedness (Network Risk)
"The contagion process leading to a systemic crisis throughout the entire financial system can progress with rapidity due to interconnectedness and financial networks in the markets and infrastructures, including the payment system."

### 2.5 Herding and Information Contagion (Behavioral Risk)
"A crisis typically peaks when contagion through interconnectedness... are accompanied by herding behavior and information contagion, resulting in fire sales, inadequate liquidity, and financial market collapse."

## 3. Historical Crisis Manifestations

[RAW] Per Perry Warjiyo (p.1660-1686), recurring FSS vulnerabilities across episodes:

- **Latin America 1980s:** Excessive fiscal expansion + government debt accumulation
- **Asian Financial Crisis 1997/1998:** Financial liberalization → credit growth → property bubbles → private external debt buildup
- **GFC 2008/2009:** Leverage in housing + securitized instruments + global interconnectedness → system collapse
- **Euro Area 2011-2012:** Sovereign-bank nexus + sudden capital stops

## 4. Post-GFC Institutional Framework

[RAW] FSS maintenance requires coordination across four institutional pillars:

1. **Microprudential Supervision:** Bank regulator ensures individual institution soundness
2. **Macroprudential Policy:** Central bank manages systemic risk and procyclicality
3. **Monetary Policy:** Central bank prevents macroeconomic shocks; LOLR function
4. **Fiscal Authority:** Government prevents insolvency cascades; crisis resolution coordination

## 6. Linkage with Macrofinancial Framework

[RAW] Per Figure 14.1 (Perry), FSS policy operates across four nested dimensions:
1. Microfinancial sector (individual bank soundness)
2. Macronational (domestic macroeconomic stability)
3. Macrointernational (global spillover resilience)
4. Macrofinancial linkages (financial system ↔ real economy feedback loops)

The new post-GFC dimension emphasizes linkage management at level 4, which was previously neglected.
