## Thesis

The concept of **Lex Monetae** provides the legal architecture for the transition from commodity money to fiat money. It establishes that a sovereign state possesses the exclusive right to define the asset used for the settlement of obligations. While seemingly an absolute power, Alexander Düring highlights that Lex Monetae has critical limits in international commerce and can become a tool for clandestine wealth redistribution through currency debasement.

## Definition

Lex Monetae stipulates that the laws of the sovereign jurisdiction determine what constitutes "money" for the purposes of that jurisdiction. This includes:

1. **Legal Tender (Forced Money):** Prescribing which assets courts must recognize as sufficient to discharge monetary debts.
2. **Taxation Base:** Because sovereigns command large payment flows via taxes, prescribing a specific legal tender lends immediate and immense systemic significance to that asset.
3. **Coinage Rights:** Historically, this manifested as the sovereign's right to "stamp" their own coinage and regulate its purity and weight. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]

### Historical Precedent: Pax Westphalica (1648)
The operation of Lex Monetae can be traced back to the **Pax Westphalica**, the peace treaty ending the 30 Years War. This treaty set important precedents for international law by specifying that restitution payments were to be made in the local currency of the respective sovereigns. This effectively acknowledged that each sovereign held the right to control their own domestic monetary settlement asset. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### 1. The Euro Crisis Limit (Article 118 TFEU)
During the 2010 Eurozone crisis, some commentators argued that Lex Monetae gave member states the right to exit the Euro, revive their national currencies, and repay Euro-denominated debts in devalued local currency without defaulting.
- **The Legal Obstacle:** Under EU Law, **Article 118 of the Treaty on the Functioning of the European Union** establishes the Euro as the currency of the Union. By joining the Euro, member states effectively "signed away" their Lex Monetae rights over their national monetary systems.
- **Contractual Integrity:** Unilaterally changing the denomination of an existing security to the detriment of bondholders is considered an illegal contract modification in most modern legal systems, regardless of domestic Lex Monetae claims. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]

### 2. The International Friction
While Lex Monetae is robust within national borders, it is largely absent from international treaties. Consequently, the right of a sovereign to change the value of monetary claims by altering the currency is generally **unavailable in cross-border funding**. International creditors typically require payment in a stable, mutually agreed-upon currency (e.g., USD or EUR) rather than accepting the domestic decree of a debtor nation. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]

### 3. Societal Rejection
If a sovereign legislates a money that is unsuitable for daily transactions (e.g., through extreme hyperinflation), society may choose to ignore Lex Monetae and use other means of exchange, such as foreign currencies (Dollarization) or informal commodities. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]
