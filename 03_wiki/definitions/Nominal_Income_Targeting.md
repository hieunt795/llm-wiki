## Thesis
Nominal income targeting is a hypothetical monetary policy strategy where the central bank targets the growth rate of nominal GDP (the sum of inflation and real output growth). [extracted]

## Definition
The objective function (loss function $L$) for nominal income targeting is:
$$L = \frac{1}{2} [(\pi_t + \Delta y_t) - (\pi + \Delta y)^*]^2$$
where $\pi_t + \Delta y_t$ is nominal output growth. [extracted]

### Advantages
- **Automatic Stabilizer:** Facilitates a compromise between inflation and output during supply-side shocks. [extracted]
- **Flexibility:** Prevents the drastic output declines that might occur if a central bank targeted prices rigidly during a supply shock. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Implementation Difficulty:** Hard to identify the cut-off between short-term and long-term shocks in practice.
- **Credibility Gap:** May erode the central bank's ability to anchor pure inflation expectations, as it allows for inflation variability. [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Hall & Mankiw (1993):** Showed that inflation and output would have performed more smoothly under this regime in the past.
- **McCallum (1988):** Conducted counterfactual experiments showing the potential superiority of nominal income targeting. [extracted]
