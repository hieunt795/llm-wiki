## Summary

[RAW-BOOK:5] **Inflation** = rising prices over time; **Deflation** = falling prices; **Disinflation** = slowing rate of price increases. Two reference frames: (1) **Nominal frame**: measures returns in cash terms without adjusting for purchasing power loss. Example: earn 5% return on 100 = 105 cash. (2) **Real frame**: measures returns in terms of goods/services purchased. If inflation is 3%, that 105 buys only ~102 units worth of goods (lost 3 units of purchasing power). Real return = 105/103 - 1 ≈ 1.94%. **Fisher equation** relates: (1 + nominal rate) = (1 + real rate) × (1 + expected inflation). Used extensively by bond markets to decompose yields into real return + inflation compensation.

## Key Definitions

[RAW-BOOK:5] **Inflation**: General rise in price levels of goods and services; measured by inflation indices (CPI, RPI, etc.). **Purchasing Power**: Amount of goods/services that can be bought with a given amount of money. Inflation erodes purchasing power (same cash buys fewer goods). **Nominal Return**: Return measured in cash (not adjusted for inflation). **Real Return**: Return measured in purchasing power (inflation-adjusted). **Expected Inflation**: Market's forecast of future inflation, embedded in nominal bond yields. **Realized Inflation**: Actual inflation that occurs during holding period. **Breakeven Inflation**: Implicit inflation rate in market where nominal yield = real yield + breakeven inflation (derived from inflation-linked bond spreads).

## Fisher Equation

[RAW-BOOK:5] Mathematical relationship: (1 + i_nominal) = (1 + i_real) × (1 + i_inflation). Rearranged: i_nominal ≈ i_real + i_inflation (approximation valid for small rates). Interpretation: nominal bond yield = real return required by investor + compensation for expected inflation. If nominal yields rise but real yields unchanged, market expects higher inflation. Widely used by central banks and fixed-income traders to disentangle rate expectations (real component) from inflation expectations.
