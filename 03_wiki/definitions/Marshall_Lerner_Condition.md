## Thesis

Currency depreciation is a "double-edged sword." While it makes exports cheaper, it also makes imports more expensive. Perry Warjiyo explains that for the stimulus to work, the **Volume effect** (selling more goods) must outweigh the **Price effect** (paying more per unit). The Marshall-Lerner (ML) condition is the mathematical threshold for this transition. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=98]]

---

## Mechanism: The Elasticity Threshold

The ML condition is expressed as:
$$|\epsilon_x| + |\epsilon_m| > 1$$

### 1. High Elasticity (Stimulus works)
Found in countries where the export basket consists of **Manufactured Goods**.
- **The Logic:** Buyers of televisions or cars are highly price-sensitive. A 10% price drop leads to a >10% surge in demand.
- **Outcome:** Total export revenue (in foreign currency) increases. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=98]]

### 2. Low Elasticity (Stimulus fails)
Found in countries exporting **Primary Commodities** (oil, coal, palm oil) or importing **Essential Goods** (medicine, fuel).
- **The Logic:** Global demand for oil is inelastic; buyers won't buy much more just because the currency of one producer dropped.
- **Outcome:** The country receives less foreign currency for the same volume of exports, while the cost of essential imports spikes. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=98]]

## EME Context: The Indonesia Case Study

Perry Warjiyo provides empirical evidence for the ML condition in Indonesia:
- **The Evidence:** Each 1% depreciation of the rupiah eventually increases the total trade balance by 0.72%.
- **The Composition:** This success is driven by the **Non-oil and gas sector** (elasticity of 0.77), which compensates for the more inelastic energy sector.
- **Strategic Note:** The ML condition is a **long-term** law. In the short term, the [[J-Curve_Effect_Mechanism|J-Curve]] dominates due to temporary inelasticity of existing contracts. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=100]]
