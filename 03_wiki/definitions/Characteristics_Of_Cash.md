## Thesis

While all forms of money share abstract properties (means of exchange, etc.), **Cash** possesses specific operational features that ensure its survival despite the rise of digital banking. These properties create high utility but also significant systemic costs for storage, transportation, and security.

## Definition

Düring identifies four crucial properties of cash that are often overlooked by classical economists:

1. **Immediate Finality of Payment:** Cash transfers are non-reversible and settled as soon as the physical exchange occurs. Unlike credit card or electronic payments, there is no "unwind risk" related to uncovered obligations or chargebacks.
2. **Anonymity:** Transactions can be completed between two parties without either party knowing the identity of the other. This provides absolute privacy but also makes cash a preferred medium for illicit activities.
3. **Decentralisation:** Cash transactions do not rely on third-party intermediaries, specifically not on a bank providing account or verification services. The value resides in the token itself.
4. **Identifiability:** Users can readily identify the asset as authentic and distinguish it from counterfeits without requiring specialized laboratory equipment (e.g., standard security features on banknotes).

### Boundaries / Conditions: The Cost of Cash

The very properties that make cash valuable also make it expensive to maintain.
- **Security & Theft:** Because it is anonymous and anonymous and decentralized, the incentive to steal cash is higher than for any other asset class.
- **Logistics:** Maintaining a cash system requires massive infrastructure for safe storage, special armored transport (e.g., stocking remote ATMs), and central bank processing/shredding.
- **The "Invisible" Tax:** Users rarely perceive these costs, but they are intrinsically tied to the benefits of finality and anonymity.
