## Thesis

The ancient and religious prohibition of **Usury** (charging interest) rests on the assumption that a lender has surplus cash while a borrower is in a desperate, emergency situation. In modern finance, this relationship has been structurally inverted: "lenders" are often middle-class pensioners saving for retirement, while "borrowers" are wealthy corporations or sovereigns seeking capital for strategic gain. This demographic shift is the primary driver behind the normalization of interest.

## Definition

The historical case against interest was built on three distinct intellectual and moral traditions:

### 1. The Aristotelian Philosophical Critique (Ancient Greece)
Aristotle viewed usury as the most unnatural form of wealth-getting. To him, money was a social convention intended solely as a medium of exchange, not a productive asset in itself.
- **The Gain Critique:** Profiting from exchange is "justly censured" because men gain from one another without producing new goods.
- **The Usury Quote:**
> *"The most hated sort, and with the greatest reason, is usury, which makes a gain out of money itself, and not from the natural object of it. For money was intended to be used in exchange, but not to increase at interest."* [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=4]]

### 2. The Scriptural Mandate (Judaism, Christianity, Islam)
- **Judaism:** Restricted interest primarily to fellow Jews (*brothers*), while allowing it for *strangers*.
  - *Deuteronomy 23:19–20:* *"Thou shalt not lend upon usury to thy brother... Upon a stranger thou mayest lend upon usury; but unto thy brother thou shalt not lend upon usury."* [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=4]]
- **Christianity:** The ban was absolute for centuries. The turning point was the papal encyclical ***Vix pervenit*** (1745) by Benedict XIV. It established a legal distinction between exploitative usury and legitimate interest for lending money, although it reaffirmed the moral rejection of "making a profit from money itself." [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=4]]
- **Islam (Shar’iah):** Retains the strictest prohibition on **riba** (interest). This is not just a moral rule but a legal absolute that prohibits "unconditional repayment obligations." It rules out investing money for interest but allows sharing in business profits (equity). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=4]]

### 3. The Ethical Dimension of the "Poor Borrower"
The book of *Exodus 22:25* provides the ethical anchor:
> *"If thou lend money to any of my people that is poor by thee, thou shalt not be to him as an usurer, neither shalt thou lay upon him usury."* [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5]]
This implies a fundamental inequity: the lender is wealthy, and the borrower is poor. In this context, charging interest is seen as the exploitation of another's survival needs for pecuniary gain. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The Demographic Inversion of Modern Finance
Düring argues that the moral stigma against debt remains because we haven't fully internalized the change in who the "lender" and "borrower" really are:
- **Historical Assumptions:** Lender = Rich; Borrower = Poor. Exploitation is the primary risk.
- **Modern Reality:** Lender = Pensioners/Middle Class (registered with pension funds/insurance); Borrower = Sovereigns/Corporations (wealthy entities). The "exploitation" risk is now frequently reversed, where sovereigns or large firms might "purpose" the erosion of their obligations to lenders. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5]]

### The Persistence of Predatory Lending
While interest is normalized, modern legal codes (upper limits on rates, KYC rules) maintain safeguards against **Predatory Lenders**. These are lenders who seek out borrowers expected to default so they can seize their assets—a modern iteration of the ancient moral concern. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=6]]
