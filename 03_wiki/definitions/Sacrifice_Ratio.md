## Thesis
The sacrifice ratio measures the cost of disinflationary policy in terms of the percentage of one year's real GDP lost to reduce inflation by one percentage point. [extracted]

## Definition
Central banks face a dilemma: reducing inflation drastically may trigger a severe recession. The sacrifice ratio quantifies this trade-off. [extracted]

### Mathematical Estimation
Based on the linear Phillips Curve:
$$\text{Sacrifice Ratio} = \frac{\Delta \text{Output Loss}}{\Delta \text{Inflation Reduction}}$$

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Anticipation:** If disinflation is fully anticipated and the central bank is credible, the ratio may be near zero (Sargent, 1983). [extracted]
- **Flexibility:** If prices and wages adjust quickly, the cost is lower.
- **Linearity:** The ratio may differ depending on whether the economy is overheating or in a downswing (non-linear Phillips Curve). [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Okun (1978):** Estimated a ratio of 10:1 (10% GDP loss for 1% inflation reduction).
- **Gordon & King (1982):** Estimated a ratio of 3.
- **Ball (1994):** Estimated a ratio of 2.3 using an atheoretical direct measurement approach.
- **United States 1979:** Radical tightening by the Fed led to a deep recession and high unemployment, illustrating the sacrifice ratio in practice. [extracted]
