## Thesis

Society's definition of money has transitioned from physical "Commodity Money" (anchored in intrinsic material value) to modern "Fiat Money" (anchored in sovereign decree and social agreement). Alexander Düring argues that while these systems seem diametrically opposed, even commodity money contains a "fiat" component through the convention of its use as a means of payment. The primary difference lies in the nature of the "straightjacket" that limits money creation: physical metal supply versus societal expectations.

## Definition

Money predicated on the perceived intrinsic material value of the underlying asset. Common examples include gold, silver, or highly durable consumables.
- **The Consumable Anchor:** In regions where mining was late (e.g., Central Europe), salt served as money because it was scarce, durable, and possessed alternative utility as food.
- **Islamic Law Precedent:** Shar’iah law explicitly recognizes **six commodities** that must be traded "equal for equal, and hand to hand": gold, silver, wheat, barley, dates, and salt. These rules govern modern FX transactions. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=11]]
- **The Stability Paradox:** It is common to believe that a gold standard anchors the value of other goods. Düring argues the reverse is also true: the monetary use of gold anchors the value of gold itself. This is illustrated by the massive volatility in gold prices relative to consumer indices after the **Nixon Shock** of 1971 (President Nixon's suspension of gold convertibility). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=11-12]]

### Debasement and Forgery
Historically, commodity systems were plagued by manipulation:
- **Debasement:** Governments issuing coins with lower purity to clandestinely reduce their own debt burdens.
- **Clipping:** Private forgers cutting the edges of coins to steal metal. This was effectively ended by **Sir Isaac Newton** during his tenure at the Royal Mint through the invention of the **rolling of coins** (milled edges). [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13]]

## Definition

Money (*Fiat:* Latin for "let there be") derived exclusively from sovereign decree ([[Lex_Monetae]]) and social consensus. It has zero intrinsic utility.
- **Early Form (Wampun):** Caribbean tribes used sea shells or curious stones (Wampun) which had no inherent value, representing money as a pure social agreement.
- **Modern Shift:** All modern currencies are fiat. The transition was often marked by the suspension of specie payments, such as the Bank of England's mandated suspension in 1797 during the Napoleonic Wars. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=13-14]]

### The Economically Meaningless Promise
Modern bank notes often retain historical language that is now circular:
- **Example:** The English five-pound note states "I promise to pay the bearer on demand the sum of..."
- **The Reality:** Because the note is **Legal Tender**, the Bank of England fulfills this promise simply by taking the note and handing the same (or a identical) note back to the bearer. The "promise" is a legal fiction; the value is entirely based on social contract. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=15]]

### The Transition: Specie vs. Bank Notes
Paper money exists in two forms that bridge the commodity-fiat gap:
1. **Certificates:** Documentary evidence of physical metal stored somewhere. These are 100% backed and originated in China (9th-11th century).
2. **Bank Notes:** Debt certificates payable on demand. The transition to banking occurs when the "trustee" of the metal becomes a "banker" by lending the physical specie to a third party while the certificates remain in circulation. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=14]]
