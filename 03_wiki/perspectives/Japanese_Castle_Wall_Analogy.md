## Thesis

Alexander Düring uses the architecture of Osaka Castle to argue that **occasional policy mistakes may have a hidden systemic purpose**. Just as a wall that becomes steeper toward the top apportionments the maximum damage to the most capable enemies, a central bank that successfully suppresses all small shocks may enable the build-up of massive leverage that eventually results in a fatal collapse.

## The Model: Design vs. Outcome

### 1. The European Wall (Linear)
- **Design:** Constant slope.
- **Outcome:** Shocks (attackers) fail at all levels proportionally. Constant small injuries/volatility.

### 2. The Japanese Wall (Convex/Steepening)
- **Design:** Gentle slope at the bottom, near-vertical at the top.
- **Mechanism:** "Adverse Selection." Poor attackers fail early with minor injuries. The most dangerous attackers reach the top before falling, resulting in fatal injuries.
- **Policy Parallel:** A central bank that offsets all incoming minor shocks (keeping the economy 'high' on the wall) leaves the system exposed to unforeseen shocks that trigger major, catastrophic deleveraging.

## Research Insight: The Minsky Connection
This analogy supports the **Minsky theory of the business cycle**: "Stability is destabilizing." A central bank that is "always right" creates an environment of low perceived risk, encouraging the very leverage that leads to the next "Minsky Moment." Active monetary policy may therefore have a purpose precisely because it may on occasion be **wrong**, flushing out excessive risk before it becomes systemic.
