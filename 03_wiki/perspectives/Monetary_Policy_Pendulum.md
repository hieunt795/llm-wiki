## Thesis

Central banks are rarely focused on all objectives equally. History shows a "pendulum" effect where a prolonged focus on one goal (e.g., price stability) often creates a blind spot that leads to a crisis in another area (e.g., financial stability), forcing the pendulum to swing back.

## The Pendulum Swings

### Swing 1: The Stability Era (1840-1914)
The pendulum was balanced between gold-anchored price stability and LoLR-based financial stability.

### Swing 2: The Political Era (1940-1970s)
The pendulum swung violently toward **Economic Growth** and fiscal support, leading to the "Great Inflation."

### Swing 3: The ITF Era (1980-2007)
The pendulum swung back to **Price Stability** and market efficiency. The success of Inflation Targeting (low inflation, low rates) led to a "Great Moderation" which ironically masked the buildup of systemic financial risks.

### Swing 4: The Post-GFC Era (2008-Present)
The GFC forced the pendulum back toward **Financial System Stability**. Central banks now recognize that price stability is a prerequisite but *not a guarantee* of macro-stability. "There is no macrostability without financial stability."

## Drivers of the Pendulum

- **Economic Conditions:** High inflation (1970s) drives focus to prices; high unemployment or banking collapse (2008) drives focus to stability.
- **Academic Thinking:** The rise of New Keynesian synthesis supported the ITF era; the failure of these models to predict 2008 led to the rise of macro-financial linkage theories (e.g., Stiglitz, Borio).
- **Political Pressure:** Democratically elected governments often push the pendulum toward growth, requiring institutional independence to swing it back to prices.
