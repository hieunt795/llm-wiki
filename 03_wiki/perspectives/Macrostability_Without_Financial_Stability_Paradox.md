## Thesis

The GFC turned standard academic thinking (New Keynesian synthesis) on its head. The assumption that stable inflation (ITF) would automatically lead the economy back to a steady state was proven wrong. Instead, stability itself can be a driver of risk—a phenomenon known as the **Financial Accelerator**.

## The Failure of the Single Mandate (Pre-2008)

Prior to 2008, central banks operated on three faulty assumptions:
1. **Price Stability = Macro Stability:** If inflation is low, everything is fine.
2. **Efficient Markets:** There are no frictions in the financial system; currency and credit are perfect substitutes.
3. **Microprudential Sufficiency:** If individual banks are sound, the whole system is sound.

## The Reality Post-GFC

- **The Financial Cycle:** Tends to amplify the economic cycle. Low rates stimulate rapid growth, which leads to excessive credit and asset price bubbles (stocks/housing).
- **Procyclicality:** Macrofinancial linkages create self-reinforcing loops. Rising asset prices increase collateral value, which encourages more borrowing, which further inflates asset prices.
- **Inevitable Frictions:** Asymmetric information and risk-taking behavior mean the financial system is always in a state of flux, capable of turning fragility into systemic risk without warning.

## Conclusion
Central banks must return to a **Dual Mandate**: achieving currency value stability *while* actively supporting financial system stability through a coordinated policy mix.
