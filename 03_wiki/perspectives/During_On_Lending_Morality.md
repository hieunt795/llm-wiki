## Thesis

Düring’s perspective on the morality of fixed income and debt is a fundamental challenge to the historical stigma attached to "usury." He contends that the ethical debate must evolve to reflect modern financial demography, where the "lender" is often a collective entity composed of middle-class savers whose own welfare depends on the successful repayment of the borrower.

## Thesis

Düring's perspective on the morality of fixed income and debt revolves around several key points:

### 1. The Shakespearean Defense (Henry V)
Düring draws a parallel between a King's responsibility for his soldiers and a lender's responsibility for a borrower using an argument from Shakespeare's *Henry V* (4.1). 
- **The Concept of "Purposing":** A King who sends a soldier on a mission does not "purpose" the soldier's death, even if the soldier dies. Similarly, a lender does not purpose the default or destitution of the borrower.
- **The Argument:** Most lenders prefer a borrower who pays on schedule. A pensioner invested in a mortgage does not seek the liquidation of the property; they seek the originally expected income stream. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5]]
> *"The King is not bound to answer the particular endings of his soldiers... for they purpose not their deaths when they propose their services."* [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5]]

### 2. The Mutual Convenience Shift
Historically, debt was viewed through a moral lens of "victimhood." Düring argues that for businesses, debt is a choice of convenience:
- **Control Preservation:** Business owners often prefer to pay interest on capital rather than form an "equitable partnership" (equity) where they have to share control and future profits. 
- **Modern Reality:** Borrowers are no longer just the poor; they are often sophisticated entities seeking capital to maximize ventures. Interest is simply the "rent" paid for that capital. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=5-6]]

### 3. Predatory Lending as an Exception
While Düring defends the systemic function of lending, he is not a radical deregulator. He acknowledges that **predatory lenders**—who seek out borrowers likely to fail in order to pursue their assets following default—do exist. 
- **Role of Regulation:** Modern legal codes (upper limits on interest, know-your-client rules) are necessary not because lending is inherently evil, but to prevent these specific, non-productive abuses of the legal system. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=6]]

## Mechanism

### The Stance of the Author
Düring explicitly declares that this book is **not neutral**. 
- **Finance as a Service:** He views fixed income analysis as an imperfect human endeavor. Practitioners must understand when tools fail and recognize their social responsibility. 
- **Societal Impact:** Debt is not just a mathematical entry; it is a social convention that impacts wealth distribution. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=7-8]]
