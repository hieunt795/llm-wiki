## Thesis

While the term "Inflation Targeting" sounds rigid, nearly all modern central banks (including the Fed and Bank Indonesia) practice **Flexible ITF**. The debate is centered on the parameter **$\lambda$** in the social loss function—how much pain in the real economy is acceptable to reach the inflation target?

## Comparison

### 1. Strict ITF ($\lambda = 0$)
- **Goal:** Minimize $(\pi_t - \pi^*)^2$ only.
- **Behavior:** Known as "Inflation Nutters" (Mervyn King, 1996). The central bank will raise interest rates aggressively to kill inflation even if unemployment is soaring.
- **Context:** Useful for banks with **Zero Credibility** who need to prove their "toughness" to the markets.

### 2. Flexible ITF ($\lambda > 0$)
- **Goal:** Minimize $\frac{1}{2} [(\pi_t - \pi^*)^2 + \lambda x_t^2]$.
- **Behavior:** The central bank allows inflation to return to the target more gradually to minimize "Output Variability."
- **Paradox:** Perry Warjiyo notes that flexible targeting is actually more complex to communicate, as the bank must explain why it is "tolerating" temporary high inflation.

## The Consensus: FIT is Superior
Modern research shows that Strict ITF can lead to massive "Social Costs" during supply shocks (e.g., an oil price spike). Flexible ITF allows the bank to look through temporary shocks while maintaining long-term anchor.
