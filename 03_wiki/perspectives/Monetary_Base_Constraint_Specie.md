## Thesis

Alexander Düring argues that while a metal-based monetary system is often praised for its inflationary discipline, it serves as an unsustainable "straightjacket" for a modern, globalized economy. The constraint for physical reserves (specie) means that monetary expansion is strictly tied to physical commodity availability. In the face of the massive growth in modern international trade, this physical tether inevitably leads to catastrophic domestic contractions or geopolitical conflicts to secure reserve metal.

## Thesis

The core of the argument against a return to commodity-based money centers on the mathematical and physical impossibility of its implementation in the modern world:

### 1. The "Straightjacket" Effect
In a metallic system, the monetary base is strictly constrained by the **Reserve Ratio**. Because the supply of precious metal (gold or silver) cannot be rapidly increased, the economy's ability to create credit and facilitate growth is physically limited by mining output. Any sudden surge in economic productivity that outpaces gold discovery will lead to severe deflation and economic stagnation as the money supply fails to keep up. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=16]]

### 2. Trade Volume Disparity (The Math of Gold)
The scale of modern international trade far exceeds the physical output of precious metals. Düring provides a striking comparison:
- **Case Study (2019):** The quarterly trade deficit of the USA alone averaged approx. $154bn.
- **Global Gold Supply:** In the same period (2018), total annual world gold production (3,332 tons) was worth only $135.5bn.
- **The Physical Imbalance:** Settling the US trade deficit alone would require more gold than the entire world produces in a year. Under a gold standard, this would necessitate continuous, massive outflows of physical gold, rapidly depleting the domestic monetary base of the USA and causing an immediate, severe economic depression. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=16]]

### 3. Geopolitical Consequences: The Opium Wars
The historical desperate need to rebalance metallic reserves often leads to war. Düring notes that the **Opium Wars** between the UK and China were fundamentally linked to the drain of sterling silver from the UK to India and China. To offset this drain and preserve its metallic monetary base, the UK systematically exported opium to China to generate the necessary silver income to rebalance its reserves. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=16]]

## Mechanism

Düring argues that while fiat money avoids these physical limits, it replaces them with **expectations-based constraints**:

1. **The Fiat Adjustment:** A large trade deficit in a fiat system creates doubts about the currency's future value, leading to depreciation.
2. **The Result:** This depreciation (ideally) makes the deficit country's goods cheaper and more competitive, correcting the imbalance over time without the catastrophic domestic contraction required by a metallic drain.
3. **The Consequence:** The physical constraint of metal is replaced by the psychological constraint of the market's faith in the sovereign. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch02.md#page=16]]
