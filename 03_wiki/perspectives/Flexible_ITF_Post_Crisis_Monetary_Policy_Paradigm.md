## Thesis
[RAW] Perry Warjiyo's perspective (2019): The 2008 GFC exposed ITF limitations, requiring fundamental paradigm shift toward flexible ITF incorporating macroprudential coordination and financial stability objectives alongside price stability.

## 1. Analytical Perspective - Crisis-Driven Framework Evolution

[RAW] Pre-GFC ITF consensus (1990s-2007):
- Central bank mandate = Price stability (low inflation) focus
- ITF framework sufficient for monetary policy governance
- Independence + Transparency + Target commitment = Policy credibility

[RAW] Post-GFC realization (2008 onward):
- **Lesson 1**: Financial system stability prerequisite for monetary policy effectiveness
- **Lesson 2**: Strict price stability focus insufficient when financial system fragility undermines transmission mechanisms
- **Lesson 3**: New central bank mandate required to "simultaneously overcome one of the prerequisites to successful ITF implementation, namely a sound and efficient financial system"

[LLM] The crisis demonstrated that ITF as standalone framework was "necessary but not sufficient" for macroeconomic stability—required complementary macroprudential policy layer.

## 2. The Global Context: Unprecedented Economic Shock

[RAW] Perry Warjiyo documents the GFC magnitude that forced paradigm reconsideration:

**IMF Forecast Revisions Mid-2008:**
- Initial projection: Global economic growth ~3.9%
- Revised continuously downward through 2008-2009
- Eventual outcome: Global growth ~3% with decelerating trend
- Deep contractions in advanced economies unprecedented since WWII

**Structural Damage vs. Cyclical Downturn:**
- Unforeseen decline in potential output (not just cyclical recession)
- Pre-2007 era: Robust growth + stable inflation ("Great Moderation")
- Post-2007: Weaker growth trajectory + elevated structural unemployment

[RAW] "For example, in the middle of 2008, the IMF still projected global economic growth at around 3.9% but ubiquitous uncertainty and risk compelled the international institution to revise down its projection again and again."

## 3. Policy Implications - Flexible ITF Framework Requirements

[RAW] Warjiyo identifies essential modifications to ITF implementation:

### Shift from Strict to Flexible ITF
[RAW] Pre-crisis ITF terminology:
- "Strict ITF" ("Inflation Nutters"): Central bank focuses exclusively on inflation gap
- "Flexible ITF": Central bank weights inflation gap, output gap, and interest rate smoothing

[RAW] Post-crisis reality: **No central banks apply strict ITF**. All practice flexible ITF variants that:
1. Consider financial system stability indicators
2. Accommodate temporary inflation shocks absent medium-term target threat
3. Balance price stability with output growth considerations
4. Integrate macroprudential policy tools

### Macroprudential Policy Integration
[RAW] New central bank policy mix required:
- Monetary policy: Traditional inflation targeting with medium-long term focus
- Macroprudential policy: Real-time financial system stability monitoring and intervention
- **Coordination requirement**: Optimal monetary-macroprudential policy mix formulation
- **Institutional implication**: Central bank mandate expanded to "concurrently maintain monetary and financial system stability"

[LLM] This represents fundamental mandate evolution: from single-objective (price stability) to dual-objective (price + financial stability) central banking.

### Enhanced Micro-Sectoral Understanding
[RAW] Macro-level modeling insufficient; required granular understanding:
1. **Price-setting mechanisms** by sector (critical for inflation pass-through)
2. **CPI component behavior** during shocks (differentiate core vs. headline pressure sources)
3. **Bank investment portfolio dynamics** (understand financial sector risk accumulation)
4. **Rupiah and FX money market conditions** (capture exchange rate-inflation nexus)

[LLM] This reflects post-crisis lesson: Financial system microstructure matters for transmission mechanism effectiveness—not captured in standard macro models.

### Communication Strategy Enhancement
[RAW] Monetary policy transparency alone insufficient; required:
- Continuous dialogue with academic community
- Market participant education on inflation expectations framework
- Public communication on financial stability considerations
- Clear explanation of monetary-macroprudential policy coordination

## 4. Indonesian-Specific Implementation Path

[RAW] BI's institutional response post-GFC (Chapter 15 elaborates):
- Shift to macroprudential policy framework (2010 onward)
- Integration of financial stability with monetary policy formulation
- Enhanced monitoring of systemic risk indicators
- Coordination with regulatory authorities (OJK, Ministry of Finance, LPS)

[RAW] Fundamental principle: "When formulating a post-crisis monetary policy strategy, the central bank must strengthen financial system stability to ensure a stable economy and financial system in terms of the macroeconomy and financial sector."

## 5. Rationale - Why Strict ITF Insufficient

[RAW] Three key vulnerabilities strict ITF cannot address:

1. **Asset Price Bubbles**: Pure inflation targeting ignores asset price inflation (credit-driven equity/real estate booms)
2. **Procyclicality Risk**: Monetary tightening during boom amplifies credit cycles
3. **Financial System Feedback Loops**: Credit contraction → demand destruction → inflation fall → but financial crisis simultaneously wreaks transmission mechanism damage

[LLM] The GFC revealed central banks need dual mandate framework: Use monetary policy for medium-term price stability, macroprudential tools for financial cycle stability.

## 6. Tension: Flexible ITF Challenges vs. Benefits

[RAW] Trade-offs with expanded flexible ITF approach:

**Benefits:**
- Prevents financial system stability deterioration that would undermine all monetary objectives
- Provides framework flexibility to maintain credibility during supply shocks
- Integrates financial regulation with monetary policy coordination

**Challenges:**
- Multiple objectives reduce policy clarity (vs. single inflation target)
- Instrument independence more complex (monetary tools + macroprudential tools)
- Communication complexity increases (must explain dual-objective tradeoffs to public)
- Measurement challenges (how to define "financial stability"?)
