## Thesis

Traditional monetary theory (both Classical and Keynesian) is based on the demand for money as a medium of exchange. Joseph E. Stiglitz argues that this focus is obsolete in a world of financial engineering. Perry Warjiyo bóc tách ranh giới của paradigm mới này: rủi ro và hành vi của ngân hàng trong việc cung ứng **Tín dụng** mới là trái tim của truyền dẫn tiền tệ, chứ không phải là lượng tiền M0 hay M2 do NHTW phát hành. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=69]]

## 1. Why Credit Matters (The Shift)

Stiglitz identifies two fundamental reasons why traditional metrics (M1/M2) are losing relevance:
- **Financial Innovation:** Products like Money Market Accounts (MMA) and credit cards have blurred the definition of money, decoupling transaction volume from central bank control.
- **The Intermediation Gap:** Monetary policy affects bank deposits directly, but it only affects the economy if those deposits are transformed into **Loans**. This transformation is governed by risk-aversion and asymmetric information. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=70]]

---

## 2. The Logic of Market Imperfection

Unlike general equilibrium models, the Stiglitz paradigm assumes financial markets are **always imperfect**.

### A. Asymmetric Information
Banks cannot perfectly know the borrower's probability of default. This leads to:
- **Adverse Selection:** Increasing interest rates may attract riskier borrowers while driving away safe ones.
- **Moral Hazard:** Higher rates may induce borrowers to take excessive risks to cover the higher interest cost.

### B. Credit Rationing
Because of these risks, the price mechanism (interest rate) fails to clear the market. Banks reach a **Bank-optimal Rate** where their expected return is maximized. Even if demand for loans remains high, banks will refuse to lend more, resulting in **Credit Rationing**. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=71-72]]

---

## 3. Strategic Implications: Policy Mix

Since "Credit Matters," central bank policy based solely on short-term interest rates is "impotent" in managing systemic risk.
- **The Solution:** Monetary policy must be strengthened by **Macroprudential** regulations that directly affect the quantity and standards of credit (e.g., LDR, reserve requirements).
- **The Target:** Output and inflation are driven by the *availability* of credit to fund production and consumption, not just the *price* of that credit. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=70]]
