## Thesis

The coexistence of private "Inside Money" with state-issued "Outside Money" is a modern compromise between two radical intellectual extremes. Proponents of **Free Banking** argue that the market should determine the quality and volume of money, while advocates of **Positive Money** argue that the state must hold a total monopoly on money creation to prevent instability. Alexander Düring observes that the popularity of these schools follows the economic cycle: markets are trusted during booms, while the state is called upon during recessions.

## Mechanism

Free banking proponents suggest that private banks should be allowed to issue banknotes and create deposits freely, with minimal central bank intervention.
- **Mechanism:** Users of currency would decide which banks to trust based on their transparency and prudence. Prudent banks would see their currency trade at par, while reckless banks would see their notes trade at a discount or be rejected.
- **Argument:** This market discipline forces bank managers to behave prudently (mirroring Basel Pillar III principles). 
- **Düring's Critique:** Historical evidence shows that free banking arrangements tend to end in instability and "trouble," as the coordination cost of trusting hundreds of disparate issuers becomes unmanageable during panics. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=37-38]]

### Perspective II: Positive Money / Vollgeld (The State Extreme)

Advocated in Germany as *Vollgeld*, this model seeks to outlaw the creation of private credit money ([[Bank_Money_Creation]]).
- **Mechanism:** Private banks are prohibited from expanding the money supply. To issue a loan, they must first borrow the necessary funds from the central bank. They function essentially as asset managers or payment processors.
- **The Credit Monopoly:** The total volume of credit in the economy is exactly controlled by the government.
- **Argument:** This eliminates bank runs (since deposits are 100% backed by sovereign money) and allows the government to capture all seigniorage.
- **Düring's Critique:** Giving authority over money creation to the economy's largest borrower (the government) creates a dangerous conflict of interest. It risks turning a decentralized market into a command economy where the state dictates the **allocation of credit**. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=38-39]]

### Boundaries / Conditions

### The Cyclical Popularity
Düring notes a distinct pattern in the advocacy of these ideas:
- **Boom Times:** Private actors feel constrained by oversight and advocate for **Free Banking** as a natural expression of enterprise.
- **Recession Times:** Public fear of bank failure leads to calls for state intervention and **Positive Money** concepts. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=38]]

### The Public Bank Fallacy
Positive money advocates often assume government-controlled banks are safer. Düring points out that public banks (like the German or Austrian *Landesbanken*) have historically collapsed just as frequently as private ones. He argues that running a public bank is no less complex than a private one, and state control does not inherently solve the problem of computational complexity in credit allocation. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=39]]
