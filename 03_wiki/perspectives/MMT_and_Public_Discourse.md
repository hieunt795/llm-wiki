## Thesis
Public economic discourse is straitjacketed by ideological metaphors (the government as household) that work at an unconscious, emotional level. Conservative economists have successfully deployed these to support austerity and fiscal conservatism; MMT's challenge is not economic proof but **linguistic and cognitive**, requiring new conceptual frames and educational reconstruction of public understanding.

## 1. The Problem: Macroeconomic Illiteracy in Public Discourse

[RAW] "Macroeconomics concepts, such as real GDP, inflation, the unemployment rate, the fiscal deficit, and the interest rate, make headlines on a daily basis. Finance segments on national news broadcasts increasingly discuss macroeconomic issues with frequent recourse to complex terms that are not well understood by most media commentators or the public."

[RAW] "Consequently, from the outset the public discourse contains significant errors that render it almost impossible for participants to make informed assessments of macroeconomic developments independently of the associated politics."

**Chain of illiteracy:**

```
Complex economic concepts (fiscal deficit, inflation, rates)
    ↓
Journalists and commentators lack training (degrees in humanities, not econ)
    ↓
Oversimplified narrative (government = household)
    ↓
Public internalizes metaphor unconsciously
    ↓
Immune to evidence (feels intuitively true)
    ↓
Policy becomes constrained by common sense ("government can't spend forever")
```

[RAW] "Economic concepts such as the government fiscal deficit contain nuances that make an unambiguous assessment of their meaning difficult. One cannot conclude for example, that a deficit equal to two per cent of GDP signals a more expansionary fiscal stance by government than a deficit of half the size. These complexities are lost to the public but are fundamental to an accurate understanding of the issues."

[LLM] Example: "Deficit of 2% of GDP" can reflect:
- **Secular situation:** Stable growth, mature private sector (Japan: 3% deficit, <1% growth)
- **Cyclical situation:** Recession recovery (US 2009: 10% deficit, -2.5% growth)
- **Compositional:** Tax cut (revenue ↓) vs. spending rise (same deficit effect, opposite multiplier)

A 2% deficit in a recession is **expansionary stimulus**; a 2% deficit in a boom is **restraint**. Raw number is uninformative.

## 2. The Role of Framing and Cognitive Metaphors

[RAW] "Framing refers to the way an argument is conceptualised and communicated by speakers and listeners. Conceptual models and representations are used in this process. Research in cognitive philosophy and cognitive linguistics suggests that the models that constrain our thinking operate at a largely unconscious level, and that the abstract concepts we draw on are 'largely metaphorical', 'imaginative', and 'emotionally engaged' (Lakoff and Johnson, 1999: 3–4)."

**Key insight:** Metaphors operate **pre-consciously**. Humans process economic concepts through **metaphorical mappings**, not pure logic.

**The household budget metaphor (dominant frame):**

```
GOVERNMENT spending is like HOUSEHOLD spending
├─ "Government must balance budget like household"
├─ "Government debt is burden on children"
├─ "Can't spend more than you earn forever"
├─ "Must save before you spend"
└─ "Moral imperative to avoid deficits"
```

[RAW] "Proponents of neoclassical macroeconomics have been extremely successful in their use of common metaphors to advance their ideological interests. Ideologically loaded myths – in modern parlance, 'fake knowledge' – are accepted unchallenged by the public as truths. Thus ideology triumphs over evidence and we accept falsehoods as truth."

## 3. Cognitive Biases Reinforce Dominant Framing

[RAW] "Recent psychological studies have highlighted the extent to which pre-existing biases influence the way in which we interpret factual information, including straightforward statistical data. This presents a further problem for the communication of research findings that may be counterintuitive or controversial, or that challenge the dominant discourse about public policy design, as in the case of economic austerity or climate change."

**Confirmation bias:** People selectively process data that confirms their pre-existing metaphors:
- Presented with evidence that austerity deepened recessions → brain pattern-matches to "government is wasteful" frame
- Interpret the same evidence as "proves government spending doesn't work"

**Narrative drift:** Progressive commentators adopt conservative language while opposing austerity:
- Say "We need to invest in infrastructure for long-term growth" (frames spending as **investment**, not consumption — implicitly concedes household budget logic)
- Better frame: "Unemployment is waste of human resources; government should employ anyone at minimum wage"

[RAW] "Further, it is often the case that the language that is used to advocate a progressive alternative actually serves to reinforce conservative myths (see Lakoff, 2010; Wray, 2013). It has been suggested that language is so important in learning that there is a need to develop new terms that are not associated with orthodox economics in order to communicate a new progressive macroeconomics, such as is developed in this textbook."

## 4. Two Competing Visions of the Economy

[RAW] "Figure 8.1 represents the conservative mainstream economics view, where the basic assumption is that 'people and nature exist primarily to serve the economy' (Shenker-Osorio, 2012: Location 439)."

### Vision A: Conservative (Economy as Natural System)

```
"Economy" is a natural entity beyond human control
    ├─ Self-regulating, self-correcting (invisible hand)
    ├─ Government intervention = interference with natural processes
    ├─ Markets reward merit; unemployment is choice
    ├─ Poverty is moral failure; inequality is incentive
    └─ "Undeserving poor" make bad choices → suffer legitimately
```

[RAW] "The economy is enshrined as an entity – almost a deity – that is essentially beyond our control although it recognises our endeavours, and rewards – or punishes – us accordingly. We are required to have faith, work hard and make the necessary sacrifices for the good of the economy; those who do not are rightfully deprived of such rewards."

[RAW] "The economy is also constructed as a living entity. If the government intervenes in the competitive process and provides an avenue by which the 'undeserving' (lazy and so on) can also receive rewards, then the system becomes 'sick'. The solution is to restore the economy's natural processes (its 'health'), which entails the elimination of government intervention such as minimum wages, job protection, and income support."

[LLM] Key metaphors:
- Economy = living organism (health/sickness)
- Unemployment = natural / deserved
- Welfare = unnatural interference

### Vision B: Progressive (Economy as Public Creation)

```
"Economy" is a human creation (institution) designed to serve public purpose
    ├─ We can redesign institutions to serve different goals
    ├─ Unemployment = market failure (insufficient aggregate demand)
    ├─ Inequality = policy choice (tax structure, wage floors, investment)
    ├─ Full employment = achievable goal (not magic)
    └─ Public purpose = democratic priority
```

## 5. Why MMT Has Struggled for Traction

[RAW] "MMT has struggled to gain traction in wider economic and political debates due to: (1) An incomplete understanding of key macroeconomic terms amongst economics commentators, especially journalists, and the wider community, including policy makers and the general public; and (2) The deployment of key macroeconomic terms in the context of pervasive cultural metaphors to support policy interventions that effectively benefit a privileged few at the expense of the majority."

**Barrier #1 — Illiteracy:**
- Journalists don't understand sectoral balances, reserve operations, or endogenous money
- Revert to household analogy (easiest mental model)

**Barrier #2 — Ideological deployment:**
- Conservative think tanks + media outlets produce "research" that frames deficits as inherently bad
- Framing becomes common sense ("everyone knows government can't spend forever")
- Policy makers internalize this common sense → fiscal conservatism becomes default

## 6. The GFC as Refutation: Why Mainstream Failed

[RAW] "Prior to the Global Financial Crisis (GFC), mainstream economists pronounced the business cycle dead and declared that we had entered a period of 'great moderation' (Stock and Watson, 2002; Lucas, 2003; Bernanke, 2004). These economists categorically failed to foresee the catastrophic consequences of the policy of deregulation of the labour and financial markets that they promoted."

[RAW] "It is reasonable to expect that professional failure on the scale of the GFC would have led to a re-evaluation of the paradigm within which these economists work, and to major changes in economic curricula and research. Mainstream economists however have failed to alter their teaching approaches in any significant way."

[LLM] Despite empirical falsification, mainstream economics retained hegemony because:
- Academic institutions have sunk costs in existing frameworks
- Ideological alignment with political power (financial industry funds think tanks)
- Conservative metaphors are emotionally resonant (people intuitive believe government is household)

## 7. The Language Problem: How Progressives Undermine Themselves

[RAW] "There remains a role for better education of essential concepts in addition to a reconsideration of how the concepts are related. But there is a tension between any benefits that may accrue from the development of such a new language, and the necessity to couch the argument in terminology that the public are familiar with when discussing economic outcomes."

**Dilemma:**
- **New language** (endogenous money, sectoral balances, functional finance) is precise but unfamiliar — public dismisses as jargon
- **Familiar language** (investment, deficit, borrowing) perpetuates old metaphors — public understands in wrong way

**Example of linguistic pitfall:**
- Progressive says: "We must invest in infrastructure (deficit spending)"
- Public hears: "Government borrowing like household taking loan" (implicitly validates household budget logic)
- Conservative: "Exactly, and households can't borrow forever"
- Progressive loses argument linguistically even though economically correct

## 8. Path Forward: Reconstruction of Understanding

[RAW] "In this textbook, we base our exposition around MMT, which is a coherent macroeconomic narrative providing new insights into the policy opportunities available to a government and their likely outcomes."

**Requirements for MMT traction:**
1. **Educational reconstruction:** Teach real operational flows (spending → deposits → taxes → reserve management), not metaphors
2. **New terminology:** Develop language not tainted by orthodox connotations
3. **Institutional leverage:** Academic research centers + public intellectuals to counter conservative think tank dominance
4. **Policy wins:** Demonstrate MMT-consistent policies working (full employment, price stability)
