## Thesis

The US Federal Reserve under Alan Greenspan (1987-2006) is the prime example of this strategy. By incrementally raising the FFR (e.g., 1994-1995) *before* inflation appeared, the Fed achieved a "Great Moderation" without formally committing to a target like the UK or New Zealand.

## Pros and Cons

### Pros
- **Maximum Flexibility:** The central bank can respond to any shock (financial, real, or political) without being constrained by a specific rule.
- **Success-Driven:** Proved highly effective in the US during the 1990s.

### Cons
- **Lack of Transparency:** Markets must constantly "second-guess" the Fed, leading to unnecessary shocks and uncertainty.
- **Low Accountability:** There are no clear criteria to evaluate if the central bank has succeeded or failed.
- **The "Greenspan Put" Risk:** Success depends on strong individuals; there is no institutional guarantee that future leaders will be as capable.
- **Time Inconsistency:** Highly vulnerable to political pressure (e.g., the storm of criticism from Congress in 1997 after a 25 bps hike).
