## Perspective

The unprecedented expansion of central bank power since 2008 has led to a dilemma regarding institutional legitimacy. Alexander Düring analyzes the shift from **Input Legitimacy** (adhering strictly to legal mandates and predefined rules) to **Output Legitimacy** (the ends justifying the means). This debate is most acute in the Eurozone, where the ECB faces severe institutional constraints. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75]]

## Thesis: The Logic of "The End Justifies the Means"

The term "Output Legitimacy" (used in public communications by former ECB board member Jörg Asmussen) argues that the necessity of a policy's success—such as saving the Euro—legitimizes actions that might otherwise fall outside a narrow interpretation of a central bank's mandate. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75]]

### 1. The Philosophical Critique
Alexander Düring invokes Karl Marx to critique this logic: **"If the end justifies the means, then the end is unjustified."** (German: *Wenn der Zweck die Mittel heiligt, dann ist der Zweck unheilig.*) 
- **The Logical Failure:** From a scientific standpoint, "output legitimacy" is meaningless because we can only observe one outcome (the present state). Any alternative outcome (e.g., "the system would have collapsed without QE") is pure conjecture. Legitimacy cannot result from conjecture. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75-76]]

### 2. The Legal Conflict (The German Court vs. The ECB)
The tension between input and output legitimacy reached a boiling point in May 2020, when the **German Constitutional Court** ruled that the ECB's Public Sector Purchase Programme (PSPP) was **unlawful (ultra vires)**.
- **The Argument:** The German court argued that the ECB had failed to provide an ex-ante "proportionality" justification. It ruled that a central bank must explicitly weigh the negative side effects of its policy against the expected positive contributions to its mandate.
- **The Significance:** This was a direct judicial rejection of "output legitimacy." It asserted that the ECB cannot expand its power purely by claiming its actions are "necessary" without detailed, rule-based justification of their proportionality. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=76]]

## Systemic Implications: The Institutional Dilemma

The ECB's reliance on output legitimacy stems from its unique design:
- **Asymmetric Governance:** It must manage a symmetric currency area but has no single finance ministry for coordination.
- **Limited Competencies:** EU member states have jealously guarded their prerogatives, leaving the ECB as the only institution capable of comprehensive action during a crisis, despite having been "carefully designed not to be able to act other than in very narrow ways." [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75]]
