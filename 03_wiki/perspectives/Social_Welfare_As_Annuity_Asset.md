## Thesis

Traditional wealth analysis focuses on traceable assets (cash, real estate, bonds). However, Düring argues that in modern social democracies, the right to access services like the NHS or receive a monthly stipend (like Germany's €409/month in 2017) represents a massive **Annuity Asset** that is missing from personal balance sheets but present on the national liability ledger.

## Case Studies: Valuation of "Invisible" Assets

### 1. The UK NHS Asset
- **Cost per head:** ~£1,800 per year (2015/16).
- **Valuation Logic:** A citizen with a 30-year life expectancy at a 1.8% discount rate.
- **Equivalent Wealth:** Simply by being registered, the citizen effectively holds an asset worth **£41,400**.
- **The Trade-off:** To access these services without the NHS, an individual would need to have saved this amount.

### 2. German Welfare Stipend
- **Amount:** €409/month (unconditional).
- **Valuation Logic:** A 30-year annuity at then-current interest rates.
- **Equivalent Wealth:** Effectively an asset worth **€127,000**.
- **Consequence:** Recipients of these unconditional claims are "well off" in asset terms, even if they have zero bank savings.

## The National Balance Sheet Connection

These "assets" for citizens have a mirror image on the **National Balance Sheet**:
- They must be funded by **taxes** or **government debt**.
- The UK government debt of £29,400 per head (2016) is the funding engine for these implied annuity assets.
- **Taxpayers** are the ultimate "contingent debtors" in this massive, society-wide fixed income relationship.

## Research Insight: The Virtual Creditor-Debtor Relationship
Modern society has legislated unconditional claims that resemble a fixed income stream. This creates a virtual relationship where claimants have a claim on taxpayers that is economically indistinguishable from a bond investor's claim on an issuer.
