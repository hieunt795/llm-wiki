## Thesis

Alexander Düring challenges the assumption that "perfect" monetary policy—defined as the total elimination of economic volatility—is desirable. He argues that active monetary policy may have a purpose not because it is always right, but precisely because it may on occasion be wrong. These "mistakes" serve a vital evolutionary function: they inject enough uncertainty into the system to prevent the unchecked accumulation of risk.

## Thesis

Düring uses the unique architecture of Japanese castle walls (e.g., Osaka Castle) to illustrate the adverse selection of risk in monetary policy.

### 1. The Design (The Steepening Slope)
Unlike linear European walls, Japanese walls become progressively steeper towards the top. 
- **The Rationale:** This design creates a filter for attackers. 
- **Adverse Selection:** Weak or incapable soldiers fail near the bottom, sustaining only minor injuries. The most capable and dangerous enemies reach the top, where their eventual fall results in fatal injuries. The wall selectively apportions the maximum damage to the greatest threats. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=41]]

### 2. Application to Monetary Policy
A central bank that successfully offsets every minor shock to the economy is building a "perfectly smooth" economic wall.
- **The Steady State:** The economy remains close to maximum output for longer periods.
- **The Build-up:** During this period of perceived stability, economic actors build up excessive risk positions (leverage) because they no longer fear failure.
- **The Catastrophe:** When a shock eventually arrives that is beyond the central bank's capacity to react, the economy has "further to fall." The very stability of the system ensured that the eventual failure would be catastrophic rather than corrective. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=42]]

### 3. The Minsky Link
Followers of **Hyman Minsky** would argue that periodic shocks generated by mistaken policy decisions are necessary. They "flush out" elevated leverage before it becomes systemic. 
- **The Lesson:** Stability is destabilizing. A central bank that interprets its mandate solely as "leaning against the cycle" (taking away the punch bowl) may be ensuring that when the party does end, the hangover is fatal. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=41-42]]

## Mechanism

### The Fallacy of Decomposition
Central banks face a difficult communication task when choosing between stability and resilience.
- **The Macroscopic View:** Lowering aggregate demand is a logical macroscopic variable to ensure long-term stability.
- **The Personal Reality:** At the microscopic level, this translates into job losses for individual workers. 
- **The Conflict:** Explaining that a "controlled" recession is necessary to prevent a "uncontrolled" collapse is politically fraught, as the central bank appears to be terminating employment contracts even though it is merely changing the probabilities of outcomes. [[Fixed_Income_Alexander_During_Ch01.md#page=42]]
