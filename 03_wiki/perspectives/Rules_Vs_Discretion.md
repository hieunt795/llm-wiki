## Thesis
The rules vs. discretion debate focuses on whether monetary policy should be guided by a predefined mechanistic formula (rules) or by the judgement and flexibility of policymakers (discretion). [extracted]

## Argument
### 1. Rules-Based Policy
- **Consistency:** Reduces uncertainty and eliminates the **Time-Inconsistency Problem**. [extracted]
- **Transparency:** Allows the public to verify central bank commitment.
- **Inflation Bias:** Prevents policymakers from creating surprise inflation to boost short-term output. [extracted]
- **Variants:** Constant rules (k-percent) or Feedback rules (Taylor/McCallum).

### 2. Discretion-Based Policy
- **Flexibility:** Allows the central bank to respond to unique or unforeseen shocks (e.g., global financial crises). [extracted]
- **Information:** Rules only capture a fraction of economic dynamics; discretion utilizes broad datasets and expert judgment. [extracted]

## Boundaries / Conditions
- **Modern Consensus:** Central banks cannot rely fully on pure discretion. Rules are a prerequisite for "sound" policy, but they are often applied with a degree of flexibility (constrained discretion). [extracted]

## Evidence / Source Anchors
- **Friedman (Monetarist):** Advocated for a constant k-percent money growth rule to avoid destabilizing fine-tuning. [extracted]
- **Kydland & Prescott (1977):** Proved that discretion leads to higher inflation without output gains. [extracted]
