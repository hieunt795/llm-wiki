## Thesis

The "Fear of Floating" is a multi-dimensional phenomenon where economic agents—from Central Banks to Corporate Treasurers—systematically avoid the theoretical efficiency of floating regimes in favor of the certainty of fixed benchmarks. This behavior is driven by high pass-through risks and budgetary constraints.

---

## 1. Macro Perspective: Exchange Rate Intervention (Warjiyo)

In international macroeconomics, "Fear of Floating" refers to countries that officially claim to have a floating exchange rate but intervene aggressively to minimize volatility. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=104]]

### Drivers (Emerging Markets Focus)
- **High ERPT (Exchange Rate Pass-Through):** Concerns that currency depreciation will lead to uncontrolled domestic inflation.
- **Currency Mismatch:** Large external debts denominated in foreign currency (USD) mean depreciation can trigger corporate bankruptcies and recessions (Balance Sheet Effect). [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=105]]
- **Trade Impact:** EMEs often have high import content in their exports, meaning depreciation does not always provide the expected competitive boost.

### Consequences
- This fear results in greater variability in **Foreign Reserves** and **Interest Rates** as they are sacrificed to stabilize the currency. Calvo and Reinhart (2002) found that only 20% of "floating" regimes are truly free-floaters. [[Perry_Central_Bank_Policy_P3.md#page=107]]

---

## 2. Market Perspective: Interest Rate Reluctance (Düring)

In fixed income markets, the term describes the institutional reluctance of issuers and investors to utilize variable interest rates (FRNs) despite their theoretical efficiency in managing duration risk. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch18.md#page=175]]

### The Deterministic Bias
- **Budgetary Certainty:** Corporations and governments prioritize predictable interest expenses. Floating rates complicate financial planning and earnings guidance, making fixed-rate "Bullet" bonds the safer political and administrative choice. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch18.md#page=175]]

### The Complexity Barrier (Convexity)
- **Hedging Friction:** Structured floaters (CMT/CMS) introduce complex convexity risks. Dealers must hedge these with swaps, exposing the structure to **Basis Risk** (e.g., the CMS–CMT spread). During market stress, these "hidden" risks make floating debt structurally unattractive. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch18.md#page=175]]
