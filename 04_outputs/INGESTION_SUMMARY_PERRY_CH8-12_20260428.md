### 7. **Credibility_Problem_Five_Causes.md** (Mechanism)
**Location:** `E:\Wiki Link\03_wiki\mechanisms\`  
**Lines Addressed:** 23-27 (combined)  
**Confidence:** 4/5

**Core Content:**
- [RAW] Five distinct credibility problem causes:
  1. **Time inconsistency** (Kydland-Prescott): Period-by-period temptation to deviate from announced commitment
  2. **Internal inconsistency**: Sectoral coordination failures (fiscal-monetary contradictions)
  3. **Asymmetric information**: Public lacks understanding of central bank commitment and capabilities
  4. **Stochastic economic shocks**: Unpredictable events cause policy programs to fail
  5. **Political uncertainty**: Lack of domestic political support undermines credibility
- [RAW] Indonesia 2000-2005 example: All five causes combining to require extended transition period
- [RAW] Credibility building requirements: Rules-based policy, internal consistency, transparency, shock preparation, political support

### 8. **Central_Bank_Independence_Dimensions.md** (Definition)
**Location:** `E:\Wiki Link\03_wiki\definitions\`  
**Lines Addressed:** 29-34 (combined)  
**Confidence:** 5/5

**Core Content:**
- [RAW] **Bofinger (2001) three-dimension synthesis:**
  1. **Goal independence**: Central bank sets monetary policy objectives (high: Fed; limited: UK/Canada/NZ; low: ECB)
  2. **Instrument independence**: Central bank autonomously sets operational targets and controls policy instruments
  3. **Personal independence**: Decision-making body exists without formal direction or informal government pressure
- [RAW] Additional dimensions proposed: Financial independence (Baka 1994, Mboweni 2000)
- [RAW] Comparative analysis:
  - Federal Reserve: Highest goal independence (broad mandate, dual mandate)
  - ECB: Limited goal independence (explicit price stability focus)
  - UK/Canada/NZ: Low goal independence (government sets inflation target value)
- [RAW] Debate: Should elected officials set goals (democratic legitimacy) vs. central bank autonomy?
- [RAW] Theoretical link: Independence → credibility → policy effectiveness through commitment devices

### 9. **Monetary_Policy_Transparency_Definition.md** (Definition)
**Location:** `E:\Wiki Link\03_wiki\definitions\`  
**Lines Addressed:** 36-37 (combined)  
**Confidence:** 5/5

**Core Content:**
- [RAW] **Poole (2003)**: "Accurately conveying accurate information, including all the information market participants need to form opinions on monetary policy"
- [RAW] **IMF Code of Good Practices (Sundarajan et al. 2003)**: Objective, framework, decisions, rationale, data, accountability disclosed in understandable/accessible/timely manner
- [RAW] **Geraats (2001) Five dimensions:**
  1. Political transparency: Inflation target, preferences, goals disclosed
  2. Economic transparency: Data, models, forecasts disclosed
  3. Procedural transparency: Meeting minutes, voting records (controversial)
  4. Policy transparency: Decisions, directives, forward guidance disclosed
  5. Operational transparency: Implementation details, market interventions disclosed
- [RAW] **Three drivers of transparency proliferation:**
  1. Independence demands accountability
  2. ITF implementation requires communication
  3. Financial market development necessitates expectation management
- [RAW] **Effectiveness evidence**: Inverse correlation between transparency and inflation; no evidence of output volatility exacerbation
