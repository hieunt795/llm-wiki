> **Thesis:** The ECB's institutional architecture is fundamentally defined by a 'Two-Pillar' compromise between Monetarist and Keynesian philosophies, leading to an ongoing structural conflict between strict legal mandates (Input Legitimacy) and the practical necessities of crisis management (Output Legitimacy).

**Key Mechanisms / Evidence:**
Unlike the Federal Reserve, which operates under a unified dual mandate within a single sovereign state, the European Central Bank (ECB) was built upon a fragile political and philosophical compromise. Its architecture is defined by the tension between the strict, inflation-focused traditions of the German Bundesbank and the broader macroeconomic goals of the wider Eurozone. This architecture manifests in two core dimensions: a theoretical "Two-Pillar" analytical framework, and a perpetual legal struggle between rules and outcomes. When established in 1999, the ECB avoided choosing between competing macroeconomic schools by adopting both, creating a dual-analytical system. ### [[Output_Legitimacy_In_Central_Banking]] | Output Legitimacy in Central Banking
- **Confidence:** 5
- **Source:** `03_wiki\perspectives\Output_Legitimacy_In_Central_Banking.md`

> **Thesis:** The concept of 'output legitimacy' in central banking suggests that the success of a policy's outcome justifies the means used to achieve it, a logic that creates profound legal and philosophical conflicts with democratic institutional mandates.

**Key Mechanisms / Evidence:**
The unprecedented expansion of central bank power since 2008 has led to a dilemma regarding institutional legitimacy. Alexander Düring analyzes the shift from **Input Legitimacy** (adhering strictly to legal mandates and predefined rules) to **Output Legitimacy** (the ends justifying the means). This debate is most acute in the Eurozone, where the ECB faces severe institutional constraints. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75]] The term "Output Legitimacy" (used in public communications by former ECB board member Jörg Asmussen) argues that the necessity of a policy's success—such as saving the Euro—legitimizes actions that might otherwise fall outside a narrow interpretation of a central bank's mandate. [[During_Fixed_Income_Ch10.md#page=75]] Alexander Düring invokes Karl Marx to critique this logic: **"If the end justifies the means, then the end is unjustified."** (German: *Wenn der Zweck die Mittel heiligt, dann ist der Zweck unheilig.*) - **The Logical Failure:** From a scientific standpoint, "output legitimacy" is meaningless because we can only observe one outcome (the present state). Any alternative outcome (e.g., "the system would have collapsed without QE") is pure conjecture. Legitimacy cannot result from conjecture.
