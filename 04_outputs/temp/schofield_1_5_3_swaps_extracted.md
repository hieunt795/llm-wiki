## 1.5.3 Swaps (Schofield: Trading Fixed Income, 2011)

**Source:** Schofield, Fixed Income Fundamentals — Interest rate swaps mechanics and variations

---

### Interest rate swaps

In its most basic form an interest rate swap consists of a periodic exchange of cash flows with one referenced to a fixed rate while the other is referenced to a floating rate of interest, such as a particular maturity of LIBOR. Swaps are traded on a notional amount basis, which is usually fixed. The notional amount of a swap is merely a reference value and does not represent an actual cash flow. It will simply determine the magnitude of any cash flow that is subsequently exchanged. Swaps are typically long term, with maturities that may extend out to 30 or 50 years. Although the deals have a long-term maturity, the exchange of cash flows will take place on a more frequent basis. The cash flows are calculated on a simple interest basis and are paid in arrears. Each market has adopted its own conventions as to the frequency of these payments. For example, in the USD market the convention is a semi-annual payment of fixed for a quarterly payment of LIBOR. Where the payment dates coincide, it is market convention for the cash flows to be netted. However, since the transaction is OTC, all of the terms and conditions are negotiable and so there are many different variations of the simple vanilla 'fixed/float' structure.

#### Example: Net Payment Calculation

To illustrate the principles involved, assume both fixed and floating are paid semi-annually, with the rates for the period being 5.00% and 4.50%, respectively. Cash flows are denominated in GBP, the notional amount is £10 million and there are 182 days in the 6-month period.

Since the two payments coincide, there will be a net payment of £24,931.51 in favour of the receiver of fixed.

#### Swap Quotation

Swaps are quoted on a bid-offer basis. The quotation is given in terms of the fixed rate. The key learning point is that since many investment banks will be running 'matched positions' (a mix of pay and receive positions that are profitable overall), the LIBOR cash flows are assumed to cancel out.

Some practitioners describe swaps as 'long' or 'short':
- At the **bid price**, the market maker is said to be 'long' the swap (a 'buying' position), buying a stream of LIBOR cash flows and paying a single fixed rate
- At the **offer price**, the market maker is selling the swap, delivering a stream of LIBOR cash flows for a single fixed price

#### Dealing Margins

Customers may end up paying more or receiving less than quoted interbank rates as banks apply a dealing margin comprising two factors:

1. **Liquidity charge**: The 'cost' of hedging the interest rate risk of entering into a single swap. If it is difficult to immediately offset the risk or if the transaction is on a large notional amount, this charge will be higher.

2. **Credit charge** (or Credit Value Adjustment): Reflects the risk of client default. Interbank and hedge fund clients with cash collateral agreements will have fairly small credit charges, but lower-quality or less-liquid collateral will incur larger charges.

---

**Key Insight:** Swaps can be thought of as a multi-period, cash-settled forward contract executed at a uniform fixed rate.
