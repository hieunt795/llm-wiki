# Impossible Trinity in the Modern Environment: Vietnam as a Paradigmatic Case of the Correlated Pressure Trap

---

## Executive Summary

The Impossible Trinity — the principle that an open economy cannot simultaneously achieve exchange rate stability, free capital mobility, and monetary policy autonomy — remains the binding constraint in open-economy macroeconomics. This paper argues that the 2025–2026 global environment has not merely tightened this constraint but altered its character in a specific and underappreciated way: **correlated external pressure from a single trigger set is eliminating the corner-switching mechanism that normally makes the trilemma manageable.**

Under normal conditions, a central bank navigating the trilemma can adaptively shift its position within the triangle — sacrificing more exchange rate stability when FX reserves thin, or reclaiming monetary autonomy when capital flows ease. This adaptive corner-switching is the practical policy response that makes the trilemma a *managed trade-off* rather than an *inescapable trap*. What 2025–2026 removes is precisely this flexibility: GFC tightening, tariff shocks, and geopolitical realignment hit all three corners simultaneously from the same external trigger vector, leaving no viable direction to shift.

For Vietnam, this external constraint compounds a pre-existing structural problem: no pure corner is viable. A hard peg fails due to insufficient reserves (import cover: ~3.0–3.5 months, at the IMF threshold boundary). A free float fails due to a three-stage Exchange Rate Pass-Through that transmits currency depreciation into import prices at a coefficient of ~0.41 — too high for inflation anchoring under free float. Capital account closure fails because Vietnam's FDI-led growth model structurally requires open capital flows. The State Bank of Vietnam (SBV) is not choosing Policy Mix as an optimal strategy; it is compelled toward it by structural exclusion from all pure corners.

This paper makes three analytical contributions. First, it clarifies the theoretical position of "simultaneous erosion" within the trilemma–dilemma–quadrilemma debate: simultaneous partial loss across all corners is not a refutation of the classical trilemma but its worst-case prediction for middle-ground countries — the novel finding is the *correlated shock mechanism* that eliminates corner-switching as a response. Second, it identifies an **ERPT Credibility Trap** that creates hysteresis in Vietnam's Policy Mix: the FX anchor that prevents inflation today prevents the regime credibility needed to structurally reduce ERPT, locking Vietnam into a local equilibrium of high pass-through and FX dependence. Third, it provides a structured empirical scorecard of Vietnam's current policy position with specific identification of failure thresholds.

**Keywords:** Impossible Trinity, Global Financial Cycle, Exchange Rate Pass-Through, Monetary Policy, Vietnam, Policy Mix, Capital Flow Management, Correlated Pressure Trap

## 1. Introduction

The Impossible Trinity originates with Mundell (1963) and Fleming (1962) and was formalized empirically by Obstfeld, Shambaugh, and Taylor (2005): a central bank in an open economy cannot simultaneously fix its exchange rate, maintain free capital mobility, and conduct an independent monetary policy. It must sacrifice one corner of the triangle. For decades, this framework shaped EME monetary policy design: choose your corner, accept the trade-off, and manage accordingly.

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis reopened the theoretical debate. Helene Rey (2013) presented evidence that global capital flows and credit conditions are driven primarily by Fed policy and VIX, synchronized across countries regardless of exchange rate regime — collapsing the trilemma to a dilemma in which capital controls, not exchange rate flexibility, determine the degree of monetary autonomy. Han and Wei (2018) refined this to a "2.5-lemma": the trilemma holds for tightening shocks from the center country, but fails for easing shocks — capital controls become effective precisely when the center country conducts QE. Kim (2024) confirmed the time-horizon split empirically: the trilemma governs short-term interest rate autonomy; the dilemma governs long-term financial condition independence.

This paper enters the debate at a specific moment — 2025–2026 — when these frameworks are not just theoretically interesting but simultaneously operative. The Fed holds short-term rates at 3.50–3.75% (a tightening stance where, per Han-Wei, the trilemma should hold for short-term autonomy) while fiscal dominance effects push 10-year yields to 4.32% independently of the policy rate (a long-end dynamic where the dilemma applies). Tariff shocks inject supply-side inflation that monetary policy cannot address through demand-side tools. Geopolitical realignment fragments capital flows by political logic rather than return maximization. The three main frameworks in the literature — trilemma, dilemma, and quadrilemma — are all operative simultaneously.

Vietnam represents a paradigmatic case study in this environment for two reasons. First, its structural constraints are unusually sharp: high ERPT rules out free float; thin reserves rule out hard peg; FDI dependence rules out capital account closure. Vietnam is not *choosing* to operate from the middle of the triangle — it is *structurally excluded from all corners*. Second, Vietnam's de jure / de facto regime gap is unusually wide: legally mandated as an Inflation Targeting Framework, operationally run as an FX targeting regime with credit quota as the primary instrument. This gap is itself informative about the costs of trilemma management under real-world institutional constraints.

**Research Question:** Under what conditions does the classic trilemma transform from a managed trade-off — amenable to corner-shifting — into a simultaneous erosion trap where corner-switching itself becomes unavailable? And what does this structural shift imply for Vietnam's Policy Mix strategy?

**Contributions:** This paper makes three contributions to the analytical literature on EME monetary policy. (1) It identifies the *Correlated Pressure Trap* as the specific mechanism by which 2025–2026 GFC conditions worsen the trilemma beyond its classical characterization — not by making individual corners harder to hold, but by correlating pressure across all corners from the same trigger set, eliminating adaptive corner-switching. (2) It identifies an *ERPT Credibility Trap* specific to Vietnam: the FX anchor that controls inflation in the short term prevents the regime transition needed to structurally reduce ERPT, creating path dependence in Vietnam's policy constraint set. (3) It provides a structured empirical scorecard assessing Vietnam's current policy mix against Warjiyo's (2019) three-layer framework, with specific quantification of reserve adequacy risk and banking system fragility.

**Structure:** Section 2 reviews the theoretical evolution from trilemma to dilemma to quadrilemma, and Section 2.4 addresses the consistency question directly. Section 3 presents the simultaneous erosion mechanism for 2025–2026. Section 4 applies the framework to Vietnam with empirical evidence, including the ERPT Credibility Trap. Section 5 examines the Fed's parallel constraint. Section 6 maps unresolved tensions, with a revised framing of T5. Section 7 presents the main conclusions. Section 8 provides policy recommendations.

## 2. Evolution — From Trilemma to Dilemma to Quadrilemma

### 2.1 Global Financial Cycle: Rey's Dilemma (2013)

[WIKI: [[Monetary_Vs_Financial_Trilemma]], source: Perry Warjiyo p.34]

Helene Rey (2013) presented evidence that global capital flows and leverage are driven primarily by **Fed policy** and **VIX** — regardless of the exchange rate regime of the receiving country.

```text
REY'S GLOBAL FINANCIAL CYCLE — TRANSMISSION MECHANISM

  FED EASING                              FED TIGHTENING
       │                                        │
       ▼                                        ▼
  Risk appetite ↑                         Risk appetite ↓
  VIX falls                               VIX rises
       │                                        │
       ▼                                        ▼
  Capital flows → EME ↑                  Capital flows ← EME ↓
       │                                        │
       ▼                                        ▼
  Credit expands vs. CB intention         Credit contracts vs. CB intention
       │                                        │
       ▼                                        ▼
    [Float regime AND Peg regime affected identically]

  CONCLUSION: Exchange rate is NOT a shock absorber
               → Trilemma reduces to DILEMMA:

  Open Capital  ──→  No Autonomy    (regardless of peg or float)
  Managed Capital ──→ Partial Autonomy (regardless of peg or float)
```

**Implication:** Float does not insulate. Whether Vietnam floats or pegs, its financial conditions are synchronized with the Fed cycle through the global risk-appetite mechanism.

### 2.2 Financial Trilemma: Obstfeld's Quadrilemma (2015)

[WIKI: [[Monetary_Vs_Financial_Trilemma]], source: Perry Warjiyo p.35]

Maurice Obstfeld extends the framework by adding **Financial Stability** as a fourth objective. Global risk premium spillovers — transmitted via long-term interest rates, not short-term — bypass exchange rate regimes entirely. Even when a central bank holds its short-term policy rate independently, long-term financial conditions are governed by the global cycle.

Quadrilemma: A central bank must simultaneously manage (1) price stability, (2) exchange rate stability, (3) financial stability, and (4) capital flow volatility — with an instrument set insufficient to cover all four. FX Intervention (FXI) + Capital Flow Management (CFM) + Macroprudential tools represent Warjiyo's (2019) practical prescription: "blur the corners" by maintaining partial stability in all objectives simultaneously.

### 2.3 False Trilemma: Armenter's Framework (2026)

[RAW-CLIP: Roc Armenter, "False Trilemmas," Philadelphia Fed, 2026-03-12]

Armenter's critical contribution is recognizing that the trilemma is not a law of nature — it is a signal that the *current institutional configuration* has reached the frontier of what is feasible. The trilemma is:

- **Locally true:** With current institutional quality, fiscal framework, and capital market depth fixed, the three objectives cannot be simultaneously achieved.
- **Globally false:** If institutional variables (CB independence, fiscal consolidation, capital market depth) improve, the frontier shifts outward and the trilemma can be broken.

For Vietnam in 2026: the global variables needed to break the trilemma are locked by external political economy factors (US tariff trajectory, geopolitical realignment, global fiscal dynamics). Locally true + globally locked = effectively permanent within a 2–3 year horizon.

### 2.4 Trilemma, Dilemma, or 2.5-lemma? Addressing the Consistency Debate

A common critique of papers in this space is that the trilemma, dilemma, and quadrilemma cannot all be simultaneously true — they make different claims about what the operative constraint is, and using them interchangeably produces theoretical inconsistency.

This critique is valid but resolvable. The three frameworks are not competing theories making the same prediction — they operate at **different time horizons and across different parts of the yield curve**:

```text
FRAMEWORK RECONCILIATION — WHERE EACH APPLIES

  ┌──────────────────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Framework        │ Domain of Validity                            │
  ├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Mundell-Fleming  │ Short-term interest rate autonomy.            │
  │ Trilemma         │ Empirically confirmed: OST (2005), Klein &    │
  │                  │ Shambaugh (2015). Corner choice matters for   │
  │                  │ policy rate independence.                      │
  ├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Rey Dilemma      │ Long-term financial conditions and credit     │
  │ (2013)           │ cycles. Exchange rate regime does not insulate│
  │                  │ long-end or credit conditions from GFC.       │
  │                  │ Kim (2024): confirmed for long-rate autonomy. │
  ├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Han-Wei 2.5-lemma│ Shock direction asymmetry. Trilemma holds     │
  │ (2018)           │ for Fed tightening (current 2026 cycle).      │
  │                  │ Dilemma applies for Fed easing/QE episodes.   │
  ├──────────────────┼───────────────────────────────────────────────┤
  │ Obstfeld         │ Fourth dimension: financial stability.        │
  │ Quadrilemma      │ Operative when long-end conditions diverge    │
  │                  │ from policy rate (term premium channel).      │
  └──────────────────┴───────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  2026 SPECIFICS: We are in a tightening cycle (trilemma governs
  short-rate autonomy) + fiscal dominance long-end divergence
  (quadrilemma/dilemma governs long-rate conditions) SIMULTANEOUSLY.
  All four frameworks are operative at the same time — not contradicting
  each other but operating on different dimensions of the policy space.
```

**On the internal consistency of T5 (Simultaneous Erosion):** The most important clarification for this paper is that *simultaneous partial loss across all three corners is not a refutation of the classical trilemma — it is its prediction for middle-ground countries.*

The trilemma says: you cannot fully achieve all three. A country operating from the middle of the triangle (Vietnam's forced position) will achieve *partial* levels of each objective — and in a stress scenario, all three will show partial deterioration simultaneously. This is exactly what the trilemma predicts. The mistake would be to interpret "all three erode" as "the trilemma is wrong" — in fact it is the trilemma being precisely confirmed.

**What IS novel in 2025–2026** is not the simultaneous erosion per se but the **Correlated Pressure Trap mechanism**: under normal conditions, a middle-ground country can *adaptively shift* toward whichever corner offers the most defense in a given cycle. When FX is under pressure, sacrifice more capital mobility. When inflation rises, sacrifice more FX stability for rate flexibility. This corner-shifting — the practical management response — remains viable as long as the pressures on different corners are *uncorrelated* or come from different sources.

When all corners face pressure from the **same external trigger vector** (GFC tightening → USD strength → capital outflows → import cost increase → all three objectives simultaneously degraded), corner-shifting itself becomes unavailable: every adaptive move sacrifices a dimension that is *also already under pressure*. This is the analytical contribution that goes beyond existing frameworks, and is empirically observable in Vietnam's 2025 data: SBV sacrificed autonomy (rate held at 4.5%) but FX stability still deteriorated (VND -3.98%) because GFC pressure on FX exceeded FXI capacity independent of the rate decision.

## 3. Modern Impossibility 2025–2026 — Why the Trilemma Is Worse Than Ever

[WEB-2026-04-29: CFR; Morningstar; LPL Research; iShares; streetstats.finance]

The classical model assumed the three corners are **independent** — sacrifice one, protect two. The dangerous novelty of 2026 is that all three corners have **positive correlation under stress** — eroded *simultaneously* by the same trigger, making trade-off logic non-operational.

### 3.1 Corner FX Stability — Eroded by GFC

Fed holds at 3.50–3.75% (after 75bps cuts in late 2025), but USD strengthens due to term premium >0.8% (highest since 2011) + US fiscal uncertainty. EME faces double pressure: capital outflow (USD yield attractive) + import cost rise. VND lost 3.98% in the first ten months of 2025 despite SBV intervening $4.4B — demonstrating that GFC pressure exceeded FXI capacity when the cycle is sustained. [WEB-2026-04-29: fastbull; streetstats.finance]

### 3.2 Corner Monetary Autonomy — Eroded by GFC + Instrument Mismatch

Rey (2013): float does not protect autonomy — GFC synchronizes financial conditions globally via VIX, bypassing exchange rate regime. In 2026, even the Fed — the *originator* of GFC — is losing control over the long end: 10Y yield at 4.32% despite Fed funds at 3.50–3.75% → fiscal dominance loop (deficit → Treasury supply → term premium → long-end rises regardless of Fed).

Tariff shock adds an instrument mismatch layer: cost-push inflation (supply-side shock) cannot be resolved by demand-side tools (policy rate). Rate hike → stagflation deepens. Rate cut → inflation expectations unanchor. No dominant strategy exists at the policy rate level. [WEB-2026-04-29: CFR; +34bps in 7 days post-tariff announcement]

### 3.3 Corner Capital Mobility — Eroded by Geopolitical Realignment

Tariff escalation and geopolitical bloc formation are fragmenting capital flows by political logic, not return maximization. FDI relocates according to supply chain logic (Vietnam benefits from China+1 dynamics), but this is *managed* mobility — contingent on political relations, tariff regimes, and supply chain continuity — not the *free* mobility of classical Mundell-Fleming. Currency manipulator watch-list risk adds a further constraint: SBV cannot deploy FXI freely when doing so risks triggering US Treasury designation, which would trigger tariff penalties and undermine the FDI inflow that FXI is trying to protect.

### 3.4 Armenter's "Global Lock" — Why the Trilemma Cannot Be Broken Now

[RAW-CLIP: Armenter 2026] The trilemma becomes globally false only if the institutional variables (quality of fiscal framework, capital market depth, CB independence) can be improved. In 2026, the most important such variables are locked by external political economy:

- Tariff policy: non-negotiable in a 2–3 year horizon
- US fiscal trajectory: deficit path deteriorating with no credible consolidation plan
- Geopolitical realignment: US-China decoupling is mid-process with no reversal path

**Locally true + globally locked = effectively permanent over a 2–3 year horizon.**

## 4. Vietnam — Why No Corner Is Viable and the Policy Mix Is Incomplete

[WEB-2026-04-29: OECD Survey 2025; vietnamnews.vn; fastbull.com; fulcrum.sg] + [RAW-BOOK: Warjiyo (2019), Ch.8]

### 4.0 Structural Constraints — Why Vietnam Is Trapped in the Middle

**Corner A (Fixed + Open Capital → sacrifice Autonomy completely):** Not viable. Vietnam's FX reserves are insufficient to defend a hard peg when the GFC cycle turns. Asian Financial Crisis 1997 demonstrated the outcome: countries with hard pegs and thin reserves face speculative attacks that exhaust reserves quickly. A currency board (Hong Kong model) requires reserves equal to 3–4× the monetary base and deep financial markets — Vietnam has not reached that threshold.

**Corner B (Float + Open Capital → sacrifice FX Stability):** Not viable due to ERPT ~0.41 at the import price stage. VND depreciation of 1% → import prices increase ~0.41%, feeding into CPI at a rate of ~0.22% per 1% depreciation (three-stage transmission). Free float during a GFC downturn (USD appreciating) = uncontrolled imported inflation that breaks the inflation anchor entirely. This is a structural constraint, not a policy preference — SBV is compelled to anchor FX because of how deeply exchange rate moves transmit into domestic prices.

**Corner C (Fixed + Capital Controls → sacrifice Capital Mobility):** Vietnam's historical model until the 2000s, but structurally incompatible with the current export-led, FDI-anchored growth model. FDI provides $20–25 billion per year in FX supply — the primary natural hedge against import demand. Closing the capital account eliminates this hedge, severs the technology transfer channel, and places Vietnam outside the global supply chain networks that are the core of its competitiveness.

**Conclusion:** No corner is viable. Policy Mix is not an optimal choice — it is the only choice available given structural constraints.

### 4.1 Vietnam's Position in the Triangle

Vietnam is not at any pure corner. A three-layer analysis:

- **De jure (declared):** ITF — NHNN Law 2010 mandates price stability; National Assembly sets 4.5% inflation target for 2026.
- **De facto operational target:** Exchange rate — SBV publishes USD/VND central reference rate each morning with ±5% band. This is the real daily management instrument.
- **Intermediate instrument:** Credit quota — 15% credit growth ceiling for 2026, allocated room by bank. Quantity-based, not price-based. OECD 2025 recommends transition to price-based instrument.

### 4.3 Conditions Under Which the Policy Mix Fails

Three specific failure modes:

1. **USD strength sustained >12 months:** FX reserves are depleted if SBV must defend continuously. At ~3.0–3.5 months import cover (see Section 4.4), VN is at the IMF threshold. A sustained dollar cycle depletes buffer to below minimum adequate levels. FXI becomes non-viable → SBV must either widen the band (accept more VND depreciation) or allow accelerated ERPT into CPI.

2. **Sudden FDI deceleration:** Vietnam uses FDI inflow ($20–25B/year) as natural FX supply to offset import demand. If supply chain relocation pauses (US-China deal, Vietnam tariff escalation, or regional competition), FX pressure rises while the natural hedge contracts simultaneously.

3. **Credit quota → NPL surge:** Credit growth of 15–17% sustained in a slow-growth environment → NPL accumulation. Official NPL at ~2%, but including restructured loans under Decree 08/2023 framework, effective NPL is ~4–5%. If NPL surges, SBV must inject liquidity → balance sheet expands → fiscal space for FX defense contracts → FX defense and financial stability become jointly unmanageable.

### 4.5 The ERPT Credibility Trap — A New Structural Constraint

The ERPT data in Vietnam's wiki reveals a dimension of the trilemma that the classical framework does not capture: **ERPT is not an exogenous structural parameter but a function of monetary regime credibility**.

```text
ERPT THREE-STAGE TRANSMISSION (Warjiyo 2019)

  VND Depreciation 1%
        │
        ▼ Stage 1 (Port Prices)
        ERPT = 0.63
        │
        ▼ Stage 2 (Import Prices)
        ERPT = 0.41  ← SBV's primary concern
        │
        ▼ Stage 3 (CPI)
        ERPT = 0.22  ← Final inflation impact

  REGIME DEPENDENCE (Warjiyo 2019, p.99–100):
  High-inflation history → ERPT ~0.79 (agents expect pass-through)
  Low-inflation history  → ERPT ~0.50 (firms absorb in margins)
  Post-ITF (Brazil)     → ERPT 0.72 → 0.05 (expectations anchored)
  Post-ITF (Indonesia)  → 10% depreciation: 6% CPI → 3% CPI
```

The critical insight: Vietnam's ERPT at the import price stage (~0.41) is not purely a structural feature of an open, import-dependent economy — it is **partly a consequence of lacking a credible ITF anchor**. When agents expect the central bank to use the exchange rate as its primary nominal anchor, they have no reason to absorb FX cost shocks into margins; they pass them through immediately. High ERPT is partly self-fulfilling under an FX-targeting regime.

**The Credibility Trap:**

```text
ERPT CREDIBILITY TRAP — VIETNAM'S HYSTERESIS LOOP

  Step 1: High ERPT (~0.41)
     → FX depreciation directly passes into import prices
     → Inflation risk from VND weakness is structurally high
          │
          ▼
  Step 2: SBV anchors on FX rate
     → Rate frozen at 4.5%, credit quota as primary instrument
     → De facto FX targeting, not ITF
     → No credible inflation anchor independent of FX
          │
          ▼
  Step 3: Agents learn the regime
     → Price-setters embed FX expectations directly
     → No reason to absorb FX costs in margins
     → ERPT STAYS ELEVATED (~0.41)
          │
          └────────────────────────────────── LOOP BACK TO STEP 1

  THE TRAP: FX anchor needed to PREVENT ERPT from hurting inflation
            FX anchor PREVENTS the ITF credibility that would
            REDUCE ERPT structurally
            → Vietnam is stuck in a local equilibrium of
              HIGH ERPT + FX DEPENDENCE

  ESCAPE: Requires deliberately accepting short-term ERPT volatility
          to build ITF credibility (Brazil/Indonesia path)
          Preconditions: reserve buffer, fiscal space, CB independence
          Horizon: 3–5 years of credibility-building
          Political cost: HIGH (inflation spike during transition)
```

This trap has an important implication for the Policy Mix assessment: the credit quota is not merely an inefficient instrument — it actively undermines the credibility transition. When the primary tool for containing demand is quantity-based (room allocation) rather than price-based (rate signal), there is no learning mechanism by which agents can form inflation expectations anchored to a rate-setting CB. The OECD's recommendation to "transition to price-based instrument" is correct, but it understates the challenge: it is not a technical instrument swap but a multi-year credibility-building exercise requiring fiscal consolidation and reserve accumulation as preconditions.

## 5. US/Fed 2026 — The Paradox of the GFC Creator

Mechanism: Fiscal deficit → Treasury supply increases → term premium rises above 0.8% → 10Y yield at 4.32% despite Fed funds at 3.50–3.75% → mortgage rates elevated → credit conditions tighten beyond Fed's intention. The Fed is "holding" but financial conditions are "tightening" via an exogenous channel it cannot directly manage.

**T1 — Rey Dilemma vs. Warjiyo Policy Mix:**
If float makes no difference (Rey), Policy Mix is delay, not solution. The empirical question is: what percentage of monetary autonomy does FXI+CFM actually restore in Vietnam? Per Han-Wei (2018), capital controls matter more during easing shocks than tightening — Vietnam is currently in a tightening shock environment, meaning capital controls provide less protection than in a QE cycle. This is an empirical gap requiring a dedicated BIS/IMF study on EME Policy Mix effectiveness under different shock regimes.

**T2 — ERPT: Structural Necessity or Long-Term Trap:**
ERPT ~0.41 (import price stage) justifies FX anchor for short-term inflation control. But the ERPT credibility trap (Section 4.5) shows this creates hysteresis: FX anchor prevents building the ITF credibility that would reduce ERPT. Vietnam is trading short-term inflation stability for long-term financial fragility — an intertemporal trade-off the classical trilemma does not capture.

**T3 — Armenter False Trilemma vs. 2026 Reality:**
Globally false if institutional variables improve. But in 2026, these variables are locked by external political economy. Resolution: the trilemma is falsifiable — but the 10-year reform program needed to break it (capital market depth, CB independence, fiscal consolidation) cannot be initiated during a stress cycle. The prescription and the current policy space are in different time horizons.

**T4 — FXI and Currency Manipulator Risk:**
If SBV uses FXI to maintain VND weaker than fair value, US Treasury designation as currency manipulator triggers tariff penalties → FDI slowdown → FX pressure increases → more FXI needed → escalation loop. SBV must calibrate FXI to appear as "volatility smoothing" rather than "resisting appreciation" — a political constraint that limits the operational scope of its primary Policy Mix tool.

**T5 — Resolving the "Simultaneous Erosion" Claim:**

The critical clarification is that T5 is *consistent with* the trilemma, not a contradiction of it. What is novel is the mechanism:

```text
T5 RESOLUTION: CORRELATED PRESSURE TRAP

  CLASSICAL TRILEMMA (middle-ground position):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Operating from the middle = partial loss in all 3   │
  │ This is EXACTLY what the trilemma predicts          │
  │ It is NOT a refutation — it is a confirmation       │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  WHAT MAKES 2025-26 DIFFERENT (the novel contribution):
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │ Under NORMAL conditions:                            │
  │   → Pressure on corners comes from different        │
  │     sources, at different times                     │
  │   → Adaptive corner-shifting available:             │
  │     If FX pressured: sacrifice more autonomy        │
  │     If inflation rises: sacrifice more FX stability │
  │   → Policy Mix WORKS because pressures are          │
  │     uncorrelated                                    │
  │                                                     │
  │ Under CORRELATED PRESSURE (2025–26):               │
  │   → Same trigger (GFC tightening) hits ALL corners  │
  │   → No safe direction to shift corner               │
  │   → Sacrificing autonomy doesn't protect FX        │
  │     (GFC pressure on FX persists regardless)       │
  │   → Corner-shifting itself becomes unavailable      │
  │   → Policy Mix BREAKS because pressure correlation  │
  │     eliminates the trade-off that makes it work    │
  │                                                     │
  │ EMPIRICAL CONFIRMATION (Vietnam 2025):              │
  │   SBV sacrificed autonomy (rate frozen at 4.5%)    │
  │   VND still lost -3.98% despite $4.4B FXI          │
  │   Autonomy sacrifice did NOT protect FX stability   │
  │   → Correlated Pressure Trap confirmed              │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

This reframing is the paper's core theoretical contribution: the trilemma predicts partial loss at intermediate positions; the **Correlated Pressure Trap** is what explains why 2025–2026 makes this partial loss uniquely hard to manage — not because the trilemma fails but because the adaptive mechanism that makes it livable (corner-switching) is removed by the correlation structure of external shocks.

## 7. Conclusion

The Impossible Trinity remains theoretically and empirically valid. The trilemma–dilemma–quadrilemma debate in the literature does not resolve to one winner: the trilemma governs short-term interest rate autonomy; the dilemma governs long-term financial condition independence; the quadrilemma captures financial stability spillovers from the long end. In 2025–2026, all three are simultaneously operative at different layers of the same policy problem.

Vietnam represents the paradigmatic case of the trilemma's worst-case prediction: a country structurally excluded from all pure corners, forced to operate from the middle of the triangle. High ERPT rules out free float; thin reserves rule out hard peg; FDI dependence rules out capital account closure. The Policy Mix that results is not an optimizing choice but a structural necessity.

The paper's three analytical contributions can be summarized as follows:

**First, the Correlated Pressure Trap mechanism:** The novel feature of 2025–2026 is not that corners erode simultaneously — that is what the trilemma predicts for middle-ground countries. The novel feature is that the external pressure on all three corners originates from the same trigger set (GFC tightening), positively correlating the simultaneous pressure and eliminating corner-switching as the adaptive management tool. Vietnam's 2025 data provides empirical confirmation: sacrificing monetary autonomy (rate held at 4.5%) did not protect FX stability (VND -3.98%), because the GFC pressure on FX was independent of the rate decision.

**Second, the ERPT Credibility Trap:** ERPT is not exogenous — Brazil reduced it from 0.72 to 0.05 by building ITF credibility. Vietnam's FX anchor, while necessary for short-term inflation control, prevents the regime transition that would structurally reduce ERPT through credibility. The result is hysteresis: high ERPT justifies FX anchor, FX anchor prevents ITF credibility, ITF absence keeps ERPT elevated. The OECD's recommendation to "transition to price-based instrument" is correct but underspecified — the credibility trap requires a sequenced program of reserve accumulation, fiscal consolidation, and explicit CB mandate clarification before a price-based transition can be attempted.

**Third, reserve adequacy as the binding constraint:** Vietnam's FX reserves at ~3.0–3.5 months import cover sit exactly at the IMF adequacy threshold. The distance between current reserves and the credibility breach point — not the current absolute level — is the primary near-term vulnerability. FXI draws down this buffer when sustained. A 12-month USD strengthening cycle of >10% would require intervention in the range of $15–20B to defend the band, potentially breaching the 3-month threshold.

## 8. Policy Recommendations

Policy recommendations are organized by time horizon, reflecting the distinction between emergency stabilization (0–2 years), structural improvement (2–5 years), and regime transformation (5–10 years).

**Short-term (0–2 years): Preserve buffers, formalize constraints**

(1) *FXI calibration to reserve adequacy threshold.* SBV should establish an internal rule linking FXI deployment pace to the distance above the 3-month import cover floor. Intervening to smooth volatility is consistent with US Treasury criteria; intervening to deplete reserves below IMF thresholds is not a viable strategy. Transparent internal limits would also improve forward guidance on reserve commitment.

(2) *Implement Countercyclical Capital Buffer (CCyB) at a non-zero level.* Vietnam's CCyB is currently at 0% — the lowest feasible setting. Implementing CCyB at even 0.5–1.0% during the current period of relative credit expansion would build a capital buffer that can be released in a stress scenario, reducing the NPL-to-liquidity transmission risk identified in failure mode 3. This is a low-cost, high-option-value policy.

(3) *Currency manipulator risk management.* SBV should formally track the criteria for US Treasury currency manipulator designation — the $15B bilateral trade surplus threshold, the FXI threshold as % of GDP, and the current account surplus — and calibrate FXI accordingly. FXI communication should emphasize volatility-smoothing rather than level-defense.

**Medium-term (2–5 years): Structural instruments reform**

(4) *Credit quota to sector-differentiated macroprudential levy.* Replace the annual quantity-based credit growth ceiling with sector-differentiated macroprudential levies — higher capital requirements for real estate and speculative lending, lower for productive-sector credit. This preserves the macroprudential function of credit control while creating a price signal that allows rate mechanism to function. OECD 2025 supports this direction. Resistance from banking sector (which benefits from quota-as-entry-barrier) is the main implementation obstacle.

(5) *Formal CFM framework.* Codify capital flow management rules as a transparent, rules-based regime — distinguishing FDI (welcome, minimal restrictions), portfolio equity (conditional openness), short-term debt (managed via reserve requirements), and derivative positions (monitored). Ad hoc CFM creates uncertainty for FDI; a formal framework allows investors to price political risk explicitly, improving FDI quality if not quantity.

(6) *CB operational mandate clarification.* Formally separate the SBV's credit quota target-setting process from the Ministry of Finance/government directive channel. A published monetary policy framework document — stating explicitly that the primary mandate is price stability, with exchange rate stability as a supporting objective — would begin the credibility accumulation process. This does not require full CB independence (a longer-horizon reform) but does require a clear mandate hierarchy.

**Long-term (5–10 years): Regime transformation toward genuine ITF**

(7) *ERPT reduction program.* The precondition for everything else. Modeled on Brazil and Indonesia's post-ITF experiences, a 5-year program targeting ERPT reduction from ~0.41 to <0.25 at the import price stage would require: sustained below-target inflation outcomes, transparent inflation forecast publication, and active communication linking rate decisions to inflation forecast rather than FX movements. This requires reserves >$120B ($4+ months cover) as a buffer for the volatility that will occur during the credibility-building phase.

(8) *Capital market deepening.* Government bond market (~$80B) is insufficient for price discovery or yield curve formation. Corporate bond market reform — rebuilding after the 2022–2023 crisis via transparent issuance standards, mandatory rating, and professional investor requirements — would provide a domestic financial benchmark independent of FX and allow SBV to observe inflation expectations directly. A deep domestic capital market also reduces the structural FDI dependence that makes capital account closure catastrophically costly.

(9) *Managed float transition with pre-announced widening schedule.* When preconditions (ERPT <0.25, reserves >$120B, formal ITF credibility established) are met, a phased widening of the FX band from ±5% to ±7.5% to ±10% over 3–4 years would allow genuine float to emerge gradually, with SBV FXI retained as backstop rather than primary anchor. Pre-announcing the widening schedule is essential: it gives firms time to develop FX hedging instruments and reduces the shock of regime transition.

**Feasibility Assessment:**

| Recommendation | Feasibility | Main Constraint |
|---|---|---|
| CCyB at non-zero level | High | Regulatory process only |
| FXI calibration rules | High | Internal SBV decision |
| Currency manipulator mgmt | High | Already practiced informally |
| Credit quota → macropru levy | Moderate | Banking sector resistance |
| Formal CFM framework | Moderate | Requires inter-agency coordination |
| CB mandate clarification | Moderate | Requires political cover |
| ERPT reduction program | Low–moderate | Long horizon, credibility risk |
| Capital market deepening | Low–moderate | Post-crisis regulatory trust needed |
| Managed float transition | Low | Requires all preconditions met first |

The honest assessment is that all short-term recommendations are actionable within the current institutional framework. Medium-term recommendations require coordination across government and the banking sector — feasible but not automatic. Long-term recommendations require a degree of CB independence and fiscal consolidation that goes beyond the SBV's own mandate, making them contingent on a broader political economy shift that the SBV cannot engineer alone.

The trilemma, in the end, is locally true because the conditions for making it globally false — institutional quality, capital market depth, fiscal space — are political products as much as economic ones. For Vietnam, breaking the trilemma in the long run requires building those conditions deliberately and sequentially, starting with the short-term steps that are already within reach.
